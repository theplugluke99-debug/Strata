## 1. CATEGORY OVERVIEW — WHAT RESIN FLOORING IS

### 1.1 Definition

Resin flooring is a category of floor finish applied in liquid or semi-liquid form and cured in situ to produce a seamless, continuous floor surface. The term covers a wide range of chemically distinct systems — from thin decorative coatings (0.3mm) to thick structural systems (9–12mm) — all sharing the characteristic of being poured, rolled, or trowelled onto a prepared substrate and curing through a chemical reaction.

The defining characteristic of resin flooring across all system types is **seamlessness** — there are no joints in the finished floor surface (apart from designed movement joints). This makes resin floors the preferred specification for:
- Environments requiring hygienic, bacteria-free floors (food production, healthcare, pharmaceuticals)
- Environments requiring chemical resistance (industrial, laboratories)
- Environments requiring easy cleaning and decontamination
- High-impact or heavy-load environments where individual tiles would fail
- Aesthetic applications requiring a monolithic, continuous surface

### 1.2 Market Positioning

Resin flooring occupies a specific market position:
- **It is not a DIY product in any serious application** — the chemistry, substrate preparation requirements, and application skill needed for a durable result require professional training and equipment
- **It is primarily specified in commercial, industrial, healthcare, food production, and high-specification residential**
- **Microcement and decorative overlays** bridge into the premium residential market and are increasingly specified by interior designers for contemporary residential kitchens, bathrooms, and living areas
- **Polished concrete** occupies a similar design-led residential and commercial position

### 1.3 The Spectrum of Systems

From thinnest to thickest; from lightest to heaviest duty:

| System | Typical dry film thickness | Primary application |
|--------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| Thin film epoxy coating | 0.3–1.0mm | Light commercial; decorative; dust-sealing |
| Self-smoothing (self-levelling) epoxy | 2–4mm | General commercial; pharmaceutical |
| Epoxy mortar screed | 4–9mm | Heavy industrial; food production |
| Polyurethane (PU) coating | 1–3mm | Commercial; some food industry |
| PU mortar screed | 6–9mm | Food and beverage; heavy wear |
| MMA coating/screed | 1–6mm | Cold rooms; rapid cure required |
| Polyaspartic coating | 0.5–3mm | Garages; decorative commercial |
| Microcement overlay | 2–5mm | Decorative residential and hospitality |
| Terrazzo (epoxy-bound) | 10–15mm | Public buildings; luxury interiors |
| Polished concrete | In-situ concrete | Contemporary commercial and residential |

## 2. RESIN CHEMISTRY — THE FOUNDATION

### 2.1 What All Resins Have in Common

All resin flooring systems share the fundamental characteristic of **cross-linking polymerisation** — a chemical reaction that converts a liquid or semi-liquid monomer/pre-polymer system into a hard, solid, chemically resistant network of polymer chains. Understanding this chemistry explains why:
- Surface preparation is critical (the adhesion depends on chemical bonding to the substrate)
- Temperature affects curing (cold slows or stops the reaction; heat accelerates it)
- Moisture in the substrate causes problems (water interferes with the chemical cure)
- Once cured, resin is extremely difficult to remove

### 2.2 The Two-Component System

Most professional resin flooring products are two-component (2K) systems:
- **Component A (base/resin):** The reactive polymer or prepolymer; contains the polymer chain precursors
- **Component B (hardener/curing agent):** The chemical that initiates and completes the cross-linking reaction

When combined in the correct mix ratio, the hardener reacts with the resin, initiating a cross-linking reaction that proceeds to completion (full cure) over a defined period. The mix ratio must be exact — incorrect ratios produce a floor that is either too soft (insufficient hardener), too brittle (too much hardener), or has uncured residue (very poor performance).

**One-component systems:** Some coatings (moisture-curing polyurethane, some epoxy systems) are one-component — they cure by reacting with atmospheric moisture or atmospheric oxygen rather than a separate hardener. These are simpler to apply but generally have lower performance than two-component systems.

### 2.3 Pot Life, Open Time, and Cure Time

- **Pot life:** The time after mixing the two components during which the material remains workable and can be applied. Critical for specification — a material with 20-minute pot life must be applied within 20 minutes; thereafter it thickens and cannot be spread properly.
- **Open time:** Related to pot life; specifically the time available to work and finish the surface after application
- **Walk-on time:** The time after application before light foot traffic is permissible; typically 12–24 hours for epoxy; 4–6 hours for MMA
- **Full cure:** The time to achieve full mechanical and chemical resistance; typically 7 days for epoxy; 1 hour for MMA; 24 hours for polyaspartic

**Temperature and cure:** Cross-linking reactions are temperature-dependent:
- Below approximately 10°C, most epoxy systems cure very slowly or incompletely
- At 5°C, many epoxy systems essentially stop curing
- MMA systems cure at temperatures down to −30°C — a unique advantage
- Higher temperatures accelerate cure but shorten pot life
- The ambient and substrate temperatures at application must be checked; typically a minimum of 10°C for epoxy and 5°C for PU systems

## 3. EPOXY FLOORING SYSTEMS

### 3.1 Epoxy Chemistry

**Epoxy resins** are formed from the reaction of epichlorohydrin with bisphenol A (BPA) or other polyols to form epoxide groups (three-membered rings containing oxygen). These epoxide groups are highly reactive. When combined with an amine, amidoamine, or anhydride hardener:

Epoxide + Amine → Cross-linked epoxy polymer network

The result is a very hard, strong, chemically resistant thermoset polymer. Unlike thermoplastics (which can be re-melted), the cross-linked epoxy network is permanent and cannot be reversed.

**Key properties of cured epoxy:**
- Very high compressive strength (typically 60–100 MPa)
- Excellent adhesion to concrete, steel, and other substrates
- High chemical resistance (acids, alkalis, solvents — with specific formulations)
- Low moisture permeability
- Good abrasion resistance
- Susceptible to UV degradation (yellowing and chalking under UV light — epoxy is not suitable for external applications or sunlit rooms without UV-stable topcoat)
- Brittle relative to polyurethane — will crack under impact or significant substrate movement
- Limited operating temperature: cured epoxy can soften or lose adhesion if the floor surface reaches 50–60°C (relevant in commercial kitchen cooking areas, near industrial ovens)

### 3.2 Epoxy System Types

**Epoxy primer:**
- The first coat applied to the prepared substrate
- Penetrates the substrate pores; provides chemical bond between substrate and subsequent coats
- Thin (0.1–0.2mm); often solvent-based for deeper penetration; increasingly water-based for VOC compliance
- Low-viscosity primer: penetrates concrete pores; seals against rising moisture to a degree

**Thin-film epoxy (paint-type coating):**
- 1–3 coats; total dry film thickness 0.3–0.8mm
- Dust-sealing; basic protection; aesthetic enhancement of concrete
- Limited durability in heavy traffic
- Typical applications: warehouses requiring dust control; light commercial; parking decks (specialist systems)

**Self-smoothing (self-levelling) epoxy (SLE):**
- The most widely specified general commercial epoxy system
- Applied by roller, squeegee, or trowel at 2–4mm thickness
- Flows to a smooth, level finish under gravity (the "self-levelling" property)
- Products available with various aggregate additions for anti-slip
- Seamless, hygienic, attractive floor surface
- Wide range of colours (the pigment is incorporated into the epoxy)
- Applications: pharmaceutical manufacturing; electronics; food packaging; office environments; corridors; laboratories
- Aggregate broadcast: fine quartz or coloured glass/EPDM rubber granules can be broadcast (scattered) into the wet epoxy for texture, colour effect, and anti-slip

**Epoxy mortar (trowel-applied):**
- Epoxy resin + amine hardener + a high loading of fine aggregate (quartz sand, silica flour)
- Applied at 4–9mm thickness using a serrated screed bar and hand trowel
- Extremely high compressive strength (>70 MPa); very high abrasion resistance
- For: heavy industrial; food and beverage production; areas with heavy wheel loads, forklifts, pallet trucks
- The aggregate loading means it is significantly more impact-resistant than pure epoxy and much harder than PU systems
- Can be specified with anti-slip aggregate surfacing
- Coved skirting integral: the mortar can be applied to the base of walls in a cove former to create a seamless hygienic coved skirting (as vinyl coving — Lexicon 06, Section 15)

**Epoxy screed:**
- A hybrid between self-levelling and mortar; applied at 3–6mm; trowelled flat
- High aggregate loading but more fluid than mortar
- Good impact and abrasion resistance; easier application than full mortar system

**Flake/chip broadcast epoxy:**
- Self-smoothing epoxy as base coat; coloured vinyl or EPDM flakes broadcast (scattered) densely into the wet base coat; sealed with clear epoxy topcoat
- Produces a speckled, multi-colour decorative appearance
- Very popular in garage floors, showrooms, sports facilities
- The flakes provide texture (anti-slip) and hide any underlying substrate variation
- Topcoat seal determines performance: polyaspartic seal is the premium option (UV stable; hard)

### 3.3 Epoxy and UV — A Critical Limitation

Standard epoxy formulations yellow, chalk, and lose gloss under UV exposure. In any space receiving direct sunlight or strong UV (skylights, glass roofs, south-facing rooms), either:
- Specify a UV-stable aliphatic polyurethane or polyaspartic topcoat over the epoxy base
- Or specify a polyaspartic or aliphatic PU system throughout (see Sections 6 and 4)
- Never specify standard epoxy as the topcoat in a sunlit space

### 3.4 Epoxy Colour Range and Aesthetics

- Custom RAL colours available from most manufacturers
- Wide standard colour range: light grey, mid grey, dark grey, industrial yellow (safety marking), red oxide, cream, white
- Clear epoxy: applied over decorative aggregate, coloured concrete, or terrazzo to seal and protect
- Metallic epoxy: mica or metalite pigment flakes producing a swirled, iridescent appearance; primarily residential decorative; applied with a notched squeegee to produce the pattern; sealed with PU or polyaspartic topcoat

## 4. POLYURETHANE FLOORING SYSTEMS

### 4.1 PU Chemistry

**Polyurethane (PU) resins** are formed from the reaction of a polyol with an isocyanate to produce urethane linkages:

Polyol (–OH) + Isocyanate (–NCO) → Urethane linkage (–NHCOO–)

The resulting polymer network has very different properties from epoxy:
- **More flexible and elastic than epoxy** — PU has significantly higher elongation at break; it can deform without cracking; this makes it more tolerant of minor substrate movement and thermal cycling
- **Better impact resistance** — the elastic network absorbs impact rather than fracturing
- **Excellent abrasion resistance** — particularly for PU screed systems used in food production
- **Temperature resistance:** Most PU flooring systems resist higher service temperatures than epoxy; some PU screeds are rated to 120–140°C (relevant in commercial kitchens near cooking equipment)
- **UV stability:** Aliphatic PU systems (using aliphatic rather than aromatic isocyanates) are UV-stable; they do not yellow under sunlight — a critical advantage over epoxy

**Aromatic vs aliphatic PU:**
- **Aromatic PU:** Uses aromatic isocyanates (MDI, TDI); lower cost; better chemical resistance; but yellow under UV — not suitable as visible topcoat in sunlit areas (acceptable as primer or base coat)
- **Aliphatic PU:** Uses aliphatic isocyanates (HDI, IPDI); UV-stable; does not yellow; used as the topcoat in UV-exposed areas; also used in exterior applications

### 4.2 PU System Types

**PU coating:**
- Thin to medium (1–3mm); applied by roller or squeegee
- Used as a topcoat over epoxy or other base systems; provides UV stability, flexibility, and gloss retention
- Also used as a standalone system in commercial applications with moderate chemical exposure
- More flexible than epoxy; better suited to substrates with residual movement

**PU screed / cementitious PU (CPU):**
- A unique hybrid: polyurethane prepolymer + amine hardener + calcium aluminate cement + aggregate
- This combination produces a material with the flexibility of PU and the breathability of cementite
- Applied at 6–9mm by trowel
- **The dominant system in the food and beverage sector** — key advantages:
  - Withstands thermal shock: boiling water cleaning (100°C) and steam sterilisation (120°C+) do not crack or debond the floor
  - Excellent abrasion and impact resistance
  - Good chemical resistance (organic acids, cleaning chemicals, hot oils)
  - Can be installed in occupied cold rooms and food production areas in cold weather (down to −20°C substrate temperature with specialist products)
  - Anti-microbial additives can be incorporated
- The most specified heavy-duty commercial system in breweries, dairies, meat processing, confectionery, bakeries

**Moisture-tolerant PU screed:**
- Some PU screed products are specifically formulated to tolerate moisture in the substrate
- Allow installation at substrate RH up to 95–98% RH with appropriate primer
- Very significant advantage in basement and cold room environments

### 4.3 PU vs Epoxy — When to Specify Which

| Property | Epoxy | Polyurethane |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| Compressive strength | Very high | High |
| Flexibility | Low (brittle) | High (elastic) |
| Impact resistance | Moderate | Very good |
| Thermal shock resistance | Poor | Excellent (PU screed) |
| UV stability | Poor (aromatic) | Good (aliphatic) |
| Chemical resistance | Excellent | Very good |
| Operating temperature | 50–60°C max (some) | 120–140°C (PU screed) |
| Abrasion resistance | Very good | Very good |
| Moisture tolerance | Low (standard) | High (some systems) |
| Installation temperature | Min 10°C | Min 5°C (standard); lower with specialist |
| Cost | Lower | Higher (PU screed) |
| Best for | Pharmaceutical; electronics; dry industrial | Food and beverage; thermal shock areas |

## 5. METHYL METHACRYLATE (MMA) FLOORING

### 5.1 MMA Chemistry

Methyl Methacrylate (MMA) flooring is based on acrylic chemistry — the polymerisation of methyl methacrylate monomers, initiated by a peroxide catalyst:

MMA monomer + Catalyst → Poly(methyl methacrylate) — PMMA

The catalyst is applied separately (typically as a powder or liquid sprinkled/added at mixing) and initiates rapid free-radical polymerisation. The reaction is exothermic (generates heat) and extremely fast.

**Key characteristics:**
- **Extremely fast cure:** Walk-on time 1–2 hours; full cure in 2–4 hours; the fastest-curing professional resin system available
- **Temperature range:** Cures from −40°C to +40°C — the only resin system that reliably cures in sub-zero temperatures; the critical advantage for cold room and freezer floor installation
- **Very strong adhesion:** PMMA bonds very strongly to concrete and other substrates
- **UV stable:** Does not yellow under UV (unlike aromatic epoxy) — suitable for sunlit areas without additional topcoat

### 5.2 MMA Limitations

- **Very strong smell:** MMA monomer has a powerful, pungent odour (like very strong nail varnish); requires full respiratory PPE (respirator with organic vapour cartridges) during application; the odour dissipates within a few hours of cure but is extremely unpleasant and potentially harmful if unprotected
- **Very short pot life:** 10–20 minutes at normal temperatures; experienced applicators only; the fast cure that is the key advantage is also the primary challenge
- **Brittle when cured:** PMMA is less flexible than PU; not the best choice for substrates with significant movement
- **Fire hazard (monomer):** MMA monomer is highly flammable; no ignition sources during application; fire safety protocol essential

### 5.3 MMA Applications

- **Cold rooms and refrigerated warehouses:** The definitive application; only system reliable at sub-zero temperatures
- **Food processing areas requiring rapid return to service**
- **Outdoor areas and car parks** (UV stable; withstands temperature extremes)
- **Repair and patching** of other resin floors requiring rapid return to service
- **Hospital and pharmaceutical** where rapid installation turnaround is essential

## 6. POLYASPARTIC FLOORING

### 6.1 Chemistry and Background

Polyaspartic coatings are a sub-category of aliphatic polyurea chemistry, developed in the 1990s as a faster-curing, UV-stable alternative to conventional two-component PU:

Polyaspartic ester (amine component) + Aliphatic isocyanate → Polyaspartic polymer

**Key properties:**
- **UV stable:** Aliphatic isocyanate structure does not yellow under UV — ideal for sunlit or outdoor applications
- **Very fast cure:** Walk-on time 1–4 hours; full service in 12–24 hours; much faster than standard epoxy
- **High flexibility:** Better elongation than epoxy; tolerates substrate movement
- **Good chemical resistance**
- **Low VOC:** Many polyaspartic systems have very low solvent content
- **Wide temperature application range:** Down to −20°C for some formulations

### 6.2 Polyaspartic Applications

- **Garage floors** (residential and commercial): UV stable (sunlight through garage doors); fast cure; good chemical resistance to oil, fuel, and automotive fluids; the premium residential garage floor system
- **Car showrooms and dealerships:** Aesthetically superior to standard epoxy; UV stable; often combined with flake broadcast
- **Outdoor and covered external areas:** Terraces, car parks, loading bays
- **Commercial retail environments** with natural light
- **Topcoat over epoxy:** Polyaspartic as a final topcoat over epoxy base system provides UV stability to an otherwise UV-sensitive base

### 6.3 Polyaspartic vs Epoxy — Residential Garage

| Factor | Epoxy (standard) | Polyaspartic |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|
| UV stability | No (yellows in sunlight) | Yes |
| Cure time | 12–24 hours walk-on; 7 days full | 2–4 hours walk-on; 24 hours full |
| Scratch resistance | High | Very high |
| Flexibility | Low | Good |
| Chemical resistance (oil/fuel) | Good | Good |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Professional install | Yes | Yes |

**The professional recommendation for any domestic garage floor:** polyaspartic or epoxy with polyaspartic topcoat.

## 7. CEMENTITIOUS OVERLAYS AND MICROCEMENT

### 7.1 What Cementitious Overlays Are

Cementitious overlays are thin polymer-modified cement products applied over existing floors to renew surfaces, create a seamless appearance, or apply a decorative finish. Unlike resin systems, the binder is cement (calcium aluminate or Portland cement) modified with polymer latex or powder to improve adhesion, flexibility, and durability.

### 7.2 Microcement (Microcemento / Micro Beton)

**Overview:**
Microcement (also called microtopping, micro concrete, micro beton, or Microcemento) is a thin, polymer-modified cementitious coating applied in very thin layers (typically 2–4mm total) to produce a seamless, concrete-like appearance on any surface — floors, walls, furniture, and even objects.

It is not concrete — it does not have the structural properties of concrete. It is a decorative coating system.

**Composition:**
- Portland or calcium aluminate cement
- Fine quartz or marble aggregate (particle size typically 0–0.5mm or 0–1.0mm)
- Polymer binder (PVA, SBR, or acrylic latex) — provides adhesion, flexibility, and water resistance
- Pigments — in a wide range of earthy, contemporary tones
- Sealer — a topcoat of PU, polyaspartic, or wax (see Section 7.3)

**Key characteristics:**
- Seamless appearance over large areas; no grout lines; the continuous surface that distinguishes it from tiled finishes
- Very thin application (2–4mm total); minimal floor build-up; suitable over existing finishes
- Can be applied to: concrete, screed, timber subfloor (with adequate preparation and primer), existing tile, LVT, walls, wet rooms
- Highly aesthetic — the mottled, artisanal appearance of concrete appeals strongly to contemporary interior design
- Not structural; not waterproof in itself (must be sealed); not without vulnerability

**Limitations:**
- Requires professional application; very technique-sensitive; visible marks, trowel lines, and inconsistencies are common with inexperienced applicators
- Requires 3–5 layers with adequate drying between; total installation time 3–7 days minimum
- Must be sealed (typically 2–3 coats of PU or polyaspartic topcoat)
- Despite the concrete aesthetic, it is relatively soft (the cement binder is weaker than standard concrete); can show impact marks and scratches in heavy use
- The sealed surface requires the same care as other sealed stone/concrete floors — no harsh chemicals; re-seal periodically

### 7.3 Microcement Sealer Options

The topcoat determines the final appearance and performance:

**Matt PU sealer:**
- Flat, almost chalky appearance; closest to natural concrete
- Moderate durability; requires re-sealing every 1–3 years in residential use
- The most popular for residential living rooms and bedrooms

**Satin/semi-gloss PU:**
- Slight sheen; more practical than matt (easier to clean)
- Better for kitchens and bathrooms

**Polyaspartic topcoat:**
- The premium option; UV-stable; very hard; better chemical resistance
- Used in commercial applications of microcement
- Retains matt or satin appearance without yellowing

**Wax finish:**
- Traditional Mediterranean finishing; very low sheen; natural feel
- Not waterproof; requires regular re-waxing; only for dry, low-traffic areas

### 7.4 Microcement in Wet Areas

Microcement in bathrooms and showers has become a popular luxury residential specification. The technical requirements are non-trivial:

- The substrate must be waterproofed (tanked) before microcement application in wet rooms
- The microcement is applied over the tanking membrane; it is a finish, not a waterproofing layer
- All wall/floor junctions, pipe penetrations, and corners must be sealed in silicone (not grout)
- The polyaspartic or PU sealer must be food-grade quality or specifically rated for wet area use
- The floor must be sealed with a minimum 3 coats; floor areas in direct water contact (shower base) require 4–5 coats
- Anti-slip additive must be incorporated into the topcoat for shower floors (fine aggregate particles added to the final coat before application)

### 7.5 Application Process — Microcement

The professional installation sequence:
1. **Substrate preparation:** Grind, repair, and prime existing surface
2. **Basecoat (primer layer):** Polymer-modified adhesion coat; allows subsequent layers to bond
3. **First microcement layer:** Trowel-applied; 1–1.5mm thick; allowed to dry fully (typically 2–4 hours)
4. **Second microcement layer:** Same thickness; different trowel direction to the first layer (cross-trowelling ensures uniform coverage); dry
5. **Sanding:** Light sand with fine-grit sanding pad or orbital sander; removes trowel marks and slight surface roughness; creates consistent surface for sealing
6. **First sealer coat:** Applied by roller or brush; penetrates microcement; allowed to dry
7. **Second sealer coat:** Full coverage; allow to dry
8. **Third sealer coat (where specified):** Final gloss/sheen level; allow to cure
9. **Wax finish (if specified):** Applied over sealed surface; buffed

**The technique element:** The trowel pattern in microcement is part of the aesthetic — circular, linear, or organic trowel marks create a distinctive artisanal appearance. The skill of the applicator is directly visible in the finished floor.

## 8. TERRAZZO — MODERN AND TRADITIONAL

### 8.1 What Terrazzo Is

Terrazzo is a composite floor finish consisting of marble, granite, quartz, glass, or other decorative aggregate set in a cementitious or resin binder, applied in situ, then ground and polished to reveal the aggregate. The result is a seamless floor with the visual richness of natural stone combined with the design flexibility of a poured material.

Terrazzo has been used for approximately 500 years — Venetian workmen (terrazzieri) ground and polished marble chips set in mortar on the terraces of Venetian palaces. Modern terrazzo can use virtually any aggregate and any binder, creating an almost unlimited design palette.

### 8.2 Traditional (Cementitious) Terrazzo

**Composition:**
- Portland cement binder (grey or white)
- Marble, granite, glass, or mother-of-pearl aggregate in specified sizes
- Pigments to colour the cement matrix
- Dividing strips (zinc, brass, aluminium, or plastic): inserted before pouring to create bay divisions, patterns, and movement joints

**Application:**
1. Prepare and prime the structural subfloor
2. Apply an underbed of cementitious mortar screed (typically 30–50mm)
3. Install dividing strips (the metal or plastic section that divides pattern bays); these become the movement joints and define the design
4. Pour and trowel the terrazzo mix within each bay; compact firmly
5. Allow to set and cure (typically 24–48 hours minimum)
6. Grind the surface progressively from coarse to fine (starting at 24 or 36 grit; progressing to 400 grit)
7. Fill voids with matching slurry; re-grind
8. Polish to desired sheen (800–3000 grit); crystallise if required
9. Seal with penetrating impregnating sealer

**Thickness:** Typically 10–15mm terrazzo layer over the mortar underbed; total floor build-up 40–65mm — significant structural loading consideration (as per natural stone, a structural engineer should assess upper floors)

**The design potential:** The aggregate, binder colour, and dividing strip pattern can create virtually any design — geometric patterns, company logos, figurative patterns, flowing organic designs. This is why terrazzo is specified in museums, airports, public buildings, schools, and prestigious commercial interiors.

### 8.3 Epoxy Terrazzo

**Composition:**
- Epoxy resin binder (coloured)
- Same aggregate options as cementitious: marble, glass, mother-of-pearl, recycled material
- Dividing strips (thinner than cementitious terrazzo due to thinner overall system)

**Advantages over cementitious:**
- Applied much thinner (10–15mm total vs 40–65mm for traditional)
- Faster installation
- Non-porous: the epoxy matrix has essentially zero porosity; stain resistance is excellent without sealing
- Stronger: epoxy binder is stronger than cement
- Available in a wider range of binder colours (epoxy can be pigmented to any colour)

**Applications:**
- Schools (very durable; easy to clean; design potential)
- Hospitals (seamless; hygienic; bacterially clean)
- Airports (heavy traffic; long-lasting; bespoke design)
- Retail and hospitality (bespoke aesthetic; durable)
- Residential (bespoke luxury; increasingly specified by interior designers)

### 8.4 Recycled and Sustainable Terrazzo

A growing niche: terrazzo using recycled content in the aggregate:
- Recycled glass (wine bottles, post-industrial glass): vivid colour; popular in contemporary installations
- Recycled ceramic and porcelain (tile waste)
- Recycled marble offcuts from other stone processing
- Seashells, mother-of-pearl
- This gives terrazzo one of the most compelling sustainability narratives of any floor covering: a potentially recycled-content, long-lasting, fully repairable product

## 9. DECORATIVE CONCRETE AND POLISHED CONCRETE

### 9.1 What Polished Concrete Is

Polished concrete is not a separate product — it is an existing concrete slab that has been mechanically ground and polished to reveal the aggregate and create a highly reflective surface. The concrete must already be present; polished concrete cannot be created by applying a coating.

**The polishing process:**
1. Assess the existing concrete: strength, flatness, aggregate type and distribution, presence of cracks or repairs
2. Coarse grinding (30–50 grit metal bond diamond segments): removes the surface layer; levels the floor; begins to expose aggregate
3. Progressive grinding: 100–200 grit; finer scratches
4. Honing (400–800 grit): surface becomes smooth and reflective
5. Application of chemical hardener (lithium or sodium silicate densifier): penetrates the concrete pores; reacts with calcium hydroxide to form calcium silicate hydrate (CSH); hardens the surface and fills micro-pores
6. Further polishing (1500–3000 grit): mirror finish achievable
7. Protective sealer: penetrating impregnating sealer or polyaspartic topcoat

### 9.2 Polished Concrete Terminology

**Cream polish:** The grinding removes only the very surface layer; the creamy top paste of the concrete (the aggregate-poor surface zone) is polished; minimal aggregate exposure; very smooth

**Salt and pepper:** Light aggregate exposure; the fine aggregate particles of the concrete are visible in the ground surface; the most common specification

**Full aggregate (pebble) exposure:** Deep grinding (typically >3mm removal) exposes the full aggregate — the stones used in the concrete mix are fully visible; a dramatic, natural appearance; the aggregate type and size must be considered at concrete design stage if polished concrete is planned

**Exposed aggregate concrete:**
Different from polished concrete in that the aggregate is exposed during casting (either by surface retarder applied before pour, washed off later, or by mechanical exposure of precast elements); typically used externally

### 9.3 Concrete Requirements for Polishing

Not all existing concrete can be polished to a good result:
- **Strength:** Minimum 25–30 MPa compressive strength; weak concrete surfaces crumble during grinding
- **Aggregate distribution:** The aggregate must be reasonably uniformly distributed; if the concrete was poorly mixed, the ground surface will show uneven aggregate distribution
- **Surface defects:** Cracks, repairs, and stains will be visible in a polished surface — this is not necessarily a problem aesthetically (the industrial character of polished concrete incorporates these features) but must be communicated to the client
- **Flatness:** If the floor is significantly uneven, grinding will level it but remove varying amounts of material; aggregate exposure will be uneven

### 9.4 Polished Concrete in Residential

Growing trend in residential — contemporary, industrial aesthetic; loft apartments, open-plan kitchen-living, architects' houses:
- New build: specify the concrete slab design with polished concrete in mind (appropriate aggregate; clean mix; good curing to minimise surface cracking)
- Renovation: assess the existing slab; many older concrete slabs have too much variation for a high-quality residential polish; manage expectations
- The feel underfoot: very hard and cold without UFH; with UFH, the thermal mass of the concrete slab makes it one of the most comfortable heated floor systems

### 9.5 Limitations of Polished Concrete

- **Not DIY:** Professional diamond grinding equipment; extensive skill
- **Hairline cracks:** Almost universal in concrete; they will be visible; consider whether they add to or detract from the aesthetic
- **Cold underfoot** (without UFH)
- **Can be slippery when wet** if polished to high gloss without anti-slip sealer
- **Hard underfoot** — fatigue in standing environments; consider comfort matting in kitchen areas

## 10. PERFORMANCE CLASSIFICATION AND SYSTEM SPECIFICATION

### 10.1 BS EN 13813 — Screed and Floor Material Classification

The primary performance classification standard for all in-situ applied floor materials including resin, cement, and anhydrite screeds:

**Classification designations:**
- **CT:** Calcium sulphate screed (anhydrite)
- **CA:** Calcium aluminate screed
- **C:** Cementitious screed (Portland cement)
- **SB:** Synthetic resin binder screed

**Compressive strength (C-designation):** C16, C20, C25, C30, C40 (minimum compressive strength in MPa)
**Tensile strength (T-designation):** T1.5, T3, T5 etc.
**Wear resistance (A/B/designations under EN 13892-4):** Important for commercial resin screeds

**Example specification: SB-C40-T3-W3-AR0.2**
- SB: Synthetic resin binder
- C40: 40 MPa compressive strength
- T3: 3 MPa tensile strength
- W3: water penetration depth ≤3mm
- AR0.2: abrasion resistance loss ≤0.2cm³ (15cm²)

### 10.2 Specifying by Use Case

A proper resin floor specification should state:
1. The system type (self-smoothing epoxy; PU screed; MMA; etc.)
2. The total system thickness (including primer; base coat; topcoat)
3. Performance requirements (compressive strength; tensile strength; abrasion resistance; chemical resistance)
4. Anti-slip specification (PTV or DIN 51130 R-value)
5. Aesthetic requirements (colour; gloss level; aggregate)
6. The specific product manufacturer and system reference (or "equivalent approved")
7. Surface preparation method
8. Substrate requirements

## 11. SUBSTRATE REQUIREMENTS — THE CRITICAL FOUNDATION

### 11.1 The 80/20 Rule for Resin Floors

In resin flooring failures, approximately 80% are attributable to substrate-related causes:
- Inadequate surface preparation
- Moisture in the substrate
- Contaminated substrate
- Inadequate substrate strength
- Poorly specified primer

**The resin system is only as good as what it is bonded to.** The most expensive, highest-performance resin coating will fail prematurely if the substrate is not properly prepared.

### 11.2 Concrete Substrate Requirements

**Minimum concrete compressive strength:** 25 MPa (C25 concrete) for most resin systems; some systems specify 30 MPa minimum. Below this, the bond may exceed the tensile strength of the concrete — adhesion failure occurs in the concrete, not at the adhesive interface.

**Surface moisture — the critical parameter:**

For standard epoxy systems: maximum **4% MC** by Carbide Bomb (CM meter) test — the standard rapid test for concrete MC in the resin flooring industry

Alternatively: **75–80% RH** by hygrometer in insulated box (BS 8204-6 method)

**Why moisture is uniquely critical for resin:**
- Moisture vapour rising through concrete can lift the resin coating from below — osmotic blistering
- Even if the surface appears dry, moisture vapour trapped beneath a fully bonded resin coating creates a partial vacuum as it attempts to escape; the resin blisters and debonds
- Some epoxy systems react with water during cure, producing a white haze (blushing) or a weaker, more porous polymer network

**For substrates with higher moisture:**
- Moisture-tolerant epoxy primers: can be applied at up to 5–6% MC (CM)
- Two-component epoxy DPM: applied at 6–8% CM; provides a barrier coat before the decorative system
- PU screed systems: inherently more tolerant; some apply up to 9–10% MC
- For very wet substrates: address the moisture source; install a physical DPM if possible

**Surface tensile strength:**
The resin adhesion must not exceed the tensile strength of the concrete surface. Test method: pull-off test (EN 1504-3); measure the force required to pull a steel dolly from the surface. Minimum acceptable pull-off strength for most resin systems: **1.5 N/mm²** (MPa); some specifications require 2.0 N/mm².

If the pull-off test fails in the concrete (the concrete fractures rather than the interface): the surface requires additional preparation (grinding to remove the weak zone) or concrete repair.

### 11.3 Existing Resin Floors as Substrate

When applying a new resin system over an existing one:
- The existing system must be fully bonded (tap test; pull-off test)
- The existing surface must be abraded (diamond grinding or shotblasting) to provide a mechanical key
- Chemical compatibility: confirm the new primer/base coat is compatible with the existing resin type (epoxy over epoxy is generally fine; epoxy over aged PU is more complex — manufacturer guidance required)
- The existing system's total thickness adds to the overall build-up — consider floor level implications

## 12. SUBSTRATE PREPARATION METHODS

### 12.1 Shotblasting

**The gold standard substrate preparation for resin flooring:**

A machine containing steel or iron shot (small spheres or irregular pellets) is propelled at high velocity against the concrete surface. The shot impacts and dislodges the surface layer, creating a consistent, clean, mechanically keyed surface.

**Process:**
- Blast machine traverses the floor in overlapping passes
- Speed controls depth of preparation: slow = aggressive; fast = lighter
- A vacuum/cyclone recovers the shot and spent surface material simultaneously — dust-free operation
- The prepared surface resembles light sandblasting in appearance; consistent texture; all laitance removed; contamination removed; pore structure opened

**Profile achieved:** Sa2½ (near-white metal) or ISO 8501 equivalent on concrete
**CSP (Concrete Surface Profile) typically achieved:** CSP 3–5 (ICRI scale); appropriate for most resin systems

**Why shotblasting is preferred:**
- The most thorough preparation of all methods
- Very fast for large areas
- Dust-controlled (contained within the machine)
- Consistent; operator skill has less influence than grinding

**Limitations:**
- Requires access for the machine (typically 800–900mm minimum door width)
- Not suitable for very weak or friable concrete (can dislodge aggregate, leaving a weakened surface)
- May be too aggressive for thin, polished, or screeded surfaces
- Not suitable for small areas or confined spaces (grinding preferred)

### 12.2 Diamond Grinding

- Diamond-tipped grinding wheels (planetary or single-head rotary grinder) remove the concrete surface mechanically
- More controllable than shotblasting; used for: small areas; correcting specific defects; accessing areas inaccessible to blast machine
- Slow compared to shotblasting for large areas
- Dust: requires vacuum extraction attachment; generates significant RCS dust
- Can be combined with DPM application if needed before grinding final surface

### 12.3 Scarifying

- A rotating drum with hardened carbide or steel cutters; more aggressive than grinding; removes significant material
- Used for: removing existing coatings or contamination; preparing severely damaged surfaces
- Leaves a rough, grooved surface that requires grinding after to smooth for some thin-film systems

### 12.4 Chemical Preparation

- **Acid etching (dilute hydrochloric acid):** Applied to concrete surface; reacts with calcium carbonate; removes laitance and opens pores
- Much less reliable and thorough than mechanical preparation; not the professional standard for industrial resin
- Acceptable for light-duty residential decorative applications (garage floors with thin epoxy coating)
- Never the primary preparation method for industrial or heavy commercial; always combine with mechanical preparation
- Generates hazardous acid waste that must be neutralised and disposed of correctly

### 12.5 Repairs Before Resin Application

All cracks, holes, and surface defects must be repaired before resin application:
- **Cracks:** Chase out to minimum 3mm width; fill with epoxy crack filler or low-viscosity epoxy injection
- **Holes and voids:** Clean out; fill with fast-setting repair mortar or epoxy paste
- **Delaminated or weak surface:** Cut back to sound concrete; repair with polymer-modified repair mortar; allow to cure; re-prime

## 13. PRIMERS AND DPMs

### 13.1 Why Primers Are Essential

A primer serves several functions:
1. **Adhesion promoter:** Chemical bond between substrate and system
2. **Sealer:** Closes surface pores so the subsequent layer does not sink in and lose material
3. **Dust binder:** Binds any remaining loose particles to the surface
4. **Compatibility layer:** Provides a consistent surface chemistry for the base coat

**Always prime before applying any resin floor system.** Applying a resin system to an unprimed concrete surface typically results in the system being wicked into the concrete (wasted material) or pinhole formation (air escaping from unprimed pores).

### 13.2 Primer Types

**Low-viscosity epoxy primer:**
- Standard primer for most epoxy and PU systems on dry concrete
- Penetrates deeply into pores; bonds strongly to concrete
- Back-rolling required: after application, a roller is run over the surface to redistribute primer that has pooled in surface depressions and ensure even coverage

**Moisture-tolerant epoxy primer:**
- For substrates with slightly elevated moisture (4–5% CM); cures in the presence of moisture without blushing
- Does not fully solve a damp substrate problem but extends the tolerance window

**Water-based acrylic primer:**
- Lower performance than epoxy; for light-duty decorative applications
- Fast dry; low odour; low VOC
- Not for industrial or demanding commercial applications

**Two-component epoxy DPM (Damp Proof Membrane):**
- High-build epoxy (typically 2× coats; total 300–400 microns)
- Forms a full barrier coat preventing moisture vapour transmission
- Applied at higher MC substrate than standard primer (up to 7–8% CM depending on product)
- Essential when substrate cannot be dried to the normal limit; the DPM is the last resort before more invasive moisture remediation

## 14. APPLICATION METHODS AND TECHNIQUES

### 14.1 Roller Application

**Used for:**
- Thin-film epoxy and PU coatings (1–3mm)
- Primer coats
- Clear sealers over decorative systems

**Tools:**
- Short-pile roller (6–9mm nap) for smooth surfaces; medium pile (12mm) for slightly textured
- Roller frame and extension pole
- Cut-in brush for edges and angles
- Roller trays or direct bucket application

**Technique:**
- Work in consistent passes (W or M stroke followed by lay-off passes)
- Maintain wet edge to avoid lap marks
- Apply second coat at 90° to the first (cross-hatch technique) for even coverage
- Ensure even mil thickness; use wet film gauge to check thickness during application

### 14.2 Squeegee and Trowel (Self-Levelling Application)

**Used for:**
- Self-levelling epoxy (2–4mm)
- Thin epoxy screed
- Microcement

**Tools:**
- Gauge rake (serrated bar that sets the material depth): ensures consistent thickness
- Spiked roller: used immediately after gauge-raking to release entrapped air bubbles; the spikes break the surface tension, allowing air to escape; must be done while the material is still fluid (within the pot life)
- Walk-in spiked shoes (to avoid leaving footprints while the spiked roller is applied)

**Sequence:**
1. Mix the two components thoroughly in the correct ratio
2. Pour mixed material onto the floor in controlled sections
3. Spread with gauge rake to the correct depth
4. Immediately follow with spiked roller (de-aeration)
5. Move to next section; maintain wet edge
6. Work back from the far wall toward the exit

**The de-aeration step is critical:** Air bubbles in self-levelling epoxy create a "pinholes" appearance in the cured floor. Spiked rolling while still fluid prevents this. In warm conditions (short pot life), the spiked rolling window is narrow.

### 14.3 Trowel Application (Mortar Systems)

**Used for:**
- Epoxy mortar; PU screed
- Thick build-up systems

**Tools:**
- Steel screed bar (gauging bar): sets the depth of the material
- Steel trowel: initial spreading and levelling
- Float: smoothing and compacting
- Power trowel (helicopter): for large areas; applies the compressive and smoothing force mechanically

**Technique:**
1. Set datum pegs or screed rails at the correct height
2. Pour and spread the pre-mixed mortar between rails
3. Strike off level with the gauging bar
4. Float to remove marks and compact
5. Final texture (if required): fine aggregate applied and trowelled in; or surface left float-finished

### 14.4 Broadcast (Scatter Coat)

- Applied immediately after the base coat; while the material is still wet/tacky
- Coloured quartz, EPDM granules, vinyl flakes, or mica flakes scattered uniformly over the surface
- Allow base coat to cure
- Excess aggregate swept or vacuumed off
- Seal coat applied over the aggregate

**Broadcast aggregate serves:**
- Anti-slip (particularly quartz broadcast)
- Aesthetics (flake systems; coloured quartz)
- Coverage of substrate imperfections (flake systems)

## 15. MOVEMENT JOINTS IN RESIN SYSTEMS

### 15.1 Why Resin Floors Still Need Movement Joints

A common misunderstanding: because resin floors are seamless, they do not need movement joints. This is incorrect.

The concrete substrate moves — thermally and with moisture cycling. A resin system bonded to moving concrete must either:
- Accommodate the movement (impossible for a thin rigid epoxy system)
- Or crack where the movement is highest

**BS 8204-6** requires movement joints in resin floors:
- At all construction joints in the substrate (the joint in the concrete must have a corresponding joint in the resin)
- At all structural movement joints in the building
- At changes of direction or plan
- At bay boundaries in large floors (typically 5m × 5m maximum for thin epoxy coatings; up to 10m for thick PU screed depending on the system and substrate)

### 15.2 Movement Joint Detail

- Saw-cut the resin system after cure using a diamond saw
- Insert a compressible backing rod
- Fill with a compatible flexible sealant (polyurethane or silicone appropriate for the environment)
- The sealant must be the last material placed — it cannot be ground or recoated over once cured

**Cover strips:** In some commercial environments, an aluminium or stainless steel movement joint cover strip is specified to protect the sealant joint from wheeled traffic

## 16. UNDERFLOOR HEATING COMPATIBILITY

### 16.1 Resin Systems Over UFH

All resin systems are compatible with UFH in principle — the thermal resistance of a 2–4mm resin coating is negligible. The considerations are:

**Substrate-related:**
- The UFH screed must be fully commissioned and dried to the MC limit before applying resin
- The screed must be at the correct temperature when the resin is applied: not below 10°C for most systems; not above 30°C (high temperature shortens pot life dramatically and affects film formation)
- Switch off UFH 48 hours before application; resume after full cure (7 days for epoxy)

**Performance:**
- Epoxy softening at elevated temperature: standard epoxy may soften above 55–60°C surface temperature; in normal UFH operation (surface temperature ≤27°C) this is not a concern
- PU screed and MMA: both rated for higher service temperatures; no concern with standard UFH

**The very low R-value advantage:**
- Resin flooring is one of the best floor coverings for thermal efficiency over UFH
- A 3mm epoxy system over a heated concrete slab: the concrete's thermal mass is essentially directly accessible; the floor heats rapidly and retains heat well

## 17. SLIP RESISTANCE IN RESIN FLOORS

### 17.1 The Challenge

Smooth, polished resin surfaces (particularly clear-sealed polished concrete and self-smoothing epoxy) can be very slippery when wet. Specifying and achieving adequate slip resistance is a critical safety requirement.

### 17.2 Anti-Slip Methods

**Aggregate broadcast:**
- Fine quartz, aluminium oxide, or carborundum broadcast into the wet surface coat
- The aggregate particles protrude slightly above the cured surface, creating mechanical friction
- Most effective and durable method
- The aggregate must be sealed in with a clear topcoat to prevent it loosening in service

**Anti-slip additive in the topcoat:**
- Fine aggregate particles mixed into the final topcoat before application; applied uniformly with a roller or squeegee
- Less visible than broadcast aggregate; more uniform appearance
- Slightly less effective than broadcast for aggressive anti-slip requirements

**Textured surface:**
- During application of the self-levelling system: a roller or brush dragged across the surface before cure creates a fine texture
- The texture is integral to the cured system; does not wear away
- Aesthetically less refined than a smooth system

**Slip-resistant aggregate topcoat:**
- A pre-formulated topcoat with aggregate already incorporated; applied by squeegee or roller
- Convenience product; good for repair and renovation

### 17.3 Anti-Slip Ratings for Resin Floors

- **BS 7976-2 Pendulum Test:** PTV ≥36 (low slip risk); the UK assessment standard
- **DIN 51130 R-value:** R9 (general commercial), R10 (wet commercial), R11–R12 (industrial with oil/grease)
- Testing should be performed on the installed system, not estimated from materials specification alone — the actual slip resistance of a broadcast aggregate floor depends on aggregate particle size, density of application, and the topcoat system

## 18. PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 18.1 Blistering / Bubbling

**Symptoms:** Raised blisters (from pinhole size to 50mm+) in the resin surface, appearing after installation.

**Causes:**
1. **Moisture osmosis (substrate too wet):** Water vapour rising from the substrate; as it encounters the impermeable resin barrier, it accumulates; the pressure builds; the resin lifts in blisters. The blister contains water or a milky fluid. This is the most common cause of resin floor failure.
2. **Pinholes / de-aeration failure:** Small bubbles not released by spiked rolling during application; the self-levelling coat has small holes rather than smooth film; distinct from large blisters
3. **Resin applied over contaminated surface:** Oil, dust, or curing compound prevented adhesion; air pockets formed at the contamination points
4. **Temperature differential:** Cold substrate + warm resin; the resin laid down a thin skin before the substrate warmed; the expanding air beneath lifted the skin

**Diagnosis:**
- Blister content: water/milky = moisture osmosis; air = air entrapment or de-aeration failure
- Distribution: random across the floor = moisture (especially where vapour pressure is highest — over voids or drainage channels in the slab); clustered in areas = contamination
- CM test the substrate where blisters are cut open

**Action:**
- Moisture blisters: cut open all blisters; allow to dry; investigate the moisture source; repair with epoxy repair paste; if widespread: may require full system removal, substrate treatment, DPM application, and reinstallation
- Pinholes / de-aeration: patch with appropriate repair material; for widespread pinholing: thin flood coat to seal
- Contamination blisters: cut out; clean; spot repair

### 18.2 Delamination (Peeling)

**Symptoms:** The resin system lifts away from the substrate in large sheets or areas; may manifest as a hollow sound when tapped.

**Causes:**
1. **Laitance not removed:** The weak surface layer of concrete provided a false bond; the resin adhered to the laitance, not the concrete; when stressed, the laitance separated
2. **Contamination:** Oil, grease, or curing compound prevented adhesion
3. **Primer omitted:** The resin did not bond properly to the unprimed substrate
4. **Low concrete tensile strength:** The pull-off strength of the concrete was lower than the adhesive strength of the resin; delamination occurs in the concrete just below the surface

**Diagnosis:** Examine the underside of the delaminated area — concrete on the back of the peeled resin sheet = delamination in the concrete (tensile failure of substrate); clean resin on the back = adhesive failure at the interface (contamination or primer failure)

### 18.3 Cracking

**Causes:**
1. **Substrate crack reflected:** A crack in the concrete has reflected through the resin system; can occur with both epoxy and flexible PU if the crack movement is significant
2. **Thermal movement:** The resin system expanded or contracted beyond its strain capacity; typically at large unjointed areas
3. **Impact damage:** Mechanical crack from heavy object dropped; point load beyond the system's impact resistance
4. **Incorrect cure:** Under-mixed or incorrect ratio during application; the cured film is brittle and weak; cracks under minimal stress

**Substrate crack mitigation:** Epoxy injection into the crack before applying the resin system; or saw cut and seal the crack as a movement joint; or use a PU system with higher flexibility

### 18.4 Discolouration

**Causes:**
1. **UV yellowing (epoxy):** Classic failure of aromatic epoxy as topcoat in UV-exposed areas; uniform yellowing across the whole floor
2. **Chemical staining:** Specific chemicals have attacked the finish; localised staining; test the stained area's chemical resistance
3. **Amine blushing:** During application in humid or cold conditions, amines from the hardener migrate to the surface before the epoxy cures; produces a whitish, greasy or hazy surface; can usually be washed off while fresh but affects adhesion of subsequent coats
4. **Wear:** In high-traffic areas, the topcoat has been abraded; lighter reflection from abraded vs intact areas creates a visual pattern of traffic lanes

## 19. MAINTENANCE AND CARE

### 19.1 Routine Maintenance — All Resin Systems

**Sweeping and dust removal:**
- Regular (daily in commercial); use a soft-bristle broom or dust mop
- Grit abrades the topcoat over time; regular removal is key to extended topcoat life

**Mopping:**
- pH-neutral cleaning solution (pH 6–8)
- Wet mop; allow to dry
- No harsh acids or bleach at high concentration on epoxy (attacks the polymer over time)
- PU screed: slightly more tolerant of alkaline cleaning; most commercial food-area cleaners (pH 9–11) are acceptable; confirm with manufacturer

**Machine scrubbing (commercial):**
- Rotary machine with appropriate pad (soft to medium; not aggressive abrasive)
- Neutral or mildly alkaline detergent
- The appropriate frequency depends on traffic and contamination levels

### 19.2 Anti-Slip Surface Maintenance

**Critical:** Broadcast aggregate surfaces must be thoroughly cleaned periodically to maintain their anti-slip effectiveness — contamination fills the aggregate pockets and reduces friction (as per safety vinyl — Lexicon 06, Section 19.4)

- Periodic aggressive machine scrubbing with stiff brush pad to clean out aggregate valleys
- Re-test PTV periodically in critical applications (healthcare, food production)

### 19.3 Polished Concrete Maintenance

- Sweep regularly; damp mop with neutral cleaner
- Re-apply penetrating sealer annually (residential) or as required (commercial)
- Re-polish (machine burnish) periodically to restore reflectivity
- For high-gloss polished concrete: a floor polish maintenance coat (acrylic emulsion) builds a sacrificial film that is stripped and renewed periodically without having to re-grind

### 19.4 Repair of Resin Systems

Unlike tile or wood, resin systems can be partially repaired:
- Small damage (scratch, chip, chemical stain): clean the area; apply compatible repair resin; feather to match level; topcoat to match original; colour match is the challenging aspect — any repair area will show a slight visual boundary
- Widespread wear (topcoat abraded): abraded surface must be recoated; the challenge is colour matching the new coat to areas where the original coat is still intact; in practice, a full re-topcoat of the entire floor is usually needed for a uniform result
- Moisture blisters: repair only after the moisture source has been permanently resolved

## 20. COMMERCIAL APPLICATIONS BY SECTOR

### 20.1 Food and Beverage Production

**The most demanding sector for resin flooring.** Requirements:
- **Thermal shock resistance:** Floors are subject to hot water (60–90°C) cleaning, steam (120°C+), and cold production environments simultaneously; only PU cementitious screed systems can withstand this regime
- **Chemical resistance:** Cleaning chemicals, organic acids (from production), caustic soda, peracetic acid disinfectants
- **Coved skirtings:** Integral resin coving to eliminate the floor/wall junction (HACCP compliance)
- **Anti-slip:** DIN 51130 R11 minimum; R12 in cooking/oil areas
- **Drainage:** Fall to floor drains; typically 1:80 fall minimum; drains must be integral to the resin system

**Specification:** PU mortar screed (6–9mm) with integral quartz anti-slip broadcast; factory-matched coved skirting

**Key manufacturers:** Remmers, Flowcrete, Sika, ARDEX, Polyflor (resin division), Kemperol

### 20.2 Healthcare and Pharmaceutical

**Requirements:**
- Seamless; no joints; easy to decontaminate
- Chemical resistance to disinfectants (including bleach solutions, alcohols, hydrogen peroxide)
- Anti-static options (electronics manufacturing; hospital operating theatres where electromagnetic interference must be avoided)
- Coved skirtings in clinical areas
- Epoxy self-smoothing 2–4mm + PU topcoat or polyaspartic topcoat is the standard pharmaceutical/electronics specification
- Anti-static epoxy (with carbon fibre or conductive aggregate and earthing points) for electronics clean rooms

**Specific to pharmaceutical:** GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice) compliance requires floors that can be validated for cleanliness and do not harbour contamination; seamless resin with coved skirtings is the GMP specification standard

### 20.3 Industrial and Warehousing

- Epoxy mortar or self-levelling system 3–6mm
- Abrasion resistance for heavy wheel traffic (forklifts, pallet trucks)
- Line marking: safety lines, bay marking, pedestrian walkways applied in contrasting coloured resin
- Anti-slip broadcast for high-traffic pedestrian areas
- Very large areas: shotblasting preparation essential; large-area application equipment (power squeegee, power trowel) required

### 20.4 Retail and Hospitality

- Polished concrete: restaurants, bars, retail — the contemporary aesthetic
- Microcement overlay: boutique hotel bedrooms, spa areas, high-end residential renovation
- Metallic epoxy: showrooms, wine bars, residential kitchens (the decorative aesthetic)
- Terrazzo: museums, airport terminals, luxury retail (Westfield-type environments), schools and universities

### 20.5 Residential

- Polished concrete: contemporary houses, loft apartments, architecture-led design
- Microcement: kitchens, bathrooms, living areas
- Garage floors: epoxy or polyaspartic flake system

## 21. HEALTH, SAFETY, AND VOCs

### 21.1 VOC Emissions During Application

Resin flooring application generates significant VOC emissions:

**Epoxy VOCs:**
- Cycloaliphatic and aliphatic amines (hardeners): sensitisers; can cause occupational asthma and contact dermatitis
- Reactive diluents in some formulations: aromatic compounds
- Modern water-based epoxy systems: significantly lower VOC than solvent-based

**PU VOCs:**
- Isocyanates: among the most significant occupational respiratory sensitisers; once sensitised, even trace exposure causes severe reactions
- COSHH assessment mandatory for any isocyanate-containing product
- The HSE has issued specific guidance on isocyanate exposure in flooring

**MMA VOCs:**
- MMA monomer: pungent; narcotic at high concentration; flammable
- Full respiratory protection (organic vapour respirator) mandatory

**Microcement:**
- Lower VOC than resin systems; however the sealer coat (PU or polyaspartic) has similar VOC profile to pure resin systems

### 21.2 PPE Requirements

**Minimum PPE for all resin floor application:**
- Eye protection (chemical splash goggles)
- Chemical-resistant gloves (nitrile or neoprene; not latex — epoxy penetrates latex)
- Chemical-resistant coverall or dedicated work clothing
- Adequate ventilation or respiratory protection

**For MMA application:** Full face respirator with organic vapour cartridges
**For isocyanate-containing systems:** Forced-air respirator (APF20 minimum); no unprotected personnel in the area

**Skin sensitisation warning:**
Epoxy resins and hardeners are potent skin sensitisers. First exposure may cause no reaction; subsequent exposures can trigger an increasingly severe allergic response (contact dermatitis). Once sensitised, the worker may be unable to work with epoxy products at any concentration. Prevention: skin contact avoidance from the first application.

### 21.3 Indoor Air Quality Post-Installation

After installation and cure:
- Most resin systems are effectively inert when fully cured; VOC emissions post-cure are very low
- Ventilate during and for at least 24 hours after application
- GREENGUARD Gold and other low-VOC certifications are available for some residential microcement and decorative resin products
- FloorScore certification relevant to residential resin products

## 23. SUSTAINABILITY

### 23.1 Resin Flooring and Embodied Carbon

- Resin systems are petroleum-derived polymer products; significant embodied carbon in production of epoxy resins, isocyanates, and MMA monomers
- However: long lifespan (typically 15–30 years in commercial before major refurbishment) and the ability to be recoated/repaired rather than replaced reduces the lifecycle environmental impact vs products requiring full replacement

### 23.2 Bio-Based Resins

A growing development area:
- **Bio-based epoxy resins:** Using epoxidised plant oils (soybean, linseed) as partial or full replacement for bisphenol-A epoxy; available from specialist manufacturers; lower fossil fuel content but current performance not fully equivalent to conventional epoxy in demanding applications
- **Bio-based hardeners:** Cashew nut shell liquid (CNSL)-derived hardeners available; bio-content without compromising performance significantly
- **Markets are moving:** BASF, Sika, and other large manufacturers are developing bio-based resin products; the specification of truly sustainable high-performance resin systems is coming

### 23.3 Terrazzo Sustainability

Terrazzo is one of the most sustainable resin floor options:
- Long lifespan (30–100+ years for cementitious terrazzo; 20–40 years for epoxy terrazzo before topcoat renewal)
- Restorable: grinding and re-polishing returns a damaged terrazzo to new condition repeatedly
- Recycled aggregate: the aggregate can be entirely recycled content (glass, ceramic waste, marble offcuts)
- Zero individual tile manufacturing energy: the material is made in situ with bulk materials
- At end of life: the cementitious material can be crushed as aggregate

### 23.4 Polished Concrete Sustainability

- The slab already exists (renovation application): no new materials required beyond the chemical hardener and sealer
- The grinding process generates concrete dust (waste) but no new material is added
- For new build: the structural concrete slab serves double duty as the floor finish; no additional floor covering material required
- Very long lifespan: polished concrete with periodic maintenance is essentially permanent
- The hardener (lithium silicate) becomes part of the concrete matrix; no ongoing chemical additions beyond periodic re-sealing

## 24. TRADE TERMINOLOGY GLOSSARY

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Amine blushing** | White greasy surface haze on epoxy caused by amine hardener migrating to surface in humid/cold conditions during cure |
| **Anti-static floor** | Resin floor with conductive aggregate and earthing points; dissipates static electricity; electronics manufacturing and operating theatres |
| **Broadcast aggregate** | Aggregate (quartz, EPDM, flakes) scattered into wet resin base coat for anti-slip or aesthetics |
| **BS 8204-6** | UK standard for synthetic resin flooring design and installation; the primary resin floor reference |
| **Carbide bomb (CM meter)** | Rapid on-site moisture test for concrete; measures MC by carbide gas pressure method |
| **COSHH** | Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002; mandatory risk assessment for all resin products |
| **CPU (Cementitious PU)** | Cementitious polyurethane screed; the hybrid system dominant in food and beverage sector |
| **Cross-linking** | The chemical reaction forming a network of polymer chains; the curing process of all thermoset resin systems |
| **CSP (Concrete Surface Profile)** | ICRI scale of concrete surface roughness (1–10); CSP 3–5 typically required for resin adhesion |
| **De-aeration / spiked rolling** | Rolling a spiked roller over freshly applied self-levelling resin to release trapped air bubbles |
| **Densifier** | Lithium or sodium silicate solution reacting with concrete to harden surface; used in polished concrete |
| **DPM (epoxy)** | Two-component epoxy damp proof membrane; applied as a barrier coat on moisture-rich substrates |
| **Epoxy** | Thermosetting resin system based on epoxide groups; excellent adhesion and chemical resistance; UV unstable (aromatic) |
| **Epoxy mortar** | Epoxy resin + aggregate; high thickness; maximum compressive strength; heavy industrial application |
| **Flake / chip system** | Decorative resin system with vinyl or EPDM flakes broadcast into base coat; sealed with clear topcoat |
| **GMP (Good Manufacturing Practice)** | Pharmaceutical manufacturing standard; requires seamless hygienic floors with coved skirtings |
| **HACCP** | Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points; food safety; requires coved skirtings and sealed floor joints |
| **Isocyanate** | Reactive chemical component in PU systems; potent respiratory sensitiser; strict COSHH controls required |
| **Laitance** | Weak surface layer of cement/water on concrete; must be removed before resin application |
| **Metallic epoxy** | Decorative epoxy system using mica/metalite pigments to produce iridescent, swirled surface |
| **Microcement** | Thin polymer-modified cementitious coating; 2–4mm; seamless concrete aesthetic; decorative residential and hospitality |
| **MMA** | Methyl Methacrylate; acrylic resin system; fastest cure; cures at very low temperatures; pungent odour |
| **Osmotic blistering** | Blistering of resin floor caused by moisture vapour pressure from substrate lifting the coating |
| **Pinholing** | Small holes in cured self-levelling resin; caused by air not released by spiked rolling |
| **Polyaspartic** | Aliphatic polyurea-type resin; UV stable; fast cure; used for garage floors and UV-exposed commercial |
| **Pot life** | Time after mixing two components during which material remains workable |
| **Pull-off test** | EN 1504-3 test measuring tensile bond strength of resin to substrate; minimum 1.5 N/mm² |
| **PU screed** | Polyurethane cementitious screed; flexible; excellent thermal shock resistance; food and beverage standard |
| **Resin flooring** | Seamless in-situ applied floor system curing through chemical polymerisation |
| **Salt and pepper polish** | Polished concrete with light aggregate exposure; fine aggregate particles visible |
| **Self-levelling (SLE)** | Resin system with sufficient fluidity to level under gravity; applied at 2–4mm; standard commercial epoxy system |
| **Shotblasting** | Mechanical substrate preparation using propelled steel shot; gold standard for resin floor preparation |
| **Terrazzo** | Aggregate (marble, glass, quartz) set in cementitious or epoxy binder; ground and polished; seamless pattern floor |
| **Thermoset** | Polymer that permanently cross-links during cure; cannot be re-melted; all resin floor systems are thermosets |
| **Two-component (2K)** | Resin system requiring mixing of two components (resin + hardener) before application |
| **Walk-on time** | Time after application before light foot traffic is permitted |
