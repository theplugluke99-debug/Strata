## 1. TIMBER SCIENCE — UNDERSTANDING WOOD AS A MATERIAL

### 1.1 Wood Anatomy

Understanding why solid wood moves — and how to control that movement — requires a grounding in wood anatomy.

**The tree cross-section from centre outward:**
- **Pith:** The very centre; earliest growth; often unstable and brittle; avoided in flooring-grade timber
- **Heartwood:** The denser, darker inner wood; cells are dead and often contain resins, tannins, and extractives that provide colour, durability, and decay resistance; used for flooring
- **Sapwood:** Outer rings; lighter in colour; contains living cells; more susceptible to insect attack and decay; generally excluded from quality flooring grades; may be present in rustic/natural grades
- **Cambium:** The thin growing layer just beneath the bark; not present in milled timber
- **Bark:** Removed in milling

**Annual growth rings:** Each ring represents one year of growth — the lighter (early wood / spring wood) grows rapidly and has larger, thinner-walled cells; the darker (late wood / summer wood) is slower-grown with smaller, denser cells. This ring structure creates the characteristic grain pattern of timber.

**Rays:** Horizontal ribbons of cells running radially from pith to bark. In some species (particularly oak) these are very prominent and are referred to as "medullary rays" or "ray fleck" — the silver/silky lustrous marks seen in quarter-sawn oak. In other species, rays are barely visible.

### 1.2 Grain Direction and Cut

The angle at which a log is sawn into boards creates fundamentally different grain patterns, stability characteristics, and aesthetic appearances.

**Plain-sawn (flat-sawn / through-and-through):**
- The most common and economical cut
- Boards cut tangentially through the log
- Growth rings appear as arching, cathedral-style grain patterns on the face
- The growth rings run at less than 30° to the face
- **Greatest dimensional movement** in response to humidity change — tangential movement is approximately twice radial movement in most species
- More prone to cupping and surface checking
- Wider boards available from same diameter log

**Quarter-sawn:**
- Log first quartered, then each quarter sawn with cuts approximately perpendicular to the growth rings
- Growth rings run at 60–90° to the face
- Produces straight, parallel grain lines on the face
- In oak: prominent ray fleck (medullary ray figure) on the face — the distinctive "feathered" or "tiger" appearance
- **Greater dimensional stability** — radial movement is approximately half tangential movement
- Better resistance to cupping and surface wear
- Less yield per log — more expensive
- Suitable for wide board applications where stability is critical

**Rift-sawn:**
- Cut at approximately 30–60° to the growth rings
- Produces a very straight, consistent grain with no ray fleck
- Similar stability to quarter-sawn; minimal figure
- Very wasteful cut — expensive
- Used in premium applications where consistent grain is essential
- Common in white oak flooring for contemporary minimalist interiors

**Live-sawn (French cut):**
- The entire log is sawn parallel, like slices of bread
- Each board shows varying grain pattern — some quarter-sawn in the middle, some plain-sawn at the outer boards
- Maximises material yield
- The "French oak" appearance — varied figure across a floor

### 1.3 Wood Movement — The Central Challenge of Solid Wood Flooring

Wood is hygroscopic — it continuously exchanges moisture with the surrounding air. As it absorbs moisture, the cell walls swell; as it loses moisture, they shrink. This movement is permanent, ongoing, and unavoidable. All solid wood flooring installation must be designed around managing this movement.

**Equilibrium Moisture Content (EMC):**
- Wood will reach an equilibrium moisture content that corresponds to the temperature and relative humidity of its environment
- The relationship between air RH and wood EMC is expressed in the sorption isotherm
- Key reference values at normal indoor temperatures:
  - 35% RH → approximately 7–8% EMC
  - 50% RH → approximately 9–10% EMC
  - 65% RH → approximately 12% EMC
  - 75% RH → approximately 14–15% EMC
- UK interior conditions typically produce an EMC range of 8–12% over an annual cycle (summer vs winter, depending on heating and ventilation)

**Movement coefficients:**
- **Tangential movement coefficient (plain-sawn):** The fractional change in width per 1% change in MC
  - Oak: approximately 0.24% per 1% MC change (i.e., a 150mm board changes ~0.36mm per 1% MC)
  - Jatoba: 0.28%
  - Maple: 0.29%
  - Pine: 0.19%
- **Radial movement coefficient (quarter-sawn):** Approximately half the tangential value
  - Oak: approximately 0.14% per 1% MC change

**Practical calculation:**
> Width change (mm) = Board width (mm) × Movement coefficient × ΔMC (%)

Example: Plain-sawn oak board, 180mm wide, experiencing a 4% MC change:
> 180 × 0.0024 × 4 = 1.73mm movement per board

In a room 4m wide with 180mm boards (approximately 22 boards): total movement ≈ 38mm — this is why expansion gaps of 15–20mm at walls are often specified for wide-board oak.

**Tangential vs radial movement summary:**
| Cut | Typical movement | Cupping tendency |
|-----|-----------------|-----------------|
| Plain-sawn | High | Higher |
| Quarter-sawn | ~50% of plain-sawn | Much lower |
| Rift-sawn | Similar to quarter | Low |

### 1.4 Janka Hardness

The Janka hardness test measures the force required to embed a 11.28mm steel ball to half its diameter into the wood surface. It is the primary indicator of resistance to denting and wear in flooring applications.

| Species | Janka Rating (lbf) | Notes |
|---------|-------------------|-------|
| Balsa | 70 | Reference — too soft for flooring |
| Pine (Scots) | 870 | Soft; dents easily but characterful |
| Spruce | 700 | Too soft for flooring |
| American Cherry | 950 | Moderate; fine-grain; elegant |
| Black Walnut | 1,010 | Moderate; premium aesthetic |
| Teak | 1,070 | Oily; good outdoor durability |
| American White Oak | 1,360 | The UK reference species; excellent |
| European Oak | 1,120 | Slightly softer than American; beautiful |
| Hard Maple | 1,450 | Very hard; excellent durability |
| Ash | 1,320 | Excellent; open grain; pale |
| American Hickory | 1,820 | Very hard; prominent grain; rustic |
| Brazilian Cherry (Jatoba) | 2,350 | Extremely hard; reddish; darkens |
| Santos Mahogany | 2,200 | Very hard; fine grain |
| Cumaru | 3,540 | Among hardest flooring timbers |
| Ipe (Lapacho) | 3,680 | Exceptionally hard; used outdoors |

**Practical implication:** Janka values above 1,000 lbf are generally considered suitable for residential flooring. For commercial or high-heel traffic, above 1,200 lbf is preferred. Softer woods (pine, cherry, walnut) will show dents and scratches more readily — this may be desired (character/patina) or not.

## 2. HARDWOOD SPECIES — PROPERTIES AND SPECIFICATION

### 2.1 European Oak (Quercus robur / Quercus petraea)

The dominant and most commercially significant flooring timber in the UK and Europe.

**Properties:**
- Janka: ~1,120 lbf
- Tangential movement: ~0.24% per 1% MC
- Colour: pale blonde to mid-brown; heartwood slightly more golden; greying/silvering with age and light
- Grain: coarse, open grain; prominent ray fleck in quarter-sawn
- Tannin content: very high — reacts with iron (black staining) and with alkaline treatments to produce grey/ebonised effects
- Natural durability: Class 2 (durable) heartwood; good resistance to rot without treatment
- Drying: prone to checking and honeycomb if dried too rapidly; requires careful kiln drying
- Finishing: accepts all finishes; oils and hardwax oils particularly suited; reacts beautifully with lye, whitewash, and fuming treatments
- Availability: widely available FSC-certified; sustainable European production

**Commercial forms:** Strip, plank, wide board (to 300mm+ in premium products), parquet block (see Lexicon 3 for engineered oak)

**Variants:**
- **Smoked/fumed oak:** Ammonia fuming darkens the entire board by reacting with tannins; consistent deep brown/grey; not just a surface treatment
- **White/lye-treated oak:** Alkali treatment extracts some tannins, creating a bleached, white-grey appearance
- **Thermally modified oak:** Heat treatment under steam at 160–220°C reduces hygroscopicity and darkens colour; more dimensionally stable; lower strength
- **Character oak / rustic oak:** Higher proportion of knots, sap, and natural feature allowed by grade

### 2.2 American White Oak (Quercus alba)

**Properties:**
- Janka: ~1,360 lbf — harder than European oak
- Movement: similar to European oak
- Colour: pale cream to light tan; more consistent than European oak
- Grain: closed-pore grain compared to European oak; less pronounced ray fleck
- Tyloses: present in white oak's pore structure, making it naturally water-resistant (historically used for whisky barrels)
- Finishing: accepts all finishes; particularly popular with matte oils for Scandinavian aesthetics

**Distinguishing from European oak:** American white oak tends to be paler, more consistent in colour, with a slightly tighter grain. It lacks the pronounced medullary ray fleck of European quarter-sawn oak.

### 2.3 American Red Oak (Quercus rubra)

- Janka: ~1,290 lbf
- Pinkish-red tone in heartwood; pores open (lacks tyloses)
- Not water-resistant due to open pore structure
- Very widely used in North America; less dominant in UK market
- Open grain requires pore-filling before lacquer finishing for smooth result

### 2.4 Ash (Fraxinus excelsior / Fraxinus americana)

**Properties:**
- Janka: European ash ~1,320 lbf; American ash ~1,320 lbf
- Very pale cream to light beige; ring-porous grain pattern; often steamed to achieve more uniform appearance
- Straight, open grain; prominent growth rings create a dramatic figure in plain-sawn boards
- Good elasticity and shock resistance (used in sports floors — see commercial applications)
- Movement: moderate; similar to oak
- Finishing: accepts all finishes; pale colour highlights grain

**Market note:** Ash has faced significant supply pressure due to Ash Dieback (Hymenoscyphus fraxineus) devastating European ash populations. Supply chain disruption is ongoing and affects pricing.

### 2.5 Hard Maple (Acer saccharum)

**Properties:**
- Janka: ~1,450 lbf — one of the hardest domestic temperate species
- Very pale, almost white; minimal colour variation; clean, fine grain
- Closed pore — very smooth surface
- Highly reflective finish possible — used in dance floors and basketball courts
- Movement: high (tangential ~0.29% per 1% MC) — significant seasonal movement
- Finishing: can be difficult — closed grain can make oil penetration slow; lacquer finishes most common

**Variants:**
- **Bird's Eye Maple:** Distinctive circular figure caused by abnormal growth; rare; premium pricing
- **Quilted Maple:** Wavy, three-dimensional figure; used in specialist applications

### 2.6 American Black Walnut (Juglans nigra)

**Properties:**
- Janka: ~1,010 lbf — moderate; will show dents in heavy use
- Rich chocolate-brown heartwood; contrasting pale sapwood
- Fine, straight grain; smooth texture
- Premium aesthetic — among the most sought-after residential flooring timbers
- Movement: moderate (tangential ~0.19%)
- Finishing: oils complement natural colour; lacquer can look plastic over rich walnut tones
- Colour changes: lightens significantly with prolonged UV exposure — darker central areas may become more uniform over time

**Note:** European walnut (Juglans regia) is available and similar in character; generally lighter brown; more expensive as less supply.

### 2.7 Merbau / Kwila (Intsia bijuga / Intsia palembanica)

**Properties:**
- Janka: ~1,925 lbf — very hard
- Deep reddish-brown; interlocked grain; distinctive yellow deposits visible in pores
- Yellow deposits (silica/mineral) will bleed onto adjacent materials when wet — must be cleaned immediately after installation; use pH-neutral first cleaning
- Very durable; decay resistant
- **Sustainability concern:** Merbau is under severe harvest pressure in Southeast Asia; illegal logging is widespread; ONLY specify with verified FSC or PEFC chain-of-custody; be cautious with any unverified supply chain

### 2.8 Bamboo (Poaceae family)

**Technical note:** Bamboo is not a hardwood — it is a grass — but it is processed into flooring and often categorised alongside hardwood in the trade.

**Types:**
- **Horizontal bamboo:** Culm (stem) layers pressed horizontally; wide nodes visible
- **Vertical bamboo:** Culm strips pressed vertically; finer, more uniform appearance
- **Strand-woven (carbonised/natural):** Shredded fibres compressed under high pressure with adhesive; much denser than solid bamboo (Janka ~3,000 lbf for strand-woven)
- **Carbonised:** Heat-treated to brown colour; softer than natural bamboo

**Properties:**
- Strand-woven bamboo: excellent hardness and durability
- Standard bamboo: softer than most hardwoods; dents more easily
- Low moisture absorption (compared to wood) but not immune to movement
- Movement in humidity: can expand and gap significantly — acclimatisation essential
- Formaldehyde emissions: historically a concern with adhesive used in processing; specify E1 or E0 emission class products
- FSC certification is available for bamboo plantations; verify supply chain

### 2.9 Other Commonly Specified Species

| Species | Origin | Janka (lbf) | Colour | Notes |
|---------|--------|-------------|--------|-------|

| Cumaru (Tonka Bean) | South America | 3,540 | Reddish-brown; fine grain | Extremely hard; difficult to sand and nail |
| Ipe (Lapacho) | South America | 3,680 | Dark olive-brown | Most for outdoor decking; used in commercial interiors |

## 3. SOFTWOOD SPECIES USED IN FLOORING

Softwoods are conifers (gymnosperm trees). Despite the name, some softwoods are harder than some hardwoods, but as a category they are generally softer and more prone to denting than hardwood flooring species.

### 3.1 Scots Pine (Pinus sylvestris)

- Janka: ~870 lbf
- The original British domestic flooring timber — virtually all pre-1960 UK homes have pine floorboards
- Pale yellow-cream sapwood; amber-orange heartwood; darkens to honey over time
- Very attractive natural character: prominent growth rings, resin pockets, knots
- Will dent and scratch — part of the character and patina of the product
- Important for restoration of period properties — original boards should be sanded and restored wherever possible rather than replaced
- New Scots pine flooring is available for restoration projects (match to existing; allow for patina difference)
- Generally sold as T&G (tongue and groove) in traditional 70–100mm strip widths

### 3.2 Other Softwood Species

- **European spruce (Picea abies):** Very pale, almost white; sometimes used in Scandinavian-style flooring; low durability
- **Larch (Larix decidua):** Harder than Scots pine; attractive grain; used in some specialist applications
- **Heart pine (longleaf pine, reclaimed):** Dense, resin-rich old-growth pine reclaimed from historic structures; significantly harder than new growth pine; premium reclaimed product; Janka ~1,225 lbf
- **Douglas fir (Pseudotsuga menziesii):** Technically a softwood; orange-red with dramatic straight grain; used in commercial and loft-conversion contexts

## 4. GRADING SYSTEMS

### 4.1 European Grading — EN 13756 and Industry Grades

Wood flooring is graded by appearance, not structural strength. Grading criteria include: knot size and frequency, sapwood presence, grain deviation, checks, splits, and colour variation. There is no single universal grading standard across all manufacturers — grades are largely manufacturer-defined — but the EN 13756 standard provides a framework, and the following grades are broadly consistent across the European market:

**Prime / Select / AB:**
- The highest grade; most consistent appearance
- Very few or no knots; minimal colour variation; no sapwood; tight grain
- Maximum colour uniformity
- Also called "Prime," "Select," "Grade A," or "AB" depending on manufacturer
- Premium pricing; minimalist, contemporary interiors

**Natura / Classic / ABC:**
- Mid-range grade; natural variation present
- Small, tight knots permitted; slight colour variation; minimal sapwood
- Most popular grade — the balance of natural character and consistency
- Also called "Nature," "Classic," "Natural," or "ABC"

**Rustic / Character / ABCD:**
- Full character grade; knots, sap, colour variation, grain deviation all present
- May include small checks and resin pockets
- Most "natural" appearance; popular in farmhouse, country, and exposed-brick interiors
- Also called "Rustic," "Character," "Country," or "ABCD"

**Tavern / Cottage / D:**
- Lowest commercial grade; significant knots, sap, and feature
- Some boards may have loose knots (filled or plugged during manufacture)
- Budget-oriented product segment

**Note on grading consistency:** Two manufacturers' "Prime" grades may differ significantly. Always request samples before specification on large projects. The grade naming is entirely unstandardised — "Natural" at one manufacturer = "Rustic" at another.

### 4.2 American Grading — NOFMA and NHLA Standards

**NOFMA (National Oak Flooring Manufacturers Association) grades:**
- **Clear:** Virtually no character; most uniform
- **Select and Better:** Minor character; dominant grade in US residential
- **No. 1 Common:** Noticeable variation; knots and colour; character floor
- **No. 2 Common:** Full character; significant variation; similar to rustic

**NHLA (National Hardwood Lumber Association):** Governs rough lumber grading, also referenced in flooring; Firsts and Seconds (FAS) = clearest boards.

### 4.3 Parquet Block Grading

- Parquet blocks (see section 5) are typically graded as First Quality, Second Quality, or Seconds
- First Quality: consistent colour and grain; tight knots only or none; minimal sap
- Second Quality: character permitted; suitable where some variation is acceptable
- Reclaimed parquet: no grading standard; condition-based assessment

## 5. MILLING PROFILES AND BOARD DIMENSIONS

### 5.1 Strip vs Plank vs Wide Board

**Strip flooring:**
- Width: typically 40–85mm
- Thickness: 18–22mm (UK standard solid strip)
- Traditional appearance; popular in period properties
- Lower movement per board due to narrower width
- More seams per unit area

**Plank flooring:**
- Width: 85–150mm
- Thickness: 18–22mm
- The mainstream modern residential product
- Balances aesthetics with manageable movement

**Wide board / feature plank:**
- Width: 150–300mm+
- Thickness: 20–25mm (thicker boards needed for stability in wider formats)
- Premium; architectural; Scandinavian-inspired interiors
- Requires quarter-sawn or rift-sawn for stability at wide widths
- Plain-sawn wide boards will show significant seasonal gapping and cupping — must communicate this to customer
- Expansion gaps of 15–20mm or more required at all walls

**Parquet blocks:**
- Small rectangular blocks: typically 225×70×22mm or 230×70×20mm (traditional UK)
- Laid in herringbone, basketweave, or brick pattern
- Originally solid oak, pine, or maple; now also available in engineered construction (see Lexicon 3)
- Traditional laying: fully adhered; no tongue and groove between blocks — abutted

**Parquet strips / finger parquet:**
- Smaller version: typically 160×40×12mm; 200×40×15mm
- Laid in herringbone pattern; very fine, intricate appearance
- Common in period refurbishments of Victorian and Edwardian properties

### 5.2 Tongue and Groove (T&G) vs End-Matched

**Standard T&G:**
- Tongue on one long edge; groove on the other
- Boards must be cut to length on site; no end locking
- Butt joints at board ends must be staggered — minimum 300mm between end joints in adjacent rows

**End-matched T&G:**
- Tongue and groove on both long and short edges
- Boards lock in all four directions
- Stronger flooring; allows floating installation; reduces site waste (can start a row with a short piece)
- Now standard in most premium solid strip and plank products

### 5.3 Edge Profiles

**Square edge:**
- No profile on edge; boards abut flat
- Produces a completely flat floor with hairline joint
- Any height variation between boards is visible as a ridge — requires very flat subfloor
- Traditional parquet and premium strip floors

**Micro-bevel (micro-V):**
- Very slight chamfer on board edge — typically 0.5–1.0mm
- Creates a hairline shadow line between boards
- Hides minor height variation; easier to clean than a sharp square-edge joint
- Most common profile in contemporary residential

**Bevel (V-groove / eased edge):**
- More pronounced chamfer — typically 1.5–3mm
- Creates a visible V-groove between boards
- Emphasises individual boards; rustic/country aesthetic
- Hides greater height variation — more forgiving of subfloor imperfection
- Collects dust in groove — may not suit allergy sufferers or high-cleanliness environments

**Distressed edges:**
- Hand-scraped, wire-brushed, or mechanically distressed edge profile
- Random, irregular appearance; rustic character
- Part of the "hand-scraped" or "brushed and oiled" product aesthetic

**Eased edge / round-over:**
- Gentle radius on top edge
- Softens the joint; comfortable underfoot
- Common in gymnasia and dance floors

### 5.4 Face Width vs Nominal Width

Always clarify: "face width" is the visible board width after tongue is engaged. Nominal width includes the tongue. A board sold as "150mm" may have a face width of 143mm once tongued-and-grooved. Calculate material requirements on face width.

### 5.5 Board Thickness and Wear Layer

- **Total thickness:** 18mm, 20mm, 22mm are the UK standard solid wood thicknesses
- There is no separate "wear layer" in solid wood — the entire thickness is available for sanding
- A 20mm solid board can typically be sanded 5–8 times over its lifetime
- Minimum thickness to nail-fix: 18mm to timber; 20mm recommended for concrete (nailing through adhesive bed)

## 6. MOISTURE SCIENCE AND ACCLIMATISATION

### 6.1 The Critical Importance of Moisture Management

More solid wood flooring failures arise from moisture-related problems than from any other cause. Every installer must understand and control moisture at three stages:
1. Subfloor moisture content before delivery
2. Acclimatisation of boards to site conditions
3. Ongoing maintenance of appropriate interior humidity

### 6.2 Target Moisture Content

**Ideal installation MC for solid wood flooring:**
- UK interior conditions (centrally heated): **8–10% MC**
- Unheated spaces or variable environment: up to 12% MC
- The floor should be installed as close to its expected in-service EMC as possible
- If installed too dry (in summer, heated room): boards will absorb moisture and expand → compression, buckling
- If installed too wet: boards will lose moisture and shrink → gapping between boards

**Kiln-dried timber:** Most commercial hardwood flooring is kiln-dried to 6–9% MC at point of manufacture. During storage, transport, and distribution, MC may change. Never assume delivery MC matches installation target.

### 6.3 Measuring Moisture Content

**Pin-type moisture meter:**
- Two metal probes inserted into wood surface; measures electrical resistance between pins
- Resistance decreases as MC increases
- Accurate for relative measurements and screening
- Calibration must be set to species (different wood densities affect reading)
- Measures only the surface layers (top 6mm approx)
- The standard site measurement tool

**Resistance (pin) meter calibration:** Most meters have species correction settings. European oak is typically the reference species. For dense tropical hardwoods, apply species correction.

**RF (radio frequency) moisture meter:**
- Non-invasive; measures through the depth of the board
- Useful for checking boards without marking the surface
- Less accurate than pin meter at specific MC values; good for trend/screening

**Oven-dry (gravimetric) test:**
- Most accurate method; rarely used on site
- Weigh sample → dry in oven at 103°C until constant weight → calculate: MC% = (wet weight − dry weight) ÷ dry weight × 100
- Reference method for calibration

### 6.4 Subfloor Moisture Requirements

**Concrete subfloor:**
- Maximum **75% RH** (measured to BS 8203) before wood flooring installation
- For solid wood flooring directly nailed to concrete (rare): apply DPM before installation
- For adhesive-down wood over concrete: most adhesive manufacturers specify ≤75% RH; some moisture-tolerant adhesives permit up to 85% RH (confirm with data sheet)
- Always test; never assume

**Timber subfloor:**
- Maximum **14% MC** for timber subfloor boards/panels
- If above 14%: find and eliminate moisture source; allow to dry before proceeding
- Significant implication: new build properties with inadequately dried screeds or plaster are very common — always test

### 6.5 Acclimatisation

**Purpose:** Allow boards to reach a moisture content appropriate to the room they will be installed in, before installation. This minimises post-installation movement.

**Process:**
1. Bring boards to the installation site (the actual room, not a garage or outbuilding)
2. The room must be at "living conditions" — heated/ventilated as it will be in use; not a building site with open windows and no heating
3. Open packaging to allow air circulation — or stack with spacers between bundles
4. Measure board MC at delivery; measure room conditions (temperature and RH)
5. Continue to monitor until MC is stable and within target range (8–10% for UK heated interiors)

**Typical acclimatisation times:**
- Standard strip/plank (18–22mm): minimum 48–72 hours in stable conditions
- Wide boards (150mm+): minimum 5–7 days
- Parquet blocks (dense/thick): minimum 5–7 days
- Boards delivered in cold/humid conditions: longer acclimatisation needed
- In practice, always measure rather than rely on time alone

**Common mistake:** Fitters leaving boards in packaging stacked on pallets for 48 hours in an unheated space and calling it "acclimatised." This is inadequate. The boards must be open to the room air, and the room must be at lived-in conditions.

**Room conditions during installation:**
- Heating should be operational for minimum 14 days before installation (to stabilise new-build RH)
- Windows should be closed; ventilation normal
- Wet trades (plastering, concrete, screeding) must be complete and dry
- Ideal room temperature: 15–22°C
- Ideal RH: 40–60%

## 7. SUBFLOOR REQUIREMENTS

### 7.1 Flatness and Level Tolerances

**Tolerance for direct-nail and glue-down solid wood:**
- BS 8203 standard: maximum **3mm deviation under a 2m straight-edge**
- For floating installation: same tolerance
- Hollows and ridges outside tolerance: fill with self-levelling compound (SLC) or grind down

**Tolerance for parquet:**
- Parquet blocks are thin and can telegraph subfloor irregularities more than strip planks
- Maximum 2mm under 2m straight-edge is preferred for parquet

### 7.2 Concrete Subfloors

- **Moisture test:** BS 8203; maximum 75% RH (or to adhesive manufacturer specification)
- **DPM:** If moisture reading is above 75% or risk of moisture ingress exists, apply a two-component epoxy DPM before the adhesive bed
  - Popular epoxy DPMs: Sika Level-01 Primer, F. Ball Stopgap F77, Bostik Ultra, Mapei Primer G
  - Allow DPM to cure fully before applying wood adhesive (typically 12–24 hours at 20°C)
- **New screeds:** Minimum 28 days drying before testing; UFH screeds require commissioning cycle first
- **Surface prep:** Grind off laitance (weak surface layer), old adhesive, and contamination
- Prime concrete surface with appropriate primer before adhesive application

### 7.3 Timber Subfloors

**Existing T&G floorboards:**
- Check for movement, bounce, rot, and insect damage
- Nail/screw down all loose boards — nail heads punched below surface
- Gaps over 3mm: fill with flexible wood filler or lay 6mm plywood overlay
- Moisture: maximum 14% MC; check with pin meter at multiple locations
- Ensure adequate ventilation to underfloor void if suspended timber ground floor — check airbricks are unobstructed

**Plywood overlay:**
- Minimum 18mm structural-grade plywood for nail-fix installation (to give adequate nail grip)
- Minimum 9mm plywood for glue-down
- Fix at 200mm grid with screws; stagger all joints
- Allow expansion gap between sheets: 2mm

**Chipboard:**
- Standard 22mm P5 (moisture-resistant) chipboard as subfloor: acceptable for glue-down and floating installation
- Not ideal for nail-fix — nail withdrawal less predictable than plywood
- Check for delamination or water damage at edges

**OSB:**
- OSB 3 (moisture-resistant) at 18mm+ acceptable for most installation methods
- Surface may be rough — overskim with SLC if using glue-down

### 7.4 Radiant Heating Concrete (Screeds with UFH)

- Anhydrite/calcium sulphate screeds: widely used with UFH; different preparation from cement screed
- Anhydrite screeds must be sanded to remove surface laitance (floor sander, 40-grit)
- Must be primed with a dedicated anhydrite primer before SLC or adhesive
- Adhesive compatibility: check manufacturer adhesive is approved for use on anhydrite
- Do not apply water-based SLC directly to anhydrite — use appropriate primer first

## 8. INSTALLATION METHODS

### 8.1 Overview of Methods

Solid wood flooring can be installed by three principal methods:

| Method | Subfloor | Typical Application |
|--------|----------|---------------------|
| Secret nail/staple | Timber (plywood/boards) | Residential; direct to timber; ground/upper floor |
| Glue-down (full bond) | Concrete; plywood | Residential; commercial; basements; over UFH |
| Floating | Any (with underlay) | Where nailing or gluing not practical; limited by movement |

**Floating is generally NOT recommended for solid wood in widths over 90mm** — the accumulated movement across many boards becomes very large and cannot be adequately controlled with perimeter expansion gaps alone. Floating is primarily an engineered wood installation method (see Lexicon 3). However, narrower solid strip (40–75mm) can be floated in domestic applications.

### 8.2 Installation Direction

**Direction of boards relative to room:**
- **Perpendicular to main window:** The most common recommendation — boards run away from the primary light source; seams are less visible as light travels along, not across, the boards
- **Perpendicular to floor joists (if nailing to joists directly):** Provides structural bearing
- **Diagonal installation:** 45° laying — reduces the appearance of short rooms; uses more material (~10% wastage increase); more cuts

**Pattern installations:**
- **Herringbone:** Boards laid at 90° to each other in a V-pattern; requires establishing a precise centre line
- **Chevron:** Similar to herringbone but boards cut at 45° on the end; joins meet in a point rather than staggered
- **Basketweave:** Groups of parallel boards alternating direction in blocks
- **Brick bond:** Standard offset staggered joint (like brickwork) — default for plank flooring

**Determining starting point:**
- Check if room walls are parallel and square (they rarely are perfectly)
- Snap chalk lines 300mm from each wall as reference
- Establish a working line perpendicular to intended board direction
- The first row is the critical reference — if it goes in slightly off-true, the whole floor follows

## 9. FIXING SYSTEMS — SECRET NAILING AND STAPLING

### 9.1 Secret Nailing (Secret Fixing)

Secret nailing (also called "blind nailing") involves driving nails through the tongue of each board at an angle, so the nail head is hidden by the groove of the next board. This is the traditional and most authentic fixing method for solid wood flooring on timber subfloors.

**Process:**
1. First row: lay boards against wall (or first chalk line); use face nails (nails through the face of the board, near the wall — these will be covered by skirting/beading)
2. Subsequent rows: drive nail through tongue at 45–50° angle using a floor nailer (manual or pneumatic)
3. Each board: minimum one nail every 300mm along its length; one nail within 50mm of each end; additional nails at any defect or cup
4. The groove of the next board slides over the nail head — the fixing is entirely concealed
5. Last row (at far wall): face nail as per first row; will be covered by skirting

**Nail types and sizes:**
- **Cleat nails:** L-shaped; best holding power in tongue; recommended
  - Typical size: 38mm × 1.6mm (for 18–20mm board)
  - 45mm cleats for 22mm board or dense hardwoods
- **Staples:** Diverge-leg staples driven through tongue; faster to install; holding power adequate for most applications
  - 15.5 gauge staples: 38mm or 45mm length depending on board thickness
- **Smooth shank nails:** Less holding power than cleated; not preferred for dense hardwoods
- **Annular ring / spiral shank nails:** Used with hand nailing for maximum grip

**Nail/staple spacing:**
- Maximum 300mm centres along each board
- Minimum within 50mm of board ends
- Dense species (Jatoba, Cumaru): 200mm centres; pre-drilling may be needed for tongue integrity

**Manual nailer (mallet nailer):** A purpose-built floor nailer struck with a mallet. The mechanism drives the nail at the correct angle with one blow. Essential for production nailing; cannot damage boards. Adjustable for different board thicknesses.

**Pneumatic nailer:** Air-powered; considerably faster; same mechanism. Requires compressor (80–100 PSI operating pressure). The professional standard for large areas.

### 9.2 Pre-Drilling

- Required for very dense or brittle species (Ipe, Cumaru, dense tropical hardwoods)
- Drill pilot hole through tongue at 45° before nailing
- Prevents tongue splitting and allows proper nail seating
- Use a drill bit slightly smaller than nail shank diameter

### 9.3 Face Nailing

Used where secret nailing is not possible (first and last rows near walls; parquet blocks; narrow pieces):
- Nail driven through face of board
- Set nail slightly below surface using nail punch
- Fill nail hole with matching wood filler or sawdust/adhesive paste
- On parquet: all blocks are face-nailed or fully adhered

## 10. ADHESIVE-DOWN INSTALLATION

### 10.1 Applications

- **Over concrete subfloor:** The standard method — nailing to concrete is impractical in most cases (though machine nailing through adhesive bed into concrete is possible with specialist equipment)
- **Where UFH is present in concrete:** Glue-down maintains closer contact with heated subfloor for better heat transfer than floating
- **Wide board installations:** Full adhesion controls movement more effectively than nailing alone
- **Parquet:** Traditionally fully adhered; all parquet blocks/strips are glue-down

### 10.2 Adhesive Types for Wood Flooring

**Single-component moisture-curing urethane (MS polymer):**
- The industry-standard wood flooring adhesive in professional installation
- Cures by reacting with atmospheric and subfloor moisture
- Provides a flexible, elastic bond — allows the slight movement of wood while maintaining adhesion
- Open time: 20–45 minutes typically; full cure: 24–48 hours
- Good sound-dampening properties (elastic nature)
- Solvent-free; low VOC (important for wellbeing and compliance)
- Suitable for most wood species; check compatibility with oily species (teak, Merbau)
- Application: notched trowel (V-notch or A2 pattern); spread rate per data sheet
- Brands: Sika T-54, Bostik Parquet, Mapei Ultrabond P990, F. Ball Styccobond F49

**Two-component epoxy adhesive:**
- Very strong, rigid bond; once cured, minimal movement accommodation
- Used where maximum stability is required (gymnasium, dance floor, commercial)
- No flexibility — not suitable where wood movement is significant
- Shorter working time than urethane; must mix in correct ratio

**Solvent-based contact adhesive:**
- Used for parquet block installation historically; now largely superseded by MS polymer
- Apply to both surfaces; wait for tack; press boards into position — no repositioning
- Solvent vapour concerns; poor suitability for UFH applications

**Water-based dispersion adhesive:**
- Cheaper; lower performance than urethane
- Risk of moisture transmission from adhesive affecting wood MC — must be used with DPM on concrete
- Not recommended for solid wood over concrete without DPM

### 10.3 Trowel Selection and Spread Rate

Correct trowel notch is critical — too little adhesive = inadequate coverage; too much = adhesive squeeze-up between boards.

- **V-notch trowel:** Most common for strip and plank; 2.5×2.5×3mm or 3×3×3mm
- **A2 (square-notch) trowel:** 3×3×3mm; for wider boards and parquet blocks
- **Spread rate:** Typically 600–900g/m² for urethane adhesives; confirm with data sheet
- **Coverage check:** After laying a board, lift and inspect — minimum 80% coverage for residential; 95% for commercial/heavy traffic

### 10.4 Installation Process (Glue-Down)

1. Prime subfloor as required (essential on concrete; confirm primer with adhesive manufacturer)
2. Allow primer to cure
3. Apply adhesive with notched trowel; work in sections of approximately 0.5–1m² to stay within open time
4. Lay boards into adhesive; press firmly; tap with rubber mallet to ensure full contact
5. Check alignment and spacing continuously
6. Clean adhesive from face immediately — urethane cured on a wood face is very difficult to remove
7. Do not walk on boards for minimum 24 hours (open the adhesive may allow boards to shift)
8. Apply edge tape and weighted planks to hold boards flat during cure if any cupping tendency
9. After full cure (48 hours): fit skirting; apply finishing

**Adhesive on oily species (teak, Merbau, Iroko):**
- Natural oils in these species can significantly inhibit adhesive bonding
- Wipe board faces with acetone or white spirit immediately before laying to degrease
- Apply adhesive promptly while surface is clean
- Use a specifically tested urethane adhesive for oily species

## 11. FLOATING INSTALLATION

### 11.1 When to Float Solid Wood

Floating is suitable for solid strip in widths up to approximately 90mm. Above 90mm, accumulated movement across many boards becomes unmanageable and floating is not recommended.

**Suitable applications:**
- Narrow solid strip (40–75mm) in apartments or areas where nailing is not permitted
- Renovation situations where subfloor damage is a concern
- Areas of minor subfloor moisture risk (the underlay acts as a barrier)

### 11.2 Underlay for Floating Solid Wood

- A combined underlay/vapour barrier is essential over concrete
- 1mm–2mm PE foam with integrated DPM (polythene sheet)
- Acoustic underlay (cork or PU foam with DPM) for upper floor installations
- Keep underlay thin — thick, soft underlay under narrow solid boards creates excessive flex and stress on T&G joints

### 11.3 Board Connections

- Floating solid wood relies on T&G joints locking boards together laterally
- For floating installation, T&G joints may be glued with PVA/D3 wood glue or left unglued (dry-float)
- Glued T&G floating: boards move as a single large panel — expansion gap management is critical
- Dry-float (no glue): boards can move individually; less risk of buckling but more joint noise

## 12. EXPANSION GAPS AND MOVEMENT JOINTS

### 12.1 Perimeter Expansion Gap

**All solid wood floors must have a perimeter expansion gap at every fixed structure:**
- Walls, skirting boards, columns, door frames, kitchen island bases, hearths, bath panels — everything
- The gap allows the floor to expand seasonally without compression against fixed structures

**Minimum expansion gap sizes:**
| Board width | Typical gap (per EN 13756 guidance) |
|-------------|--------------------------------------|
| Up to 70mm | 10mm minimum |
| 70–120mm | 12–15mm |
| 120–180mm | 15–18mm |
| 180mm+ | 18–20mm+; calculate for specific species and anticipated MC range |

**Calculation method (BS 8201):**
> Required gap (mm) = Room width (m) × 1.5mm per 1m width (conservative rule of thumb)

For a 5m-wide room with 180mm oak boards: calculate expected movement:
> 180mm × 0.0024 × (expected MC range, typically 4%) × (number of boards) or simply calculate total floor panel width change

**Critical note:** Expansion gaps must be free and clear — no adhesive, debris, or cables in the gap. The gap is filled visually by skirting board or a discreet beading profile.

### 12.2 Intermediate Movement Joints

In large rooms (over approximately 10m in either direction), a mid-room movement joint must be incorporated:
- The expanding floor panel is divided into segments no larger than 10×10m
- Joint finished with a T-bar or expansion strip cover
- This principle is similar to movement joints in tiled floors
- Failure to incorporate intermediate joints in large areas results in compression buckling

### 12.3 Doorways

- In all doorways, an expansion/threshold strip is required
- This interrupts the floor panel and allows each room's floor to move independently
- For rooms separated by a loadbearing wall: mandatory joint at the door threshold
- The cover strip must have sufficient tolerance to allow movement in both directions

### 12.4 T-Bars, Reducers, and Cover Strips

- **T-bar (H-bar):** Covers an intermediate joint between two floors at the same level
- **Reducer:** One side for wood floor, the other tapers down to a lower adjacent floor (tile, vinyl)
- **End cap:** Covers the edge of a floor where it terminates (e.g., at a step)
- **Stair nosing:** Covers the leading edge of a wood floor at the top of stairs
- Materials: solid wood to match floor; aluminium (for durability); brass (traditional)
- Fix threshold bars to subfloor, not to the wood floor itself — the floor must be free to move beneath

## 13. UNDERFLOOR HEATING WITH SOLID WOOD

### 13.1 The Challenge

Solid wood and underfloor heating are technically compatible but require careful management. UFH systems create elevated temperatures and can produce very low RH conditions in the room (heated air expands and can hold more moisture — if the interior is not humidified, RH drops sharply in winter, causing wood to lose MC and shrink, producing gaps).

### 13.2 Species Suitability

Not all species are suitable over UFH. Recommended species:
- **Most suitable:** European oak (particularly quarter-sawn), thermally modified oak, ash, hard maple, engineered wood (see Lexicon 3 — far superior to solid over UFH)
- **Less suitable:** Wide plain-sawn boards; teak (oily); acacia (high movement); bamboo (variable)
- **Critical parameter:** Use only quarter-sawn or rift-sawn boards for UFH applications; plain-sawn wide boards are strongly not recommended

### 13.3 Maximum Surface Temperature

- **Maximum floor surface temperature: 27°C** (for wood flooring)
- UFH system must be programmable and capable of maintaining this limit
- A surface thermometer should be used to verify; thermostats control water/cable temperature, not necessarily surface temperature
- Exceeding 27°C consistently will cause irreversible cracking, checking, and MC reduction

### 13.4 UFH Commissioning Protocol Before Installation

1. Commission UFH system at least 28 days after screed installation (cement) or 7 days (anhydrite)
2. Run UFH at initial 20°C for 3 days; increase 5°C per day to maximum operating temperature
3. Hold at maximum temperature for minimum 7 days
4. Reduce by 5°C per day to room temperature
5. Switch off 48 hours before wood floor installation
6. After installation: switch on at minimum setting; increase by 5°C per day over one week back to operating temperature

### 13.5 Maximum Board Width Over UFH

- Solid wood: maximum 100mm width recommended by most adhesive and flooring manufacturers
- Some manufacturers approve up to 120mm with their specific adhesive systems
- Wide board solid wood over UFH: high risk; most manufacturers recommend engineered wood instead
- Engineered wood (Lexicon 3) is strongly preferred for all UFH applications

### 13.6 Adhesive for UFH

- Must be specifically rated for UFH application and temperature
- Urethane (MS polymer) adhesives are generally UFH-rated; confirm with data sheet
- Do not use water-based or solvent-based adhesives over UFH
- Full-bond adhesion (no floating) is mandatory over UFH for solid wood

## 14. FINISHING SYSTEMS — SITE-APPLIED

### 14.1 Why Finishing Matters

The finish system determines:
- Surface durability and wear resistance
- Appearance (sheen level, colour tone)
- Maintenance requirements
- Repair and refinishing approach
- Moisture exclusion at the surface
- The right finish for the species, usage level, and aesthetic preference is critical to long-term satisfaction

### 14.2 Oils

**Penetrating oils (Danish oil, Tung oil, Linseed oil-based):**
- Penetrate into the wood fibres; do not form a surface film
- Leave a natural, low-sheen appearance that feels like "real wood" underfoot
- The wood can breathe; moisture exchange continues
- Require regular maintenance: periodic re-oiling every 1–3 years depending on traffic
- Easy to locally repair — re-oil affected area; blends invisibly
- Good for natural wood aesthetic; popular in Scandinavian and rustic interiors
- Not as protective against water staining as lacquer or hardwax oil
- Application: wipe on with cloth or brush; allow penetration; wipe off excess; repeat

**Hardwax oils (Rubio Monocoat, Osmo Polyx, Pallmann Magic Oil):**
- A premium development combining penetrating oil and wax components
- More surface protection than pure oil while retaining the natural feel
- Slight surface enhancement without film build
- Rubio Monocoat: a single-coat system using a reactive molecule that bonds to wood fibres; claimed to require no primer or subsequent coats; eco-friendly
- Osmo Polyx: two-coat system; plant-based oils and waxes; excellent durability; low sheen
- Re-oiling for maintenance is easy and spot-repairable
- Very popular in contemporary residential; increasingly specified in light commercial
- Application: thin coats; do not over-apply; buff with machine padder for even finish

**UV-cured oil (factory-applied):** See section 15.

### 14.3 Lacquers (Varnishes)

**Water-based polyurethane lacquer:**
- Forms a hard, durable, transparent film on the wood surface
- Higher surface protection than oil; more resistant to water and spillage
- Modern water-based lacquers have very low VOC; fast dry time (2–4 hours recoat time)
- Available in dead flat (3–5% sheen), matt (10–20%), satin (30–40%), semi-gloss (50–60%), gloss (80%+)
- When scratched or worn, the damage is visible as a break in the film — "white scratches" visible in matt lacquer
- Spot repair is difficult — worn areas need complete recoating for an invisible finish
- Life between full sanding and recoating: 5–15 years depending on traffic and maintenance
- Application: roller or brush; minimum 3 coats on raw wood (1 primer/sealer + 2 finish); light sand between coats (screen/buff coat)

**Oil-modified polyurethane (solvent-based):**
- Traditional lacquer; high durability; yellows over time (particularly on pale woods: maple, ash, beige oak)
- High VOC; significant solvent smell; longer dry times
- Largely superseded by water-based in residential; still used in some commercial contracts for extreme durability

**Acid-catalysed lacquer (conversion varnish):**
- Very hard, durable two-component lacquer
- Extremely good chemical and wear resistance
- Used in commercial and gymnasium contexts
- Requires precise mixing; relatively high VOC

### 14.4 Stains and Colourants

**Water-based stains:**
- Penetrate the wood surface; colour the fibres
- Applied before sealer/lacquer
- Wide colour range; consistent results on uniform species
- Grain-enhancing: dark stains on open-grain species (oak) emphasise the grain structure beautifully
- Must be compatible with topcoat (oil-based stains under water-based lacquer can cause adhesion issues — use water-based stain)

**Reactive stains (chemical treatments):**
- React chemically with the wood's own extractives/tannins
- Lye (potassium carbonate solution): reacts with tannins in oak to produce bleached, grey-white appearance
- Iron acetate (steel wool in vinegar): reacts with tannins to produce grey/ebonised effect; can be used with tea solution to intensify
- Fuming (ammonia): gaseous ammonia reacts with tannins in a sealed chamber; uniform dark grey/brown
- These are irreversible — cannot be stripped; must be tested on sample piece

**Oils with integrated pigment:**
- Hardwax oils available in a wide range of tinted options
- Colour and protection in one product; easy to apply and maintain

### 14.5 Sealing and Finishing Sequence (Site-Applied)

**Raw sanded wood — oil finish:**
1. Sand to final grit (typically 80–100 grit for rustic; 100–120 grit for contemporary)
2. Clean dust thoroughly (vacuum; tack cloth)
3. Apply first coat of oil; work section by section; wipe off excess after 20 minutes
4. Allow to cure per manufacturer guidance (typically 12–24 hours)
5. Light buff with white pad on buffer
6. Apply second coat if specified; repeat
7. Cure fully before allowing traffic (typically 24–48 hours)

**Raw sanded wood — lacquer finish:**
1. Sand to final grit (typically 120 grit before first coat)
2. Apply sealer coat; allow to dry (2–4 hours water-based)
3. Screen/denib with 120–150 grit screen or fine pad
4. Apply second coat; dry; screen
5. Apply final topcoat (total minimum 3 coats for raw wood)
6. Allow 24–48 hours before light traffic; 5–7 days before heavy use

## 15. FINISHING SYSTEMS — FACTORY-APPLIED

Most modern pre-finished solid wood flooring is finished in the factory with UV-cured systems.

### 15.1 UV-Cured Lacquer

- Lacquer applied in multiple coats in factory; cured instantly by UV light at each coat
- Extremely hard, durable surface
- 7–10 coats typical for premium products
- Advantages: boards ready to use immediately after installation; no dust; no drying time; consistent quality
- Disadvantages: scratches show as white marks; re-sanding may remove bevel; edge-to-edge colour matching after sanding may differ from original factory finish

### 15.2 UV-Cured Oil (Hard Wax Oil)

- Factory-applied hardwax oil cured by UV
- Better natural wood feel than UV lacquer; easier to maintain and spot-repair
- Popular premium option for contemporary floors
- Maintenance: periodic re-oiling with compatible home care product; oil penetrates between factory oil layers

### 15.3 Pre-Finished vs Site-Finished — Decision Guide

| Factor | Pre-finished | Site-finished (raw) |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| Occupation time | Immediate | 3–7 days drying |
| Dust/disruption | None | Significant |
| Finish consistency | Factory QC | Depends on contractor skill |
| Bevel retention | Factory bevels remain | Bevels may be sanded off |
| Customisation | Limited | Unlimited |
| Maintenance repair | Spot-repair difficult | Sand and refinish area possible |
| Cost (installation) | Higher board cost | Higher labour cost (sanding) |
| For renovation/bespoke | Less suitable | Preferred |

## 16. THRESHOLD DETAILS AND TRANSITIONS

### 16.1 Wood to Tile

- Height difference: wood floor typically 18–22mm above subfloor; tile typically 10–15mm
- Use a reducer profile that transitions from wood height to tile height with a gentle ramp
- Reduce trip hazard: maximum step height at threshold 6mm (Approved Document M, Building Regulations)
- At fire doorways: consult fire door certification; some threshold profiles affect fire door sealing

### 16.2 Wood to Carpet

- Where wood is higher: carpet can tuck under a metal threshold strip
- Where carpet is higher: carpet gripper transitions to wood with an end cap
- The junction should be clean and manageable — avoid complex height differences

### 16.3 Wood to Wood (Matching)

- Adjacent rooms: boards run through under the door if possible (floor is continuous)
- If a joint is required at the door: T-bar cover strip
- Always consider pile direction, board width, and whether the two floors were laid at the same time

### 16.4 Wood at Stairs

- **Bullnose (stair nose) profile:** Matches the wood floor; forms the exposed leading edge of each stair tread; also used at the landing/stair transition
- Fit stair nosing before treads if fitting wood to the stair itself
- The stair nosing must be face-fixed (face nailed or screwed) and may also be adhered with construction adhesive

## 17. PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 17.1 Gapping Between Boards

**Symptoms:** Visible gaps between boards, particularly in winter.

**Causes:**
1. **Normal seasonal movement** — boards lose moisture in winter (heating) and shrink; gaps close in summer. If gaps are consistent and close seasonally, this is normal and appropriate.
2. **Installed too wet** — boards installed above target MC (>10%); as they dry to equilibrium, they shrink and gap. Gaps may be wider than seasonal norm and may not fully close.
3. **Room environment too dry** — RH below 40% in winter; boards dry out excessively. Solution: humidification.
4. **Insufficient expansion gap left** — perimeter gap consumed expansion; boards "locked" in summer; in winter they release from wall and gaps appear internally.
5. **Inadequate adhesive coverage** — partially adhered boards curl or separate.

**Assessment:**
- Measure gap width; monitor over the year
- If gaps close in summer: normal
- If gaps persist year-round: installed too wet, room too dry, or inadequate adhesive

**Threshold for concern:** Gaps exceeding 1–2mm year-round in standard plank width suggest an installation or moisture problem, not normal seasonal behaviour.

### 17.2 Cupping

**Symptoms:** Board edges higher than the centre; concave across width (face is concave = "cupped").

**Causes:**
1. **Moisture differential between face and back of board** — the face is losing moisture faster than the back (e.g., excessive heating with no humidification), or the back is gaining moisture from a damp subfloor. The wetter face expands more, causing the board to cup with the wet face concave.
2. **Subfloor too wet** — moisture ingressing from below
3. **Spill or cleaning water from above** — top surface wet; temporary cupping may resolve as moisture equalises

**Critical distinction:** Cupping is often reversible if the cause is addressed promptly. Permanent set ("compressionset") occurs if boards are crushed together while cupped and then dry in that position, leaving permanent deformation.

**Action:**
- Identify and eliminate moisture source
- Improve room humidity balance
- Allow floor to equalize
- Do not sand a cupped floor — if cupping is active, sanding the high edges will leave a crowned floor when the boards recover. Wait for full dryness and stability, then sand.

### 17.3 Crowning

**Symptoms:** Board centre higher than edges; convex across width (opposite of cupping).

**Causes:**
1. **A cupped floor that was sanded before drying** — the high edges were sanded off; when the board dried and returned to flat, the sanded edges are lower than the original flat face, leaving a crown
2. **Over-dry conditions followed by moisture increase** — boards contracted and were crushed; then absorbed moisture and expanded in the centre

**Action:**
- Sand only when absolutely stable
- Address humidity control

### 17.4 Buckling / Tenting

**Symptoms:** Boards pop up from subfloor; pronounced ridge forms.

**Causes:**
1. **Insufficient expansion gap** — floor expanded but had no room to expand; enormous compression force builds; the path of least resistance is upward (buckling)
2. **Excessive moisture** — flooding or major spillage; wood expands dramatically
3. **Failure of adhesive bond** — glue-down installation where adhesive failed; boards lift under expansion forces

**Action:**
- Remove affected boards; identify and eliminate moisture source if present
- In an expansion-gap failure: boards may return to flat once gaps are created (remove skirting to relieve pressure) — assess after 2 weeks
- Severely buckled boards may have permanent set and require replacement

### 17.5 Squeaking and Movement

**Symptoms:** Audible creak or squeak underfoot; may be accompanied by slight board movement.

**Causes:**
1. **Nail-fixed floor — nails working loose** — boards rubbing against loose nail shank
2. **Board-to-board contact in floating installation** — slight T&G movement under load
3. **Board contacting subfloor directly** — particularly on floating floors where underlay has been compressed
4. **Subfloor itself squeaking** — the squeak is in the subfloor, not the wood floor; particularly common in timber-frame buildings with un-fixed floorboards
5. **Debris in expansion gap** — small pieces of wood/stone preventing free movement; boards grind against them

**Diagnosis:**
- Walk over the floor methodically to localise the squeak
- Try: pressing the board firmly down while walking — if squeak disappears, the board is lifting from subfloor; if squeak persists, it is board-to-board or subfloor
- Check subfloor from below if accessible

### 17.6 Staining and Discolouration

**Iron tannate staining (black staining):**
- Black stain where iron (nails, metal tools, water with iron content) contacts oak
- Oak's very high tannin content reacts with iron to form iron tannate — the same chemistry as black ink
- Prevention: use stainless steel or galvanised fixings; clean tools immediately; do not use iron bucket for mixing
- Removal: oxalic acid solution applied carefully; may lighten but rarely fully removes

**White haze under oil or lacquer:**
- In oil: insufficient wipe-off of excess oil during application; oil cures on surface rather than penetrating
- In lacquer: moisture trapped under lacquer film (applied in humid conditions)
- In oil: sand back and re-oil with correct technique
- In lacquer: if extensive, must sand and recoat; if minor, can sometimes buff out

**Bleed-through of adhesive:**
- Urethane adhesive bleeding up through joints between boards
- Prevention: use correct spread rate; do not over-apply adhesive; apply slight floor pressure
- If adhesive bleeds up: remove immediately with mineral spirits; cured adhesive requires solvent-based remover or careful razor scraping — risk of surface damage

## 18. SANDING AND RESTORATION

### 18.1 The Sanding Process

Sanding removes the existing finish system, surface stains, minor surface damage, and a thin layer of wood to restore a smooth, clean surface.

**Minimum wood thickness for sanding:** The board must be thick enough to accommodate material removal. Typically:
- A sanding cycle removes 0.5–1.5mm of wood per complete sand
- Solid wood (18–22mm): can be sanded 5–8 times over its lifetime
- Parquet (20–22mm): 4–6 times
- Pre-finished solid wood: check with manufacturer; some factory bevels will be removed on first sand

### 18.2 Equipment

**Belt sander (drum sander / large floor sander):**
- A drum-mounted abrasive belt; 200–250mm drum width
- Most aggressive material removal; used for main floor area
- Must be kept moving at all times — stopping with drum engaged will create visible dip in floor
- Run at 45° to board direction on first coarse pass (this removes cupping and surface irregularities more efficiently than sanding along the grain); subsequent passes along the grain
- Common machine: Lagler Hummel, Lägler FLIP, Bona FlexiSand

**Edge sander:**
- Disc sander for sanding areas that the large drum sander cannot reach (perimeters, under radiators)
- Harder to control; risk of circular scratch marks if not blended carefully into main field
- Work at 45° to grain; blend with main field

**Orbital finishing sander (random orbital / planetary):**
- Multi-head or random orbital for final finish sanding
- Removes drum and edge sander marks
- Bona FlexiSand, Lagler Trio: multi-head random orbital; very effective
- Final passes should be along the grain

**Dustless sanding systems:**
- Integrated vacuum extraction; reduces airborne dust by 95%+
- Now the professional standard: health and safety compliance; reduced clean-up
- Dust from sanding is flammable; avoid open flames during and after sanding

### 18.3 Abrasive Progression

The abrasive sequence (grit progression) determines the final surface smoothness:

**For old floors with heavy finish:**
1. **36 or 40 grit:** Heavy coarse cut; removes old thick lacquer, paint, or heavy contamination
2. **60 grit:** Intermediate; removes 36-grit scratches
3. **80 grit:** Finer intermediate
4. **100 grit:** Pre-finish; surface is now fine enough for most oil applications
5. **120 grit:** Final grit for lacquer; very smooth surface
6. **150 grit screen:** Final burnish before first lacquer coat

**For light re-sand (existing oil floor, minimal wear):**
1. **60 grit:** Removes oil and light surface wear
2. **80 grit**
3. **100 grit:** Apply oil

**Key principle:** Never skip more than one grit. The previous grit's scratches must be fully removed before progressing. If scratches from a coarser grit remain under the finish, they will be visible, particularly at raking light angles.

### 18.4 Final Inspection Before Finishing

After sanding to final grit, before applying any finish:
- Vacuum thoroughly; wipe with tack cloth
- Inspect in raking light (side lighting at low angle) — reveals scratches, marks, and uneven areas invisible under direct light
- Address any remaining marks before finishing — they cannot be corrected once finish is applied

### 18.5 Sanding Solid Parquet

Parquet blocks are laid in multiple directions (e.g., herringbone). Sanding across the grain of all blocks simultaneously:
1. **First pass:** 45° diagonal to the herringbone direction
2. **Second pass:** 45° in the opposite direction
3. **Final passes:** Along the dominant direction of the room
This sequence ensures all blocks are sanded across (not along) their grain in at least one pass, ensuring even material removal.

## 19. MAINTENANCE AND CARE

### 19.1 Daily/Routine Care

**Sweeping / dust mopping:**
- Daily in commercial; regular in residential
- Fine grit particles are the primary cause of scratching — remove before they abrade the finish under foot traffic
- Microfibre mop or dust mop; avoid oil-treated mop cloths on lacquered floors (may leave residue)

**Vacuuming:**
- Use a hard floor setting / bare floor attachment — no beater bar
- Suction only; rotating brushes scratch lacquered surfaces

**Protective measures:**
- Felt pads under all furniture legs — the single most effective prevention of scratching
- Non-rubber-backed rugs at entranceways — rubber-backed rugs can cause discolouration of some lacquers
- High heel protection: specify this to customers; high spike heels deliver enormous pressure per unit area (potentially 10,000 lbf/in² from a stiletto heel — exceeds most wood hardness)
- UV protection: UV-filtering window film significantly slows UV fading and uneven bleaching

### 19.2 Mopping and Liquid Cleaning

**All wood floors: damp mop only. Never wet mop. Never use steam cleaners.**

- Excess moisture on a wood floor will cause staining, swelling, and finish damage
- Wring mop until almost dry — no visible water should be deposited on the floor
- pH-neutral hardwood cleaner (or very dilute wood floor specific cleaner)
- After mopping: buff dry immediately if any visible moisture remains
- Product-specific cleaners: Osmo Wash & Care, Rubio Monocoat Soap, Bona Hardwood Floor Cleaner

### 19.3 Oil Floor Maintenance

- Periodic re-oiling: annually in residential; more frequently in commercial traffic areas
- Clean thoroughly before re-oiling
- Apply oil sparingly with cloth or buffer pad; wipe off all excess
- A floor that has been over-oiled (too frequent application, excess not removed) will develop a tacky, sticky surface — requires light sanding and re-oiling
- Spot repair: sand localised area, re-oil; will blend when dry

### 19.4 Lacquered Floor Maintenance

- No spot re-application of lacquer (the new patch will show different sheen)
- Maintenance: use only lacquer-specific cleaner (no polish, no wax — these can affect adhesion of future coats)
- "Buffable" maintenance coats: some lacquer systems allow a burnish coat of maintenance lacquer applied by flat pad without sanding — extends time between full sands
- Deep scratch repair: requires localised sanding and re-lacquering; difficult to achieve invisible result without full re-sand

## 20. BRITISH AND EUROPEAN STANDARDS

**BS 8201:2011 — Code of practice for flooring of timber, timber products and wood-based panels:**
- The primary UK standard for solid wood and engineered wood flooring installation
- Covers: subfloor preparation; moisture content requirements; acclimatisation; fixing methods; expansion gaps; underfloor heating
- Essential reference document for all solid wood installation

**BS 8203:2017 — Installation of resilient floor coverings — Code of Practice:**
- Referenced for subfloor preparation and moisture testing methodology
- RH threshold 75% for concrete subfloors

**EN 13756:2002 — Wood flooring — Terminology:**
- Defines standard terminology for wood flooring products
- Reference for grading, dimensions, and construction definitions

**EN 13489:2002 — Wood flooring — Multilayer parquet elements (now also covers engineered):**
- Applies to engineered and parquet elements; performance classification

**EN 13990:2004 — Wood flooring — Solid softwood floor boards:**
- Specification for solid softwood flooring products

**EN 14342:2005+A1:2008 — Wood flooring — Characteristics, evaluation of conformity, and marking:**
- The compliance framework for CE marking of wood flooring products in the EU and UK
- Covers: reaction to fire; release of formaldehyde; slip resistance; thermal conductivity
- Products placed on the UK/EU market must carry CE (EU) or UKCA (UK post-Brexit) marking under this standard

**EN ISO 24334:2021 — Resilience / resistance to indentation:**
- Measures residual indentation; relates to Janka hardness in practical flooring context

**BS EN 13893 — Measurement of slip resistance:**
- Applied to wood flooring in commercial assessments; dynamic coefficient of friction measurement
- Oiled wood surfaces may be more slippery when wet than lacquered surfaces — relevant to public area specification

**DIN 68702 (German standard):**
- Widely referenced for parquet block dimensions and quality; often cited by European manufacturers

**Formaldehyde emission standards:**
- EN 717-1 (perforator method) and EN 717-2 (gas analysis)
- E1 emission class: ≤0.1 ppm formaldehyde in air; the standard requirement for EU/UK
- E0: ≤0.05 ppm; for sensitive environments
- F4 star (Japan): equivalent to very low emission; sometimes cited by Asian manufacturers

## 21. SUSTAINABILITY AND CERTIFICATION

### 21.1 FSC (Forest Stewardship Council)

- The most recognised international forest certification standard
- Certifies that timber was harvested from responsibly managed forests
- Chain of Custody (CoC) certification tracks the timber from forest to manufacturer to supplier
- FSC 100%: all timber from FSC-certified forests
- FSC Mix: a mix of FSC-certified, controlled, and/or recycled sources
- FSC Recycled: all timber from verified post-consumer or pre-consumer reclaimed sources
- **Specify FSC-certified products for all UK commercial and public sector projects**; increasingly required in BREEAM assessments

### 21.2 PEFC (Programme for Endorsement of Forest Certification)

- An umbrella organisation endorsing national forest certification schemes
- Recognises many national standards including UK Woodland Assurance Standard (UKWAS)
- Broadly equivalent to FSC in sustainability credibility for UK timber species; less rigorous in some tropical supply chains
- FSC is generally preferred for tropical hardwoods

### 21.3 BREEAM Credits for Timber

- BREEAM (Building Research Establishment Environmental Assessment Method): the dominant UK sustainable building rating system
- Mat 05 credit (Responsible Sourcing of Materials): requires responsibly sourced timber with valid FSC or PEFC certification
- Specification of certified timber flooring contributes to Mat 05 credits

### 21.4 The UK Timber Regulation (UKTR)

- Post-Brexit equivalent of the EU Timber Regulation (EUTR)
- Makes it illegal to place timber products on the UK market that were illegally harvested
- Requires "due diligence" from those placing timber on the market
- Relevant when specifying tropical species — supply chain verification is essential

### 21.5 Reclaimed Wood Flooring

- Salvaged timber from demolished buildings, old factory floors, wine barrels, railway sleepers, barn beams
- Premium product: aged character impossible to replicate; centuries-old timber often denser and harder than new growth
- Challenges: variable dimensions; may need remilling; nail holes; staining; old finishes containing lead paint (risk assessment required)
- Authenticity: verify source; market has some fraudulent "aged" products
- No FSC certification applicable — provenance documentation required instead

## 22. TRADE TERMINOLOGY GLOSSARY

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Acclimatisation** | Allowing boards to reach equilibrium MC for the installation environment before fitting |
| **Annular ring nail** | Nail with rings around shank; high withdrawal resistance; used in dense hardwoods |
| **BCF** | Bulked Continuous Filament — not used in wood context; see fibre lexicon |
| **Bevel** | Chamfered edge profile on board edge; creates V-groove between boards |
| **Blind nail** | Nail driven through tongue so head is hidden by adjacent board's groove; aka secret nail |
| **Broadleaf** | Botanical classification of hardwood trees (angiosperms); produces non-uniform wood structure |
| **Cambium** | The growing layer of a tree just inside the bark |
| **CE marking / UKCA** | Conformity marking indicating product meets relevant EU/UK standards (EN 14342) |
| **Checking** | Small surface cracks in wood, typically at the end grain; caused by rapid drying |
| **Cleat nail** | L-shaped nail for secret nailing through tongue of wood flooring |
| **Compression set** | Permanent deformation of wood fibres under sustained compression; boards do not recover shape |
| **Conifer** | Softwood tree classification (gymnosperm); needle-bearing; generally softer |
| **Crown (crowning)** | Board centre higher than edges; opposite of cupping; often result of sanding a wet cupped floor |
| **Cup (cupping)** | Board edges higher than centre; caused by moisture differential between face and back |
| **DPM** | Damp Proof Membrane; applied to concrete subfloor to block moisture vapour transmission |
| **EMC** | Equilibrium Moisture Content; MC at which wood is in equilibrium with surrounding air |
| **End-matched** | Board profiled with T&G on both long and short edges |
| **Engineered wood** | Multi-layer board construction; hardwood face veneer over cross-ply core (see Lexicon 3) |
| **Face weight** | Not used in wood context (see Lexicon 1) |
| **FSC** | Forest Stewardship Council; international forest certification organisation |
| **Grain** | The pattern of wood fibres and cells visible on the board face |
| **Hardwood** | Timber from broadleaf (deciduous) trees; not necessarily hard |
| **Heartwood** | The dense inner wood of a tree; darker; more durable; used in flooring |
| **Herringbone** | A V-pattern laying method for parquet strips or blocks |
| **Honeycombing** | Internal checking in wood caused by excessive kiln drying; not visible on surface |
| **Hygroscopic** | Absorbs and releases moisture in response to atmospheric humidity |
| **Janka hardness** | Measurement of wood hardness via ball-embedment test; key flooring durability indicator |
| **Kiln-dried (KD)** | Timber dried in a controlled kiln to a specific MC (typically 6–9% for flooring) |
| **Knot** | Where a branch was attached to the trunk; creates a circular or oval figure in the board |
| **Lacquer** | Film-forming clear finish; creates a protective surface layer over wood |
| **Laitance** | Weak surface layer of cement on concrete screed; must be removed before adhesive |
| **Live-sawn** | Log sawn in parallel slices from one face; "French cut"; shows varied grain |
| **MC** | Moisture Content; expressed as percentage of oven-dry weight |
| **Medullary rays** | Horizontal cell ribbons in wood; create "ray fleck" figure in quarter-sawn oak |
| **Micro-bevel** | Very slight chamfer on board edge; creates hairline shadow joint |
| **Movement coefficient** | The fractional dimensional change per 1% MC change; varies by species and cut |
| **Nail-down** | Installation method using nails through tongue to fix boards to timber subfloor |
| **Oxalic acid** | Used to treat iron tannate (black) staining on oak |
| **Parquet** | Pattern-laid flooring using small blocks or strips; traditionally solid wood |
| **PEFC** | Programme for Endorsement of Forest Certification schemes |
| **Pile direction** | Not applicable to wood flooring; see Lexicon 1 |
| **Plain-sawn** | Log sawn tangentially; produces arching grain patterns; most economical cut |
| **Pre-finished** | Flooring with factory-applied finish; ready to use after installation |
| **Prime grade** | Highest appearance grade; most consistent colour and grain |
| **Quarter-sawn** | Log sawn with cuts perpendicular to growth rings; more stable; ray fleck in oak |
| **Raking light** | Inspection technique using side-angled light to reveal surface imperfections |
| **Ray fleck** | The lustrous, silky figure seen in quarter-sawn oak; caused by medullary rays |
| **Rift-sawn** | Cut at 30–60° to growth rings; straight grain; no ray fleck; wasteful but premium |
| **Rustic grade** | Lower appearance grade; knots, sap, and colour variation present |
| **Sapwood** | Outer wood rings; lighter; less durable; usually excluded from quality grades |
| **Secret nail** | See blind nail |
| **Softwood** | Timber from coniferous trees; generally softer; Scots pine, spruce, larch |
| **Squeak** | Noise from wood floor; caused by loose nails, board-to-board contact, or subfloor issues |
| **T&G** | Tongue and groove; milling profile that interlocks adjacent boards |
| **Tannin** | Polyphenolic compounds in oak (and others); responsible for iron staining and reactive treatments |
| **Thermally modified** | Wood heat-treated to reduce hygroscopicity and improve stability |
| **UFH** | Underfloor Heating; system embedded in screed or subfloor |
| **UKCA** | UK Conformity Assessed; post-Brexit UK equivalent of CE marking |
| **UKTR** | UK Timber Regulation; prohibits illegally harvested timber on UK market |
| **Urethane adhesive** | MS polymer / moisture-curing adhesive; standard for wood flooring glue-down |
| **Wear layer** | The usable wood thickness available for sanding; in solid wood, the entire board thickness |
