## 1. GEOLOGY — UNDERSTANDING HOW STONE FORMED

### 1.1 Why Geology Matters to Flooring

A flooring professional does not need a geology degree, but understanding the fundamental origin of each stone type explains:
- Why some stones are hard and others soft
- Why some polish to a mirror and others cannot
- Why some are frost-resistant and others shatter in a single winter
- Why some absorb oil instantly and others resist it
- Why marble develops etching from acidic liquids
- Why slate splits in planes and granite does not
- Why two pieces of "the same stone" from the same quarry can look completely different

Every distinctive behaviour of every stone has a geological explanation. Learning those explanations is the difference between a technically competent stone specifier and someone who merely knows the names.

### 1.2 The Rock Cycle — The Three Categories

All rock on Earth forms through one of three processes, and the resulting rock type belongs to one of three categories:

**Igneous rocks:** Formed by the cooling and solidification of magma (molten rock). If the magma cools slowly underground, large crystals form (intrusive igneous — granite, gabbro). If it erupts and cools rapidly at the surface, small or no crystals form (extrusive igneous — basalt, pumice).

**Sedimentary rocks:** Formed by the accumulation and lithification (compression and cementation) of sediments — particles of eroded rock, mineral precipitation from water, or the remains of organisms. Limestone forms from calcium carbonate — the shells and skeletons of marine organisms; sandstone from compressed sand; travertine from mineral-precipitating springs.

**Metamorphic rocks:** Formed when existing rocks are subjected to heat and pressure deep within the Earth, causing their mineral structure to recrystallise without melting. Marble is metamorphosed limestone; quartzite is metamorphosed sandstone; slate is metamorphosed mudstone or shale.

### 1.3 Mineral Hardness — The Mohs Scale

The Mohs scale of mineral hardness ranks minerals from 1 (softest — talc) to 10 (hardest — diamond). The hardness of the minerals in a stone determines:
- Whether it can be scratched by common materials
- Whether it can be polished to a high gloss
- How resistant it is to abrasion in service

| Mohs level | Reference mineral | Common material equivalent |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------------|
| 1 | Talc | Fingernail can scratch easily |
| 2 | Gypsum | Fingernail can scratch |
| 3 | Calcite | Copper coin can scratch |
| 4 | Fluorite | Steel knife can scratch |
| 5 | Apatite | Steel file can scratch |
| 6 | Orthoclase feldspar | Steel knife barely scratches |
| 7 | Quartz | Scratches glass; common sand |
| 8 | Topaz | Scratches quartz |
| 9 | Corundum (ruby/sapphire) | Scratches topaz |
| 10 | Diamond | Scratches everything |

**Critical flooring implications:**
- **Calcite (Mohs 3):** The primary mineral in limestone and marble. This means marble and limestone can be scratched by a steel blade, etched by acids (which dissolve calcium carbonate), and are significantly softer than granite or quartzite
- **Quartz (Mohs 7):** The primary mineral in quartzite, sandstone, and granite. Quartz-dominant stones cannot be scratched by any common item (keys, steel blades) and are far more abrasion-resistant in service
- **Feldspar (Mohs 6):** Major component of granite. Resists scratching; cannot be polished to the same degree as marble

## 2. STONE TYPES — IGNEOUS

### 2.1 Granite

**Geological origin:**
Granite is an intrusive igneous rock — it formed from the slow cooling of magma deep beneath the Earth's surface over millions of years. The slow cooling allows time for large mineral crystals to form, which gives granite its characteristic speckled, crystalline appearance. The primary minerals are quartz (20–60%), feldspar (50–80%), and mica (biotite or muscovite).

**Properties relevant to flooring:**
- Very hard (Mohs 6–7; quartz component provides scratch resistance)
- Water absorption: very low (typically 0.1–0.5%); naturally frost-resistant
- High compressive strength and density
- Can be polished to a high gloss (the feldspar component takes polish well)
- Very resistant to staining (low porosity)
- Resistant to acids (no calcium carbonate; will not etch)
- Very good abrasion resistance — excellent for high-traffic commercial floors
- Heavy: typically 2,600–2,800 kg/m³

**Colour and appearance:**
- Determined by mineral composition and the presence of trace minerals
- Common palette: grey, black, pink, red, white, blue-grey
- Classic examples: **Absolute Black** (India); **Baltic Brown** (Finland); **Kashmir White** (India); **Paradiso** (India); **Blue Pearl** (Norway)
- All granites have a crystalline, speckled appearance; no two are identical but the variation is less dramatic than marble

**Application:**
- Ideal for: heavy commercial floors; entrance lobbies; kitchen floors; external paving; stair treads
- Where not to use: the only limitation is purely aesthetic — granite's speckled appearance does not suit every interior style

**Cutting and working:**
- Very hard; requires diamond tools
- Takes all surface finishes (polished, honed, flamed, sandblasted)
- Flamed finish (thermal treatment) creates very anti-slip texture; excellent for external

### 2.2 Basalt

**Geological origin:**
Extrusive igneous rock — formed from lava that cooled rapidly at the Earth's surface. Fine-grained; dark grey to black; very dense and hard.

**Properties:**
- Extremely hard and dense; Mohs 6–7
- Very low water absorption; frost-resistant
- Very high compressive strength; excellent for heavy use
- Dark, consistent appearance; takes honed finish well; high-polish possible but shows scratches
- Nearly black when polished; dark grey when honed — elegant contemporary aesthetic

**Application:**
- Very popular in contemporary minimalist commercial and residential interiors
- Used for kitchen floors, commercial lobbies, pool surrounds (anti-slip when flamed)
- Often sold as "lava stone" in certain forms (particularly the porous volcanic basalt from France — Auvergne lava)

**Note — Auvergne Lava (French Lava Stone):**
A specific product in the market — volcanic basalt from the Auvergne region of France; available unfilled (porous, textured) or filled with resin/cement to create a smoother surface; a premium artisanal product popular in French and high-end British kitchens

### 2.3 Slate (Igneous Origin Debate)

Slate is typically classified as metamorphic (see Section 4.3) but some commercial "slates" are technically fine-grained volcanic tuffs or siltstones. The distinction matters little in practice; the properties section (Section 17) covers all commercial slate products.

## 3. STONE TYPES — SEDIMENTARY

### 3.1 Limestone

**Geological origin:**
Limestone is a sedimentary rock formed primarily from the accumulation of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) — the skeletal remains of marine organisms (shells, corals, foraminifera) deposited on the floors of ancient shallow seas. Over millions of years these sediments are compressed and cemented into rock. Many limestones contain visible fossils — the shells and creatures of those ancient seas.

**Primary mineral:** Calcite (CaCO₃) — Mohs 3

**Properties relevant to flooring:**
- Relatively soft (Mohs 3–4); scratches with steel
- Porosity: very variable — from very dense compact limestone (low porosity) to highly porous open-textured limestone; generally more porous than granite or porcelain
- **Acid sensitivity:** The most critical limitation — calcium carbonate is dissolved by acids. Vinegar, citrus juice, wine, carbonated drinks, and many cleaning products will etch (dissolve) the surface of limestone, creating dull, matt patches on polished surfaces. This is not a stain — it is physical material removal. The effect is permanent unless the stone is re-honed or re-polished.
- Thermal sensitivity: less susceptible than marble to thermal shock but still sensitive to rapid temperature change
- Frost resistance: variable depending on density; only very low-porosity limestone is frost-resistant; must test to EN ISO 10545-12
- Colour: warm tones — cream, beige, ivory, buff, grey, brown; the warm palette is its primary aesthetic strength
- Pattern: from uniform (some French limestones) to highly patterned with visible shells and fossils (Jura marble, Cotswold fossil limestone)

**Classification of limestones:**
- **Compact limestone (calcaire):** Dense, fine-grained; low porosity; takes good polish; French limestones (Burgundy, Angers, etc.) — the finest grades for interior floors
- **Shell limestone (coquina / fossil limestone):** High shell content; variable texture; warm character
- **Oolitic limestone:** Formed from tiny spherical calcium carbonate particles (ooids); Bath Stone, Portland Stone — UK domestic limestones
- **Travertine:** A related CaCO₃ material — covered separately below

**Major limestone origins and commercial names:**
- **French limestone:** Burgundy, Angers Beige, Gris Saint-Vaast, Champenoise — the benchmark fine limestones for luxury interiors
- **Portuguese limestone:** Moleanos, Azul Valverde — widely used in commercial and hospitality
- **Turkish limestone:** Jerusalem Gold, Light Emperor, various Cream/Beige marbles often sold as limestone
- **UK limestone:** Portland, Bath Stone, Purbeck — historic and heritage use; not often used as tiles today
- **Indian limestone:** Kota stone (blue/grey siltstone); widely used in commercial in India; available in UK

**When to use limestone:**
- Interior dry areas where the acid-etching limitation is understood and accepted by the client
- Living rooms, dining rooms, hallways (with appropriate sealing and maintenance protocol)
- NOT in kitchens (acid from food and drink; constant risk of etching)
- NOT unprotected in bathrooms without clear maintenance commitment
- External: only frost-resistant limestone; must test

### 3.2 Travertine

**Geological origin:**
Travertine is a form of limestone deposited by mineral springs — particularly hot springs and geothermally active areas. Carbon dioxide-rich groundwater dissolves calcium carbonate from limestone; when the water emerges as a spring and the CO₂ escapes, the calcium carbonate precipitates out of solution and accumulates in layers. The process is relatively fast geologically (human timescales in some active geothermal areas).

The primary source of commercial travertine: **Tivoli, near Rome, Italy** — used continuously since Roman times (the Colosseum is built from travertine). Other sources: Turkey, Iran, Mexico, Peru.

**Characteristic feature — the voids:**
The most distinctive characteristic of travertine is its naturally porous, voided structure. As the calcium carbonate layers form, organic material (plants, gas bubbles) is trapped and then rots away, leaving cavities and channels in the stone. These voids are either:
- **Filled travertine:** The voids are filled (at the quarry or processing plant) with cement, resin, or grout; the surface is ground flat; the result is a relatively uniform, smooth surface
- **Unfilled travertine:** The voids are left open; the surface has a characteristic pitted, tactile appearance; more rustic aesthetic; the voids collect water and dirt in service (sealing and maintenance important)
- **Tumbled travertine:** Small tiles or pavers mechanically tumbled to produce rounded, worn edges and antique appearance; popular in Mediterranean-style interiors

**Properties:**
- Calcium carbonate based — same acid sensitivity as limestone; must not be cleaned with acidic products
- Moderate to high porosity (higher than limestone in unfilled form)
- Must be sealed (all travertine)
- Takes polish well (filled and honed travertine can be polished to a reasonable gloss)
- Warm cream, ivory, beige, and caramel colour palette
- Very distinctive layered appearance — the flow-banding of the mineral spring deposition is visible

**Applications:**
- Interior floors in dry areas: excellent aesthetic; requires consistent maintenance
- Bathrooms: suitable if sealed properly and maintained; acid sensitivity requires neutral cleaners only
- External: only frost-resistant varieties; the voids can collect water and freeze; must be adequately frost-tested

### 3.3 Sandstone

**Geological origin:**
Sandstone is formed from the cementation of sand-sized (0.0625–2mm) mineral particles — primarily quartz — by mineral cements (silica, calcium carbonate, iron oxide). The colour of sandstone is largely determined by the cement: silica cement produces white/grey; iron oxide cement produces red, brown, or yellow.

**Properties:**
- Hardness: depends on cement and grain; quartz grains themselves are hard (Mohs 7) but the cement bond may be weaker
- Porosity: generally high (10–25%); very susceptible to staining without sealing; some varieties are very porous
- Frost resistance: variable; high-porosity sandstone is not frost-resistant
- Abrasion resistance: moderate to good; quartz grain composition provides some abrasion resistance
- Chemical resistance: silica-cemented sandstone resists acids; calcium carbonate-cemented sandstone does not

**Colour palette:** Red, buff, brown, grey, cream, yellow — warmer palette than most stones; excellent for period and country house interiors

**UK varieties:**
- **York stone:** Natural buff/grey sandstone flags from Yorkshire; the quintessential British outdoor paving stone; used for centuries in northern UK streets, courtyards, and garden paths; very variable thickness (natural split face flags, not sawn)
- **Caithness stone:** Flagstone from northern Scotland; grey-blue; used for interior and exterior
- **Cotswold stone:** Oolitic limestone rather than sandstone, but similar aesthetic
- Reclaimed York stone flags: highly prized; uneven surface; character; not suitable for interior installation without resurfacing

**Indian sandstone:**
The dominant source of budget-to-mid-range external stone in the UK market:
- Kandla Grey, Raj Green, Fossil Mint, Golden Yellow, Autumn Brown
- Generally moderate quality; significant variation; porosity and frost resistance varies dramatically by source quarry
- Must be tested and specified by reliable supplier — the Indian sandstone market has significant quality inconsistency
- Prone to iron staining (iron oxide in some varieties bleeds rust-coloured staining when wet)
- Requires sealing before and after installation

**Applications:**
- External paving: the primary use in UK; garden terraces, pathways, commercial external spaces
- Interior floors: possible in dry areas with sealing; the rustic aesthetic suits specific interior styles
- Not recommended for kitchen floors (high porosity + staining risk)

### 3.4 Other Sedimentary Stones

**Slate (sedimentary — argillite/mudstone):**
Siltstone or mudstone that has undergone partial metamorphism; covered in Section 17

**Limestone aggregates and composite:**
Some "stone" products are aggregate (crushed limestone or marble) bound in cement or resin — terrazzo (see also Lexicon 09)

## 4. STONE TYPES — METAMORPHIC

### 4.1 Marble

**Geological origin:**
Marble is metamorphosed limestone. When limestone is subjected to heat and pressure (during mountain-building or intrusion of hot magma), the calcium carbonate recrystallises into larger, interlocking crystals of calcite (or dolomite in dolomitic marble). This recrystallisation destroys the original sedimentary texture (fossils are usually obliterated) and creates the characteristic crystalline, sugary-textured stone. The veining and colour patterns in marble develop when impurities (clay minerals, iron oxides, graphite, silica) are deformed and stretched during metamorphism.

**The world's most famous marbles:**
- **Carrara (Bianco Carrara, Statuario):** Michelangelo's marble; Carrara, Tuscany; pure white to white-with-grey veins; the reference standard for white marble
- **Calacatta:** Adjacent to Carrara but rarer and more dramatic; white ground with bold grey/gold veins; commands very high prices
- **Nero Marquina:** Black marble with white veins; Spain
- **Portoro:** Black with gold and white veins; Italy (historically Liguria)
- **Verde Guatemala:** Deep green with white veins; Guatemala
- **Emperador:** Brown and dark veining; Spain
- **Onyx:** Translucent banded calcium carbonate; technically not classical marble but related; used for backlit panels and decorative features

**Properties:**
- Mohs hardness: 3–4 (calcite); can be scratched by a steel blade
- **Acid etching:** THE critical limitation; identical to limestone — any acidic substance dissolves the calcite surface; etching creates permanent dull patches on polished marble
- **Polishability:** Marble polishes to an extraordinary mirror-like finish due to the uniform crystalline calcite structure; this is its primary aesthetic appeal
- Porosity: variable; most commercial marble is moderate porosity (0.5–2%); some are quite porous
- **Thermal sensitivity:** Marble can crack under thermal shock; avoid very hot or cold spills on polished marble
- **Veining variability:** The veining in marble is completely natural and random; no two slabs are alike; for book-matched installations (see below) slabs must be selected in matched pairs
- Frost resistance: poor for polished finishes; not recommended for external UK use unless honed and low-porosity
- **Colour stability:** Some marbles are prone to colour change ("yellowing" of white marble under UV or atmospheric exposure; "greening" of certain coloured marbles — discuss with supplier)

**Book-matched marble:**
For feature walls, feature floors, or large open-plan installations, book-matched slabs are specified:
- Two adjacent slabs from the quarry are opened "like a book" — one is mirrored of the other
- The veining in one slab mirrors the veining in the adjacent slab symmetrically
- Creates a dramatic, architectural appearance
- The slabs must be matched, numbered, and laid in sequence; cannot be rearranged

**Application:**
- Luxury residential bathrooms, master bedrooms, entrance halls
- Premium hotel lobbies, penthouse installations
- NOT in kitchens (acid etching from food and drink inevitable)
- NOT for heavy commercial traffic without significant maintenance commitment (polished marble scratches and dulls in heavy use)
- For commercial use: honed rather than polished is far more practical

### 4.2 Quartzite

**Geological origin:**
Quartzite is metamorphosed sandstone. When quartz sandstone is subjected to heat and pressure, the quartz grains recrystallise and fuse together into a dense, interlocking quartzite rock. The result is a stone that is effectively all quartz — the hardest common mineral.

**This is not to be confused with "engineered quartz" (Silestone, Caesarstone) which is a manufactured product.**

**Properties:**
- Mohs hardness: 7 (quartz); very scratch-resistant
- Very low water absorption (dense, interlocking quartz matrix)
- **Acid resistant:** No calcium carbonate; acids do not etch quartzite — a major advantage over marble and limestone
- Excellent frost resistance (low porosity)
- Abrasion resistance: excellent; suitable for heavy traffic
- Polishes to a good gloss (not as brilliant as marble but very good)
- Very hard to cut — requires good diamond tooling

**Colour and appearance:**
- White, cream, grey, silver, pale blue, golden; often with fine veining
- Premium quartzites (Taj Mahal, Sea Pearl, Macaubas, Super White) have a visual similarity to marble but with superior performance
- This has made quartzite very popular as a "marble look but durable" alternative for kitchen floors and countertops

**Caution — "Quartzite" labelling:**
The natural stone market has a persistent problem: many stones sold as "quartzite" are actually marble or limestone — they simply look like quartzite. The label "white quartzite" frequently refers to a white marble that is actually acid-sensitive. Always test: apply a drop of dilute hydrochloric acid (or lemon juice) to an inconspicuous area — if it fizzes or etches, it contains calcium carbonate and is NOT quartzite.

**Application:**
- Kitchen floors (the stone that should be used instead of marble in kitchens)
- Bathroom floors and walls
- Heavy commercial and hospitality
- External: good frost resistance; suitable for most UK external applications

### 4.3 Slate

Covered fully in Section 17. Geological summary:
- Metamorphosed mudstone/siltstone/shale at low to medium metamorphic grade
- Primary minerals: mica, chlorite, quartz
- The foliation (layered structure) allows slate to be split into thin, flat sheets — the cleavage planes follow the alignment of mica crystals formed under pressure

### 4.4 Schist

**Geological origin:**
Schist is a medium-grade metamorphic rock with a strongly foliated (layered/platy) texture. It has been metamorphosed at higher temperatures and pressures than slate. Primary minerals: mica (muscovite, biotite), quartz, feldspar, and potentially garnet, hornblende.

**Properties:**
- The foliation (layering) creates a slightly uneven surface and a tendency to split along the mica planes
- Not as amenable to cutting as granite; surface texture is natural and non-uniform
- Attractive sparkle/glitter from mica flakes
- Moderate hardness; moderate porosity

**Applications:**
- Used in some natural "rough" stone contexts; artisan and heritage applications
- Not widely used in mainstream commercial flooring due to splitting tendency

## 5. SURFACE FINISHES

Surface finish is a critical specification decision for natural stone — it affects aesthetics, slip resistance, durability, and maintenance requirements.

### 5.1 Polished

- The stone surface is progressively ground with decreasing abrasive grades from coarse to very fine, then polished with powder, buffing pads, or chemical treatment
- Produces a mirror-like reflective surface
- Achievable on: marble, granite, quartzite, limestone (if dense enough), travertine (filled)
- Maximises colour depth; dramatic visual impact; maximises reflectivity
- **Disadvantages:** Shows every scratch, watermark, and footprint; highest maintenance burden; slippery when wet (usually PTV <36 on many stones); not recommended for floors in residential with high traffic or wet areas

### 5.2 Honed

- Ground to a fine, consistent, flat surface without the final polishing step
- Produces a smooth, satin or matte surface with controlled sheen (typically 10–30 gloss units)
- Available on all stone types
- Better slip resistance than polished (though still check PTV for wet areas)
- More practical for floors than polished — hides scratches and wear more effectively
- Still shows watermarks and marks on light-coloured stones (particularly Carrara)
- **The professional recommendation for most stone floors:** honed provides the aesthetic of the stone without the maintenance intensity of polished

### 5.3 Brushed / Antiqued

- The surface is wire-brushed or given an acid wash to selectively remove the softer mineral components, leaving the harder minerals proud
- Creates a textured, worn, aged appearance
- Often combined with tumbling (rounded edges) for a fully aged aesthetic
- Good slip resistance (texture)
- Hides scratches and wear well — the already-textured surface is forgiving

### 5.4 Sandblasted / Bush-Hammered

- **Sandblasted:** High-pressure abrasive blasting creates a fine, even texture; good slip resistance; slightly lightens the stone colour
- **Bush-hammered:** A mechanical hammer with a pyramidal tip creates a grid of impact marks; more textural than sandblasted; excellent anti-slip; used for external stone, stair treads, industrial contexts

### 5.5 Flamed (Thermal)

- High-temperature flame rapidly applied to granite or some hard stones
- The differential thermal expansion of different mineral crystals causes the surface to spall slightly, creating a rough, highly textured surface
- Excellent anti-slip (R11+ typically)
- Primarily for granite; lightens the colour; external applications and stair nosings
- Not applicable to limestone, marble, or travertine (thermal shock shatters calcareous stones)

### 5.6 Split (Riven / Cleft)

- Natural stone split along its natural cleavage planes
- Slate: the definitive riven surface; the foliation planes are the split faces
- Quartzite, schist, some sandstones: natural riven faces
- The surface is entirely natural; no two tiles are identical in texture
- The riven surface is inherently textured and non-slip
- The tiles are not uniform in thickness — installation requires more adhesive or a thick-bed system

### 5.7 Tumbled

- Tiles or small pieces placed in a rotating drum with abrasive material; corners are rounded; edges chipped
- Creates an aged, antique appearance; simulates centuries-old wear
- Popular in travertine, limestone, and slate
- The rounded edges mean no sharp grout joint definition is visible

### 5.8 Leather / Leathered

- A brushed finish applied to granite (and occasionally other stones) that creates a soft, satin texture
- Softer than sandblasted; more tactile than honed
- Better slip resistance than polished; less textural than sandblasted
- Growing popularity in residential kitchens

## 6. STONE FORMATS — SLABS, TILES, AND FLAGS

### 6.1 Sawn Tiles

- Cut from sawn blocks using multi-blade saw or wire saw to consistent thickness
- Standard thicknesses: 10mm, 12mm, 15mm, 20mm, 30mm
- Rectified tiles: precision-ground to tight dimensional tolerance after sawing
- Non-rectified: slightly variable dimensions; wider joint widths required
- Formats: as per ceramic tiles (Section 3 of Lexicon 07) — 300×300mm to 1200×600mm and larger

### 6.2 Large Slabs

- Entire quarry blocks cut into slabs: typically 2400–3200mm × 1200–1800mm
- Used for: large format flooring; continuous wall cladding; kitchen worktops; feature surfaces
- "Bookmatch" slabs: adjacent slabs sequentially numbered; must be laid in matched pairs for symmetrical veining
- Weight: 10mm marble slab at 2700×1500mm = approximately 110kg; mechanical handling essential

### 6.3 Flags and Paving

- Larger format slabs for external paving: typically 600×600mm to 900×600mm; 20–50mm thickness
- Natural split (riven) surface or sawn and textured surface
- York stone flags: reclaimed or new-cut; irregular thickness; variable sizing
- Calibrated flags: sawn to consistent thickness for easier installation with thin-bed adhesive

### 6.4 Sets, Cobbles, and Setts

- Small cubic or rectangular stone blocks: granite sets, cobblestones
- External use; roads, courtyards, garden hard landscaping
- Installation: sand-bedded traditionally; adhesive mortar for modern specification

### 6.5 Mosaic Stone

- Small stone tesserae on mesh backing (as per ceramic mosaic)
- Marble, travertine, granite, slate available in mosaic format
- Complex patterns: opus incertum (random irregular pieces), opus regulatum (grid), opus vermiculatum (follows subject outlines)
- Pool surrounds, feature areas, art installations

## 7. PHYSICAL PROPERTIES AND TESTING

### 7.1 The Critical Tests for Stone Flooring Specification

Before specifying any natural stone in a demanding application, the following test data should be obtained from the supplier:

**EN ISO 10545-3 / EN 13755 — Water absorption:**
- For frost resistance: must demonstrate ≤0.5% for external use; many stones fail this
- Stain resistance and maintenance protocols depend on porosity

**EN ISO 10545-12 / EN 12371 — Frost resistance:**
- Mandatory for external specification
- The stone is subjected to 25–56 freeze-thaw cycles; inspected for spalling, cracking, or loss of strength

**EN 1925 — Flexural strength (modulus of rupture):**
- Resistance to bending and breaking under load
- Thin large format stone (10–12mm) requires adequate flexural strength to span subfloor irregularities and resist concentrated loads

**EN 1926 — Compressive strength:**
- Resistance to crushing; relevant for heavily loaded applications

**EN 1341 / EN 1342 — Slip resistance of external stone:**
- Measurement of slip resistance of sawn, split, and surface-treated stone for external paving

**EN 14157 — Abrasion resistance (Böhme):**
- Volume of material removed by standardised abrasion wheel under specified load
- Critical for floor specification in commercial and public applications
- British Standard equivalent: BS EN 14157

### 7.2 Density and Weight

Natural stone is heavy — significantly heavier than any other floor covering. This has structural implications:

| Stone type | Typical density (kg/m³) |
|------------|------------------------|
| Granite | 2,600–2,800 |
| Marble | 2,500–2,700 |
| Limestone | 1,800–2,600 (very variable) |
| Travertine | 2,200–2,550 |
| Quartzite | 2,600–2,700 |
| Slate | 2,700–2,900 |
| Sandstone | 1,800–2,600 (very variable) |

**Structural calculation:**
- 20mm granite tile: 0.020m × 2,700 kg/m³ = 54 kg/m²
- 30mm granite flag: 81 kg/m²
- Plus adhesive/mortar bed: typically 20–50 kg/m²
- Total loading: 75–130 kg/m²

This loading must be within the structural capacity of the floor. For upper floors (timber joist structures): a structural engineer must confirm that the floor can carry the stone loading before specification. Many domestic timber floor structures cannot safely carry 20mm stone without structural reinforcement.

### 7.3 Thermal Conductivity

- Natural stone has high thermal conductivity — it transmits heat readily
- This is excellent for UFH systems: stone floors over UFH heat up efficiently and retain heat well
- Granite thermal conductivity: ~2.5–3.5 W/mK (compare oak wood: ~0.15 W/mK)
- Stone floors feel cold underfoot without UFH because heat from feet is rapidly conducted into the large thermal mass of the stone

## 8. POROSITY, ABSORPTION, AND SEALING

### 8.1 Why Sealing Matters

Natural stone pores are continuous channels from the surface to the interior. Without sealing, liquids (oil, wine, juice, water with minerals) penetrate the stone, staining it permanently. The staining agent cannot be removed from the stone interior without specialised restoration techniques.

**Every natural stone floor except very dense granite should be sealed before grouting and before use.** Even granite benefits from sealing in kitchen applications.

### 8.2 Stone Porosity Classification for Sealing Decisions

| Stone type | Typical open porosity | Sealing priority |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Polished granite | 0.1–0.3% | Low (still recommended in kitchens) |
| Flamed granite | 0.3–0.8% | Moderate |
| Marble (polished) | 0.3–1.5% | High |
| Quartzite | 0.1–0.8% | Moderate |
| Dense limestone | 0.5–3% | High |
| Porous limestone | 3–15% | Very high — multiple coats |
| Travertine (filled) | 1–3% | High |
| Travertine (unfilled) | 5–20% | Very high |
| Sandstone | 10–25% | Very high — multiple coats |
| Slate | 0.1–3% | Moderate to high |

### 8.3 Sealer Types

**Impregnating sealers (penetrating / sub-surface):**
- The professional standard for natural stone
- The sealer penetrates the stone pores; the carrier (solvent or water) evaporates; the active compound remains in the pores, lining them and resisting liquid penetration
- The sealer does not form a film on the surface; the stone's natural appearance is unaffected
- Types:
  - **Silicone-based:** Cost-effective; good performance; requires re-application every 1–3 years
  - **Siloxane-based:** Better UV resistance; good for external use
  - **Fluoropolymer (fluorocarbon):** Superior water and oil repellency; longer-lasting; premium products (HMK S34, LTP Mattstone, Lithofin MN)
  - **Colour-enhancing impregnating sealer:** Deepens and enriches the natural colour while protecting; popular for dark slate and some sandstone

**Topical sealers (surface film-formers):**
- Applied on top of the stone surface; form a protective film
- Lacquer-finish or wax-finish appearance
- More prone to scratching, scuffing, and peeling than impregnating sealers
- Can turn yellow with UV exposure or age
- Not the professional recommendation for high-quality natural stone (the film obscures the natural stone appearance)
- Acceptable for: low-budget applications; slate with a colour-enhanced oil applied at installation (classic sealed slate with dark oil finish)

### 8.4 Sealing Process

1. Stone must be clean and dry before sealing
2. Allow new installation minimum 48–72 hours after grouting before sealing (allows grout and adhesive to cure and any construction moisture to escape)
3. Apply sealer generously with a cloth, sponge, or brush; work in sections
4. Allow to penetrate per manufacturer guidance (typically 5–15 minutes)
5. Buff off all excess sealer before it dries — cured sealer on the surface creates a white haze that is very difficult to remove
6. Allow full cure before trafficking (typically 24–48 hours; some sealers: 4 hours)
7. Second coat: apply after first coat has cured; for porous stone (sandstone, porous limestone): 3–4 coats recommended
8. Re-apply as required: test by placing water drops on surface; if they no longer bead, the sealer is depleted

### 8.5 The "Burn Test" for Sealer Residue

When a white haze appears after sealing:
- The sealer cured on the stone surface rather than absorbing
- Warm the area with a heat gun (carefully) — this may re-activate the sealer and allow buffing
- Or use the manufacturer's sealer remover/alkaline degreaser to dissolve the surface residue
- Prevention: always buff off excess before it dries; never seal when air temperature is very high (speeds surface drying)

## 9. SUBFLOOR REQUIREMENTS

### 9.1 Natural Stone — The Strictest Subfloor Requirements

Natural stone imposes the strictest subfloor requirements of any floor covering because:
1. It is rigid and cannot flex
2. It is heavy (structural loading assessment required)
3. Many stones are brittle and crack under point loading on unsupported areas
4. Adhesive voids allow moisture to collect and cause staining from below
5. The quality of the installation is permanent — bad prep is visible forever

**Flatness tolerance:**
- Standard stone tiles (up to 600mm): **3mm under 2m straight-edge AND 1mm under 200mm** (same as large format ceramic — any tile over 300mm in practice)
- Large format stone (over 600mm) and slabs: **2mm under 2m straight-edge AND 1mm under 300mm**
- External stone (large paving): **5mm under 3m straight-edge** (more tolerance due to thick mortar bed)

### 9.2 Concrete Subfloors

- Maximum **75% RH** before installation (BS 5385)
- New screeds: minimum 28 days; RH test
- Anhydrite screeds: sand; prime; use anhydrite-compatible adhesive
- Laitance removal: mandatory
- All cracks and holes filled

### 9.3 Timber Subfloors — Special Importance

**The structural load issue:**
Natural stone is approximately 5–10× heavier per unit area than vinyl or carpet. A domestic upper floor designed for 1.5 kN/m² imposed load may not safely carry 20mm marble tile + adhesive + screed build-up. A structural engineer must assess any upper floor application before specifying stone.

**Deflection:**
BS 5385-3 specifies maximum L/360 deflection for tiles on timber — practically impossible to achieve without structural reinforcement in most existing domestic timber floors. A decoupling membrane (Schlüter DITRA or equivalent) significantly improves tolerance of minor deflection but does not overcome the fundamental limitation.

**Recommended approach for stone on timber upper floors:**
1. Structural assessment
2. Minimum 18mm structural plywood overlay (or concrete topping if structural loading allows)
3. Decoupling membrane (Schlüter DITRA, Mapeguard, or equivalent)
4. Flexible C2S2 adhesive
5. Movement joints at maximum 3m intervals and at all perimeters
6. If in doubt: use porcelain tile instead of natural stone (lighter; more tolerant)

## 10. ADHESIVE AND BEDDING SYSTEMS

### 10.1 Adhesive Requirements for Natural Stone

Most natural stone requires **white or near-white adhesive** — grey cementitious adhesive can discolour translucent or light-coloured stones (particularly white marble, translucent limestone, or white quartzite) as the grey shows through. This is not cosmetic — it can permanently alter the appearance of a premium stone floor.

**Rule:** Always use white adhesive for: white marble; light limestone; translucent stone; veined stone where the adhesive bed could show through

### 10.2 EN 12004 Classification for Natural Stone

- **Minimum: C2S2** for all stone in wet areas or over UFH
- **C2S2E (extended open time):** Essential for large format stone slabs and complex layouts — large slabs take longer to position and level
- **C2S2T (anti-slump):** For wall stone and large tiles that may slide before adhesive cures
- For marble specifically: some marble contains iron minerals (pyrite inclusions); a moisture-curing adhesive (standard cementitious) can cause rusting of these inclusions; check with stone supplier whether a specific adhesive is recommended

### 10.3 Thick Bed (Sand and Cement)

- Traditional installation method for stone; still used for thick flags and large-format stone
- Provides: level adjustment (accommodates variation in flag thickness); a stable, dense bed
- Mix: 4 parts sharp sand : 1 part cement (semi-dry — not wet mortar)
- Thickness: 25–75mm
- A slurry coat of cement paste (neat cement mixed to cream consistency) is applied to the bedding face of the stone immediately before placing
- Limitations: labour-intensive; heavy; total floor build-up is significant

### 10.4 The Bedding Slurry for Thick Stone

- For large stone flags laid on a thick sand-cement bed: a slurry of neat cement (or adhesive) applied to the back of the stone immediately before pressing it into the bed
- The slurry fills the micro-texture of the stone back and the surface of the sand-cement bed, ensuring full contact
- Failure to apply the slurry results in partial bonding, voids, and eventual de-bonding of heavy stone flags

## 11. GROUT SYSTEMS FOR NATURAL STONE

### 11.1 Grout Selection

**Cementitious grout (CG) for stone:**
- Use unsanded grout for narrow joints (≤3mm): finer grain prevents scratching polished surfaces
- Use sanded grout for wider joints (3mm+)
- CG2WA preferred for all floor applications — improved abrasion resistance and water resistance
- Colour: use a colour that complements the stone; mid-tone grey typically works well with most light stones; dark grout with dark stone

**Epoxy grout (RG) for stone:**
- Required in: commercial kitchens; swimming pools; healthcare; any application requiring chemically resistant joints
- Benefit: completely stain-proof joints; the epoxy joint will never discolour
- Challenge: high cost; difficult application; very short working time; requires experience

**The marble scratch warning:**
Some sanded cementitious grouts contain quartz particles (Mohs 7). On polished marble (Mohs 3–4), grouting can scratch the polish if sanded grout is dragged across the surface. Solutions:
- Use unsanded grout for all polished marble (and polish any post-grouting marks)
- Or apply tape to the surface of polished marble before grouting and peel off after
- Or grout with epoxy (smoother application)

### 11.2 Acid-Washing Grout Haze — CRITICAL WARNING

Acid washing (dilute hydrochloric acid) is sometimes used to remove cementitious grout haze from floor tiles. This is suitable for vitrified porcelain and granite. It is **absolutely forbidden on:**
- Marble
- Limestone
- Travertine
- Any calcareous stone

Hydrochloric acid immediately and permanently etches calcium carbonate. Even a dilute acid wash will destroy the surface of polished marble.

Grout haze on calcareous stone must be removed with:
- The specific manufacturer-approved grout haze remover (most are pH neutral or mildly alkaline for use with stone)
- Or careful mechanical removal with a damp sponge and the correct grout cleaner
- Never acid

## 12. MOVEMENT JOINTS FOR NATURAL STONE

### 12.1 Requirements

Natural stone requires movement joints on the same basis as ceramic tile (BS 5385):

**Perimeter joints:** At all walls, columns, and fixed structures: minimum 6–10mm; filled with silicone matching the grout colour

**Intermediate joints:**
- Standard stone installation (internal, dry): maximum 4.5m × 4.5m
- Wet areas: maximum 3m × 3m
- Over UFH: maximum 3m × 3m
- External: maximum 3m × 3m (more movement in temperature extremes)

### 12.2 Movement Joint Sealant for Calcareous Stone

**Critical:** Use only neutral-cure silicone for movement joints in marble, limestone, and travertine floors.

- **Acetoxy-cure silicone** ("vinegar smell"): releases acetic acid during curing; etches adjacent calcareous stone
- **Neutral-cure silicone:** No acid release; safe for all stone types
- Check sealant pack: if it smells of vinegar, do not use near calcareous stone

## 13. INSTALLATION METHODS AND TECHNIQUES

### 13.1 Preparation Before Installation

1. Check structural loading (upper floors)
2. Prepare subfloor to specification (flat; dry; clean)
3. Set out the floor (as per ceramic tile — Section 13.3 of Lexicon 07)
4. Plan seam lines and book-match sequencing for slabs
5. For slabs: number slabs in order; mark the direction; cross-reference to laying plan

### 13.2 Back-Buttering

Mandatory for all stone tiles and slabs:
- Apply adhesive to both the subfloor (notched trowel) and the back of the stone (skim coat)
- Required coverage: minimum 95% (wet areas and large format); 80% minimum (dry internal)
- Voids under stone collect moisture; for moisture-sensitive adhesives, voids can cause bond failure; for hygroscopic stone, voids can stain the face

### 13.3 Laying Sequence for Book-Matched Slabs

1. Lay the floor plan on paper first: mark which slab goes where; mark the mirror axis
2. Prepare the adhesive bed for the first slab
3. Lay the first slab of a matched pair
4. Lay the adjacent second slab (mirrored); check the veining aligns at the joint
5. Level both slabs carefully with a level and rubber mallet; use levelling clips at joints
6. Continue in sequence
7. Allow full adhesive cure before grouting book-matched stone

### 13.4 Cutting Natural Stone

- **Wet diamond saw (table saw):** Standard for all stone cutting; water cools blade; suppresses dust; clean, precise cuts
- **Angle grinder + diamond blade:** For adjustments on site; dust control essential (stone silica dust — see COSHH)
- **Bridge saw:** Required for very large slabs (2400mm+); precision cutting
- **Manual scoring and snapping:** Not appropriate for hard stone; limited to very soft or thin stone; not recommended
- **Curved cuts:** Jigsaw with diamond blade or grinder with grinding wheel

**Silica dust from natural stone:**
- All natural stone contains crystalline silica; grinding, cutting, and drilling stone generates RCS dust
- Legal requirements identical to ceramic tile (COSHH; WEL 0.1 mg/m³)
- Wet cutting is the primary control; RPE (FFP3) when dry work unavoidable

### 13.5 Grouting Stone Floors

- Allow adhesive minimum 24 hours (standard) or per data sheet before grouting
- Mix grout correctly; apply with rubber float at 45°
- Work in small sections; clean before full set
- Polish off haze before grout fully hardens
- **Do not acid-wash calcareous stone** (see Section 11.2)
- Apply grout sealer after full cure (48 hours minimum)

### 13.6 Protecting During and After Installation

- During installation: card, foam, or hardboard protection over completed areas while remaining areas are installed
- After grouting: minimum 24 hours before light foot traffic; 7 days before heavy traffic or furniture placement
- Sealing: 48–72 hours after grouting; the stone must be dry and clean

## 14. UNDERFLOOR HEATING WITH NATURAL STONE

### 14.1 The Optimal Pairing

Stone is the ideal floor covering over underfloor heating — the two are designed for each other:

- Stone has very high thermal conductivity (2–4 W/mK vs wood 0.15 W/mK); heat transmits rapidly and efficiently from the screed through stone to the room
- Stone's thermal mass stores heat and radiates it evenly even after the UFH system has cycled off
- The cold surface of stone (a common complaint in cold rooms) becomes a luxury radiant warmth with UFH

### 14.2 Technical Requirements Over UFH

**Adhesive:**
- C2S2 mandatory: the thermal cycling of UFH requires highly flexible adhesive bond
- Some manufacturers offer specific "UFH-compatible" adhesive products with enhanced flexibility

**Movement joints:**
- Maximum 3m × 3m bay size over UFH
- Perimeter joints mandatory; minimum 8mm

**UFH commissioning protocol:**
- Same as all other flooring (Lexicon 02, 03 etc.): commission fully before installation; switch off 48 hours before; ramp up slowly (5°C/day) after installation

**Maximum surface temperature:**
- Stone: generally tolerates higher surface temperatures than wood or vinyl
- Most manufacturers and BS 5385 do not specify a maximum temperature limit for stone itself
- However: adhesive and grout may have temperature limits — check adhesive data sheet
- UFH system should still be controlled to ensure the adhesive bond is not overstressed; 35°C surface is typically a safe maximum

### 14.3 Stone Type and UFH

All stone types are suitable over UFH when correctly specified. However:
- **Marble:** Thermal cycling can accentuate natural hairline fissures over decades; use flexible adhesive; avoid very rapid temperature changes
- **Travertine:** As marble; flexible adhesive; avoid temperature extremes
- **Granite and quartzite:** Very tolerant; the hardest and most dimensionally stable stones; excellent for UFH

## 15. WET AREAS AND EXTERNAL USE

### 15.1 Bathroom and Wet Room

**Suitable stones:**
- Dense, low-porosity stones: polished granite (but check PTV — may be slippery); honed limestone (properly sealed); honed marble (with commitment to maintenance); quartzite; slate
- With appropriate sealing and maintenance protocol: most stones work in a bathroom

**Not suitable without extreme care:**
- Highly porous stone (open travertine, porous sandstone): very difficult to seal adequately; constant maintenance burden
- Polished marble in a shower floor: slippery; etches; stains; very high maintenance
- Better options for shower trays and wet room floors: honed slate (natural non-slip); honed granite (anti-slip finish); mosaic stone (many joints = effective texture)

**Wet room drainage and stone:**
- Stone slabs must be cut to incorporate the drainage fall (see Lexicon 07, Section 15.1)
- Every stone tile must be supported on a watertight, waterproofed substrate
- The joints must be filled with epoxy grout or sealed with appropriate silicone

### 15.2 External Stone Specification

**The non-negotiable requirements:**
1. **Frost resistance:** EN 12371 test; maximum water absorption typically ≤0.3% for UK external; limestone must be specifically tested; most marble and porous limestone fail
2. **Anti-slip:** Adequate for wet conditions; honed or riven finish; DIN 51130 R10 minimum for terraces; R11 for pool surrounds
3. **Adhesive:** C2S2 external-rated; or sand-cement bed with slurry; 100% coverage
4. **Grout:** CG2S external-rated; frost-resistant
5. **Movement joints:** Maximum 3m × 3m; 10mm joints

**Suitable stones for UK external:**
- Granite (all finishes): excellent; the benchmark external stone
- Very dense limestone (Jura Beige, Portland, some Burgundy): if frost-tested and confirmed
- Quartzite: good performance; check frost resistance
- Slate (dense Welsh or Indian): variable; check individual product frost resistance
- Yorkstone sandstone (calibrated and sawn): traditional; variable quality; check

**Not suitable for UK external:**
- Marble: acid rain etching; frost risk in most varieties
- Polished limestone: frost risk; staining
- Highly porous travertine: frost risk
- Most sandstone without specific frost testing and confirmation

## 16. LIMESTONE, MARBLE, AND CALCAREOUS STONE — SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS

### 16.1 The Acid Chemistry — Explained

All calcareous stones (limestone, marble, travertine) are composed primarily of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃). Acids react with calcium carbonate in a neutralisation reaction:

**CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂**

The stone material is chemically dissolved. This is:
- **Permanent:** The material is gone; it cannot be reversed by cleaning
- **Visible as etching:** Dull, matte patches on a polished surface; the polish has been dissolved
- **Irreversible without restoration:** The only correction is mechanical re-polishing or honing of the surface

**Common acids that etch calcareous stone:**
- Vinegar (acetic acid)
- Lemon and citrus juice
- Wine, beer, fizzy drinks (carbonic and other acids)
- Tomato-based products
- Coffee (weakly acidic)
- Many bathroom and kitchen cleaning products
- Scale removers
- Hydrochloric acid (strongest; used for grout cleaning — never on calcareous stone)

**Alkalis do not etch calcareous stone** — but they can damage the polish over time with prolonged contact (bleach at high concentration)

### 16.2 Communicating the Limitations to Customers

This is a critical customer communication task. Customers who buy marble or limestone for their kitchen floor without understanding the acid sensitivity will experience constant dissatisfaction. The professional obligation is to make this crystal clear before purchase:

**The conversation framework:**
- "Marble and limestone are beautiful stones but they require a different level of care from porcelain or granite."
- "Acids etch the surface permanently — that includes anything acidic: lemon juice, wine, vinegar, coffee, even some water in hard-water areas"
- "If you spill something acidic and don't wipe it up within minutes, you will see a dull patch"
- "If that's something you would find stressful, I'd recommend [quartzite / granite / porcelain with marble effect] instead"
- "If you love the marble and understand the maintenance, we'll specify the sealing and maintenance programme together"

### 16.3 Patina vs Damage

An important philosophical point for luxury stone specification:

Many interior designers and homeowners value the **patina** that marble and limestone develop over years of use — the gentle etching, worn areas, and colour changes that give an aged, lived-in quality to the stone. This is intentional in many high-end residential installations and is considered part of the stone's character.

This patina aesthetic is distinct from:
- Accidental damage from improper cleaning products
- Neglect that leads to deep staining
- Structural failure from lack of sealing

Discuss with the client whether they want a pristine maintenance programme (frequent re-polishing) or whether they are comfortable with the developing patina. Both are valid choices; the client must make them knowingly.

## 17. SLATE — SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS

### 17.1 Geological Character

Slate is a fine-grained metamorphic rock with a pronounced cleavage (foliation) caused by the alignment of mica flakes during metamorphism under directed pressure. This foliation allows slate to be split along very thin, flat planes — the fundamental property that makes it useful as a roofing and flooring material.

**Primary minerals:** Mica (muscovite, biotite, sericite), chlorite, quartz, occasionally graphite (in dark slates)

### 17.2 Commercial Slate Sources

**Welsh slate:**
- Produced from the quarries of Snowdonia (Penrhyn, Dinorwig/Dinorwic, Ffestiniog)
- Blue-grey, consistent, smooth surface; fine cleavage
- The benchmark for quality slate; among the best roofing and flooring slates in the world
- Floor products: 10mm and 12mm sawn-and-calibrated tiles; 20mm thick flags

**Spanish slate (pizarra):**
- Galicia region; dark grey-black; fine cleavage; very consistent
- Now the dominant commercial slate in the UK market (volume/price advantage)
- Quality varies by quarry; specify by quarry name from reliable suppliers

**Brazilian slate:**
- Wide range of colours: grey, black, green, multicolour (Multicolour Brazilian); rust, gold
- More variation in quality and cleavage consistency
- Multicolour Brazilian slate: a popular distinctive product with red-green-grey variation

**Indian slate:**
- Wide range: black, grey, silver, natural split surface
- Variable quality; significant range from good to very poor
- Often very inexpensive; quality must be verified by sample assessment

**African slate (South African, Zimbabwean):**
- Generally good quality; darker tones

### 17.3 Slate Properties

- Water absorption: variable (0.1–3% typically); most commercial slate is adequately low
- Frost resistance: dense, low-porosity slate is frost-resistant; the Welsh quartzite-slates are excellent for external use
- Hardness: Mohs 2–4 (mica and chlorite dominate); the surface can be scratched
- The cleaved surface is naturally non-slip; polished slate is slippery
- **Delamination risk:** All split-face slate has a tendency to delaminate (lose thin surface layers) over time, particularly when wet. Higher quality slates (Welsh) have tighter cleavage and delaminate less. Budget Indian slate can delaminate severely. Always specify calibrated, sawn-back slate to minimise delamination risk.

### 17.4 Slate Installation

- Riven (natural split) slate: variable thickness; use thick-bed adhesive with adjustable depth; or apply compensating skim of adhesive to level tiles
- Calibrated (sawn back to consistent thickness): thin-bed installation; standard adhesive
- Sealing: slate is generally not as porous as limestone; an impregnating sealer with colour enhancement is widely specified
- Classic "wet slate" look: slate oil or enhancing impregnating sealer deepens colour dramatically
- Grouting: standard CG2WA; wide joints possible with riven slate (variation in edges)

## 18. PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 18.1 Staining

**Causes:**
1. **No sealer / sealer expired:** Liquid (oil, wine, water with minerals) penetrated pores and stained the stone
2. **Acid etching (calcareous stone):** Dull patches from acid contact; different from a stain — it is dissolved stone surface
3. **Iron staining:** Some stone contains pyrite (iron sulphide) or other iron minerals; water ingress oxidises the iron to rust colour; can also migrate from the adhesive or subfloor
4. **Organic staining:** Algae, mould, or organic debris in porous external stone
5. **Efflorescence:** Salts migrating from adhesive/grout/subfloor (as per ceramic tile)

**Treatment by stain type:**

| Stain | Treatment |
|-------|-----------|
| Oil/grease | Poultice with chalk powder + acetone or specialised oil-stain remover; allow to draw out |
| Wine/coffee | Poultice with hydrogen peroxide (for light stone) or specialist remover |
| Rust/iron | Specialist rust remover (oxalic acid-based — confirm stone compatibility; do not use on calcareous stone without testing); commercial products: StoneTech Iron Out, HMK R155 |
| Acid etch | Cannot be cleaned; requires mechanical re-polishing |
| Efflorescence | Allow to dry fully; dry brush; if persistent, specialist efflorescence remover |
| Organic (algae, mould) | Specialist biocide cleaner; allow contact time; rinse |
| Hard water deposits (limescale) | Only neutral pH scale remover for calcareous stone (acid scale removers etch); or mechanical removal |

### 18.2 Cracking

**Causes:**
1. **Subfloor voids / inadequate coverage:** Unsupported area cracked under load; most common cause of isolated tile cracking
2. **Subfloor movement:** Structural movement translated to stone
3. **Omitted movement joint:** Compressive stress cracked the stone
4. **Impact:** Heavy object dropped; particularly vulnerable at thin sections and cut edges
5. **Natural stone fissure:** Some stone (particularly marble) has natural hairline fissures that were below the surface; installation stress, UFH cycling, or impact propagates the crack

### 18.3 Hollow Tiles (Debonded)

Same causes as ceramic tile (Lexicon 07, Section 18.1): inadequate coverage, contaminated substrate, wet screed, adhesive skin dried before laying.

For heavy stone: even minor adhesive voids create unsupported spans that can crack the stone under foot impact.

### 18.4 Scratching of Polished Surfaces

**Sources:**
- Grit underfoot (the primary cause of progressive dullness on polished marble and limestone)
- Chair and furniture feet without felt protectors
- Cleaning equipment (mop with grit; abrasive pads)
- The cleaning process itself if done with inappropriate cleaning agents on fine stone

**Prevention:**
- Entrance matting (4.5m minimum walk-off) at all entrances to prevent grit ingress
- Felt pads on all furniture
- Clean with appropriate pH-neutral stone cleaner and a soft microfibre mop

## 19. STONE RESTORATION — GRINDING, HONING, AND POLISHING

### 19.1 When Stone Restoration is Required

Natural stone can be fully restored to its original (or better) appearance by professional mechanical restoration:
- When the polished finish has become dull and scratched from years of use
- When etching has created extensive dull patches on marble or limestone
- When an existing honed floor needs to be polished for a new aesthetic
- When a polished floor needs to be honed down for a new more practical finish
- After flood damage or cleaning chemical damage

This is a significant differentiating advantage of natural stone over porcelain or LVT — the floor can be restored multiple times over its lifetime.

### 19.2 The Grinding and Polishing Process

Professional floor restoration uses a sequence of diamond abrasive pads mounted on a heavy rotary floor machine (typically a single-head or planetary grinder):

**Stage 1 — Heavy grinding (if significant lippage or damage):**
- Metal-bond diamond pads: 50–100 grit
- Removes significant material; levels tiles; removes deep scratches
- Generates slurry of water and stone dust; must be collected and disposed

**Stage 2 — Grinding:**
- 100–200 grit diamond pads
- Progressively finer scratches left by each pass

**Stage 3 — Honing:**
- 400–800 grit
- Surface becomes smooth and increasingly reflective
- At 400 grit: honed finish (low gloss satin)
- At 800 grit: high-honed (moderate gloss)

**Stage 4 — Polishing:**
- 1500–3000 grit diamond pads
- Surface becomes highly reflective

**Stage 5 — Crystallisation / chemical polishing (optional):**
- An oxalic acid-based powder or crystalliser is applied to the warm stone surface and burnished with a steel-wool pad under a floor machine
- The acid reacts chemically with the calcium carbonate (on marble and limestone) to create a very hard, brilliantly shiny surface — a "crystalline" layer
- Only applicable to calcareous stones (marble, limestone, travertine)
- Very high gloss but somewhat brittle; can be scratched; requires periodic re-crystallisation

**Stage 6 — Sealing:**
- After polishing: apply impregnating sealer
- The sealer protects the restored surface; extends the restoration period

### 19.3 Lippage Grinding

Where tiles are at different heights (lippage), heavy diamond grinding levels the entire floor:
- This is aggressive; significant material is removed
- After lippage grinding, the floor is honed and polished from scratch
- Only appropriate where the lippage is significant (>2mm) and the stone is thick enough to accommodate the material removal

## 20. MAINTENANCE AND CARE

### 20.1 Daily/Routine Maintenance

**Sweeping:**
- Daily in commercial; regular in residential
- Grit is the primary enemy of polished stone; removes before foot traffic grinds it into the surface
- Microfibre flat mop; do not use wet mop for routine dry cleaning

**Vacuuming:**
- Hard floor setting; suction only
- Essential in textured riven or rough stone (grit lodges in texture)

**Damp mopping:**
- pH-neutral stone-specific cleaner (e.g., HMK P21, LTP Cleaner, Lithofin MN Daily Cleaner)
- Well-wrung mop; no standing water on porous stone
- **Never use:** vinegar, lemon-based cleaners, multi-surface cleaners (often contain acids); bleach (damages some finishes); ammonia

### 20.2 Protecting Against Etching (Calcareous Stone)

- Coasters and placemats under all glassware and food items on stone surfaces
- Wipe spills immediately — acids etch within minutes on polished marble
- In bathrooms: rinse shampoo, conditioner, and cleaning products off stone promptly; many contain acids or chelating agents that can dull stone
- Install a bath panel that can be regularly removed for inspection of the floor behind

### 20.3 Annual/Periodic Maintenance

- Re-apply impregnating sealer annually (residential bathroom/kitchen) or every 2–3 years (dry residential floors)
- Professional deep clean and re-seal every 2–5 years depending on use
- For commercial: annual or biannual professional maintenance programme

## 21. COMMERCIAL APPLICATIONS

### 21.1 Retail and Hospitality

- **Hotel lobbies:** Polished marble or granite — the premium aesthetic; requires high maintenance programme with nightly buffing in luxury hotels; major hotels typically have on-site or contracted stone maintenance
- **Retail:** Granite (high durability, easy maintenance); limestone in premium boutique retail with daily maintenance
- **Restaurants:** Honed stone (not polished — too slippery and too much maintenance); granite; quartzite; slate

### 21.2 Healthcare

- Natural stone in healthcare: limited application due to joint maintenance (porous grout); slate or granite with epoxy grout in corridor specifications where aesthetics drive the specification over vinyl
- Not recommended in clinical areas without epoxy grout and thorough sealing programme

### 21.3 Public and Civic Buildings

- **Railway stations, airports:** Granite and quartzite flooring (Heathrow Terminal 5 uses large-format stone); Class 34 equivalent abrasion resistance required; anti-slip honed finish
- **Libraries, museums:** Limestone and marble with extensive maintenance infrastructure
- **Government buildings:** Portland stone, limestone, granite — heritage and civic aesthetic

## 22. SOURCING, PROVENANCE, AND SUSTAINABILITY

### 22.1 Ethical Sourcing

**Child and forced labour:** Some quarrying regions (India, Brazil, parts of Africa) have documented use of child and forced labour in the stone extraction chain. The supply chain from quarry to UK importer is often opaque.

**Best practice:**
- Specify from suppliers who can demonstrate quarry-level sourcing
- Specify FSC-equivalent stone certifications where available
- The Natural Stone Specialist (NSS) trade association and UK importers with BSI PAS 7000 supply chain security standards are markers of responsible sourcing

**Environmental impact of quarrying:**
- Open-cast quarrying removes large volumes of rock with associated landscape impact, water table disturbance, and dust
- Responsible quarries operate within ISO 14001 environmental management systems
- Quarry waste (typically 50–70% of extracted rock is waste material) is increasingly used as aggregate

### 22.2 Provenance and Authenticity

The natural stone market has persistent issues with mislabelling:
- "Marble" used for limestone that hasn't been metamorphosed (technically incorrect)
- "Quartzite" used for marble or limestone (see Section 4.2 — always acid-test)
- Country-of-origin mislabelling (Chinese granite sold as Indian; Turkish limestone sold as Portuguese)
- Reclaimed stone without provenance documentation

**Due diligence for premium specification:**
- Request quarry name, country of origin, and test data (EN ISO 10545 or equivalent)
- Request a sample batch before ordering the full quantity
- Verify frost resistance data for any external application

### 22.3 Sustainability Credentials

**The case for natural stone:**
- Truly natural; no chemical synthesis required
- Extremely long lifespan (centuries); the most durable floor covering
- Can be restored multiple times without replacement
- Inert at end of life; no toxic decomposition products
- Some stones sequester CO₂ in their mineral structure (calcium carbonate)

**The case against:**
- High embodied energy in quarrying, processing, cutting, and transport (particularly stone from distant sources)
- Quarrying has significant landscape impact
- Transport emissions for imported stone can be high (Indian and Brazilian stone shipped to UK)

**The balanced position:**
A locally-sourced stone floor (Welsh slate, Yorkshire sandstone, English limestone) has a compelling environmental case — natural, local, long-lasting. An imported tropical granite or Brazilian quartzite has a less compelling environmental case due to transport distances, though its longevity offsets some of the embodied energy over time.

## 23. BRITISH AND EUROPEAN STANDARDS

**BS 5385-5:2009 — Wall and floor tiling: Code of practice for the design and installation of terrazzo tile and slab, natural stone and composition block flooring:**
- The primary UK installation standard for natural stone flooring
- Subfloor requirements; adhesive selection; movement joints; wet area specification; specific stone types

**BS EN 1341:2012 — Slabs of natural stone for external paving:**
**BS EN 1342:2012 — Setts of natural stone for external paving:**
**BS EN 1343:2012 — Kerbs of natural stone for external paving:**
- Product standards for external natural stone products; dimension and quality requirements

**BS EN 12670:2001 — Natural stone: Terminology:**
- Definitions and classification; the reference for correct stone naming

**EN 12371:2010 — Natural stone test methods — Determination of frost resistance:**
- Mandatory test for external stone specification

**EN 1925:1999 — Natural stone test methods — Determination of water absorption coefficient:**
**EN 13755:2008 — Natural stone test methods — Determination of water absorption at atmospheric pressure:**
- Water absorption testing for stone classification

**EN 14157:2017 — Natural stone test methods — Determination of the abrasion resistance:**
- Böhme abrasion test; required for commercial floor stone specification

**EN 1926:2006 — Natural stone test methods — Determination of uniaxial compressive strength:**
**EN 12372:2006 — Natural stone test methods — Determination of flexural strength under concentrated load:**
- Structural strength testing

**BS EN 13501-1 — Reaction to fire:**
- Natural stone is typically Class A1fl (non-combustible mineral product); no fire performance concern

**BS 5385-3:2014 and BS 5385-4:2015:**
- Also relevant to stone when used in equivalent conditions to ceramic tile (see Lexicon 07, Section 22)

**Control of Substances Hazardous to Health (COSHH) Regulations 2002:**
- Silica dust from stone cutting; WEL 0.1 mg/m³

**Modern Slavery Act 2015:**
- Supply chain transparency obligations; relevant to stone sourcing from high-risk countries

## 24. TRADE TERMINOLOGY GLOSSARY

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Acid etching** | Chemical dissolution of calcium carbonate surface by acids; permanent on marble/limestone; irreversible without re-polishing |
| **Antiqued** | Stone surface treatment producing aged, worn appearance through brushing and/or acid treatment |
| **Basalt** | Dark, fine-grained igneous rock; dense; hard; excellent for flooring |
| **Böhme test** | EN 14157 abrasion resistance test; volume of material removed by weighted abrasive disc |
| **Book-match** | Two adjacent slabs from the quarry opened as a mirror image of each other; creates symmetrical veining |
| **BS 5385-5** | UK code of practice for natural stone and terrazzo flooring installation |
| **Bush-hammered** | Stone surface finish produced by mechanical percussion hammer; anti-slip; used externally and for stair nosings |
| **Calcite** | CaCO₃; the primary mineral in marble, limestone, and travertine; Mohs 3; acid-sensitive |
| **Calibrated** | Stone tile sawn to consistent thickness for thin-bed installation |
| **Cleavage (slate)** | The planes along which slate splits; determined by mica alignment during metamorphism |
| **Crystallisation** | Chemical polishing of calcareous stone using oxalic acid; produces very high gloss |
| **Decoupling membrane** | Polymer mat between subfloor and stone/tile system; absorbs subfloor movement |
| **Delamination (slate)** | Loss of surface layers from riven slate; related to cleavage plane instability |
| **EN 12004 (C2S2)** | Tile adhesive classification; cementitious, improved adhesion, highly deformable; required for stone over UFH and in wet areas |
| **EN 12371** | Frost resistance test for natural stone; essential for external specification |
| **EN 14157** | Böhme abrasion resistance test for stone |
| **Etching** | See acid etching |
| **Feldspar** | Major mineral in granite; Mohs 6; vitrifies at high temperatures |
| **Filled travertine** | Travertine with natural voids filled with cement or resin; smooth surface |
| **Flamed finish** | High-temperature surface treatment for granite; anti-slip; spalled texture |
| **Flexural strength** | Resistance to bending load; EN 12372; important for thin large-format stone |
| **Frost heave** | Ground freezing lifting tiles; prevention requires good drainage under substrate |
| **Granite** | Coarse-grained intrusive igneous rock; quartz, feldspar, mica; very hard; acid-resistant |
| **Honed** | Stone finish: smooth, satin, non-reflective; practical for most floor applications |
| **Igneous** | Rock formed from cooled magma; granite, basalt |
| **Iron staining** | Rust-coloured staining from iron minerals in stone or adhesive/substrate |
| **Lappato** | Semi-polished stone or tile finish; partly polished, partly textured |
| **Limestone** | Sedimentary CaCO₃ rock; warm colour palette; acid-sensitive |
| **Lippage** | Height difference between adjacent stone or tile surfaces |
| **Marble** | Metamorphosed limestone; crystalline calcite; polishes to mirror finish; acid-sensitive |
| **Metamorphic** | Rock transformed by heat and pressure; marble, quartzite, slate |
| **Mohs scale** | Mineral hardness scale 1–10; calcite = 3; quartz = 7; diamond = 10 |
| **Neutral-cure silicone** | Silicone sealant that cures without releasing acid; safe for calcareous stone joints |
| **Patina** | The aged surface character that develops on stone over years of use; valued in some contexts |
| **Poultice** | Absorbent material mixed with solvent or chemical; applied over stain to draw it out of stone |
| **Provenance** | The origin and supply chain documentation of a natural stone |
| **Quartzite** | Metamorphosed sandstone; very hard; quartz-dominant; acid-resistant; superior to marble for kitchen use |
| **Rectified** | Stone tile precision-ground to exact dimensional tolerance after cutting |
| **Riven / cleft** | Natural split surface of slate or sandstone along cleavage or bedding planes |
| **Sandstone** | Sedimentary rock; quartz sand cemented by mineral binder; warm colours; variable porosity |
| **Schist** | Medium-grade metamorphic rock; foliated; sparkly mica flakes |
| **Sedimentary** | Rock formed from compressed sediments; limestone, sandstone, travertine |
| **Sealer (impregnating)** | Penetrating sealer that fills stone pores without forming a surface film; the professional standard |
| **Slate** | Metamorphosed mudstone; strong cleavage; excellent non-slip riven surface; widely used in UK |
| **Slurry coat** | Neat cement paste applied to stone back immediately before thick-bed installation |
| **Terrazzo** | Composite of stone chips in cementitious or resin matrix; see Lexicon 09 |
| **Thermal conductivity** | Rate of heat transmission through material; stone has very high conductivity — ideal for UFH |
| **Travertine** | Calcium carbonate sedimentary rock deposited by mineral springs; characteristic voids; Tivoli, Italy |
| **Tumbled** | Stone tiles mechanically aged with rounded edges and chipped surfaces |
| **Veining** | Mineral-filled fractures or deformation bands in metamorphic stone; the characteristic patterning of marble |
| **Vitrification** | Glassy fusion of minerals at high temperature; what makes porcelain and granite non-porous |
| **York stone** | Traditional UK buff/grey sandstone flags from Yorkshire; quintessential British paving stone |
