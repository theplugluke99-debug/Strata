## 1. THE SUBFLOOR — WHY IT MATTERS MORE THAN THE FLOOR COVERING

### 1.1 The Hierarchy of Flooring Success

Every floor covering installation has three layers of dependency:

1. **The structure** (joists, beams, ground slab, upper concrete deck)
2. **The subfloor** (screed, boards, panels — the layer directly beneath the floor covering)
3. **The floor covering** (carpet, wood, tile, vinyl, resin)

The floor covering is the most visible layer and receives the most attention in specification. The subfloor is the least visible and receives the least attention. This imbalance is the primary reason that flooring failures occur.

**The physics are unforgiving:** A floor covering can only perform to the quality of the subfloor beneath it. A premium engineered wood floor over an uneven, damp concrete screed will ripple, cup, and fail. A £200/m² porcelain tile over an inadequately prepared substrate will crack, debond, and hollow. The most expensive floor covering money can buy cannot compensate for a bad subfloor.

### 1.2 What the Subfloor Must Provide

For any floor covering to perform correctly, the subfloor must be:

1. **Structurally sound:** No movement, deflection, spring, bounce, or flex beyond the specified limits
2. **Dry:** Moisture content within the limits specified for the floor covering type
3. **Flat:** Within the flatness tolerances required for the specific floor covering
4. **Level:** Appropriate level relative to adjacent areas and door thresholds
5. **Clean:** Free of contamination, adhesive residue, dust, oil, and organic material
6. **Compatible:** Chemically and mechanically compatible with the adhesive, primer, or underlay system being used

Each of these requirements has specific measurable criteria. Each must be verified, not assumed.

## 2. CONCRETE GROUND FLOORS — TYPES AND CONSTRUCTION

### 2.1 Ground-Bearing Slab (In-Situ Concrete)

The most common UK ground floor construction in both residential and commercial buildings built after approximately 1950.

**Construction:**
- Hardcore (crushed stone, DOT Type 1) compacted layer: typically 150–300mm
- Sand blinding layer: levels the hardcore surface; prevents hardcore from puncturing the DPM
- DPM (Damp Proof Membrane): typically 1200-gauge (300-micron) polythene sheet; lapped and taped at joints; turned up at edges to link with the wall DPC
- Concrete slab: typically C25–C30 concrete; 100–150mm residential; 150–250mm commercial
- Screed over slab: often a separate, subsequent layer (see Section 5)

**The DPM is structural moisture protection, not the preparation DPM:** The polythene DPM in the slab construction is a permanent barrier against ground moisture. The RH of the screed surface is measured relative to the construction DPM's effectiveness. If the DPM is breached, damaged, or absent (pre-1960s construction), significant moisture problems will occur regardless of any surface-applied DPM.

**Typical moisture behaviour:**
- New concrete: very high initial moisture; decreases over months and years as construction water dries out
- Established slab with intact DPM: stable, low RH; typically <65% RH within 50–75mm
- Slab without DPM (pre-1960s): moisture rises indefinitely from the ground; RH at surface can be permanent 80–100%

### 2.2 Beam and Block (B&B) Ground Floor

A suspended ground floor system using precast concrete beams and infill blocks (typically dense concrete blocks or lightweight aerated blocks):

**Advantages:**
- Creates a void beneath the floor; allows services to be run in the void
- Can be quicker to construct than a ground-bearing slab in some situations
- Less susceptible to differential settlement (useful in filled ground or areas with poor bearing capacity)

**For flooring purposes:**
- The top surface of the beam and block needs a screed overlay to provide the finished subfloor surface
- The screed must be specified to span the beam/block joints — use a reinforced screed or minimum 75mm depth
- The void beneath must be ventilated (to prevent moisture accumulation and wood rot if timber joists are present in mixed construction)

### 2.3 Existing Pre-1960s Concrete

- Often constructed without a DPM; moisture will rise continuously from below
- Frequently lower-specification concrete than modern standards; may be friable or dusty
- May contain contaminants from previous industrial use (oil, chemical spillage): relevant where an older industrial building is being converted
- Assessment: visual inspection; core extraction and laboratory testing where contamination is suspected; RH testing at multiple points
- Solution: tanking membrane (two-coat epoxy DPM) applied to the surface before any flooring

### 2.4 Asphalt Screeds (Historical)

- Mastic asphalt was used as both a DPM and a floor finish in older commercial construction
- Black, hard, slightly flexible
- Can be overlaid with SLC and modern floor coverings if structurally sound
- Cannot be ground (clogs grinding equipment); must be overlaid with SLC
- Any asphalt floor in an older building (pre-1980) should be assessed for asbestos content before any mechanical disturbance — some mastics contained asbestos fibres

## 3. CONCRETE UPPER FLOORS

### 3.1 In-Situ Reinforced Concrete (RC) Slabs

- Cast in place during construction; the standard for commercial multi-storey buildings
- Typically 175–250mm thick; reinforced with steel bars (rebar) or mesh
- Post-tensioned concrete: tendons stretched after casting provide additional structural capacity; increasingly common in flat-plate construction
- For flooring purposes: the concrete surface is the subfloor substrate; apply screed if flatness/level correction is needed; can be directly finished (polished concrete, resin, LVT glue-down) if the surface condition permits

### 3.2 Precast Concrete Planks

- Hollowcore planks or solid planks: precast off-site; craned into position
- Very efficient for large-span floor construction; widespread in residential and commercial
- For flooring: always require a screed overlay to fill joints between planks and achieve a continuous, flat surface
- The screed must be designed to accommodate differential deflection between planks: use a reinforced screed or minimum 75mm depth
- Plank joints: grout filled during construction; may still "telegraph" through thin screed overlays

### 3.3 Composite Concrete/Steel Decking

- Metal profiled decking with concrete poured on top; the deck provides both formwork and tensile reinforcement
- Common in steel-frame commercial buildings
- For flooring: the underside of the concrete topping is the subfloor surface; SLC applied as required

## 4. TIMBER SUBFLOORS — SUSPENDED FLOORS

### 4.1 Traditional Suspended Timber Ground Floor

- Timber joists spanning between sleeper walls (low brick walls bearing on the ground)
- Void beneath the joists must be ventilated via airbricks in the external walls
- Floorboards: solid timber T&G boards; typically 100–125mm wide; nailed to joists
- Very common in pre-1960s UK residential construction (Edwardian, Victorian, pre-war housing)

**Key characteristics for flooring:**
- **Movement:** The timber will expand and contract seasonally with humidity; the floor may bounce; individual boards may be loose
- **Airbrick ventilation:** Essential; blocked airbricks lead to condensation, mould, and dry rot in the joist void; must be checked before any floor covering installation
- **Gaps between boards:** Common in older floors; significant gaps (>3mm) must be filled or overlaid
- **Nail heads:** Must be punched below the surface before any floor covering that telegraphs (vinyl, LVT, linoleum)
- **Load capacity:** Domestic timber joist floors are typically designed for 1.5 kN/m² imposed load; stone tiles and heavy resin systems may exceed this — see Section 15

### 4.2 Modern Suspended Timber Upper Floor

- I-joists or engineered timber joists: engineered products (LVL, glulam, I-beam) increasingly replacing solid timber
- Decking: typically 22mm T&G chipboard (P5 moisture-resistant grade) or 18mm structural plywood
- Standard residential construction from approximately 1980 onward
- Much more consistent than traditional boards; fewer squeaks and gaps

**Chipboard subfloor assessment:**
- P5 chipboard (moisture-resistant): adequate for most floor coverings in normal conditions
- Check for swollen edges: if chipboard has been exposed to water during construction, edges swell and do not return to original profile; manifests as raised ridges at panel joints
- Check for deflection: walk the floor; identify any areas of spring or bounce
- T&G joints: ensure all panels are fully engaged; disengaged joints create height steps between panels

### 4.3 Plywood Overlays

When timber subfloors require preparation for demanding floor coverings (vinyl, LVT, tile):

**When an overlay is required:**
- Existing boards have gaps > 3mm
- Existing boards have significant unevenness or cupping
- Chipboard has swollen edges or delamination
- The floor covering requires a stiffer, more homogeneous substrate than individual boards can provide

**Overlay specification:**
- **Minimum thickness:** 6mm for carpet and most wood; 9–12mm for vinyl and LVT; 18mm for tiles (with decoupling membrane)
- **Grade:** FSC-certified structural plywood; EN 636 rated; moisture-resistant bonding (WBP — Weather and Boil Proof adhesive)
- **Fixing:** Screwed at 200mm grid; nails insufficient (pull-through over time)
- **Joints:** Stagger joints; 2mm gap between sheets (allows for expansion); joint butt (not over an existing board joint)
- **Preparation:** Fill screw heads flush; fill sheet joints with flexible filler; sand flush

### 4.4 Acoustic Performance of Timber Upper Floors

Timber upper floors require careful consideration for acoustic compliance in converted and new-build apartments:

- **Approved Document E requirements:** L'nT,w ≤45 dB impact sound (new build)
- Timber joisted floors typically achieve approximately 65–70 dB L'nT,w without treatment — approximately 20 dB worse than the target
- Solutions: resilient bars on ceiling below + mineral wool in joist void + acoustic underlay on floor above; see also Lexicon 11 (UFH) and Lexicon 12 (Commercial)
- The floor covering choice significantly affects the measured result; carpet and acoustic underlay contribute more than hard floor coverings

## 5. SCREED SYSTEMS

### 5.1 What a Screed Is

A screed is a layer of cementitious, calcium sulphate, or polymer-based material applied over a structural subfloor to provide a flat, smooth, and level surface for the floor covering. It is distinct from concrete (which is structural) and from the floor covering itself.

**Functions of a screed:**
- Create a flat, smooth surface over irregular structural concrete or beam/block floors
- Provide a level surface across large areas
- Incorporate services (underfloor heating pipes, electrical conduit)
- Provide thermal mass (UFH systems)
- Isolate the floor covering from the structural deck (floating screed)

### 5.2 Sand and Cement (Cementitious) Screed

**The traditional UK screed; still widely used.**

**Composition:** Typically 3–4 parts sharp sand : 1 part Portland cement, by volume. Water added to achieve the correct consistence.

**Types:**

**Bonded screed:**
- Applied directly to the structural concrete substrate after priming
- No separation layer between screed and slab
- Minimum thickness: 25mm
- Maximum thickness: 50mm (above this, cracking risk increases)
- Bonds chemically and mechanically to the substrate
- Used where the screed must resist horizontal shear (e.g., commercial forklift traffic areas)
- Critical requirement: the substrate must be mechanically prepared (scabbled or shotblasted); primed; laid while primer is tacky

**Unbonded screed:**
- A polythene sheet (DPM or separation membrane) is laid between the slab and the screed
- The screed is structurally independent of the slab
- Minimum thickness: 50mm (must be self-supporting without bond to substrate)
- The polythene prevents water in the fresh screed from being absorbed by the slab; allows even curing
- More tolerant of substrate surface condition than bonded screed
- Can be used where the substrate has existing contamination that prevents bonding

**Floating screed:**
- The screed is laid over a compressible layer (UFH insulation, acoustic insulation, or polystyrene)
- The compressible layer means the screed cannot be bonded to the structure
- Must be minimum 65mm depth (75mm where UFH pipes are incorporated at the bottom of the screed)
- The screed acts as a structural plate; reinforcement (steel mesh or polypropylene fibres) is required to prevent cracking from shrinkage and loading
- The most common screed type in new residential construction with UFH

**Screed drying rate:**
- The traditional "rule of thumb": 1mm drying per day for the first 40mm; 0.5mm/day thereafter
- This rule assumes: 20°C; 50% RH; adequate ventilation; no sealing of the screed surface
- Modern dehumidification drying: dramatically accelerates drying (to 1–2 weeks for a 75mm screed)
- **Temperature:** Screed should not be laid below 3°C or above 30°C
- **Curing:** Keep surface damp for the first 3–5 days (prevent too-rapid surface drying which causes surface cracking); then allow to dry naturally

### 5.3 Screed Thickness and Reinforcement

| Screed type | Minimum thickness | Reinforcement |
|-------------|------------------|---------------|
| Bonded | 25mm | Not required at minimum thickness |
| Unbonded | 50mm | Mesh or fibres for crack control |
| Floating (no UFH) | 65mm | Steel mesh or PP fibres |
| Floating with UFH (pipes in screed) | 75mm above top of pipe | Steel mesh or PP fibres |
| Floating with UFH (pipes above insulation) | 65mm above insulation | Steel mesh or PP fibres |

**Polypropylene fibres:** Increasingly used instead of steel mesh; added to the wet screed mix; distribute randomly throughout the matrix; control plastic shrinkage cracking; improve impact resistance; do not provide structural reinforcement equivalent to mesh for heavy loads

### 5.4 Sand and Cement Screed — Common Defects

**Screed cracking:**
- Normal shrinkage cracking (plastic): hairline cracks appearing within the first 24–48 hours; caused by too-rapid drying or insufficient curing
- Structural cracking: larger cracks often aligned with stress points (column lines, changes in thickness); inadequate reinforcement or excessive loading
- For flooring: all cracks must be assessed; hairline cracks (< 0.5mm) may be sealed with low-viscosity epoxy or primer; larger cracks require filling with cementitious repair compound

**Screed delamination:**
- The screed separates from the structural substrate
- Tap test: a hollow sound indicates delamination
- Causes: inadequate priming or bonding; contaminated substrate; too much water in the mix
- A delaminated screed is extremely problematic for tile and stone; these floors will crack because the screed flexes under load
- Repair: minor delamination can be injected with low-viscosity epoxy; widespread delamination requires removal and replacement

**Laitance:**
- The weak surface layer that forms on concrete and screed as the water rises during compaction
- Appears as a pale, powdery, or dusty surface
- Must be removed mechanically (grinding) before any adhesive, primer, or floor covering is applied
- If laitance is not removed: the floor covering bonds to the laitance, which then separates from the screed; this is a classic cause of tile debonding and vinyl lifting

## 6. CALCIUM SULPHATE (ANHYDRITE) SCREEDS

### 6.1 What Anhydrite Screed Is

Calcium sulphate screed (also called anhydrite screed, gypsum screed, or liquid screed) uses calcium sulphate (either anhydrite or hemihydrate) as the binder instead of Portland cement. It is typically supplied as a liquid — pumped onto the floor and self-levelling.

**Chemical basis:** Calcium sulphate hemihydrate (CaSO₄·½H₂O — plaster of Paris) or anhydrous calcium sulphate (CaSO₄ — anhydrite) reacts with water to form gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O). The crystallisation of gypsum provides the strength.

### 6.2 Advantages Over Sand and Cement

- **Self-levelling:** Supplied as a pumpable liquid; flows to fill the floor; minimal manual levelling required; faster to lay large areas (pump from a tanker on the road directly to the floor)
- **Very flat:** The liquid nature produces a very flat surface (typically ±3mm over 2m without effort); equivalent to hand-screeded S&C requires considerable skill
- **Faster drying (relatively):** Thinner section achievable; some anhydrite screeds dry to walkable within 24–48 hours
- **Lower carbon:** Less cement (high embodied carbon); calcium sulphate is a by-product of industrial processes (flue gas desulphurisation)
- **Ideal for UFH:** Very good thermal contact with UFH pipes embedded in the screed; low expansion and contraction

### 6.3 Critical Differences in Preparation for Flooring

Anhydrite screed requires fundamentally different preparation than cement screed before any floor covering is applied. Failure to follow this preparation is one of the most common causes of floor covering failures in new-build properties with liquid screeds:

**Step 1 — Sanding:**
- The surface of cured anhydrite screed is covered with a layer of crystallised gypsum and contaminants
- This surface layer must be removed by belt sanding or floor sanding before any further preparation
- If not sanded: any primer, adhesive, or SLC applied will bond to the weak surface layer, not the body of the screed

**Step 2 — Priming with dedicated anhydrite primer:**
- Portland cement-based SLCs and adhesives are not directly compatible with anhydrite screeds; the alkalinity of Portland cement can react with calcium sulphate and cause expansion
- A dedicated anhydrite primer (typically a diluted PVA or acrylic primer specific to gypsum-based substrates) must be applied before SLC or adhesive
- Allow primer to cure fully before the next step

**Step 3 — SLC application (if needed):**
- Only use an SLC specifically approved for use over anhydrite; check manufacturer compatibility
- Apply after primer has cured

**Step 4 — Tile adhesive:**
- Only use adhesive approved for use over anhydrite (C2S2 or better; specifically listed as suitable for gypsum substrates by the manufacturer)

**Step 5 — Moisture:**
- Anhydrite screeds are vulnerable to re-wetting; if water gets into the set screed, the gypsum can re-dissolve and the screed loses strength
- Test RH before installation: maximum 75% RH (same as cement screed) for most floor coverings
- Never flood-wash anhydrite screeds

### 6.4 Flow Screed Depth and UFH

- Anhydrite flow screed with UFH pipes: minimum 30–40mm above the top of the pipe (varies by system; manufacturer specification governs)
- This is significantly thinner than equivalent sand and cement screed over UFH; lower floor build-up means less impact on door clearances

## 7. FAST-DRYING AND RAPID-SETTING SCREEDS

### 7.1 The Problem: Standard Screed Drying Times

A 75mm sand and cement floating screed takes approximately 75–150 days to dry to ≤75% RH under normal conditions. In a busy construction programme, this is unacceptable — the floor covering programme is held up.

Solutions:
- **Dehumidification:** Forced drying using dehumidifiers and air movers; reduces drying time to 1–4 weeks for standard screeds; requires careful management to avoid surface cracking
- **Fast-drying screed:** Proprietary screed formulations designed to dry faster than standard

### 7.2 Proprietary Fast-Drying Screeds

Several manufacturers produce screeds specifically formulated to dry significantly faster than standard:

**Calcium sulphate-based fast-drying screeds:**
- Fibre-reinforced; tested to dry to ≤75% RH within 28 days at 75mm depth (under normal conditions)
- F. Ball Stopgap self-levelling screeds; ARDEX products; Instarmac products

**Rapid-hardening Portland cement screeds:**
- Calcium aluminate cement (CAC) or rapid-hardening Portland cement as binder
- Achieve structural strength within hours rather than days
- Foot traffic within 24 hours; floor coverings within 3–7 days
- Some products specifically designed for large-area application

**Fibre-reinforced fast-dry screed:**
- The fibre reinforcement controls shrinkage cracking as the screed dries faster than standard products
- Important: "rapid hardening" is not the same as "rapid drying" — hardening is structural (gaining strength); drying is moisture loss. A rapidly hardening screed may still need the full drying period before floor covering is applied unless specifically designed for fast drying

### 7.3 Verification Before Floor Covering

Regardless of what the screed product data sheet says about drying times:
- Always test the actual moisture content with an appropriate instrument before installing the floor covering
- Drying time estimates are based on idealised conditions; actual site conditions (temperature, humidity, ventilation) vary significantly
- The test result governs, not the product data sheet prediction

## 8. RAISED ACCESS FLOORS

### 8.1 What Raised Access Floors Are

A raised access floor (also called a false floor or computer floor) consists of a grid of metal pedestals bolted to the structural floor, supporting a field of removable panels. The void beneath the panels carries electrical, data, and HVAC services.

**Applications:** Office buildings (server rooms originally; now general open-plan offices); data centres; trading floors; some industrial.

### 8.2 Panel Types

| Panel type | Core material | Typical use |
|------------|---------------|-------------|
| Steel encapsulated | Steel shell + cement fill | Heavy duty; data centres |
| Steel encapsulated | Steel shell + woodcore | Standard office |
| Calcium sulphate (gypsum) | Calcium sulphate board | Standard office; high surface quality |
| HPL (High Pressure Laminate) | Woodcore + HPL surface | General office |
| Bare metal | Steel shell | Covered with separate floor covering |

### 8.3 Floor Covering Over Raised Access

- **Carpet tile:** The dominant covering for raised access floors; individual tile replacement when panels are lifted
- **LVT tile:** GlasBac or high-stability format; full-bond adhesive to prevent tiles lifting when panels are removed
- **Vinyl tile:** Similar to LVT; full bond
- **Engineered wood:** Less common; requires panels with adequate load rating; typically click format

**Key requirements:**
- Dimensional stability: any floor covering over raised access must not be affected by panel lifting and replacement
- Load capacity: the panel load rating must exceed the floor covering weight plus occupancy and equipment loads
- Static dissipation: in server rooms and data centres, the floor covering must be static-dissipative (ESD-rated) to protect electronic equipment

### 8.4 Pedestal Height and Floor Build-Up

- Pedestal height: adjustable; typically 50–500mm depending on void depth required for services
- The panel finish surface height determines the finished floor level
- Floor coverings add to the finish surface height: account for in threshold and door clearance calculations
- Where different finish depths (e.g., carpet tile vs LVT) occur adjacent to each other, ensure the pedestals are adjusted so both coverings finish at the same level

## 9. MOISTURE IN SUBFLOORS — SCIENCE AND TESTING

### 9.1 Why Subfloor Moisture Causes Floor Covering Failure

Water exists in a subfloor in two forms:
- **Free water (liquid):** Water in the pores of the concrete or screed; measured as moisture content (MC %)
- **Water vapour:** Moisture that has evaporated from free water and is present as vapour; measured as relative humidity (RH %)

When a floor covering is installed over a damp subfloor:

1. **Adhesive failure:** Water interferes with adhesive curing chemistry; weakens or prevents bond formation
2. **Osmotic blistering (resin):** Moisture vapour rises through the subfloor; trapped beneath the impermeable resin; blistering occurs
3. **Wood movement (wood floors):** Excess moisture is absorbed by wood; boards expand, cup, or buckle
4. **Mould growth:** Moisture above the dew point beneath an impermeable floor covering supports mould growth; hygiene and health issue
5. **Efflorescence:** Salts dissolved in moisture migrate to the surface; crystallise on or under the floor covering
6. **DPM failure (LVT):** No damage to the waterproof product itself, but condensation beneath the LVT creates mould between LVT and subfloor

### 9.2 RH vs MC — Understanding the Difference

**Relative Humidity (RH %):**
- Measures the moisture in the air within the pores of the concrete
- The standard UK measurement method for floor covering installation readiness
- The RH within the concrete equilibrates with the MC of the concrete over time
- **The universal UK limit: 75% RH for most floor coverings**
- BS 8203 specifies this limit; BS 8201 (wood) confirms it; individual product data sheets may have tighter or slightly looser limits

**Moisture Content (MC %) — Gravimetric:**
- The mass of water as a percentage of the dry mass of the material
- The fundamental measure; the most accurate
- Measured by oven-drying a sample
- Used in: timber subfloor assessment (max 14–15% MC); concrete (typically expressed as % by mass; values below 3% typically correlate with ≤75% RH)

**Carbide Bomb (CM Meter) Test:**
- Rapid site measurement of concrete MC
- A sample of concrete is sealed in a pressure vessel with calcium carbide; moisture reacts with carbide to produce acetylene gas; the pressure indicates MC
- Results in % MC; typically <3.5% CM = acceptable for most floor coverings (approximately correlates with ≤75% RH but not precisely)
- Advantages: fast (10 minutes); can be done at multiple depths by drilling to different levels
- Disadvantages: not as accurate as RH in-situ hygrometer method; destroys the sample

**RH In-Situ Hygrometer (BS 8203 Method) — THE STANDARD:**
- Small calibrated hygrometer heads installed in pre-drilled holes in the concrete
- Sealed with an insulating cover; left undisturbed for minimum **72 hours** to equilibrate
- The RH reading after 72 hours is the definitive measurement
- The holes must be drilled to approximately 40% of the screed depth (this is where moisture equilibrium representative of the whole slab is found)
- Multiple measurement points required: minimum 3 per area; additional points near drains, columns, and external walls (higher moisture zones)
- Temperature of the sensor must be noted; correct to 20°C reference if different

### 9.3 Moisture Mapping

For large commercial areas: map the moisture readings across the floor; identify zones:
- Create a grid of measurement points (typically every 20–25m²)
- Record RH at each point; produce a colour-coded plan
- High-RH zones may require DPM treatment even if the average is acceptable
- The highest reading anywhere in the floor governs — a floor is not "below 75%" if one area is at 82%

### 9.4 What Causes High Moisture in Subfloors

**New construction moisture:**
- The concrete and screed mix contains large quantities of water; this dries out over months
- Typical: a 100mm concrete slab at 150mm water/cement ratio contains approximately 35 litres of water per m²; most must evaporate before the floor is dry enough

**Rising damp:**
- Ground moisture rising through the capillary pores of the concrete where there is no DPM or the DPM has been breached
- Characteristic: the moisture reading does not reduce over time; it is a permanent moisture source
- Test: take readings over 4 weeks; if consistently high or rising, rising damp is the likely cause

**Lateral damp:**
- Moisture entering through wall/floor junctions or from adjacent wet areas (bathroom; kitchen; external wall without cavity)
- Characteristic: high readings near walls; reducing toward the centre

**Condensation:**
- In cold, humid conditions: moisture in the air condenses on the cold concrete surface
- Particularly relevant in unheated buildings during cold weather; also relevant in basement and below-grade areas

**Water leak / pipe failure:**
- Plumbing leak soaking the subfloor; washing machine overflow; wet trades (tilers leaving standing water)
- Characteristic: localised high reading; may be historical

## 10. DAMP PROOF MEMBRANES AND VAPOUR CONTROL

### 10.1 Physical DPM (Polythene Sheet)

- 1200-gauge (300-micron) polythene sheet
- Laid under the concrete slab (below the screed) as described in Section 2.1
- Where an existing slab lacks a DPM: cannot be retrofitted below the slab without major works
- Where used as an underlay DPM for floating floors (LVT, laminate, wood): a thinner PE sheet (100–150 micron) with integrated or separate DPM function
- Lapping: all joints minimum 300mm overlap; taped with polythene tape; sealed to wall DPC

### 10.2 Epoxy DPM (Two-Component Liquid-Applied)

**When the physical DPM cannot be retrofitted, or when RH is above 75%:**

A two-component epoxy coating applied to the surface of the existing concrete forms a chemical moisture vapour control layer:

**Properties:**
- Applied in 2 coats by roller or squeegee; each coat typically 200–250 microns
- Total dry film: 300–500 microns
- Provides a moisture vapour transmission rate (MVTR) sufficiently low to protect floor coverings
- Bridges hairline cracks (up to 0.3mm typically)
- Does not eliminate moisture from the slab; it controls the rate of moisture vapour transmission through the surface

**Application limits:**
- Most products applicable up to 75–85% RH substrate
- Some specialist products rated to 90–95% RH (check individual product data sheet)
- Above 90% RH: the moisture source must be investigated and remedied; no surface-applied DPM reliably works in extreme conditions

**Cure before overlaying:** Allow full cure per product data sheet (typically 12–24 hours at 20°C) before applying SLC, primer, or floor covering system

**Critical — mechanical preparation before epoxy DPM:**
- Grind off all laitance
- The epoxy DPM must bond to the concrete body, not to the weak surface layer
- A DPM over laitance will peel away from the slab as readily as any other coating

### 10.3 Surface DPM Products

Several one-component surface DPM products are available:
- SBR (Styrene-Butadiene Rubber) liquid applied by brush
- One-coat application; lower performance than two-component epoxy; lower cost
- Appropriate for: substrates at 75–80% RH where a light moisture barrier is needed as extra precaution
- Not appropriate for: substrates above 80% RH or where moisture is a confirmed ongoing problem

### 10.4 Vapour Control Layer (VCL)

A VCL is used under floating floors and underlays to control the upward movement of moisture vapour:
- Typically: 250–300 micron polythene sheet (stronger than standard DPM to resist wear)
- Installed as part of the underlay system for floating wood, LVT, or laminate
- Lap joints minimum 300mm; tape all joints
- Turn up at walls; trim behind skirting after floor installation

**Limitations of VCL underlays:**
- The VCL controls vapour transmission rate; it does not create a seal against liquid water or very high moisture
- If the RH of the subfloor exceeds 75%, a VCL underlay is not a sufficient solution — a proper DPM (epoxy or physical) is required

## 11. FLATNESS AND LEVEL — TOLERANCES AND TESTING

### 11.1 Flatness vs Level

These are distinct requirements that are frequently confused:

**Flatness (surface regularity):**
- The deviation of the surface from a true plane, measured over a specified distance
- Measured using a straight-edge of defined length; the maximum permitted gap between the straight-edge and the surface is the flatness tolerance
- Typical measurement: 2m straight-edge; record the maximum gap

**Level:**
- The height of the floor surface relative to a datum (typically an absolute height reference or a relative level across the room)
- A floor can be flat (no local undulations) but not level (the entire floor is sloped in one direction)
- Measured with a spirit level or rotating laser level

**Both are required:** A floor covering needs a flat surface to avoid telegraphing; it also needs to be level relative to adjacent rooms and door thresholds.

### 11.2 Flatness Tolerances by Floor Covering

| Floor covering | Maximum deviation | Under straight-edge length | Standard reference |
|----------------|------------------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Carpet (gripper) | 3mm | 2m | BS 5325 |
| Carpet (direct stick) | 3mm | 2m | BS 5325 |
| Engineered/solid wood (floating) | 3mm | 2m | BS 8201 |
| Engineered/solid wood (glue-down) | 3mm | 2m | BS 8201 |
| Laminate (floating) | 3mm | 2m | EN 13329 |
| LVT (flexible glue-down) | 2mm | 2m | BS 8203 |
| LVT (rigid click floating) | 3mm | 2m | BS 8203 |
| Vinyl sheet | 2mm | 2m | BS 8203 |
| Linoleum | 2mm | 2m | BS 8203 |
| Ceramic/porcelain tile (standard) | 3mm/2m AND 1mm/200mm | As stated | BS 5385 |
| Ceramic/porcelain tile (large format >600mm) | 3mm/2m AND 1mm/200mm | As stated | BS 5385 |
| Natural stone | 2mm | 2m | BS 5385-5 |
| Resin (self-levelling epoxy) | 2mm | 2m | BS 8204-6 |
| Resin (mortar screed) | 3mm | 2m | BS 8204-6 |

**The strictest tolerances** apply to: vinyl sheet, linoleum, flexible glue-down LVT, natural stone, and resin self-levelling systems — all products that telegraph subfloor imperfections because they are either very thin, or conform closely to the substrate.

**The short-range tolerance** (1mm/200mm) for large format tiles is particularly important and often overlooked — a surface can be flat over 2m but have short-range undulations that cause lippage with large tiles.

### 11.3 Testing Method

**Straight-edge test:**
- Use an accurate aluminium or steel straight-edge of the required length (2m minimum)
- Place on the prepared surface; check under the straight-edge at multiple points with feeler gauges
- Record the maximum deviation
- The test must be performed in all directions (longitudinal, transverse, diagonal) across the floor area

**Digital flatness testing (commercial):**
- Face Floor Flatness (F-number system): a US-originated system increasingly referenced in UK commercial construction; uses a proprietary profilograph device
- For most UK residential and standard commercial work, the straight-edge method is the standard

## 12. SELF-LEVELLING COMPOUNDS

### 12.1 What Self-Levelling Compounds Are

Self-levelling compounds (SLC), also called floor levellers or smoothing compounds, are polymer-modified cementitious or calcium sulphate-based products that flow to a level surface and cure to provide a smooth, hard substrate for floor coverings.

They are distinct from screeds (which are structural) and from the floor covering adhesive. They serve one purpose: to produce a flat, smooth subfloor surface.

### 12.2 Types of SLC

**Standard cementitious SLC:**
- Portland cement + fine aggregate + polymer modifiers + water
- Applied by trowel or gauge rake at 3–10mm thickness; flows and self-levels
- Cures hard in 1–3 hours; can be overlaid with floor covering within 2–24 hours depending on product
- Compatible with most primer systems and substrates when correctly primed
- Example products: F. Ball Stopgap 1200, Ardex K 15, Mapei Ultraplan, Weber Floor 4050

**Flexible SLC:**
- As above but with higher polymer content; greater flexibility
- Used over timber substrates and substrates with minor residual movement
- Maximum depth typically 6mm for flexible SLCs (more polymer = more expensive at greater depth)
- Example: F. Ball Stopgap Floor 60, Ardex K 39 Flex

**Fibre-reinforced SLC:**
- Glass or polymer fibres incorporated in the mix
- Better crack resistance; can be applied thicker
- Used where minor substrate movement is anticipated

**Rapid-hardening SLC:**
- Achieves walking strength within 30–60 minutes; can be overlaid within 2–4 hours
- Essential where programme time is critical
- Example: F. Ball Stopgap 1500, Ardex K 301, Mapei Ultraplan Eco

**Deep-fill levellers:**
- Higher aggregate loading allows application to 30–50mm in a single pour
- Replaces a thin screed in some situations; faster than hand-screeding
- Example: F. Ball Stopgap Freeflow 30, Mapei Mapefloor I 300 SL

**SLC for anhydrite substrates:**
- Specifically formulated for gypsum-based substrates; no Portland cement to react with calcium sulphate
- Example: F. Ball Stopgap 1200 Plus (anhydrite-compatible), Ardex K 10

### 12.3 Application Process

1. **Substrate preparation:** Grind laitance; repair cracks and holes; secure any loose material
2. **Prime:** Apply appropriate primer; allow to cure to correct tack (sticky but not wet)
3. **Mix SLC:** In correct water:powder ratio per manufacturer; do not over-water (weakens the compound; promotes cracking)
4. **Pour and spread:** Pour mixed SLC onto primed surface; spread with a spreader bar or gauge rake; work quickly within pot life
5. **Roll or spike:** If the product requires de-aeration (some do; many do not), use a spiked roller immediately after spreading
6. **Feather edges:** Blend edges to surrounding floor level where the SLC meets existing transitions
7. **Protect:** Keep foot traffic off the SLC for the specified time (typically 1–3 hours for hard SLC; less for rapid products)
8. **Check flatness:** Once cured, check with straight-edge; apply additional SLC to any remaining hollows if necessary

### 12.4 SLC Thickness Limits

- Most standard SLCs: 3–10mm in a single application
- For depths greater than 10mm: apply in multiple layers (allow each layer to cure before the next)
- Or specify a deep-fill leveller rated for the required depth in a single pour
- **Over-application:** Applying SLC too thick in a single pass can cause cracking as the material shrinks during cure

### 12.5 The Primer Sequence for SLC

**Always prime before SLC.** The primer:
- Seals porous surfaces to prevent the SLC being rapidly absorbed (which would cause localised weakness and possibly cracking)
- Bonds to the substrate, providing a key for the SLC
- Stabilises dusty or friable surfaces

**Primer dry time:** The SLC must be applied while the primer is at the correct tack:
- Too wet: the SLC floats on the wet primer; does not bond properly
- Too dry (beyond the priming window): the substrate is sealed; reduced adhesion
- The primer manufacturer specifies the re-coat window; typically 30 minutes to 4 hours depending on site conditions

## 13. PRIMERS — TYPES AND APPLICATIONS

### 13.1 Why Primers Cannot Be Skipped

A primer is not an optional enhancement — it is a functional requirement for the adhesive or SLC system. Skipping priming causes:
- Adhesive or SLC absorbed into the substrate (over-porous substrate); insufficient material remains at the interface; bond failure
- Pinhole formation as air escapes from unprimed pores through the adhesive or SLC
- Weak bond zones at highly porous spots
- Lifting and bubbling of thin-film products (LVT, vinyl) on unprimed surfaces

### 13.2 Primer Types

**General purpose acrylic primer (e.g., F. Ball Styccobond F41 Primer, Ardex P 4):**
- Most versatile primer; suitable for most cementitious substrates, plywood, chipboard
- Water-based; low odour; fast drying (20–40 minutes)
- Apply neat to highly porous surfaces; diluted (typically 1:5 water) on moderately porous surfaces

**SBR (Styrene-Butadiene Rubber) primer:**
- Also used as a bonding agent for screeds and repair mortars
- Diluted 1:4 for priming; undiluted as bonding slurry for screeds
- Good for concrete surfaces and plywood
- Slower drying than acrylic; slightly thicker viscosity

**Epoxy primer (two-component):**
- Required before resin floor systems (see Lexicon 09)
- Can be used as a DPM over mildly elevated moisture substrates when a moisture-tolerant formulation is specified
- Solvent-based (traditional) or water-based; solvent-based provides deeper penetration on dense concrete

**Anhydrite primer:**
- Specifically for calcium sulphate screed surfaces before SLC or adhesive
- Seals the gypsum surface without reacting adversely with the calcium sulphate chemistry
- Often diluted PVA or specialist acrylic; follow manufacturer specification

**Adhesion primer (for non-porous surfaces):**
- For laying tiles or LVT over glazed ceramic, polished stone, or dense concrete
- Creates a mechanical/chemical key on surfaces that standard adhesive would not bond to
- Example: Mapei Primer T (for tiles over tiles); Ardex P 82 (for dense surfaces)

## 14. SUBFLOOR PREPARATION FOR SPECIFIC FLOOR COVERINGS

### 14.1 For Carpet

- Flatness: 3mm/2m — the most forgiving; carpet's pile and underlay mask minor subfloor irregularities
- Moisture: ≤75% RH (adhesive for direct stick carpet, or gripper for moisture-sensitive carpets)
- Key issues: nail heads or fixings protruding above the surface (punch flush); rough concrete textures (fill with SLC); old adhesive residue (remove or grind flat)
- Over wood subfloors: ensure no springing or loose boards; ply overlay if boards uneven or gappy
- Gripper rod nailing: concrete must be strong enough for nail penetration; very soft or friable screed may not hold gripper rod nails (adhesive gripper as alternative)

### 14.2 For Engineered and Solid Wood (Glue-Down)

- Flatness: 3mm/2m
- Moisture: ≤75% RH; some adhesives permit up to 80–85% RH with DPM primer
- Critical: DPM primer or two-coat epoxy DPM applied first if substrate moisture is elevated or where moisture risk exists
- Surface: laitance removed; all cracks filled; old adhesive removed
- The wood adhesive bed also acts as a minor levelling layer; small surface imperfections (≤1mm) are absorbed by the adhesive bed

### 14.3 For LVT (Glue-Down)

- The most demanding subfloor tolerance for any resilient product: 2mm/2m
- Moisture: ≤75% RH (SLC and adhesive-sensitive products)
- The thin, conformable nature of flexible LVT telegraphs every subfloor imperfection
- Process: grind/prepare; apply DPM if needed; prime; apply SLC to 2mm/2m tolerance; prime SLC; apply adhesive at correct tack
- Embossed existing vinyl: apply embossing leveller or skim of flexible SLC before new LVT
- The SLC surface must be clean and dust-free; vacuum before adhesive application

### 14.4 For Ceramic and Porcelain Tile

- Flatness: 3mm/2m AND 1mm/200mm
- Moisture: ≤75% RH for most adhesive systems
- Over anhydrite: sand + prime with anhydrite primer + use anhydrite-compatible adhesive
- Large format tile: must achieve 1mm/200mm short-range flatness — this often requires multiple SLC applications
- Pull-off strength: minimum 1.0 N/mm² surface tensile strength for light commercial; 1.5 N/mm² for heavy commercial
- Full surface preparation including DPM where required; tile system does not benefit from residual moisture tolerance

### 14.5 For Resin Systems

- The strictest surface preparation requirements
- Shotblasting or diamond grinding: mandatory
- Surface tensile strength: minimum 1.5 N/mm² (EN 1504-3 pull-off test)
- Moisture: CM test preferred; maximum varies by system (typically ≤4% CM / ≤75% RH for epoxy; up to 6% CM for moisture-tolerant systems)
- All cracks injected or repaired
- Two-coat epoxy DPM if moisture is elevated

## 15. STRUCTURAL ASSESSMENT

### 15.1 When Structural Assessment Is Required

A structural engineer must be consulted before:
- Installing natural stone or tile on an upper timber floor (the combination of structural weight + the rigidity requirement for tiles)
- Installing any floor covering that significantly exceeds the original design load
- Removing existing screeds or floor build-up that may have been structurally contributing
- Converting an industrial or warehouse building for residential use (original structural loads may not be appropriate for domestic use)
- Any application where the deflection of the floor under the proposed loading would exceed L/360

### 15.2 Calculating Floor Loading

**Dead load (DL):** The weight of the permanent floor construction itself:
- 20mm porcelain tile + adhesive + screed: approximately 50–65 kg/m²
- 75mm floating screed: approximately 150 kg/m²
- 3mm self-levelling epoxy: approximately 5 kg/m²

**Imposed load (IL):** The weight of occupants, furniture, equipment:
- Residential: 1.5 kN/m² (150 kg/m²) per BS EN 1991-1-1
- Office: 2.5–3.0 kN/m²
- Retail: 4.0 kN/m²
- Industrial: 5.0–7.5 kN/m²+

**Total design load = DL + IL**

Compare against the original structural design load for the floor; if exceeded, structural upgrading is required.

### 15.3 Deflection Assessment

Beam deflection under load creates differential movement in the floor surface:
- BS 5628/BS EN 1995 limits deflection to L/360 for partitions and brittle finishes
- For tile and stone: L/360 maximum spanning the joist spacing
- For a 600mm joist span: maximum deflection = 1.67mm under full load
- In practice: most domestic timber floors exceed this under stone loading without modification
- Remedies: additional noggins between joists; sistering joists (doubling up); concrete topping; additional structural support below

## 16. NEW BUILD SUBFLOOR SEQUENCING

### 16.1 The Programme Challenge

One of the most consistent problems in new-build construction is the interface between the subfloor programme and the floor covering programme. The floor covering contractor is typically one of the last trades; they inherit whatever subfloor condition the preceding trades have created and must work within the time constraints of the overall programme.

**The critical path:**
1. Structural floor complete
2. Services installed (UFH pipes, electrical conduit)
3. Screed poured
4. Screed drying period (the programme constraint)
5. Wet trades complete (plaster, tiles where applicable)
6. RH test; assessment
7. Floor covering installation
8. Skirting boards; finishing

### 16.2 Screed Coordination

**Pre-screed requirements:**
- All services (UFH, electrical, drainage, plumbing) must be pressure-tested and complete before screed is poured
- Expansion joint positions agreed and marked
- Screed specification agreed with flooring contractor (type, depth, finish level)

**Screed specification coordination:**
- The screed finish level must account for the floor covering build-up height
- Floor covering contractor must specify: required screed level; surface regularity requirement; DPM requirement
- This information must be communicated to the screed contractor before work starts; not after

**Common errors to avoid:**
- Screed poured too high: insufficient floor covering build-up height; door frames now too low
- Screed poured too low: excessive SLC required; programme delay
- No DPM: discovered after screed is poured; screed must be treated with epoxy DPM at extra cost
- Screed contaminated with plaster droppings during other trades: must be ground or levelled; additional programme time

### 16.3 RH Testing Scheduling

RH testing must be planned into the programme — it is not an instant test:
- Minimum 72 hours with hygrometers in place before reading
- If the reading fails (>75% RH): further drying required; re-test after minimum 2 weeks
- The test cost (equipment, time) must be budgeted; it is not optional

## 17. RENOVATION — WORKING WITH EXISTING SUBFLOORS

### 17.1 Assessment Strategy for Renovation

Existing subfloors present challenges that new builds do not:
- Unknown history of the slab (past floods, contamination, previous floor coverings)
- Unknown original DPM status and condition
- Previous adhesive residue
- Historical patching that may have differential movement or moisture characteristics
- Potential asbestos in pre-1980 floor materials

### 17.2 Investigation Protocol

**Visual inspection:**
- Condition of existing floor covering (staining, lifting, discolouration patterns indicating moisture)
- Condition of skirting boards (black marks, staining = moisture)
- Airbrick condition (blocked airbricks = moisture risk for suspended floors)
- Previous water damage evidence

**Tap test (tile/stone):**
- Walk the floor systematically; tap with a rod or foot
- Hollow sound = delaminated tiles; indicates moisture history

**Moisture testing:**
- Multiple hygrometer positions; minimum 72 hours; map results
- CM meter test at several depths to profile moisture through the screed depth

**Pull-off testing:**
- For substrates intended to receive adhesive-bonded floor coverings; confirm minimum tensile strength

**Asbestos assessment:**
- Pre-1985 vinyl tile and adhesive (particularly black 9"×9" bitumen-based tile)
- Pre-1985 artex or floor texture coating
- Commission licensed asbestos survey before any mechanical disturbance

### 17.3 Removal of Existing Floor Coverings

**Carpet:** Lift and roll; remove gripper rod; grind or extract adhesive residue from direct-stick carpet
**Resilient (vinyl, LVT):** Lift from one corner; use floor scraper or SDS rotary stripper; residual adhesive — grind or encapsulate
**Ceramic tile:** SDS hammer drill with scraper attachment; electric tile stripper machine; residual adhesive — grind
**Wood floor (glued down):** SDS chisel or electric floor stripper; adhesive residue must be ground flat before SLC or new floor covering
**Resin coating:** Diamond grinding or scarifying; the hardness of the resin determines equipment required

**Asbestos vinyl tile:** Do not remove; encapsulate with SLC; refer to licensed contractor if removal is required

### 17.4 Overlay Strategy (Without Full Removal)

In some renovation scenarios, it is better to overlay existing floor materials rather than remove them:
- Acceptable if: the existing material is fully bonded, flat, and free of moisture problems
- Not acceptable if: the existing material is hollow, damp, unstable, or so contaminated that adhesion of the overlay is compromised

**SLC over existing tile:**
- Suitable if all tiles are fully bonded (tap test passes)
- Clean; prime with adhesion primer; apply SLC; achieves a flat surface for new floor covering
- Height build-up: account for in door clearances and threshold levels

## 18. UNDERFLOOR HEATING SUBFLOOR INTEGRATION

### 18.1 UFH and Screed — The Complete Relationship

UFH subfloor design is covered in detail in Lexicon 11. Key subfloor points:

**Screed type for UFH:**
- Anhydrite flow screed: the preferred choice for wet UFH in screed; pumpable; very flat; good thermal contact; minimum 30–40mm above pipes
- Sand and cement floating screed: traditional; deeper (minimum 65–75mm above pipes); more labour-intensive
- The screed must envelop the UFH pipes completely — no voids around pipes (voids create hot spots and prevent even heat distribution)

**Screed commissioning:**
- The commissioning protocol (Lexicon 02, 03, 07 etc.) ensures all construction moisture is driven out and the screed dimensions have stabilised before floor covering installation
- This must be specified in the UFH commissioning plan and the flooring specification; it is not automatic

**Screed RH after commissioning:**
- After the commissioning cycle, allow the screed to cool and test RH
- The commissioning cycle drives out moisture but may also temporarily raise RH in adjacent areas
- Always re-test after commissioning; never assume the screed is dry because it has been hot

### 18.2 Expansion Joint Coordination with UFH

UFH screeds expand and contract with temperature cycling. Expansion joint locations must be:
- Coordinated with the UFH layout (joints cannot cut UFH pipes; conduit sleeves required at joint crossings)
- Consistent with the tile or stone movement joint pattern above (structural joints below must correspond to movement joints in the tile installation above)
- Agreed between the UFH designer, screed contractor, and floor covering contractor at design stage

## 19. PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 19.1 Hollow Floor Covering (Drum Sound)

**Symptoms:** Tap test produces hollow drum sound under an apparently solid floor covering.

**Causes:**
1. **Delaminated screed below:** The screed has separated from the slab; the hollow is in the screed layer, not between the floor covering and screed
2. **Floor covering debonded from screed:** Adhesive failure; moisture; contamination
3. **Laitance layer:** The floor covering bonded to laitance, which is now separating
4. **Trapped air under self-levelling compound:** SLC was applied without priming; air escaped through the compound and created voids

**Diagnosis:** Drill a small exploratory hole; identify which interface is hollow; this determines the remedy

### 19.2 Screed Cracking (Pre-Installation)

**Symptoms:** Visible cracks in the screed surface before floor covering is installed.

**Types:**
- **Plastic shrinkage cracks (surface):** Fine, shallow cracks appearing within first 48 hours; caused by rapid surface drying; typically superficial; fill with low-viscosity epoxy or primer
- **Structural cracks:** Wider, often deeper cracks running through the screed; may indicate: inadequate reinforcement; excessive loading; differential settlement; restraint from fixed objects
- **Curling cracks at edges:** The screed edges curl upward or crack; caused by differential drying between the surface (dries faster) and the base; more common in thin bonded screeds

**Action:**
- Hairline (<0.3mm): seal with low-viscosity primer or epoxy injection; monitor
- Structural (>0.5mm): investigate cause; repair with epoxy injection; if caused by movement, consider a decoupling membrane above

### 19.3 Floor Covering Failure Traced to Subfloor

Where a floor covering has failed (lifted, cracked, buckled) and the subfloor is identified as the cause:

**Investigation sequence:**
1. Remove the failed floor covering
2. Conduct full subfloor assessment: moisture test; pull-off test; tap test for delamination; visual for contamination
3. Identify the failure mechanism
4. Remedy the root cause (not just the symptom)
5. Prepare subfloor correctly before reinstallation
6. Document findings for any warranty or liability discussion

| Resin (epoxy SLE) | Shotblast/grind → repair cracks → DPM if >4% MC → prime → apply resin system |
| Microcement | Clean existing surface → grind if needed → prime → microcement layers → seal |

## APPENDIX B: MOISTURE TEST QUICK REFERENCE

| Reading | Status | Action |
|---------|--------|--------|
| ≤65% RH / ≤3% MC | Excellent | Apply floor covering |
| 65–75% RH / 3–4% MC | Acceptable | Standard primer; proceed to floor covering |
| 75–80% RH / 4–5% MC | Elevated | Surface DPM or moisture-tolerant primer; check product data sheet |
| 80–85% RH / 5–6% MC | High | Two-coat epoxy DPM; confirm adhesive suitability |
| 85–95% RH / 6–8% MC | Very high | Epoxy DPM; investigate source; consider dehumidification |
| >95% RH / >8% MC | Unacceptable | Do not install; major investigation required; remedial works before flooring |
