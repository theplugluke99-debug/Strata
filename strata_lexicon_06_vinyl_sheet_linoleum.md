## 1. CATEGORY OVERVIEW AND NAMING CONFUSION

### 1.1 "Lino" — The Word Everyone Uses Wrong

When an older customer says "lino" or "linoleum," they almost certainly mean vinyl sheet flooring. This is one of the most entrenched misnomers in the entire flooring industry. Understanding why this happened matters for credibility:

**Historical sequence:**
- Linoleum was invented in 1860 and dominated domestic and commercial flooring for approximately a century
- From the 1960s onward, PVC vinyl sheet products replaced linoleum in most domestic and budget commercial applications — they were cheaper to manufacture, more flexible, came in wider widths, and required less maintenance
- The word "lino" was already so embedded in the British vernacular that people continued using it to describe the new vinyl products that replaced it
- By the 1980s and 1990s, genuine linoleum was barely available in domestic retail; virtually everything called "lino" in UK homes was actually vinyl
- Today the conflation is near-total among the general public; even some trade professionals use the terms interchangeably

**The correct distinction:**
- **Linoleum:** Made from linseed oil, cork, wood flour, limestone, and jute backing; entirely natural; no PVC; biodegradable
- **Vinyl sheet:** Made from PVC and synthetic compounds; a petroleum-derived product
- They look broadly similar when laid; they are chemically, materially, and behaviourally completely different products

### 1.2 Products in This Lexicon

This lexicon covers:
1. **Genuine linoleum** (Forbo Marmoleum, Armstrong Marmorette, Gerflor Artigo in linoleum variants)
2. **Vinyl sheet** (heterogeneous and homogeneous; cushioned and non-cushioned; safety flooring)
3. **Vinyl composition tile (VCT)**
4. **Safety / anti-slip vinyl**

LVT (Luxury Vinyl Tile in plank/tile click format) is covered in Lexicon 05. Sheet vinyl is covered here. The distinction: LVT is manufactured as individual tile or plank units; sheet vinyl comes in 2m or 4m wide rolls.

## 2. LINOLEUM — WHAT IT ACTUALLY IS

### 2.1 History and Invention

Linoleum was invented by **Frederick Walton** in 1860 in Staines, Middlesex, England. Walton observed that linseed oil forms a tough, flexible skin when it oxidises in contact with air — the same process used in oil painting. He developed a method of oxidising linseed oil at scale and combining it with other natural materials to create a durable floor covering.

The word "linoleum" derives from the Latin *linum* (flax — the source of linseed oil) and *oleum* (oil).

By the early 20th century, linoleum was the most widely-installed floor covering in the world. Scottish town **Kirkcaldy** became the global centre of linoleum production — Michael Nairn's factory (later Nairn Floors) produced vast quantities. The Kirkcaldy connection is culturally significant in UK flooring history.

### 2.2 How Linoleum Is Made

Linoleum manufacture is a complex, slow process — which is one reason vinyl replaced it commercially:

**Raw materials:**
- **Linseed oil:** Cold-pressed from flax seeds (*Linum usitatissimum*); the binder
- **Rosin (colophony):** Derived from pine tree resin; acts as a flux and binder modifier
- **Cork flour:** Ground cork granules; provides resilience and insulation
- **Wood flour:** Fine sawdust; acts as a filler and modifies texture
- **Limestone (calcium carbonate):** Mineral filler; adds dimensional stability and fire resistance
- **Jute backing:** Woven jute hessian; the carrier fabric to which the linoleum compound is applied
- **Pigments:** Mineral pigments for colour (natural, earth-tone palette; limited compared to vinyl)

**Manufacturing process:**
1. Linseed oil is heated in large shallow tanks with circulating hot air — the oxidation process ("linoxyn" formation) takes days to weeks
2. The oxidised oil (linoxyn) is combined with rosin, cork flour, wood flour, and limestone to form the "cement" compound — a warm, malleable mass
3. Pigments are added to create the required colour
4. The compound is calendered (pressed through rollers) onto the jute backing in multiple passes to achieve the required thickness
5. The sheets are hung in drying "stoves" — heated chambers — for 2–6 weeks while the linoxyn continues to oxidise and cure, hardening the product
6. The cured sheets are inspected, measured, and cut into rolls

**The result:** A completely natural product where the linseed oil has polymerised (oxidised) into a hard thermoset-like matrix. Unlike PVC (a thermoplastic), cured linoleum cannot be re-melted — the oxidation is irreversible.

### 2.3 Linoleum Construction Types

**Solid (calendered) linoleum:**
- The classical product; the compound is calendered uniformly across the full thickness
- Colour and pattern run through the full depth — the wear layer cannot be exhausted to reveal an uncoloured core
- Available as **plain** (solid colour), **marbled** (swirled colours), or **jasper** (fine multi-colour granulation)
- Standard thickness: 2.0mm, 2.5mm, 3.2mm, 4.0mm
- Roll width: 2m standard; some products 4m

**Linoleum tiles:**
- Cut from calendered sheet; various sizes (300×300mm, 333×333mm, 500×500mm)
- Allows complex pattern installation (checkerboard, borders, inlays)
- Slightly less material efficiency than sheet

**Modular linoleum (Forbo Marmoleum Click, Marmoleum Solid):**
- Linoleum surface on HDF core with click locking system
- Floating installation; no glue required
- Aimed at residential market where traditional glue-down linoleum installation is impractical or undesired
- Performance of the linoleum surface with the installation convenience of click flooring

**Linoleum sheet with foam/felt backing:**
- Some commercial linoleum products have a felt or foam backing layer
- Provides cushioning and acoustic benefit
- More common in acoustic-specification commercial products

### 2.4 The Yellowing Problem — "Buttering"

Linoleum has one well-known characteristic that has historically caused consumer confusion and complaints:

**Yellowing in dark / covered areas:**
The linseed oil oxidation process that gives linoleum its hardness is ongoing — it continues, slowly, throughout the product's life. In areas shielded from light (under furniture, in roll storage, in rooms with no UV exposure), the continued oxidation causes a yellow/amber tinge to develop on the surface.

This is called **"buttering"** in the trade.

**Critical facts about buttering:**
- It is not a defect; it is inherent chemistry of linseed oil oxidation
- It is **reversible** — exposure to light (particularly UV) reverses the yellowing; the colour returns to normal within days to weeks of being uncovered
- All linoleum, regardless of manufacturer or colour, will butter to some degree
- White, cream, and pale grey colours show it most visibly
- Very dark colours show it least
- Modern formulations (Forbo Marmoleum in particular) have reduced but not eliminated the tendency
- The customer must be briefed on this before purchase — it is the single most common linoleum complaint

### 2.5 Bacteriostatic Properties of Linoleum

A genuinely significant and evidence-based property:

**The chemistry:** As linseed oil continues to oxidise on the linoleum surface, it releases trace quantities of oxidised fatty acids — a byproduct of the polymerisation process. These compounds have measurable bacteriostatic (growth-inhibiting) and bactericidal properties against common pathogens.

**Clinical evidence:** Studies (including research by University Hospital Münster, referenced by Forbo) have found that linoleum surfaces show significantly lower bacterial counts than equivalent vinyl surfaces in hospital environments when tested in equivalent conditions without cleaning.

**Implication:** Linoleum is specified in some European healthcare environments not just for sustainability reasons but for its inherent antimicrobial properties — a property that no vinyl product possesses without the addition of a chemical biocide treatment.

**Note on claims:** This property should be presented accurately: bacteriostatic (slows growth) is not the same as bactericidal (kills bacteria); linoleum does not replace proper cleaning and infection control protocols.

## 3. LINOLEUM — PROPERTIES, MAINTENANCE AND MODERN APPLICATIONS

### 3.1 Physical Properties

| Property | Linoleum | Vinyl (for comparison) |
|----------|----------|----------------------|
| Composition | Natural: linseed oil, cork, wood flour, limestone, jute | Synthetic: PVC, plasticisers, fillers |
| Biodegradable | Yes (fully) | No |
| Carbon footprint | Low | Medium-high |
| Hardness | Moderate-hard | Variable |
| Resilience | Good (cork content) | Good |
| Thermal conductivity | Low (good insulator) | Moderate |
| Acoustic | Good (cork content absorbs impact) | Moderate |
| Bacteriostatic | Yes (inherent linseed oil chemistry) | No (without additive) |
| Antistatic | Moderate | Requires additives |
| UV stability | Good | Good (modern PU-treated) |
| Moisture resistance | Moderate (not waterproof; backing is jute — absorbs water) | High (PVC is waterproof) |
| Chemical resistance | Moderate (alkaline cleaners damage surface over time) | High |
| Temperature resistance | Good | Moderate (PVC softens above ~50°C) |
| Thickness | 2.0–4.0mm | 1.5–6.0mm |
| Colour through depth | Yes — full depth colour | No — only wear layer surface |

### 3.2 Maintenance Requirements — The Historic Downfall

Linoleum's decline was partly driven by its maintenance requirements compared to early vinyl:

**Traditional linoleum maintenance:**
- Required regular waxing or sealing with linseed oil-based polish — the surface oxidation would eventually make the surface dry, chalky, and prone to scuffing without periodic replenishment
- The polish built up over time and required periodic stripping
- The old yellow "linoleum polish" smell was a fixture of post-war British schools and hospitals
- Required professional maintenance in commercial applications

**Modern linoleum maintenance (Marmoleum and equivalents):**
- Modern linoleum is factory-treated with a TopShield (Forbo) or equivalent acrylic/PU surface treatment that significantly reduces maintenance requirements
- No waxing required with modern surface treatment
- Damp-mop with pH-neutral or slightly alkaline cleaner
- **pH sensitivity:** Linoleum is damaged by strongly alkaline cleaners (pH >10–11) and strongly acidic cleaners (pH <4) — neutral to mildly alkaline cleaning agents only
- Re-sealing every 2–3 years in commercial applications prolongs appearance and performance
- The maintenance story is dramatically better than historical linoleum — but still requires more care than vinyl

### 3.3 Modern Applications — The Linoleum Renaissance

Linoleum is experiencing a genuine commercial revival driven by:

**Sustainability credentials:**
- Naturally renewable raw materials (linseed oil, cork, wood flour, jute) — all from annually renewable or managed sources
- Biodegradable at end of life — linoleum can be composted or landfilled without persistent pollution
- Very low embodied carbon compared to PVC
- CO₂-negative linseed oil production (the growing flax sequesters CO₂)
- Forbo Marmoleum holds Global GreenTag certification; is referenced in BREEAM and LEED specification guides
- The most sustainable hard floor covering available for commercial specification

**Healthcare specification revival:**
- Forbo Marmoleum Acoustic and similar products specified in European hospital new-builds
- Both the bacteriostatic property and the sustainability credentials drive specification
- NHS sustainability targets increasingly favour natural materials

**Education:**
- Schools specifying Marmoleum as part of broader sustainability commitments
- Good acoustic performance; hard-wearing; long lifespan (20–40 years in commercial use)
- Warm, tactile surface compared to vinyl — considered beneficial in school environments

**Residential revival:**
- Small but growing residential segment among sustainability-conscious homeowners
- Kitchen and bathroom (with correct sealing — see caveat)
- Aesthetic: the "organic" mottled appearance of marbled or jasper linoleum suits contemporary natural interiors
- Available in a wide range of colours; Forbo Marmoleum range: 300+ colours

**Key brands currently producing genuine linoleum:**
- **Forbo Marmoleum:** The dominant global brand; the word "Marmoleum" has become almost as generic as "Hoover" for the product; made in Assendelft (Netherlands) and Kirkcaldy (Scotland — the original Nairn factory site)
- **Armstrong / AFI Resilient:** Marmorette and other commercial linoleum lines
- **Gerflor:** Some commercial linoleum products
- **Nairn (historical):** The original Kirkcaldy manufacturer; the brand name lives on in heritage contexts

### 3.4 Linoleum in Bathrooms and Kitchens

**Can linoleum be used in bathrooms?**

This is a nuanced answer that the AI assistant must give accurately:

- Linoleum itself is not waterproof — the PVC surface is water-resistant but the jute backing absorbs water
- However: if properly installed with fully bonded adhesive and sealed edges (silicone bead at all wall junctions), the water cannot reach the backing in normal use
- Modern surface treatments (TopShield) also provide good surface water resistance
- **Suitable for:** Bathroom floors with normal splash use; kitchen floors; utility rooms
- **Not suitable for:** Wet rooms; shower cubicle floors; areas with regular ponding water or flooding risk
- **Key requirement:** Full-bond adhesive installation (no floating); sealed all junctions; do not allow water to sit

## 4. LINOLEUM — INSTALLATION

### 4.1 General Requirements

- Always full-bond glue-down — linoleum must not be floated; the dimensional movement of the product requires adhesive control
- The product must be acclimatised in the installation room: minimum 24 hours unrolled (or loosely rolled face-out) at room temperature
- Subfloor: flat to 2mm under 2m straight-edge; dry (≤75% RH concrete); clean; primed
- Linoleum can be installed directly over many existing floor coverings if they are flat, fully bonded, and free of contamination

### 4.2 Adhesive for Linoleum

- **Water-based contact/dispersion adhesive** specifically formulated for linoleum — do not use standard vinyl adhesive, which may be chemically incompatible with linseed oil compounds
- Forbo recommends their Eurobond adhesive for Marmoleum; other manufacturers supply product-matched adhesives
- Apply to subfloor with a notched trowel; allow to reach correct tack; lay linoleum; roll with 68kg roller
- Some installers apply adhesive to both the subfloor and the back of the linoleum (contact method) — follow manufacturer specification

### 4.3 Cutting Linoleum

- Linoleum cuts well with a sharp hooked knife or a linoleum knife (chisel-tip knife)
- Score and snap possible for straight cuts in thin (2mm) products; thicker products require scored and pulled cuts
- A straight edge is essential for clean cuts
- Linoleum is slightly brittle in cold conditions — bring to room temperature before cutting
- Pre-cut the material slightly oversize; trim to fit in situ

### 4.4 Seaming

- Use heat welding to seam linoleum in commercial applications (as per vinyl — see Section 14)
- Linoleum welding rod must be natural-material linoleum rod (not PVC) — specifically matched to the product
- Linoleum can also be seamed with cold seaming compound (linoleum adhesive seam sealer) in residential applications where a welded seam is not required
- In Marmoleum sheet: seams are nearly invisible due to the marbled pattern running through the full depth; the seam shows only as a very fine line

### 4.5 Linoleum Tiles — Installation

- Dry-lay from centre of room; set out as per vinyl tile
- Adhesive to subfloor; allow to tack; press tiles; roll
- Border cuts at perimeter
- Allow adhesive to cure (24–48 hours) before foot traffic
- Seal all joints with compatible linoleum joint sealer

## 5. VINYL SHEET — CONSTRUCTION TYPES

### 5.1 Heterogeneous Vinyl Sheet

The dominant residential vinyl sheet construction. Multiple layers with different compositions:

**Layer structure (from surface to back):**

**Wear layer:** Transparent PVC; typically 0.1–0.7mm; polyurethane treatment on surface
**Print/design layer:** Photographic print on PVC film carrier
**Intermediate layers:** Additional PVC layers for body and structure; may include glass fibre reinforcement
**Foam/cushion layer (in cushioned products):** Closed-cell PVC foam or PE foam; provides comfort and acoustic performance
**Backing layer:** PVC compound or felt/fleece; provides dimensional stability and adhesion surface

**Total thickness:** 1.5–6.0mm typical; budget products as thin as 1.0mm; premium acoustic products up to 6–7mm

**Width:** 2m standard; some products in 3m and 4m widths (wider widths reduce seam frequency in large rooms)

### 5.2 Cushioned Vinyl Sheet (Cushion-Back)

The most widely sold domestic vinyl sheet in the UK. The integrated foam layer provides:
- Softer underfoot feel (significant benefit in kitchens and bathrooms where people stand for extended periods)
- Better acoustic (impact sound reduction)
- Some thermal comfort

**Types of cushioning:**
- **Chemically foamed PVC:** Standard cushion construction; PVC compound blown into foam cell structure by chemical blowing agent during calendering; very common; cost-effective
- **Felt-backed vinyl:** Vinyl bonded to a felt (mineral fibre or polyester fibre) backing; provides dimensional stability and a soft feel; used in commercial applications
- **Fibreglass-reinforced cushion:** Glass fibre layer within the foam for dimensional stability; prevents the product from shrinking in warm conditions

### 5.3 Non-Cushioned (Compact) Vinyl Sheet

Solid PVC compound construction without foam layer:
- Firmer underfoot; more durable surface
- Better suited to commercial applications (foam compresses under heavy rolling loads)
- Dimensional stability superior to cushioned (less thermal movement without foam)
- Typically thinner (1.5–3.0mm) — used in commercial situations where a thin, robust product is required

### 5.4 Felt-Backed Vinyl Sheet

- Vinyl bonded to a woven or needled felt backing
- The felt provides dimensional stability (important — helps control the tendency of large vinyl sheets to move)
- Good for re-installation: the felt allows the product to be lifted more cleanly than a direct PVC-to-subfloor bond
- Slower to install (felt absorbs adhesive more than PVC); requires more adhesive
- Common in commercial renovation applications

## 6. HETEROGENEOUS VS HOMOGENEOUS VINYL

### 6.1 Heterogeneous

A multi-layer product where each layer has a different composition and function (as described in Section 5.1). The design and colour are confined to the print/design layer — they do not run through the full thickness. When the wear layer is consumed, the print layer beneath is exposed and then damaged.

**Standard residential and light commercial vinyl is heterogeneous.**

Properties:
- Wide design range (photographic print = unlimited design possibilities)
- Design is surface-only
- Easier to manufacture; more economical
- When worn, very obvious degradation pattern

### 6.2 Homogeneous

The entire product is manufactured from the same compound — the colour and pattern run uniformly through the full thickness of the product. Cut the product in half and both faces look the same.

**Commercial heavy-duty vinyl is typically homogeneous.**

**How it is made:** Multiple layers of identically compounded PVC (with colour/pigment) are calendered and fused together under heat and pressure into a single monolithic product. The granulated pattern (typical in products like Tarkett iQ, Altro Stronghold) is achieved by using different colour PVC granules mixed and pressed together.

**Properties:**
- Colour and pattern run through full depth — even after years of heavy wear, the appearance remains consistent
- No "wear layer" concept as in heterogeneous — the entire thickness is usable material
- Typical commercial thickness: 2.0–3.0mm
- Excellent durability in heavy commercial, institutional, and industrial applications
- Must be heat-welded at seams for hygienic seamless installation
- Limited design range — the granulation/swirl patterns of homogeneous vinyl cannot replicate wood or stone effects as convincingly as heterogeneous print technology
- Standard for healthcare, education, and industrial flooring

**Key homogeneous vinyl products:**
- **Tarkett iQ Natural/Granit/Toro:** The benchmark homogeneous commercial range; excellent chemical resistance
- **Altro Stronghold / Altro Whiterock:** Safety vinyl with anti-slip aggregate
- **Forbo Eternal/Sphera:** Commercial homogeneous range
- **Armstrong Medintone:** Healthcare-focused homogeneous

## 7. VINYL COMPOSITION TILE (VCT)

### 7.1 What VCT Is

Vinyl Composition Tile (also called Vinyl Asbestos Tile in pre-1980s versions) is a heavy, dense tile product with high mineral filler loading:

**Composition:**
- PVC binder: 15–20% by weight
- Limestone/calcium carbonate filler: 65–80% by weight
- Plasticisers and stabilisers: remainder
- The very high filler loading makes VCT extremely hard, dimensionally stable, and inexpensive — but it also makes it relatively inflexible and brittle

**Construction:** Solid through-colour tile; no separate wear layer; the entire thickness is the usable material; colour runs through full depth

**Typical size:** 305×305mm (12"×12"), 457×457mm (18"×18")

**Typical thickness:** 3.2mm (1/8 inch — the traditional specification)

### 7.2 Properties

- Very hard surface — highly resistant to indentation (important in warehouses and retail with heavy loads)
- Dimensionally stable — very low thermal and hygroscopic movement
- Good chemical resistance (high mineral content)
- **Requires regular polishing/waxing maintenance:** VCT has a very low inherent sheen; without a polish/floor finish maintenance programme, the surface quickly looks dull and scuffed
- Heavy: the high calcium carbonate loading makes VCT the heaviest vinyl product by far
- Brittle: will crack if subfloor has significant flex or movement
- No cushioning — hard underfoot; poor acoustic
- Does not need heat welding at joints — the hard, dense tile sits tightly against adjacent tiles; dimensional stability is sufficient

### 7.3 VCT vs LVT — Common Confusion

VCT is sometimes confused with LVT in the trade. Key distinctions:
- VCT: very high filler; rigid; requires polishing maintenance; no wear layer in conventional sense; older product
- LVT: PVC-dominant; wear layer system; no polishing required; modern product
- In the UK market, VCT has largely been replaced by LVT and homogeneous vinyl for commercial applications; it remains more prevalent in the North American market

### 7.4 Asbestos in VCT — Critical Safety Warning

**This is the single most important safety issue in this lexicon.**

- Vinyl Asbestos Tile (VAT) was produced from the 1930s through to approximately 1985 in many countries, and in some markets until the early 1990s
- These tiles contain chrysotile (white asbestos) fibres — typically 5–30% by weight
- The adhesive used to install these tiles frequently also contains asbestos
- VAT is still present in millions of UK buildings — schools, hospitals, offices, factories, and homes built or refurbished before 1985

**Visual identification:**
- 9"×9" (229×229mm) or 12"×12" (305×305mm) size is typical of the asbestos-era tile
- Often black, dark grey, or marbled appearance; sometimes cream, buff, or green
- Very hard, brittle; cracks under impact

**Legal obligations:**
- Control of Asbestos Regulations 2012 (UK): it is illegal to disturb asbestos-containing materials without proper risk assessment and, for notifiable work, licensed contractor involvement
- Health and Safety Executive (HSE) guidance: if in doubt — do not disturb; commission a survey
- The standard approved approach for VAT: encapsulate in situ by overlaying with SLC and new flooring; do not remove unless absolutely necessary
- Any cutting, grinding, or removal of suspected VAT must only be done with proper controls

**What to do as a flooring advisor:**
- If the existing floor looks like pre-1985 vinyl tile and the customer wants to replace or overlay it: advise them to commission an asbestos survey first
- Never encourage removal without proper assessment
- Document the advice given

## 8. SAFETY / ANTI-SLIP VINYL FLOORING

### 8.1 What Safety Flooring Is

Safety flooring is a category of vinyl (or linoleum) flooring specifically engineered to maintain a minimum coefficient of friction (slip resistance) even when wet, contaminated with oil, or subjected to cleaning chemicals. It is not simply "textured vinyl" — the anti-slip property must be verified by standardised testing.

### 8.2 Anti-Slip Mechanisms

**Aggregate incorporation:**
- The most effective method
- Carborundum (silicon carbide), aluminium oxide, or quartz particles incorporated into the wear layer during manufacture
- The aggregate protrudes slightly above the surface, providing a mechanical key against the sole of footwear
- The aggregate is within the wear layer — it does not wash off or wear away quickly
- Even as the surface wears, fresh aggregate is exposed — the anti-slip property is consistent throughout the wear layer

**Surface profile/texture:**
- Raised surface profiles (studs, ribs, diamond patterns) provide mechanical friction
- Less durable than aggregate — profiles can wear down or be filled with soiling
- Used in lower-specification anti-slip products

**Chemical surface treatment:**
- Topical anti-slip treatments (applied after installation)
- Temporary — requires periodic re-application; not a substitute for aggregate anti-slip in demanding environments

### 8.3 Slip Resistance Testing Standards

**HSE Guidelines and Pendulum Test:**
- The UK's primary wet slip resistance assessment method
- Uses a pendulum with a standardised rubber slider dragged across the surface
- Result expressed as PTV (Pendulum Test Value): ≥36 PTV = low slip risk (UK guidance)
- The Pendulum test is the method used in post-accident legal investigations in the UK

**DIN 51130 (R-value) — Workplaces:**
- German standard; widely specified in UK commercial work
- A person walks across an inclined ramp covered with test oil; the minimum angle at which slipping occurs determines the R-class
- R9: 3–10° inclination; minimum for most commercial spaces
- R10: 10–19°; recommended for wet areas in commercial use
- R11: 19–27°; for commercial kitchens, wet production areas
- R12: 27–35°; highly contaminated industrial environments
- R13: >35°; exceptional slip risk (e.g., slaughter houses, fish processing)

**DIN 51097 (A/B/C) — Barefoot wet areas:**
- For areas where people walk barefoot in wet conditions: swimming pool surrounds, changing rooms, spa/wet leisure
- Class A: minimum (flat surfaces, normal wet); changing room floors
- Class B: inclined surfaces; pool surrounds; shower areas
- Class C: highly inclined wet surfaces; pool steps; ramps into pool

**EN 13893:**
- Dynamic coefficient of friction test (dry); minimum 0.30μ for general use
- Less relevant than Pendulum for wet slip risk assessment

### 8.4 Key Safety Vinyl Products

**Altro flooring (Altro Ltd., UK):**
- The dominant UK safety vinyl brand; original safety flooring innovator
- **Altro Classic 25/30/40:** Residential and light commercial safety vinyl; carborundum aggregate
- **Altro Safety 25/30:** The original commercial safety range; widely specified
- **Altro Stronghold 30:** Heavy commercial; carborundum aggregate; extremely durable wear layer
- **Altro Aquarius:** Specifically for wet barefoot areas; DIN 51097 class B and C products
- **Altro Whiterock:** For wet walls (kitchen, food production, healthcare); not flooring but from the same system

**Tarkett safety:**
- Tarkett Safetred Universal, Safetred Aqua
- Homogeneous construction with quartz aggregate

**Forbo:**
- Forbo Eternal Safety, Surestep series
- Various aggregate loadings for different slip risk environments

**Note on safety vinyl aesthetics:**
Older safety vinyl products had a very obvious industrial appearance (grey/black aggregate visible). Modern products integrate the aggregate much more subtly — Altro Ensemble, Tarkett iQ Natural Safety, and equivalent products offer realistic wood and stone effects with full anti-slip certification. The days when safety vinyl meant obviously industrial-looking flooring are largely over.

## 9. ACOUSTIC AND COMFORT VINYL

### 9.1 Acoustic Vinyl

As a distinct commercial category, acoustic vinyl products are engineered to reduce impact sound transmission significantly beyond standard vinyl products:

**Construction:**
- Heavy PVC backing compound: increased mass reduces sound transmission
- Foam or mineral-loaded acoustic layer
- Total thickness: typically 4–6mm
- Predominantly glue-down commercial products with attached heavy backing

**Performance:**
- ΔLw values: typically 14–22 dB for dedicated acoustic vinyl products
- Some premium acoustic products (Forbo Tessera vinyl, Altro Acoustic Air) achieve ΔLw 22–24 dB

**Applications:**
- Apartment buildings where impact sound compliance is challenging
- Hospital wards (quieter patient environments)
- Hotels — reducing footfall noise between floors
- Schools and office buildings

### 9.2 Comfort Vinyl (Cushion-Back Premium)

The residential cushion-back market has developed comfort-focused products where the cushioning is a primary feature:

- Thick (5–8mm total) cushioned products
- Soft, warm underfoot; thermal comfort in kitchens and bathrooms
- Anti-fatigue benefit for people who stand for extended periods
- Limited durability advantage over commercial vinyl — the foam backing is the limiting factor (can compress permanently under heavy point loads)

## 11. SUBFLOOR REQUIREMENTS

### 11.1 Critical Importance — Vinyl Telegraphs Everything

Vinyl sheet (and linoleum) is the most demanding of all floor coverings for subfloor preparation. The flexible nature of these products means that any subfloor imperfection — ridge, hollow, crack, or raised nail head — will be visible and tactile through the floor surface within days to weeks of installation.

The technical term for this is **"telegraphing"** — the subfloor defect "telegraphs" its impression through the floor covering.

**Subfloor flatness tolerance for vinyl sheet and linoleum:**
- **Maximum 2mm deviation under a 2m straight-edge** — stricter than wood or tile
- Maximum 1mm under a 600mm straight-edge (checking short-range undulation)
- No raised joints, ridges, or protruding fasteners above 0.5mm

### 11.2 Specific Subfloor Issues

**Embossed existing vinyl or VCT:**
- The embossed surface of existing vinyl (grain patterns) will telegraph through new vinyl
- Options: remove existing vinyl; apply embossing leveller (a flexible filler specifically designed to flatten embossed surfaces before new vinyl installation); or apply full SLC if the embossing is deep
- Embossing leveller: typically a flexible acrylic compound; troweled thin over the entire surface; feathers to zero at edges; dries hard and flat

**Grout lines of existing ceramic tile:**
- Lines over 2mm width: must be filled with SLC
- Even filled grout lines may telegraph over time as the filler cures and shrinks

**Plywood subfloor:**
- All sheet joints must be filled and feathered flush with SLC
- All screwheads must be countersunk below the surface
- Knot holes and grain irregularities: fill and sand flush

**Concrete — key requirements:**
- Grind laitance off new screeds before any other preparation
- Fill all cracks and holes with SLC; feather to zero
- New cement screeds: minimum 28 days; test to ≤75% RH
- Anhydrite screeds: sand; prime with dedicated anhydrite primer; then apply SLC

### 11.3 Primers

Priming is often required before adhesive application:
- **On concrete:** Primer seals porous surface; stabilises laitance; provides consistent adhesive absorption
- **On non-porous surfaces (existing glazed tile, polished concrete):** Adhesion promoter primer creates mechanical/chemical key for adhesive
- **On anhydrite screed:** Dedicated anhydrite primer essential before SLC or adhesive
- **On wood/plywood:** Primer reduces porosity; prevents adhesive over-absorption
- Allow primer to dry fully before applying adhesive; follow manufacturer guidance on recoat time

## 12. INSTALLATION — VINYL SHEET

### 12.1 Full Bond (Glue-Down)

The professional standard installation method for vinyl sheet in commercial applications and recommended for all but the smallest domestic spaces.

**Process:**
1. Prepare subfloor to 2mm/2m; prime as required
2. Pre-cut vinyl sheet: lay loosely in room first; trim to approximate fit leaving 75mm excess at all walls
3. Fold back half the sheet; apply adhesive to subfloor with notched trowel; allow to reach tack
4. Carefully lower the folded half into position; press firmly; roll with 68kg roller
5. Fold back remaining half; apply adhesive; lower; roll
6. Trim edges to final fit using a scribing tool or by pressing against the wall and cutting along the fold line
7. Apply silicone bead or trim at all wall junctions
8. Re-roll entire floor; ensure no air pockets or ridges

**Adhesive selection:**
- Same as for LVT (Lexicon 05, Section 12.1): PSA tackifier (light commercial/residential); full-bond acrylic dispersion (heavy commercial)
- For cushioned vinyl: use adhesive specifically rated for cushioned products — some adhesives interact adversely with foam
- For felt-backed: dedicated felt-backed vinyl adhesive; some systems use contact adhesive on felt backing

### 12.2 Loose Lay (Domestic)

For domestic kitchens and bathrooms where the room is small and the vinyl is light gauge:
- Trim accurately to fit; perimeter is held by the skirting board or beading
- No adhesive in field; double-sided tape under seams if applicable
- Suitable for: rooms under approximately 10m² with no complex shapes; lightweight domestic vinyl
- Not suitable for: any commercial application; rooms with heavy rolling loads; bathrooms with potential moisture ingress to subfloor

**The problem with loose lay domestic vinyl:**
- The vinyl is not secured; it can move, wrinkle, and ride up at edges over time
- Hot weather causes expansion; if not secured, the sheet may buckle
- Water can ingress under the sheet via any gap at the perimeter — particularly a concern in bathrooms and kitchens
- For any installation expected to last more than 2–3 years, partial or full bond is strongly recommended

### 12.3 Perimeter Bond

A compromise between loose lay and full bond:
- Adhesive applied only to the perimeter and under any seams
- Field is free
- More stable than pure loose lay; less disruptive than full bond if removal is needed later
- Suitable for light domestic use in smaller rooms

### 12.4 Scribing to Irregular Walls

A critical skill for vinyl sheet installation:

**Scribing method:**
1. Place the sheet in position with one edge against the straightest wall (or the longest wall)
2. The opposite edges will not fit precisely against irregular or out-of-square walls
3. Use a scribing tool (a small block of wood with a pencil fixed to the edge) or a dedicated scribing compass
4. Run the scribing tool along the wall profile; the pencil traces the exact wall profile onto the vinyl below
5. Cut along the scribed line; the sheet now fits the wall exactly
6. Repeat at all irregular edges

**Template method (for complex shapes — bay windows, curved walls, toilet bases):**
1. Create a paper or card template of the irregular area
2. Transfer to vinyl and cut
3. Test fit; trim as required

### 12.5 Cutting Around Obstacles

- **Toilet base:** Measure position precisely; create paper template around base; transfer to vinyl; cut; test fit
- **Bath panel:** Cut to shape; the vinyl runs under the bath panel if possible (allows future removal without disturbing the bath)
- **Door casings:** Undercut or scribe; flexible vinyl can be eased under a slightly undercut casing

## 14. HEAT WELDING — FULL DETAIL

Heat welding is the most important technical skill specific to commercial vinyl and linoleum installation. A complete technical breakdown:

### 14.1 Purpose

Heat welding creates a permanently fused, seamless joint between vinyl or linoleum sheets/tiles, eliminating:
- Hygienic risk from bacteria harbouring in joints
- Water ingress into and through joints
- Visual seam lines in commercial floors
- Joint failure from heavy traffic or cleaning machine impact

**When heat welding is required:**
- All commercial healthcare installations
- All food preparation areas
- All areas with wet cleaning regimes (hygienic mopping, pressure washing)
- Commercial kitchens
- Any installation where a hygiene certificate is required
- Swimming pool surrounds and wet leisure

### 14.2 Tools

**Hot-air welding gun:**
- Electric gun with adjustable temperature; 200–600°C range
- Fitted with a speed nozzle (a foot-shaped nozzle that guides the welding rod into the groove while delivering hot air)
- Temperature setting: approximately 350–400°C for PVC welding rod; approximately 250–300°C for linoleum rod (lower — natural material is more temperature-sensitive)
- Temperature consistency is critical — too hot: rod degrades and chars; too cool: rod does not fuse fully, joint is weak

**Groove cutter:**
- A hand tool or foot attachment for the welding gun
- Creates a V-shaped groove along the joint between tiles/sheets: typically 2mm wide × 2mm deep
- The groove must be clean, consistent, and centred on the joint
- Without the groove, the welding rod cannot penetrate and fuse into the joint — it sits on the surface and the joint is not welded

**Welding rod:**
- Round PVC rod: 4mm diameter standard
- Must exactly match the floor product — same PVC compound, same colour match, same manufacturer where possible
- Available in matching colours for most product ranges; some products have proprietary rods
- Linoleum welding rod: natural compound; visually matches the linoleum surface

**Trimming knife (double-quarter knife):**
- A two-stage trimming tool: the "half-moon" position trims the rod above the surface on the first pass (while still hot); the straight blade trims flush on the second pass (after cooling)
- Critical technique: the first trim must be done while the rod is still warm and soft — a clean near-flush trim; the second trim is done cold when the rod is fully hardened and can be cut cleanly flush without pulling

### 14.3 Step-by-Step Welding Process

1. **Allow adhesive to cure fully:** Minimum 24 hours after laying; the floor must be fully bonded and flat before welding

2. **Groove the joints:** Using the groove cutter, run along every joint to be welded; the groove must be:
   - Centred on the joint
   - Consistent depth (approx 2mm — not touching subfloor)
   - Clean (free of adhesive, dust, or contamination)
   - Do not groove to the wall edges — leave 50mm at each end; weld will terminate short of the wall

3. **Preheat the welding gun:** Bring to operating temperature; test on a scrap piece first

4. **Weld:** Feed the welding rod continuously through the speed nozzle while moving the gun steadily along the groove at a consistent pace (approximately 1m per 30–40 seconds for PVC; slower for linoleum)
   - Maintain consistent pressure on the rod; avoid stopping (stopping = overheating at that point)
   - The rod should fuse and become one with the floor material at the groove edges
   - The fused weld should slightly crown above the surface (this will be trimmed)

5. **First trim (hot):** Using the half-moon position of the trimming knife while the rod is still warm; trim to approximately 1mm above the surface

6. **Allow to cool:** Do not attempt second trim until the weld is fully cool (minimum 15–20 minutes; do not rush)

7. **Second trim (cold):** Using the straight blade of the trimming knife; pare flush with the floor surface; a smooth, clean, level trim

8. **Inspect:** The weld should be invisible from 1m distance in consistent lighting; the surface should be flush and smooth

### 14.4 Common Welding Defects

| Defect | Cause | Result |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Porous weld (bubbles in rod) | Temperature too high; damp rod | Joint failure; visible holes |
| Cold weld (rod not fused) | Temperature too low; moving too fast | Weld cracks and falls out |
| Charred weld (brown/black) | Temperature too high | Aesthetically poor; material degraded |
| Dragged weld | First trim too cold | Rod pulled away from groove; joint exposed |
| Crowned weld (raised above surface) | Second trim not flush | Visible ridge; trip hazard in commercial |
| Rod colour mismatch | Wrong rod used | Visually obvious seam |

### 14.5 Cold Welding

An alternative to heat welding for non-commercial applications:

- A chemical solvent in a syringe is applied along the joint between two vinyl sheets
- The solvent partially dissolves both edges; they fuse chemically as the solvent evaporates
- Much easier than heat welding; no special tools
- Results are less durable than heat welding and not hygienic/seamless in the same sense
- Used in domestic bathrooms and kitchens where a neat seam is desired but commercial hygiene standards are not required
- Not appropriate in any healthcare, food preparation, or wet commercial environment

## 15. COVED SKIRTINGS

### 15.1 What a Coved Skirting Is

A coved skirting (also called a coved upstand or flash coving) is a continuous radiused transition from the floor surface to the wall surface, eliminating the floor-to-wall junction. Instead of the floor ending at a right angle against the wall (creating a harbour for dirt and bacteria), the vinyl curves up the wall to a height of typically 100–150mm.

### 15.2 Where Coved Skirtings Are Required

- All food preparation and catering environments (environmental health enforcement)
- All clinical healthcare areas (NHS Estates guidance)
- Pharmaceutical manufacturing
- Any space with hygienic floor-washing regimes where the junction must be sealed
- Laboratories and clean rooms

### 15.3 Installation Methods

**Method 1 — Integral cove (sheet vinyl coving):**
- The sheet vinyl itself is cut, heated, and formed around a purpose-made cove former (a PVC quadrant section fixed at the wall/floor junction)
- The vinyl is softened with a heat gun; pressed around the former; adhered to both floor and wall
- Best result: seamless, same-material cove with no junction
- Requires skill to form without creasing or buckling the vinyl at internal corners

**Method 2 — Pre-formed cove section:**
- A separate extruded vinyl cove section (matching or contrasting colour) is adhered to the wall
- Easier to install; less skill required
- A junction exists between the floor vinyl and the cove section — this junction must be sealed (cold welding compound or heat weld if the materials are compatible)

**Internal and external corners:**
- Internal corners (room corners): the coving must be mitre-cut and joined; the joint heat-welded or cold-welded
- External corners (around columns, doorways): template-cut and heat-formed; skilled work

**Cove former height and radius:**
- Standard cove former: 25mm radius; 100mm upstand height
- Healthcare specification often requires 50mm radius for easier cleaning

## 16. SEAMING AND PATTERN MATCHING

### 16.1 Seam Placement Principles

As with carpet (Lexicon 01), seam placement in vinyl and linoleum is critical:

- **Minimise seams:** Use the widest available roll width (2m or 4m) to minimise the number of seams
- **Place seams away from primary traffic direction:** A seam running across the direction of foot traffic will be walked over end-on; placing the seam parallel to traffic reduces direct stress on it
- **Avoid seams under doors:** The hinge point of a door creates vertical stress at that point
- **Place seams in the lowest-traffic locations possible**
- **Seam alignment with subfloor joints:** Never place a vinyl seam over a subfloor joint — the subfloor joint creates a differential movement point; the vinyl seam above will open

### 16.2 Pattern Matching

Many vinyl and linoleum products have a pattern repeat — a regular design element that must be aligned across sheet widths:

- **Pattern repeat:** The interval at which the design repeats; stated in the product specification
- **Drop match:** Where the pattern on adjacent sheets is offset; the amount of drop is stated (e.g., 300mm drop means each sheet must advance 300mm before the pattern aligns)
- **Straight match:** Pattern aligns directly across adjacent sheets; no advance required; simpler calculation

**Material calculation for patterned products:**
> Total roll length required = (room length + pattern repeat) × number of drops (widths)
- Or per manufacturer's waste factor table

## 17. UNDERFLOOR HEATING COMPATIBILITY

### 17.1 Vinyl Sheet Over UFH

Vinyl sheet is highly compatible with UFH:
- Very low thermal resistance: the floor covering component of the system
- All vinyl products: R-value typically 0.01–0.04 m²K/W at normal thicknesses
- Maximum surface temperature: **27°C** (same as all resilient floor coverings)
- Full bond adhesive essential over UFH — floating or loose-lay will create an air gap reducing UFH efficiency; risk of localised overheating where the floor is in contact vs bridging a hollow

**UFH-specific adhesive:** As per LVT (Lexicon 05, Section 14.3) — use heat-resistant full-bond adhesive for any installation where surface temperature regularly reaches 25°C+

### 17.2 Linoleum Over UFH

Linoleum is suitable over UFH:
- Lower thermal resistance than vinyl due to cork content (better thermal conductivity than pure PVC foam)
- Marmoleum over UFH is approved by Forbo for warm-water hydronic systems
- Maximum surface temperature 27°C applies
- Full bond adhesive essential
- The UFH commissioning protocol (switch off 48 hours before installation; ramp up slowly after) applies

### 17.3 VCT Over UFH

VCT is suitable over UFH with important caveats:
- The very high mineral content gives VCT excellent thermal conductivity — it is an efficient thermal transmitter
- However: VCT is brittle; thermal cycling (repeated expansion and contraction with UFH switching) can cause cracking over time
- Full bond adhesive essential
- If VCT is specified over UFH, prefer a constant-temperature UFH system (not one that switches fully off at night) to minimise thermal cycling stress

## 18. PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 18.1 Bubbling / Blistering

**Symptoms:** Raised bubbles or blisters in the sheet vinyl surface; may feel hollow underfoot; can appear within days or years of installation.

**Causes:**
1. **Adhesive failure / inadequate rolling** — the dominant cause; sections of the sheet were not rolled or had insufficient adhesive; air pockets formed; with warmth and foot traffic the sheet lifts
2. **Subfloor moisture** — moisture vapour rising through the subfloor condenses between the adhesive and the floor covering; adhesive softens and releases; vinyl lifts; typically appears first at sheet edges
3. **Incompatible adhesive with cushioned vinyl** — some adhesives react with the foam backing, causing it to expand; the vinyl lifts as the foam expands
4. **Residual moisture in adhesive** — water-based adhesive not dried to tack before laying; steam formed as warm adhesive contacted the floor surface
5. **Solvent in adhesive** — older solvent-based adhesives or primers can dissolve the PVC compound from below; the softened PVC lifts

**Diagnosis:**
- Press on blister: does it feel like air? (air pocket / adhesive failure) or solid? (foam expansion)
- Check moisture: lift edge of vinyl and test subfloor RH
- Review installation record: was adhesive allowed to reach correct tack?

### 18.2 Edge Lifting

**Symptoms:** Sheet vinyl lifting at edges, seams, or corners; most common at the perimeter and under heavy furniture.

**Causes:**
1. **Insufficient adhesive at edges** — the most common; edges are harder to roll and press; adhesive coverage is thinner at the edge
2. **Moisture ingress at perimeter** — water getting under the edge (particularly in bathrooms) and softening adhesive
3. **Thermal movement without full bond** — perimeter-bonded or loose-lay vinyl expanding in warm weather; the free centre pushes outward and lifts the edges
4. **Furniture feet without felt pads** — point loading pulling the vinyl upward when furniture is moved

**Action:**
- Re-bond with compatible vinyl adhesive; press and weight until cured
- For persistent edge lifting: investigate moisture source; consider full bond reinstallation

### 18.3 Telegraphing

**Symptoms:** Subfloor irregularities visible and tactile through the vinyl surface; ridges, board edges, nail heads, or join lines apparent through the floor covering.

**Causes:**
- Inadequate subfloor preparation — the definitive diagnosis; no other explanation is possible
- The defect was there before installation; the vinyl faithfully reproduced it

**Action:**
- In mild cases: SLC skim applied after peeling back the vinyl (if the vinyl can be lifted cleanly without damage); re-bond
- In severe cases: complete removal, subfloor correction, and reinstallation
- Prevention is the only real answer — correct preparation eliminates telegraphing; no post-installation fix is fully satisfactory

### 18.4 Linoleum Yellowing (Buttering)

As described in Section 2.4 — thoroughly brief the customer that:
- This is normal chemistry, not a defect
- It reverses with exposure to light
- It will happen again in any covered area
- It is most visible on pale colours

### 18.5 Surface Scuffing and Black Marks

**Symptoms:** Black or grey scuff marks on vinyl surface; often from rubber shoes, chair leg rubber feet, or certain wheeled furniture.

**Causes:**
- Rubber transfer marks from shoe soles, rubber feet, or trolley wheels
- Particularly common on pale vinyl surfaces
- Rubber compounds react with PVC (as noted in Lexicon 05 for LVT) — the reaction can be surface transfer or deeper discolouration

**Cleaning:** Mineral spirits or isopropyl alcohol on a cloth; rub gently; for deep stains, a specialist vinyl cleaner
**Prevention:** Use felt gliders under rubber-footed furniture; specify chair mats where castor chairs are used

### 18.6 Cracking and Splitting (VCT and Linoleum)

**Symptoms:** Visible cracks running through VCT tiles or linoleum sheet.

**Causes:**
1. **Subfloor movement** — a crack or joint in the subfloor; the rigid VCT or linoleum follows
2. **Thermal cycling** — particularly VCT over UFH; repeated expansion/contraction
3. **Impact damage** — heavy dropped item; linoleum and VCT are brittle under impact
4. **Cold installation** — linoleum installed in very cold conditions; the product becomes brittle and cracks during fitting

## 19. MAINTENANCE AND CARE

### 19.1 Vinyl Sheet — Routine Maintenance

- Regular sweeping/dust mopping — remove grit before it scratches the surface
- Damp mop with pH-neutral cleaner; well-wrung mop; no standing water
- Clean spills immediately
- No abrasive cleaners; no solvent-based cleaners (soften PVC)
- For cushioned residential vinyl: avoid steam cleaners — the heat can delaminate the foam layer

### 19.2 Commercial Vinyl — Maintenance Programme

**Daily:**
- Dry sweep or auto-scrubber with clean water
- Spot-clean spills

**Weekly:**
- Machine scrubbing with floor cleaner (appropriate dilution per manufacturer); low-speed pad
- Rinse with clean water

**Periodic (quarterly to annually):**
- Strip old maintenance floor finish (polish) with appropriate stripping agent
- Deep clean
- Re-apply maintenance floor finish (for products requiring polish)
- Re-apply surface seal for homogeneous vinyl

**Floor finish for commercial vinyl:**
- Acrylic floor polish/emulsion: applied in thin coats; provides protective sacrificial layer; buffable to high gloss
- Products: Johnson Wax, Diversey Carefree, manufacturer-specific finishes
- Commercial homogeneous vinyl does not require floor finish in the same way as VCT; the manufacturer's maintenance coat is applied periodically

### 19.3 Linoleum — Maintenance

- pH-neutral or very mildly alkaline cleaner only (pH 7–9); no strongly alkaline products
- Forbo Marmoleum: clean with Forbo Refresh Concentrate or equivalent linoleum-specific cleaner
- Do not use bleach or ammonia-based cleaners — will damage the surface
- Periodic re-sealing with Forbo Topshield or equivalent linoleum seal extends appearance retention
- Machine scrubbing with neutral pad periodically for commercial linoleum
- Waxing: traditional wax polish is rarely needed with modern surface-treated linoleum but is used for heritage restoration

### 19.4 Safety Vinyl Maintenance

**Critical point for safety vinyl:** Certain cleaning products can fill the anti-slip aggregate, reducing its effectiveness:
- Floor polish and wax: fills the aggregate pockets; reduces slip resistance; must NOT be used on safety vinyl
- Heavy soil build-up: similarly reduces slip resistance by filling aggregate texture
- Safety vinyl must be cleaned regularly and vigorously to maintain anti-slip performance
- Periodic deep cleaning (machine scrubbing with stiff brush pad) is required to maintain aggregate exposure
- Pendulum test should be repeated periodically in commercial safety-flooring installations to verify continued compliance

## 22. SUSTAINABILITY AND ENVIRONMENTAL CONSIDERATIONS

### 22.1 Linoleum — The Most Sustainable Hard Floor Covering

**Material credentials:**
- Linseed oil: from annually harvested flax; the oil is a byproduct of linseed production for food and animal feed
- Cork: harvested from the bark of the cork oak every 9 years without felling the tree
- Wood flour: sawmill waste; no additional tree harvesting
- Jute: rapidly renewable natural fibre; low pesticide agricultural crop
- Limestone: abundant mineral; low-impact extraction relative to other minerals
- Pigments: mineral-based; increasingly non-heavy-metal

**End-of-life:**
- Fully biodegradable; breaks down in composting or landfill without persistent pollution
- Can be incinerated with fully natural combustion products (CO₂, H₂O, minimal residue)
- Forbo Marmoleum is classified as biomass fuel in some European waste frameworks

**Carbon footprint:**
- The growing flax captures CO₂
- The linseed oil production process converts atmospheric CO₂ into the polymerised matrix
- Forbo reports Marmoleum as **carbon-negative** across its lifecycle when biogenic carbon is accounted for (the carbon stored in the product exceeds the carbon emitted in its production)

**Certifications:**
- Global GreenTag GreenRate Level A: the highest level; covers material health, social equity, and ecological performance
- BREEAM and LEED credit-eligible as a sustainable material
- Phthalate-free (naturally — linoleum contains no PVC or plasticisers)

### 22.2 Vinyl Sheet — Environmental Position

As per LVT (Lexicon 05, Section 23):
- PVC-based; petroleum-derived; significant chlorine content
- Phthalate plasticiser restriction under REACH; modern products use DOTP/DINCH
- Recofloor UK: industry take-back scheme for vinyl offcuts and end-of-life products
- Some manufacturers offer product take-back (Tarkett ReStart programme)
- EPDs available from major manufacturers

### 22.3 VCT — Environmental Position

- High mineral content; lower PVC content than vinyl sheet
- Long lifespan (30–40 years with polish maintenance programme) is a sustainability strength
- Take-back programmes exist for commercial VCT in North America (Interface, Armstrong); UK less developed
- The polish maintenance programme adds chemical footprint over the product's life

## 23. TRADE TERMINOLOGY GLOSSARY

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Altro** | The leading UK safety vinyl brand; Altro Ltd., Letchworth, Hertfordshire |

| **BS 8203** | UK Code of Practice for installation of resilient floor coverings; the key installation standard |

| **DIN 51130** | German standard for workplace slip resistance; R9–R13 ramp test classification |
| **Drop match** | Pattern matching method where adjacent sheets must advance by the drop amount before pattern repeats |
| **Embossing leveller** | Flexible filler compound applied over embossed existing vinyl to create a flat surface before new installation |
| **EN 548** | European product standard for linoleum |
| **EN 649** | European product standard for PVC floor coverings (vinyl sheet and tile) |
| **Forbo** | Major flooring manufacturer; produces Marmoleum (dominant linoleum brand) and Eternal/Sphera commercial vinyl |
| **Groove cutter** | Tool creating V-groove along vinyl joint for heat welding |
| **HACCP** | Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points; food safety management system; coved skirtings and sealed joints required |
| **Heat welding** | Fusing PVC/linoleum welding rod into grooved joints to create a seamless monolithic surface |
| **Heterogeneous** | Multi-layer vinyl construction; design confined to print layer; the standard for residential vinyl |
| **Homogeneous** | Single-compound vinyl construction; colour/pattern run through full depth; the standard for heavy commercial |
| **Lino / linoleum** | Correctly: a natural floor covering made from linseed oil, cork, wood flour, limestone, and jute; commonly misused to mean any vinyl sheet floor |
| **Linoxyn** | The oxidised linseed oil polymer that forms the matrix of linoleum |
| **Marmoleum** | Forbo's brand name for linoleum; now used quasi-generically in the trade |

## APPENDIX A: LINOLEUM vs VINYL — COMPLETE COMPARISON TABLE

| Property | Genuine Linoleum | Vinyl Sheet (PVC) |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Primary material | Linseed oil, cork, wood flour | PVC (petroleum-derived) |
| Biodegradable | Yes | No |
| Bacteriostatic | Yes (inherent chemistry) | No (without additives) |
| Waterproof | No (jute backing absorbs water) | Yes (PVC is waterproof) |
| Yellowing | Yes (reversible with light) | No (modern formulations) |
| pH sensitivity | Moderate alkaline; avoid strong alkalis/acids | More tolerant of cleaning chemicals |
| Slip resistance | Moderate (with TopShield seal) | Product-dependent |
| Design range | Limited (marbled, jasper, plain; now 300+ Marmoleum colours) | Unlimited (photographic print) |
| Through-colour | Yes | No (wear layer only) |
| UFH compatible | Yes (full bond) | Yes |
| Carbon footprint | Very low / carbon-negative | Medium-high |
| End-of-life | Biodegradable; compostable | Recyclable (limited) |
| Maintenance | More care required; pH-neutral cleaners | Less demanding |
| Cost | Premium (commercial); comparable (residential) | Budget to premium range |
| Lifespan | 20–40+ years (commercial) | 10–25 years (application-dependent) |
| Typical misidentification | Always called "lino" by public | Correctly identified as vinyl |
