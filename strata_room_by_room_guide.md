## 1. KITCHEN

### The Environment
Kitchens are the most demanding domestic room for flooring. The floor must simultaneously handle:
- Spills of acids (lemon, vinegar, wine), oils, and water
- Heavy foot traffic (the busiest room in most homes)
- Prolonged standing (anti-fatigue matters)
- Dropped items (weight of pots and heavy objects)
- Heat from appliances (near cooker and dishwasher)
- Domestic-grade cleaning chemicals

### Top Recommendations

**Best overall: Porcelain tile (large format, matt or textured)**
- Completely waterproof; stain-resistant; durable; easy to clean
- Anti-slip matt or micro-textured finish (not polished)
- 600×600mm or 600×300mm format is the kitchen sweet spot
- Total installed: £70–£150/m²
- Works with any UFH system

**Best for warmth and comfort: LVT (SPC, 0.55mm+ wear layer)**
- Genuinely waterproof; warmer underfoot than tile; softer on standing feet
- A very practical kitchen choice — combines the waterproofing of tile with the warmth of wood
- Wood-effect or stone-effect — wide aesthetic range
- Total installed: £40–£65/m²
- Excellent UFH compatibility (glue-down preferred over UFH)

**Best for natural aesthetic: Quartzite or granite (honed)**
- The only natural stones suitable for kitchens; acid-resistant (no calcium carbonate)
- Honed finish (not polished) for practical slip resistance
- Premium cost but exceptional longevity and resale value
- Total installed: £120–£200+/m²
- Excellent UFH heat conductor

**Best for sustainability-conscious customers: Linoleum (Marmoleum)**
- Naturally resistant to bacteria; easy to clean; carbon-negative lifecycle
- Glue-down sheet or click tile format
- Not as waterproof as tile or LVT — needs careful joint sealing
- Total installed: £40–£58/m²

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Marble or limestone | Acid from food and drink permanently etches the surface; irreversible |
| Solid wood | Moisture and spills will cause warping, cupping, and staining over time |
| Carpet | Impossible to keep hygienic; stains; harbours bacteria |
| Polished tile or polished stone | Slippery when wet; safety risk during cooking |
| Laminate (standard) | HDF core swells on water contact at joints; kitchen is the worst environment for it |

### Key Questions to Ask

1. "Do you have children or pets? This affects how important stain resistance and durability are."
2. "Do you tend to stand in the kitchen for long periods? Anti-fatigue matters more than people expect."
3. "What's the aesthetic you're going for — contemporary, farmhouse, traditional, minimalist?"
4. "Is underfloor heating installed or planned? This limits some options and opens others."
5. "What's your budget per m²? I can work across a wide range."

### If the Customer Has UFH

- Tile and stone: the best choice — thermally excellent; no limitations
- LVT: glue-down SPC; excellent; avoid WPC with thick foam backing (R-value concern)
- Linoleum: approved for UFH (glue-down; confirmed R-value from manufacturer)
- Avoid: thick cork; standard foam underlay under anything; solid wood wider than 90mm

### Budget Anchor (20m² kitchen)

| Option | Total installed |
|--------|----------------|
| SPC LVT (mid-range) | £800–£1,300 |
| Porcelain tile (mid-range) | £1,400–£1,700 |
| Quartzite/granite (premium) | £2,400–£4,000 |

### The Conversation

*"The kitchen is the room where getting the flooring wrong really costs you — either through damage, constant maintenance headaches, or just a floor that doesn't hold up. The three things that matter most here are: can it handle water and acid spills, is it safe underfoot when wet, and will it cope with heavy daily use. Polished marble looks beautiful but the first time someone drips lemon juice and doesn't notice, you'll get a permanent dull patch. If you love the stone look, quartzite gives you that aesthetic but it's acid-resistant. If you want something warmer underfoot and easier on the budget, a quality LVT is genuinely excellent in a kitchen."*

## 2. LIVING ROOM

### The Environment
The living room is a medium-to-heavy traffic dry room. The primary demands are:
- Aesthetic quality and warmth
- Comfort underfoot
- Durability for daily foot traffic and occasional furniture moving
- Acoustic comfort (hard floors can make rooms feel echoey)
- Good appearance retention over time

### Top Recommendations

**Best overall: Engineered wood (mid-range, 3.5–4mm wear layer)**
- The most popular premium living room choice; genuine wood warmth and character
- Oak herringbone or wide plank are the dominant contemporary choices
- Durable enough for family living rooms; can be refinished when worn
- Total installed: £65–£100/m²

**Best value: LVT (SPC, 0.55mm wear layer, wide plank)**
- Very good wood-effect aesthetics at significantly lower cost than engineered wood
- Durable; easy to maintain; suitable for households with pets
- Total installed: £40–£60/m²

**Best for comfort: Carpet (80/20 wool-nylon twist, Class 22–23)**
- The warmest, most comfortable underfoot choice
- Excellent acoustic performance (no echo)
- Twist pile is the practical choice: resists footprinting; hides wear
- Total installed: £45–£80/m²

**Best for luxury aesthetic: Natural stone or large-format porcelain**
- Marble, limestone, or premium stone-effect porcelain
- Statement aesthetic; pairs beautifully with UFH
- Requires acoustic treatment or area rugs to manage echoey acoustics
- Total installed: £90–£200+/m²

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Saxony carpet (light colours) | Pile reversal/watermarking is unavoidable; shows every footprint; requires constant maintenance |
| Polished marble | Scratches; stains; etchings from everyday living; too high maintenance unless the customer is committed |
| Budget laminate | Hollow underfoot feel; edge swell if any moisture; short lifespan |
| Very thin LVT (0.3mm WL) | Will not withstand normal daily wear in a busy living room |

### Key Questions

1. "Do you have pets — particularly dogs? This makes scratch resistance and easy cleaning more important."
2. "Do you prefer the warmth and softness of carpet, or a hard floor?"
3. "If hard floor — will you want area rugs? That affects how important the acoustic performance of the floor itself is."
4. "What's the style direction — traditional, contemporary, Scandinavian, farmhouse?"
5. "Do you have children under 5? Soft flooring has real safety benefits for young children."

### If the Customer Has UFH

- Hard floor (tile, stone, LVT, engineered wood): ideal pairing; the floor warming makes hard floors dramatically more comfortable
- Engineered wood: excellent over UFH; glue-down for best thermal efficiency
- Carpet: strongly discouraged over UFH; the thermal resistance negates the UFH benefit

### Budget Anchor (25m² living room)

| Option | Total installed |
|--------|----------------|
| Carpet (mid-range) | £900–£1,300 |
| LVT (mid-range) | £1,000–£1,500 |
| Engineered wood (mid-range) | £1,600–£2,500 |
| Natural stone / premium tile | £2,500–£5,000+ |

## 3. DINING ROOM

### The Environment
The dining room has specific demands that differ from the living room:
- Chair legs dragged repeatedly across the floor (the primary wear mechanism)
- Food and drink spills under the table
- Formal or semi-formal aesthetic in many homes
- Often used less frequently than living room (lower overall traffic)

### Top Recommendations

**Best overall: Engineered wood (mid-range)**
- The most popular dining room choice; formal enough for entertaining; durable enough for daily use
- A quality chair mat under the dining table protects the floor from the most intensive wear
- Total installed: £65–£100/m²

**Best for easy maintenance: Large format porcelain tile**
- Spills clean effortlessly; no staining risk; permanent durability
- The hard surface means the acoustic experience during a dinner party can be loud — area rug under the table helps
- Total installed: £70–£130/m²

**Best value: LVT (0.55mm wear layer)**
- Excellent scratch resistance from chairs; waterproof for spills; looks good
- Total installed: £40–£60/m²

**Best for formal aesthetic: Natural stone (honed limestone or marble)**
- If the customer understands and accepts the maintenance requirements for marble
- Honed limestone with appropriate sealing works well in a dining room (less acidic spill risk than kitchen)
- Total installed: £100–£200+/m²

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Polished hardwood (high gloss lacquer) | Chair legs will scratch continuously; very visible in formal light |
| Carpet under a dining table | Food debris is impossible to clean thoroughly from carpet pile; hygiene issue |
| Light-coloured soft carpet | Red wine, gravy, oil — a dining room carpet is a stain challenge |

### Key Question
"Will you be putting an area rug under the dining table?" — If yes, the floor under the rug barely matters aesthetically and practically; focus on what's visible around the edges.

## 4. MASTER BEDROOM

### The Environment
The bedroom is the lowest-demand room for floor durability:
- Very light traffic (mostly bare feet; slippers; pyjama-clad movement)
- No significant moisture risk
- No food or drink spills (in most homes)
- Comfort and luxury are the primary drivers
- Acoustic privacy (impact sound from above in apartments)

### Top Recommendations

**Best for comfort: Carpet (80/20 wool-nylon, mid-range, twist or saxony)**
- The definitive bedroom floor; warm, quiet, comfortable underfoot
- Saxony (deeper pile, softer) is perfectly appropriate in a bedroom — the footprinting limitation doesn't matter in low-traffic use
- Light colours work well (no significant stain risk)
- Total installed: £40–£70/m²

**Best for luxury: Engineered wood or solid wood with large area rug**
- The "hotel bedroom" aesthetic — wood floor with a soft rug beside the bed
- Combines hard floor durability with the softness underfoot where it matters (beside the bed)
- Total installed: £65–£120/m² for the floor; rug additional

**Best for allergy sufferers: Hard floor (LVT, engineered wood, or tile)**
- Carpet harbours dust mites; hard floors reduce the allergen load significantly
- If the customer or their partner has dust allergy or asthma, hard floor is a genuine health recommendation
- Total installed: £40–£100/m² depending on product

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Heavy commercial carpet (short loop) | Uncomfortable; cold; wrong aesthetic for a bedroom |
| Polished tile without underfloor heating | Cold on bare feet in the morning; the single biggest complaint |
| Very cheap laminate | The hollow sound underfoot at 2am is a surprisingly common complaint |

### Key Questions

1. "Do you or anyone in the household have allergies or asthma? This is important for the bedroom specifically."
2. "Do you like getting out of bed onto a soft surface, or are you happy with a hard floor and a rug?"
3. "Is this a ground floor or upper floor bedroom? Acoustic performance between floors matters if there are neighbours below."

## 5. CHILDREN'S BEDROOM

### The Environment
Children's bedrooms have very different demands from adult bedrooms:
- Play on the floor (Lego, toy cars, art projects)
- Spills (paint, juice, craft materials)
- More intensive use than an adult bedroom
- Safety (soft landings; no sharp edges; no slippery surfaces)
- Easy to clean; inevitable accidents

### Top Recommendations

**Best overall: Carpet (stain-resistant, mid-range)**
- Soft landings for play; warm; comfortable
- Specify a stain-resistant treatment (polypropylene fibre or treated nylon with stain protection)
- Loop pile or dense twist (resists the scraping of toys better than saxony)
- Total installed: £30–£55/m²

**Best for easy cleaning: LVT (SPC, click)**
- Paint and craft spills wipe off cleanly; easy to mop
- Add a large foam play mat for softness and warmth during floor play
- Total installed: £35–£55/m²

**Best for toddlers (under 5): Cushioned vinyl sheet**
- The softest fall surface of all hard floor coverings
- Budget-friendly; wipe-clean; warm enough underfoot
- Total installed: £25–£40/m²

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Polished tile or stone | Hard, cold, slippery — genuinely dangerous for young children who fall regularly |
| Pale carpet (cream, white) | Will be stained within weeks; unnecessary stress for the parents |
| Expensive hardwood | Not the right investment for a room that will likely be refloored when the child leaves |

### Key Question
"How old are the children? A toddler's floor needs are different from a 10-year-old's."

## 6. SPARE / GUEST BEDROOM

### The Environment
The guest bedroom is the lowest-demand room in the house:
- Occasional use; infrequent traffic
- No special performance requirements
- Aesthetic appearance matters (it's a room guests will notice)

### Recommendation
This is the room to use a budget-tier product without compromise — the guest bedroom will rarely be tested. A budget carpet (Class 21) or entry-level LVT is entirely appropriate.

**Honest framing:** "The guest bedroom doesn't need a premium product — it genuinely won't see enough use to justify it. Spend the saving on a room that gets used every day."

## 7. HALLWAY AND LANDING

### The Environment
The hallway is the hardest-working floor in the house:
- Very high foot traffic (every journey in and out of the home)
- Grit and moisture brought in from outside (the floor's primary enemy)
- Hard objects (pushchairs, luggage, shopping, dropped keys)
- Must look good despite constant use
- First impression of the home

### Top Recommendations

**Best overall: Porcelain tile (textured matt, 600×600 or 600×300)**
- The most durable option; withstands grit; easy to clean; holds its appearance indefinitely
- Textured or micro-textured finish for slip resistance (especially near the front door)
- Total installed: £70–£120/m²

**Best for warmth: Engineered wood (dense twist pile quality is second)**
- Very popular hallway choice; looks beautiful; with a good quality (4mm wear layer, 7-ply core) holds up well
- A hallway sees more wear than a living room; specify a minimum 4mm wear layer and lacquer finish
- Total installed: £75–£110/m²

**Best for budget: Dense twist carpet (AC3, nylon or wool-nylon)**
- Surprisingly practical — hides dirt well; absorbs grit (reducing scratching of hard floors in adjacent rooms)
- A carpet hallway with a hard floor in adjacent rooms is a popular combination
- Total installed: £40–£60/m²

**Best for character / period properties: Encaustic tile (Victorian) or stone flags**
- Authentically period-appropriate; incredibly durable; the hallway floor of choice in quality Victorian renovation
- Total installed: £80–£200/m²

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Polished tile or marble | Lethal when wet (rain-wet shoes); the front door area is a slip-risk zone |
| Saxony carpet | Traffic lane crushing within months; shows every footprint |
| Cheap laminate | The hallway is exactly where laminate fails fastest; grit abrades the AC2 surface; door seals let moisture in |

### Key Questions

1. "Is the hallway connected directly to the front door, or is there a porch/vestibule? The first metre inside the front door needs the toughest specification."
2. "Do you have dogs or children with bikes/scooters? Tyre marks and claw marks matter in a hallway."
3. "What's the aesthetic direction — contemporary, traditional, period?"

### Budget Anchor (10m² hallway)

| Option | Total installed |
|--------|----------------|
| Dense twist carpet (AC3) | £400–£600 |
| Mid-range LVT | £450–£650 |
| Porcelain tile | £700–£1,200 |
| Engineered wood | £750–£1,100 |

## 8. STAIRS

### The Environment
Stairs are the most technically demanding domestic flooring location:
- Concentrated foot traffic on a narrow path (the nosing of each step wears faster than anything else)
- Directional load from ascending and descending
- Safety: slip resistance is critical; a fall on stairs is a serious injury risk
- Acoustic: stair noise travels through the whole house
- Aesthetic: the staircase is a visual focal point from multiple vantage points

### Top Recommendations

**Best overall: Carpet (80/20 wool-nylon twist, AC3 minimum)**
- The safest stair floor covering; excellent grip; noise-absorbing
- Specify a dense twist (not saxony — the pile direction change on each nosing is very visible in saxony)
- A stair runner (carpet with exposed wood either side) is an elegant alternative to full-width carpet
- Total installed: £15–£25 per stair (full-width); add stair nosing cost if using engineered wood treads

**Best for contemporary aesthetic: Engineered wood treads with carpet inset**
- Engineered wood stair nosing and tread with carpet on the riser; or full engineered wood treads with non-slip insert at each nosing
- Requires a non-slip tread detail at every nosing — safety non-negotiable
- Total installed: £35–£65 per stair (engineered wood tread with nosing)

**Best for period properties: Axminster or Wilton stair runner**
- The traditional luxury stair treatment; brass stair rods; exposed painted or stained timber either side
- Total installed: £25–£60 per stair (depending on carpet quality)

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Polished stone or tile | Very high slip risk on stairs; fatal accidents occur; never specify on stairs without anti-slip insets |
| Smooth LVT without anti-slip nosing | The smooth surface at the stair nosing is a slip hazard |
| Seagrass or sisal | Natural texture may be slip-resistant dry but becomes dangerous when wet |
| Laminate on stairs | Click systems are not rated for the directional load of stairs; joints fail |
| Polished wood without anti-slip detail | Every stair nosing needs a contrasting anti-slip strip |

### Key Questions

1. "Are there elderly people or young children using these stairs regularly? This escalates the safety specification."
2. "Open risers or closed? Open riser stairs have different nosing requirements."
3. "Is this a straight staircase or does it have winders (angled corners)? Winders significantly affect the carpet-fitting complexity and cost."

### Safety Rule — Non-Negotiable
Every stair nosing must have a visually contrasting anti-slip element. This is both a legal requirement under Approved Document M in public buildings and common sense in any home.

## 9. BATHROOM

### The Environment
The bathroom demands:
- Water resistance (splashes, condensation, overflow risk)
- Slip resistance when wet (bare feet; soap residue)
- Easy hygiene and cleaning
- Comfortable underfoot temperature (important in a barefoot room)
- Resistance to cleaning products

### Top Recommendations

**Best overall: Porcelain tile (textured/anti-slip, matt or textured finish)**
- The definitive bathroom floor; completely waterproof; easy to clean; durable
- Specify a minimum R10 wet slip resistance; micro-textured or slight texture
- 600×300mm or 450×450mm format most practical in standard UK bathrooms
- Total installed: £70–£120/m²

**Best for warmth and comfort: LVT (SPC, glue-down or waterproof click)**
- Warmer underfoot than tile; genuinely waterproof; excellent for families
- SPC with 0.55mm+ wear layer; click format is easy to install
- Total installed: £40–£65/m²

**Best for luxury: Stone (granite, honed quartzite, or dense limestone)**
- Beautiful; durable; works exceptionally well with UFH (the combination of warm stone underfoot is considered one of the finest bathroom experiences)
- Requires sealing; careful cleaning product selection (neutral pH)
- Total installed: £100–£200+/m²

**If the customer loves the look of marble for the bathroom floor:** Gently redirect to either quartzite (same aesthetic; acid-resistant; much more practical) or marble-effect porcelain (looks almost identical; easier maintenance).

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Marble on bathroom floor | Etches from bath products; scratches; high maintenance; particularly problematic around the shower area |
| Solid wood | Will warp, swell, and stain in a bathroom environment; not suitable |
| Standard laminate | Edge swell from water is inevitable; bathroom is the worst environment |
| Polished tile | Slippery when wet — the primary bathroom injury hazard |
| Carpet | Was once specified in UK bathrooms (1970s–1980s); hygienically unacceptable now |

### Key Questions

1. "Is there a shower in this bathroom or is it bath only? A shower greatly increases the moisture load."
2. "Do you have underfloor heating or is it planned? This will influence whether tile or stone is even more strongly recommended."
3. "Are young children or elderly people using this bathroom? Anti-slip priority increases significantly."

### Budget Anchor (6m² bathroom)

| Option | Total installed |
|--------|----------------|
| LVT (mid-range) | £240–£390 |
| Porcelain tile (mid-range) | £420–£720 |
| Stone (honed limestone) | £600–£1,200 |

## 10. EN-SUITE

### The Environment
Essentially the same as a bathroom but typically smaller (3–5m²) and often used by one or two people with better ventilation:
- Slightly lower water exposure than a shared family bathroom
- Often a design-led space adjacent to the master bedroom
- The small size means cost per m² matters less; an upgrade is often affordable

### Recommendation
This is the room to specify something special — the small area makes premium material genuinely affordable. Marble (honed), exceptional stone, or very large format tile in a 3m² en-suite might cost £600 total; the same quality in a 20m² living room would be £12,000.

**The small room opportunity:** Always mention this when a customer is hesitating on a premium material. "The en-suite is only 3m² — that's actually the perfect place to use a premium stone you love, because the small area keeps the cost manageable while making a real statement."

## 11. WET ROOM / WALK-IN SHOWER

### The Environment
A wet room (no shower tray; floor is the shower floor) is the most extreme water-exposure environment in a domestic building:
- Water falls directly onto the floor across a wide area
- Constant moisture; no drying period between uses in busy households
- The floor must drain correctly (fall to drain)
- Slip resistance is critical (wet, soapy, bare feet)
- The floor is also decorative — visible and tactile

### Top Recommendations

**Best: Anti-slip porcelain mosaic tile (DIN 51097 Class B)**
- Small mosaic tiles (25×25mm to 100×100mm) have many grout joints which create natural texture and grip
- The grout joints are a functional anti-slip mechanism; more joints = more grip
- 100% waterproof with correct tanking beneath; epoxy grout for hygienic seamless joints
- Total installed (including tanking): £120–£200+/m²

**Best for contemporary aesthetic: Anti-slip porcelain tile (large format with anti-slip finish)**
- Large format (600×300mm) with a specifically anti-slip textured surface
- Must achieve DIN 51097 Class B wet (barefoot) rating — verify with manufacturer data
- Linear drain floor allows a single direction of fall; tiles run clean parallel rows
- Total installed: £130–£220/m²

**Best for minimalist/seamless look: Microcement (with anti-slip polyaspartic topcoat)**
- The completely seamless wet room floor; no tiles, no grout
- The tanking must be beneath the microcement; the microcement is the finish, not the waterproofing
- Anti-slip additive mixed into the final topcoat coat is essential
- Total installed (including tanking): £150–£250/m²

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Polished tile or polished stone | DIN 51097 Class B cannot be achieved on polished surfaces; serious slip risk |
| Standard grout in wet room | Must use epoxy grout or the joints will harbour mould and fail hygienically |
| LVT (standard) | Not suitable as a shower floor surface; the textured click joint is a hygiene harbour; glue-down might be acceptable with correct tanking but porcelain is the professional recommendation |

### The One Non-Negotiable
A wet room floor must be tanked (waterproofed with a bonded membrane system) before any floor covering is applied. The tile or microcement is the finish; the tanking membrane is the waterproofing. Without tanking, water will penetrate and cause structural damage within months.

## 12. OPEN-PLAN KITCHEN / LIVING / DINING

### The Environment
The open-plan ground floor — the dominant design in new-build and extending homes — presents a unique challenge: one continuous floor must serve the kitchen (wet/spill zone), the dining area (chair dragging), and the living area (comfort/aesthetics), all in a visually unified way.

### The Core Challenge
Creating visual unity across a space with very different functional demands.

### Solutions

**Option 1: One floor throughout (the simplest)**
- Choose a floor that meets the highest demand (kitchen) and extend it throughout
- LVT (SPC, glue-down, wood or stone effect): waterproof throughout; durable throughout; visually unified; works with UFH
- Large format porcelain tile: the premium unified solution; completely seamless visually with minimal grout joints; durable everywhere
- Total installed: £55–£150/m² depending on product

**Option 2: Hard floor with defined soft zone**
- Hard floor (LVT, tile, or stone) throughout; large area rug in the seating area
- The rug defines the living zone; adds warmth, acoustic comfort, and soft feel in the seating area without compromising the floor specification elsewhere
- Very popular in premium open-plan homes

**Option 3: Zoning with contrasting materials**
- Different materials in different zones (e.g., tile in kitchen zone; engineered wood in living zone); separated by a threshold strip
- More design complexity; requires careful planning to avoid awkward transitions
- Works well when the zones are clearly defined architecturally (island or kitchen peninsula as natural boundary)

### Key Questions

1. "Do you have dogs or young children? A unified waterproof floor (LVT or tile) is dramatically easier to maintain."
2. "Is UFH installed throughout? This strongly influences the recommendation — tile and LVT are the ideal partners."
3. "Do you want one material throughout for visual simplicity, or are you comfortable with a visual boundary between zones?"

## 13. HOME OFFICE / STUDY

### The Environment
The home office has one specific demand that distinguishes it from a bedroom or living room: **office chair castors**.

An office chair on castors will destroy any floor covering that isn't rated for it. The repeated rolling load of a castor chair under body weight is one of the most destructive things a floor encounters.

### Top Recommendations

**Best overall: Commercial-grade LVT (0.55mm+ wear layer) or tile**
- Check the LVT is tested to EN 985 (castor chair resistance)
- Tile and stone: immune to castor chair damage
- Total installed: £45–£85/m²

**Alternative for comfort: Dense, commercial-grade carpet tile**
- Class 31–32; loop pile (not cut pile — castors damage cut pile)
- EN 985 tested
- Individual tile replacement when worn under the desk
- Total installed: £35–£60/m²

**Chair mat:** If the customer has or wants a soft floor, a polycarbonate or glass chair mat under the desk solves the castor problem while preserving the floor aesthetic elsewhere.

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Engineered wood (without chair mat) | Castor chairs will groove and dent even a hardwood floor over time |
| Saxony carpet | Castors crush and permanently flatten the pile within weeks |
| Soft cushion vinyl | Deforms under castor pressure |

## 14. UTILITY ROOM / BOOT ROOM

### The Environment
The utility room / boot room is a hard-working service room:
- Wet and muddy boots from outside
- Washing machine and dishwasher — overflow and condensation risk
- Potentially pet feeding area (water bowl spills)
- The room everyone walks through from the garden

### Top Recommendations

**Best: Porcelain tile or anti-slip safety vinyl**
- Hard-wearing; completely water-resistant; easy to mop
- Anti-slip finish essential (wet boots and tile = slip hazard)
- Safety vinyl (Altro, Tarkett) is a very practical specification for a utility room — easy to clean; anti-slip; durable
- Total installed: £50–£90/m²

**Budget option: Cushioned vinyl sheet (domestic)**
- Inexpensive; waterproof enough for this application; warm underfoot
- Total installed: £25–£40/m²

### What to Avoid
Carpet, laminate, solid wood — all unsuitable for a wet-boots environment. LVT (click) is acceptable if the joints are adequately sealed and it's a click-waterproof product.

## 15. CONSERVATORY / GARDEN ROOM

### The Environment
Conservatories and garden rooms are among the most technically challenging spaces for flooring:
- **Temperature extremes:** In summer a glass conservatory can reach 40–50°C; in winter it can drop to near-external temperatures if unheated
- **UV exposure:** Very high UV through glass panels; accelerates fading and can degrade some materials
- **Moisture from plants and gardening activities**
- Often no UFH; the floor must be comfortable without additional heating in cooler seasons

### Top Recommendations

**Best: SPC LVT (rigid core)**
- The standout choice for conservatories
- Very low thermal expansion coefficient (far more stable than laminate, WPC LVT, or wood under temperature swings)
- UV-stable with quality products (check UV stability specification)
- Total installed: £45–£70/m²

**Best for traditional aesthetic: Porcelain tile**
- No thermal expansion concerns; UV-stable; easy to clean
- Will feel cold underfoot without UFH; if UFH is installed, tile is the ideal pairing
- Total installed: £70–£120/m²

**Best for contemporary / indoor-outdoor: Natural stone (granite or quartzite)**
- If the conservatory connects to an external terrace, a continuous stone floor creates a seamless inside-outside flow
- Frost-resistant stone (granite, quartzite) can extend from inside to outside
- Total installed: £120–£220/m²

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Laminate | HDF core is moisture-sensitive; the temperature swings cause significant expansion; buckling is common in conservatories |
| WPC LVT | Higher thermal expansion than SPC; more prone to problems in temperature-variable spaces |
| Solid or engineered wood | Wood in an unheated conservatory: extreme MC swings; warping, gapping, cupping |
| Carpet | Moisture from plants; muddy feet from garden; difficult to keep clean |

### Key Question
"Is this conservatory heated throughout the year, or does it get very cold in winter?" — An unheated conservatory in a UK winter can see near-external temperatures; only the most stable products are appropriate.

## 16. BASEMENT / LOWER GROUND FLOOR

### The Environment
Basements present the single most challenging moisture environment in a residential building:
- Ground-level or below-ground concrete will often have elevated moisture (RH 75–95%+)
- Pre-1960 basements typically have no DPM
- The floor covering must tolerate the moisture environment OR a DPM must be installed first

### Key Rule
**Test moisture first. Always. Without exception.**

No floor covering should be installed in a basement without an RH test. The moisture level governs everything — product selection, DPM requirement, adhesive specification.

### Top Recommendations by Moisture Level

**If RH ≤75% (dry basement):**
- LVT (SPC click) with DPM underlay — the ideal basement floor
- Ceramic or porcelain tile (with epoxy DPM if borderline)
- Engineered wood (glue-down with moisture-tolerant adhesive; careful monitoring required)

**If RH 75–85% (elevated moisture):**
- Apply two-coat epoxy DPM; then install tile or LVT glue-down
- Do NOT install wood (engineered or solid) at this moisture level

**If RH >85% (very high moisture):**
- Investigate the source (rising damp? lateral ingress? groundwater?)
- Remedial works (tanking, external waterproofing, sump and pump) before any floor covering
- After remediation: re-test; proceed as appropriate tier above

### What to Avoid in a Basement

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Solid wood | Even with DPM, the moisture environment of most basements is too variable for solid wood |
| Standard laminate | Edge swell from moisture is almost inevitable in a basement without perfect DPM |
| Carpet directly on concrete | Moisture beneath carpet in a basement creates mould within weeks in most UK basements |

## 17. GARAGE

### The Environment
The domestic garage has specific demands distinct from all other rooms:
- Vehicles drive and park on the floor (weight; oil and fuel drips; tyre marks)
- Temperature varies from near-external in winter to hot in summer (significant UV through the door)
- Chemical exposure: motor oil, brake fluid, antifreeze, fuel
- Impact from tools and dropped items

### Top Recommendations

**Best: Polyaspartic resin floor system**
- UV-stable (critical — standard epoxy yellows in sunlight through the garage door)
- Chemical resistant to oils and fuels
- Hard; durable; looks excellent
- Available with flake broadcast for aesthetic interest and anti-slip
- Total installed: £25–£55/m²

**Good alternative: Epoxy with polyaspartic topcoat**
- Same principle — polyaspartic topcoat provides UV stability over the epoxy base
- Slightly lower cost; excellent performance
- Total installed: £20–£40/m²

**Budget: Interlocking rubber tiles**
- Easy DIY installation; can be lifted if needed; protects the concrete
- Less permanent; joints allow moisture to penetrate to concrete
- Total installed: £10–£20/m²

### What to Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Standard epoxy (without UV topcoat) | Will yellow visibly within one summer through a glass garage door |
| Carpet tiles | Oil contamination; impossible to clean properly |
| Laminate | Not rated for vehicle weight or tyre contact |

## 18. EXTERNAL TERRACE / PATIO

### The Environment
The UK external environment is the most demanding:
- Freeze-thaw cycling throughout winter
- UV and weather exposure year-round
- Wet conditions for much of the year (slip risk)
- Physical impact from garden furniture and outdoor use

### Non-Negotiables

**Frost resistance is the only hard requirement.** If the stone or tile is not frost-resistant (Group AI porcelain or specifically tested stone with ≤0.5% water absorption), it will fail within 1–3 winters in the UK through freeze-thaw damage.

### Top Recommendations

**Best: Calibrated porcelain paving (external rated)**
- Group AI porcelain; anti-slip (R10+ for flat terrace; R11 for steps)
- 600×600mm or 600×900mm format; 20mm thickness for ground-level external
- Total installed: £60–£110/m²

**Best for character: Natural stone (granite, quartzite, or frost-tested limestone)**
- Granite and quartzite: inherently frost-resistant; excellent long-term
- Limestone: only if specifically frost-tested to EN 12371; many limestones are not suitable
- Riven or honed finish; R10 minimum slip resistance
- Total installed: £75–£150/m²

**Best for traditional UK garden: Reclaimed Yorkstone**
- The most beloved British patio material; worn surface; beautiful character
- Frost-resistant (sandstone is generally tolerant of UK conditions if well-drained)
- Total installed: £80–£150/m²

### What to Avoid for External Use

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Most ceramic tile | Not frost-resistant; will spall and crack in first UK winter |
| Interior porcelain (not external rated) | Some interior porcelain has slightly higher absorption; must be specifically external-rated |
| Marble (polished) | Acid rain etching; frost risk in most varieties; polished = slippery when wet |
| Any LVT or laminate | Not rated for outdoor exposure |

### Key Question
"Is the terrace sheltered (covered pergola, under a canopy) or fully exposed?" — Sheltered terraces have somewhat lower frost and UV risk; fully exposed terraces need the strictest frost-resistance specification.

## 19. WHOLE-HOUSE / MULTI-ROOM PROJECTS

### The Design Challenge
When a customer is flooring the whole house, the key challenge is creating a coherent visual flow while meeting the different technical demands of each room.

### The Three Zones Approach

**Zone 1 — Wet rooms (bathrooms, WC, utility):**
- Tile or waterproof LVT
- Can be a different material from the rest of the house; bathrooms are contained spaces

**Zone 2 — Hard-wearing circulation (hall, stairs, kitchen):**
- The most demanding specification; must be durable and practical
- A unified material here (tile, stone, or quality LVT) gives the home a strong foundation

**Zone 3 — Living and sleeping (living room, dining, bedrooms):**
- Where comfort and aesthetics take precedence
- Wood, quality LVT, or carpet; can vary by room

### Visual Continuity Tips

- **One material in all circulation spaces** (hall + landing + stairs) creates a sense of connected space
- **Open-plan ground floor:** one material throughout avoids visual interruption
- **Hard floor ground floor + carpet upstairs** is a very popular and practical combination
- **Match tone not material:** A warm-oak LVT in the hall pairs beautifully with warm engineered oak in the living room even if they're different products

### Budget Framing for Whole-House

| House size | Typical total flooring budget (mid-range) |
|------------|------------------------------------------|
| 2-bed flat (70m²) | £6,000–£12,000 |
| 3-bed semi (90m²) | £8,000–£16,000 |
| 4-bed detached (130m²) | £12,000–£24,000 |
| 5-bed detached (180m²) | £16,000–£35,000 |

These figures include all rooms, preparation, and installation. They are averages — a full-house natural stone specification would be considerably more; a budget laminate and carpet specification would be considerably less.

## 20. RENTAL PROPERTIES

### The Landlord's Priorities
Rental flooring has a specific set of priorities that differ significantly from owner-occupied specification:
1. **Durability over aesthetics** — the floor must survive multiple tenancies
2. **Easy maintenance / replacement** — when a floor section is damaged, replacing it should be straightforward
3. **Neutral aesthetics** — broad appeal to prospective tenants
4. **Cost efficiency** — the capital expenditure must be recouped through rental yield or property value

### Top Recommendations for Rentals

**Best overall: Commercial-grade SPC LVT (0.55mm wear layer minimum)**
- Waterproof; scratch-resistant; tenant-proof in most normal circumstances
- Neutral wood or stone-effect designs appeal to the broadest tenant demographic
- Click system means individual plank replacement is possible if damaged
- Total installed: £35–£55/m²

**Best for hallways and high-traffic areas: AC4 porcelain tile**
- Effectively indestructible; no tenant can damage good porcelain
- Higher initial cost but outlasts the building's rental life
- Total installed: £65–£100/m²

**Best for bedrooms: Carpet (nylon, AC3, neutral colour)**
- Tenants prefer carpeted bedrooms; it improves rental appeal
- Specify nylon over wool/polypropylene: better stain resistance and durability for rental
- Mid-grey, warm beige, or light brown — neutral tones that photograph well
- Total installed: £35–£55/m²

### What Landlords Should Avoid

| Product | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Solid wood or engineered wood (premium) | Too expensive; tenants don't treat it with the same care as owners |
| Light-coloured carpet | Deposits will be disputed; dark carpet stains may not be visible; mid-tone is best |
| Cheap laminate (AC2, thin) | Will need replacement within 3–5 years in rental; false economy |
| Any natural stone (in main rooms) | Too expensive; maintenance knowledge cannot be assumed from tenants; sealing maintenance won't happen |

### The Landlord Conversation

*"The best flooring decision for a rental property is the one that costs you the least over a 10-year period — not the cheapest upfront. A quality SPC LVT at £48/m² that lasts 15 years costs half as much per year as budget laminate at £22/m² that needs replacing every 6 years. The AC4 porcelain tile in the kitchen and bathroom is effectively permanent — you'll never replace it between tenancies. Combined with a mid-range carpet in the bedrooms, you have a floor that will photograph well, attract good tenants, and require minimal intervention for the next decade."*
