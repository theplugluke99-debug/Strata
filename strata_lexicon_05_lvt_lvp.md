## 1. CATEGORY OVERVIEW — WHAT LVT IS AND HOW IT EVOLVED

### 1.1 Definition

Luxury Vinyl Tile (LVT) and Luxury Vinyl Plank (LVP) are resilient floor coverings constructed from multiple layers of polyvinyl chloride (PVC) and related compounds. The visible surface is a high-resolution photographic print beneath a transparent polyurethane-enhanced PVC or aluminium oxide-treated wear layer.

**LVT is genuinely waterproof** — this is the fundamental characteristic that distinguishes it from laminate and wood-based flooring. The PVC construction has no hygroscopic response; it does not swell, shrink, warp, or delaminate from moisture.

### 1.2 Historical Development

**1930s–1960s:** Vinyl composition tile (VCT) and early sheet vinyl established PVC as a practical flooring material. These were functional but aesthetically limited — the print technology did not exist to replicate natural materials convincingly.

**1980s–1990s:** Vinyl plank products emerged, still primarily commercial (healthcare, education). Heavy-gauge, direct-glue products with improving print technology.

**2000s:** Luxury Vinyl as a distinct category developed — thicker wear layers, improved embossing technology (EIR emerging), first click systems adapted from laminate.

**2010s:** Exponential growth. Rigid core technology (WPC and SPC) transformed the category — the ability to float over imperfect subfloors without glue opened a massive DIY and renovation market. Design quality improved dramatically to compete with real wood and tile aesthetically.

**2020s:** LVT is now the world's fastest-growing flooring category by volume. SPC products are displacing laminate at the mid-market; premium LVT is displacing engineered wood in moisture-risk installations; healthcare-grade LVT has evolved into sophisticated acoustically-dampened and chemically-resistant specification products.

### 1.3 Naming Confusion in the Market

The LVT category suffers from significant naming inconsistency:
- **LVT (Luxury Vinyl Tile):** Technically the original tile format; now used generically for the entire category including planks
- **LVP (Luxury Vinyl Plank):** The plank-format product; dominated by wood-effect designs
- **Vinyl plank / click vinyl / vinyl flooring:** Generic consumer terms
- **WPC (Wood Plastic Composite):** A specific rigid core type (see Section 6)
- **SPC (Stone Plastic Composite / Rigid Core):** A specific rigid core type; the fastest-growing sub-category
- **Hybrid flooring:** A trade marketing term, typically meaning SPC with attached acoustic underlay
- **LooseLay:** A specific installation method/product type (see Section 10)
- **Cushion vinyl / comfort vinyl:** Sheet vinyl with foam backing — see Lexicon 06

**Key distinction the AI must maintain:** LVT = PVC-based with a photographic print wear layer. Laminate = HDF-based with a paper/melamine print wear layer. They are frequently confused by consumers and some trade staff.

## 2. PVC CHEMISTRY — THE FOUNDATION OF VINYL FLOORING

### 2.1 PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)

PVC is a thermoplastic polymer produced by polymerisation of vinyl chloride monomer (VCM). In its pure form (UPVC), it is rigid and brittle. For flooring, it is formulated with plasticisers, stabilisers, fillers, and other additives to produce a flexible, durable, and dimensionally stable material.

**Key chemistry:**
- **Polymer backbone:** Repeating –CH₂–CHCl– units; chlorine content approximately 56% by weight
- **Polymerisation:** VCM polymerised to PVC resin (suspension polymerisation is most common for flooring-grade resin)
- **Degree of polymerisation (K-value):** Higher K-value = higher molecular weight = harder, more durable; flooring-grade PVC typically K65–K75

### 2.2 Plasticisers

Pure PVC is rigid and brittle. Plasticisers are molecules that insert between PVC polymer chains, increasing chain mobility and producing a flexible material.

**Phthalate plasticisers (historical standard):**
- DEHP (Di-2-ethylhexyl phthalate): formerly dominant; now restricted under EU REACH regulation (SVHCs — Substances of Very High Concern)
- DINP (Di-isononyl phthalate): lower hazard; still used; subject to ongoing regulatory review
- DIDP (Di-isodecyl phthalate): similar to DINP

**Modern alternative plasticisers (phthalate-free):**
- **DOTP (Di-2-ethylhexyl terephthalate):** A terephthalate-based plasticiser; considered safer; now the dominant replacement in quality LVT
- **DINCH:** Cyclohexane dicarboxylic acid ester; very low toxicity profile; used in medical and food-contact applications; specified in healthcare LVT
- **Bio-based plasticisers:** ESBO (epoxidised soybean oil) and other renewable-source plasticisers; used in eco-LVT products

**Why this matters:**
- Phthalate-free formulations are required in healthcare and education environments under UK/EU procurement specifications
- Phthalate migration: in older PVC flooring, plasticiser can migrate to the surface over time, creating a sticky residue that attracts dirt — modern stabilised formulations do not have this issue
- EU REACH compliance is mandatory for products placed on the UK/EU market

### 2.3 Stabilisers

PVC degrades under heat (processing) and UV (service). Stabilisers prevent this:
- **Lead-based stabilisers:** Formerly dominant; banned in EU flooring for decades; may be present in pre-1990s installations (asbestos-era flooring awareness applies here)
- **Calcium-zinc (Ca-Zn) stabilisers:** The current standard; safe; effective; widely used in quality LVT
- **Organic stabilisers (OBS):** Metal-free option; used in some eco-focused formulations

### 2.4 Fillers and Pigments

- **Calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) / chalk:** The primary filler in most LVT cores; high loading reduces cost and contributes rigidity (particularly in SPC)
- **Barium sulphate:** Higher density filler used in acoustic products
- **Titanium dioxide (TiO₂):** White pigment; provides opacity in base layers
- **Various pigments:** In the backing and base layers for colour identification

## 3. PRODUCT CONSTRUCTION TYPES

### 3.1 Standard Flexible LVT (Multi-Layer PVC)

The original and still widely-used construction:

**Layers from top to bottom:**

**Wear layer:** Transparent PVC + polyurethane treatment; the protective surface (see Section 4)

**Print layer (decorative layer):** High-resolution photographic print on a PVC or polyester film carrier; the visible design

**Glass fibre reinforcement layer:** A woven glass fibre mat embedded within the core; provides dimensional stability and prevents the product from shrinking or expanding beyond acceptable limits; critical for glue-down products — without adequate dimensional stability, the floor fights against the adhesive, causing it to debond

**Core layer(s):** PVC compound with fillers; provides the body of the product; may be single layer or multiple calendered layers; in premium products, a foam or cushion layer may be incorporated here for comfort

**Backing layer:** PVC compound or mineral-filled base; provides dimensional stability and an even surface for adhesive bonding or contact with subfloor

**Total thickness:** Typically 2.0–5.0mm for standard flexible LVT; commercial-grade up to 6mm

### 3.2 Cushioned LVT

A flexible LVT with an integrated foam or closed-cell cushion layer incorporated in the construction:
- Softer underfoot; better thermal comfort
- Better acoustic performance (impact sound reduction within the product)
- Typically 4–6mm total thickness
- Less dimensionally stable than non-cushioned due to foam compressibility — not suitable for very heavy castor chair loads
- Common in residential and healthcare applications

### 3.3 WPC (Wood Plastic Composite) Core LVT

A hybrid construction bridging flexible LVT and rigid LVT:

**Construction:**
- Wear layer (PVC, PU-treated)
- Print film
- WPC core: foamed PVC composite containing wood flour and plastic; lower density than SPC; lighter weight
- HPS (High-Performance Surface) or attached underlay on base

**Properties:**
- Rigid enough to bridge minor subfloor irregularities
- Lighter than SPC (foamed core)
- Warmer underfoot than SPC
- Better acoustic than SPC (foam content absorbs impact)
- Less dimensionally stable under temperature extremes than SPC
- Suitable for most residential floating applications
- Not the strongest choice for commercial or temperature-variable environments

**Typical total thickness:** 6–10mm

### 3.4 SPC (Stone Plastic Composite / Rigid Core) LVT

The fastest-growing LVT sub-category. A rigid, dense core provides structural integrity comparable to tile or laminate.

**Core composition:**
- PVC resin + calcium carbonate (chalk) filler + plasticiser + stabilisers
- Typical CaCO₃ loading: 40–60% by weight — the high mineral content gives SPC its rigidity and density
- Core density: typically 1.8–2.2 kg/m³ — significantly denser than WPC

**Construction:**
- Wear layer (PVC, PU-treated)
- Print film
- SPC core (rigid, dense)
- IXPE or EVA foam underlay (often factory-attached to base)

**Properties:**
- Dimensionally very stable — the rigid mineral-loaded core has very low thermal expansion and negligible hygroscopic response
- Bridges minor subfloor imperfections effectively (more than flexible LVT, comparable to laminate)
- Denser and heavier than WPC; harder underfoot
- Better dimensional stability under temperature change than WPC or laminate
- Excellent for areas with temperature variation (conservatories, spaces with solar gain)
- The stiffness of SPC means it does not conform to significant subfloor irregularities — flatness tolerance still critical
- Click system standard (Uniclic, Valinge 5G, or proprietary equivalent)

**Typical total thickness:** 4–8mm (SPC core typically 3.5–5mm; wear layer typically 0.3–0.7mm; foam backing 1–2mm)

## 4. WEAR LAYER SYSTEMS

### 4.1 What the Wear Layer Does

The wear layer is the transparent surface of the LVT — the layer your foot contacts. It determines:
- **Durability and scratch resistance**
- **Stain resistance**
- **Chemical resistance**
- **Slip resistance**
- **Gloss level and texture**
- **How long the product lasts before the print below is exposed**

Unlike laminate (where the wear layer is melamine/aluminium oxide), LVT wear layers are PVC-based with polyurethane (PU) enhancement.

### 4.2 Wear Layer Thickness

The single most important specification parameter for LVT:

| Thickness | Category | Application | Typical lifespan (residential) |
|-----------|----------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| 0.20mm | Ultra-budget | Not recommended | Very short; should not be specified |
| 0.30mm | Budget residential | Bedroom; very light use only | 3–5 years heavy use; 8–10 light |
| 0.50mm | Standard residential | General residential use | 10–15 years |
| 0.55mm | The residential standard | All domestic areas; light commercial | 15–20 years |
| 0.70mm | Premium residential | Hallways; high-traffic domestic | 20+ years |
| 1.0mm | Entry commercial | Light commercial; landlord/rental spec | 15–20 years commercial |
| 2.0mm | Commercial | Offices; retail; hospitality | 20–25 years |
| 2.5–3.0mm | Heavy commercial | Healthcare; education; heavy retail | 25+ years |

**The 0.55mm threshold:** In the UK market, 0.55mm has become the de facto residential standard and the minimum specification recommended by the professional trade for any residential area. Products below 0.55mm wear layer should be clearly communicated as budget/light use only.

**Wear layer vs total thickness:** These are independent. A 4mm total thickness SPC product with 0.5mm wear layer is specified by the 0.5mm wear layer for durability. A 2mm flexible LVT with 0.7mm wear layer is more durable in terms of wear resistance than the 4mm SPC. Customers frequently confuse total thickness with wear layer — always clarify both.

### 4.3 PU (Polyurethane) Treatment

The PVC wear layer alone has moderate hardness. A polyurethane (PU) topcoat significantly enhances:
- Surface hardness and scratch resistance
- Stain resistance (PU is more chemically resistant than plain PVC)
- Ease of cleaning (PU surface is smoother at a micro level)
- Gloss stability

**UV-cured PU:** The industry standard for premium products; very hard; excellent durability. Applied as a liquid and cured by UV radiation at the factory.

**Enhanced PU (enhanced urethane / ceramic-bead PU):** PU topcoat impregnated with ceramic or aluminium oxide beads; even harder surface; used in commercial-grade products to meet heavy wear requirements. Similar concept to Al₂O₃ in laminate but in a PU matrix.

**Nano-coating:** Ultra-thin ceramic or silica coating applied over PU; the current premium technology; extremely low friction against abrasive particles; claimed to be self-healing at micro-scratch level in some product marketing.

### 4.4 Anti-Slip Surface Treatments

Vinyl flooring is naturally somewhat slippery, particularly when wet. Slip resistance is managed by:
- **Surface texture:** Micro-texture or macro-embossing increases friction
- **Silica or ceramic particle incorporation:** Into the PU topcoat; significantly increases dry and wet slip resistance
- **Dedicated anti-slip surface treatment:** Applied as a top treatment; important in healthcare, wet rooms, shower areas, commercial kitchens

**Standards for slip resistance:**
- EN 13893: Dynamic coefficient of friction (dry); minimum μ 0.30 for most applications
- DIN 51130 (R-value): German standard for workplaces; R9–R13; used in commercial/industrial
- DIN 51097 (A/B/C): Barefoot wet areas (swimming pools, changing rooms); A = minimum; C = maximum slip resistance
- UKSRG (UK Slip Resistance Group) Pendulum test: used in post-incident investigations and commercial specification

## 5. PRINT AND EMBOSS TECHNOLOGY

### 5.1 Print Quality

As with laminate (Lexicon 04), print quality determines the visual authenticity of LVT:

**Rotogravure printing:**
- The original and still widely-used method; print cylinder engraved with design; fast, high-volume
- Print resolution: 50–100 lines/cm; good for wood grain; limited for fine stone detail
- Repeat: fixed cylinder circumference; repeat every 1.5–4m typical; modern long-repeat cylinders reduce this

**Digital printing (inkjet):**
- Direct printing onto the PVC film; no physical cylinder
- Effectively infinite repeat possible
- Very high resolution (equivalent to 600+ DPI); excellent for stone, marble, aged wood textures
- Growing rapidly; now the premium standard
- Allows mass customisation and bespoke design

**Pattern repeat:** As with laminate, the repeat length determines how often the same plank/tile appears in a floor. A 3m repeat means that in a 3m long room, each design appears only once before repeating. For rooms >3m, some repetition is visible. Premium products with 5m+ or digital random repeat are significantly more realistic.

### 5.2 EIR (Embossed in Register)

Identical principle to laminate (Lexicon 04, Section 4.1):
- Surface texture registered precisely to the print below
- Wood grain texture follows the printed grain lines; stone texture follows the printed stone surface features
- The gold standard for LVT visual realism
- Test: run a fingernail across the surface; texture should follow visible grain/pattern

### 5.3 Texture Types

**Hand-scraped:**
- Random undulating surface; disguises wear and hides scratches; rustic aesthetic; very popular

**Wire-brushed:**
- Mechanical texture mimicking brushed wood; open texture; good depth of character

**Smooth stone:**
- Very flat; mimics polished marble or limestone; shows marks more readily; elegant

**Matt:**
- Low sheen; hides wear effectively; practical for high-traffic areas

**High gloss:**
- Mirror-like finish; maximum light reflection; shows every mark and scratch; not recommended for high-traffic areas

**Grouted tile effect:**
- LVT tiles with a grout-joint profile moulded into the edges to simulate real grout lines
- The most convincing tile-effect LVT format
- Also available: separate flexible grout strips inserted between tiles during installation (Amtico, Karndean specialist installation)

## 6. CORE TYPES — THE CRITICAL DIFFERENTIATOR

The core is the single biggest decision in LVT specification. It determines installation method, subfloor tolerance, thermal expansion, acoustic performance, comfort, and application suitability.

### 6.1 Flexible LVT Core

**Properties:**
- Flexible PVC compound; will conform to subfloor contours
- This flexibility is a strength (conforms to minor irregularities in glue-down) and a weakness (does not bridge significant hollows; subfloor must be very flat)
- Dimensionally responsive to temperature: flexible LVT expands with heat; glue-down adhesion controls this; floating flexible LVT is unsuitable for temperature-variable environments
- Standard glue-down product; floating is possible with some products but requires excellent subfloor
- The original LVT format; still dominant in commercial applications

**Installation:** Glue-down primarily; some loose-lay products (see Section 10.3)

**Subfloor tolerance:** Maximum 3mm under 2m straight-edge for most products; some commercial healthcare products require 2mm

### 6.2 WPC Core

**Properties:**
- Foamed composite PVC/wood; lighter and warmer than SPC
- Moderate rigidity; will bridge minor irregularities better than flexible LVT
- Lower dimensional stability than SPC under temperature changes — thermal expansion of WPC is higher than SPC
- Better acoustic than SPC due to foam content (typically ΔLw 18–22 dB with attached underlay)
- Less suitable for high-temperature environments or areas with significant solar gain

**Installation:** Click floating; some products suitable for direct glue

**Subfloor tolerance:** Maximum 3mm under 2m (better bridging than flexible LVT but still requires reasonable flatness)

### 6.3 SPC Core

**Properties:**
- Rigid, dense mineral-filled core; extremely stable
- Very low thermal expansion coefficient (approximately 0.03–0.05mm/m/°C for quality SPC vs 0.06–0.10mm/m/°C for WPC)
- This low thermal expansion makes SPC suitable for:
  - Conservatories and rooms with significant solar gain
  - Under-floor heating systems
  - Large open-plan areas with long floating runs
  - Temperature-variable environments (garages, outbuildings with heating)
- Harder underfoot than WPC — can feel cold and rigid (similar to tile) without adequate underlay/foam backing
- Heavier than WPC
- The acoustic attached underlay (IXPE or EVA foam on base) is critical for SPC feel and acoustic performance

**Installation:** Click floating (standard); some products glue-down capable

**Subfloor tolerance:** Maximum 3mm under 2m (rigid core bridges better than flexible but does not eliminate the tolerance requirement)

### 6.4 Core Comparison Summary

| Property | Flexible LVT | WPC | SPC |
|----------|-------------|-----|-----|
| Rigidity | Low | Medium | High |
| Density (typical) | 1.2–1.6 kg/m³ | 0.8–1.2 kg/m³ | 1.8–2.2 kg/m³ |
| Thermal expansion | Medium-high | Medium-high | Low |
| Acoustic (product) | Low-medium | Good | Medium (with foam backing) |
| Underfoot warmth | Medium | Warm | Cool-medium |
| UFH compatibility | Excellent | Good | Excellent |
| Subfloor tolerance | Strictest (glue-down) | Moderate | Moderate |
| Installation method | Glue/loose-lay | Click float | Click float |
| Temperature variable environments | No (glue-down only) | Limited | Yes |
| Commercial suitability | Excellent (glue-down) | Limited | Good |

## 7. FORMAT TYPES — TILE, PLANK, AND SHEET

### 7.1 Plank (LVP)

- The dominant residential format; replicates wood plank flooring
- Standard width: 150–230mm; long planks increasingly popular (up to 1500mm)
- Length: 900–1500mm standard; 1800–2400mm in premium and SPC products
- Bevelled edges (micro-bevel or 4-sided bevel) standard in quality products
- End-matched T&G or click profile

### 7.2 Tile (LVT)

- Square or rectangular; replicates ceramic, porcelain, stone, or wood parquet
- Standard tile sizes: 305×305mm (12"×12"); 457×457mm (18"×18"); 610×610mm (24"×24")
- Rectangular tiles: 300×600mm; 400×800mm; simulates large format tile
- Herringbone plank tiles: individual strips for herringbone/chevron pattern
- Grout joint options:
  - Moulded edge: relief profile simulates grout line; no real grout joint
  - Real grout joint: tiles installed with gap filled with flexible grout or purpose-made grout strip (Amtico, Karndean systems)

### 7.3 Strip (Parquet)

- Narrow strips: 70–100mm × 280–400mm; intended for herringbone and parquet laying patterns
- Available as individual click-lock strips or as pre-assembled herringbone panels
- Pre-assembled panel format: typically 300×600mm panels with the herringbone pattern pre-arranged; click-locks together; faster to install; some loss of flexibility in layout

### 7.4 Sheet LVT

- Not commonly categorised as "LVT" in the trade — this is Luxury Vinyl Sheet
- 2m or 4m wide roll format; same PVC/wear layer construction as tile/plank LVT
- No joints — entirely seamless
- More waterproof in use (no joints for water ingress) but same core water resistance
- Heavier and more difficult to handle than tiles/planks
- See Lexicon 06 (Vinyl Sheet and Tile) for full coverage of sheet vinyl

## 8. PERFORMANCE CLASSIFICATION SYSTEMS

### 8.1 EN ISO 10874 — Resilient Floor Coverings Classification

The primary classification standard for LVT and all resilient floor coverings. Based on traffic intensity and usage type.

| Class | Use | Traffic | Description |
|-------|-----|---------|-------------|
| 21 | Domestic | Moderate | Bedrooms, low-use rooms |
| 22 | Domestic | General | Living rooms |
| 23 | Domestic | Heavy | Hallways, living areas |
| 31 | Commercial | Moderate | Office, hotel bedroom |
| 32 | Commercial | General | Office, school, retail |
| 33 | Commercial | Heavy | Large retail, busy office |
| 34 | Commercial | Very heavy | Airport, major department store |
| 41 | Industrial | Moderate | |
| 42 | Industrial | General | |
| 43 | Industrial | Heavy | |

**Important distinction from EN 1307 (carpet):** EN ISO 10874 is the standard for resilient floor coverings (LVT, vinyl, linoleum, rubber). EN 1307 is for textile floor coverings (carpet). Both use similar class numbering but are separate standards.

### 8.2 Wear Layer vs EN ISO 10874 Class

These are related but different:
- The EN ISO 10874 class is assigned after product testing; it is not simply a function of wear layer thickness
- However, wear layer thickness correlates strongly with class in practice:
  - 0.3mm → Class 21–22
  - 0.5mm → Class 22–23
  - 0.55mm → Class 23–31
  - 0.7mm → Class 31–32
  - 1.0mm → Class 32–33
  - 2.0mm → Class 33–34

### 8.3 T-Class (Thermal Resistance)

LVT products for use over UFH must declare their thermal resistance (R-value):
- As per EN ISO 10456 methodology
- Manufacturers test and declare R-values in product documentation
- Maximum for UFH use: 0.15 m²K/W (product only; no underlay)
- Most LVT products at 2–5mm total thickness are well within this limit (R-value typically 0.01–0.05 m²K/W)

### 8.4 Slip Resistance

Must meet minimum standards depending on application:
- EN 13893 ≥ 0.30 μ (dry) for general commercial and residential
- Higher requirements for wet areas, healthcare, and hospitality

## 9. SUBFLOOR REQUIREMENTS

### 9.1 The "Forgiving" Myth

LVT is often marketed as forgiving of subfloor imperfections. This is partially true for rigid core (SPC/WPC click) products and entirely false for flexible glue-down LVT.

**The reality:**
- Flexible glue-down LVT is extremely unforgiving — because it conforms to the subfloor exactly, every ridge, hollow, and imperfection is transmitted to the surface and visible in raking light
- SPC and WPC click products do bridge minor imperfections better than flexible LVT, but significant hollows still cause localised flexing of the boards, stress on the click joints, and over time can cause cracking of the rigid core

**Flatness tolerances:**
- Flexible glue-down LVT: **2mm under 2m straight-edge** (stricter than most flooring types)
- SPC/WPC click: **3mm under 2m straight-edge** (standard)
- Commercial flexible LVT (heavy gauge): **2mm under 2m** (mandatory for premium commercial)

### 9.2 Concrete Subfloors

- Moisture: **maximum 75% RH** (BS 8203) — this is for the adhesive bond integrity in glue-down; for floating SPC/WPC, the product itself is waterproof, but if moisture comes up through the subfloor and condenses under the floor, it can cause mould between the floor and subfloor
- Apply DPM if RH ≥75% for glue-down; use DPM underlay for floating over concrete
- Surface preparation: grind laitance; fill all cracks, holes, and joints with SLC; remove all adhesive residue
- No contamination: oil, wax, paint, or curing compound residue must be removed — critical for adhesive bond in glue-down

### 9.3 Timber Subfloors

- MC: maximum 14%
- Nail down all loose boards; no spring or flex
- Gaps over 3mm: fill with flexible filler or overlay with 6mm plywood
- Over boards: 6mm plywood overlay strongly recommended for glue-down flexible LVT — reduces subfloor telegraphing and provides a consistent adhesive surface
- Check for hollow boards that could flex under load

### 9.4 Existing Floor Coverings as Subfloor

**Over ceramic/porcelain tile:**
- Acceptable for both flexible and rigid LVT
- All tiles fully bonded (tap test)
- Grout lines: fill lines over 2mm width with flexible filler if using flexible glue-down LVT (they will telegraph to surface)
- For SPC click: grout lines ≤3mm acceptable without filling
- Remove any protruding tile lips or damaged tiles

**Over existing vinyl/LVT:**
- Flexible glue-down LVT over existing glue-down vinyl: acceptable if existing vinyl is fully bonded, flat, and in good condition; sand any embossed surface flat
- Check for asbestos in pre-1990s vinyl tile and sheet installations — see Section 9.6
- One layer maximum on top of existing vinyl; removing additional existing layers before re-installation

**Over concrete with existing adhesive residue:**
- Black bitumen adhesive (old carpet/tile adhesive): grind back or encapsulate; do not attempt to lay LVT over bitumen residue — adhesive incompatibility
- Old adhesive lumps: grind or skim with SLC before new adhesive

### 9.5 Under-Tile Heating (Electric Mat)

- SPC and flexible LVT are both compatible with electric mat UFH in thin-set under the floor
- Ensure the electric mat layer is absolutely flat before installing LVT — even the thin profile of an electric mat can create a ridge if not properly embedded in adhesive
- Some installers prefer to install the mat and then skim with a thin SLC before laying LVT

### 9.6 Asbestos in Existing Floors — Critical Safety

- Vinyl floor tiles manufactured before approximately 1985 (in some cases up to 1999) may contain chrysotile (white) asbestos
- The tiles themselves may contain asbestos fibres; the adhesive underneath frequently does
- **Do not disturb, cut, grind, or remove without proper asbestos assessment**
- HSE Guidance Note EH10 and the Control of Asbestos Regulations 2012 apply
- If in doubt: commission an asbestos survey before any work on pre-1990 floors
- The standard practice is to overlay existing asbestos vinyl tile rather than remove it — encapsulation is safer than removal in many cases
- For new LVT installation: encapsulating SLC over the existing tiles may be appropriate; confirm with asbestos surveyor

## 10. INSTALLATION METHODS — FLEXIBLE LVT

### 10.1 Glue-Down (Full Bond)

The original and most stable installation method for flexible LVT. Required for all flexible LVT products.

**Process:**
1. Prepare subfloor to tolerance (2mm/2m); prime if required
2. Apply adhesive with notched trowel; allow to reach correct tack (open time)
3. Lay tiles/planks into adhesive; press firmly
4. Roll with a 68kg roller immediately after laying and again after 30 minutes
5. No expansion gap required at perimeter (adhesion controls any minor movement)
6. Note: the adhesive bond, not perimeter gaps, controls dimensional movement in glue-down

**Adhesive types:**
- **Pressure-sensitive (PSA) tackifier:** Dries to a tack; repositionable within open time; used for most residential and light commercial flexible LVT; bonds adequately without aggressive adhesion
- **Full-bond dispersion adhesive:** Stronger permanent bond; used for heavy commercial and areas with high foot traffic or rolling loads; less repositioning flexibility
- **Acrylic adhesive:** Solvent-free; water-based; fast dry; used for most domestic and commercial applications; broad compatibility
- **Polyurethane adhesive:** Used in demanding commercial applications and for some specialty products; excellent heat resistance (good for conservatories)

**The roller — critical step:**
- A 68kg floor roller must be used immediately after laying
- Rolling ensures full adhesive contact across the entire board back
- Air pockets (unrolled areas) create hollow sections that will click underfoot and may allow moisture to pool

### 10.2 Glue-Down Layout

**Finding the centre:**
- Snap chalk lines across the centre of the room in both directions; intersection = room centre
- Dry-lay tiles/planks from centre to check border widths; adjust centre if borders would be less than half a tile wide
- For plank: start from one wall, running planks lengthwise; starting line may be the room centred or offset depending on aesthetics

**Working sequence:**
- From the centre outward in all directions (tile format)
- From the starting wall toward the opposite wall (plank format)
- Allow adhesive to reach correct tack before laying (critical — laying into wet adhesive or overly dry adhesive both cause poor bond)

### 10.3 Loose Lay (Heavy Gauge / Perimeter-Bonded)

A specific installation method used with purpose-designed heavy-gauge flexible LVT products:

**How it works:**
- The product is sufficiently heavy and dimensionally stable that it lies flat under its own weight without full-bond adhesive
- Edges and seams are bonded with double-sided tape or perimeter adhesive; field is loose-laid
- Products designed for loose lay: typically 2.5–5mm thick with high-mass backing
- Advantages: removable for access to underfloor services; fast installation; no adhesive required in field
- Limitations: not suitable for areas of high lateral force (commercial corridors, heavy rolling loads); not suitable in very hot environments (flexible PVC expands and can buckle if not bonded)
- Niche but important: used in hospitality for quick fit-out; school corridors with underfloor service access; temporary installations

### 10.4 Self-Adhesive LVT

- Factory-applied pressure-sensitive adhesive on the tile/plank back; covered with a peel-off liner
- Peel and stick installation: fast; accessible DIY
- Bond quality typically lower than site-applied adhesive
- Subfloor preparation requirements are identical to standard glue-down (cleaner, flatter, drier = better bond)
- Not recommended for commercial or high-traffic applications

## 11. INSTALLATION METHODS — RIGID CORE (SPC/WPC)

### 11.1 Click Floating — Overview

SPC and WPC products almost universally use a click floating installation method — the same mechanical locking click systems used in laminate (Uniclic, Valinge 5G, I4F, and proprietary equivalents), adapted for the dimensions and material properties of rigid core LVT.

**Key differences from laminate floating:**
- The product is waterproof — the subfloor DPM is still needed to prevent mould, but moisture will not damage the floor itself
- Thermal expansion of SPC is lower than laminate — expansion gap requirements are smaller for equivalent room dimensions
- The click profile is pressed into PVC/SPC material, not HDF — it behaves differently under stress

### 11.2 SPC Floating — Expansion Gap Requirements

Because SPC has a much lower thermal expansion coefficient than laminate or wood:
- Standard expansion gap: **8–10mm at walls** for rooms up to 8m in standard conditions
- For conservatories or rooms with high solar gain: **10–12mm** (temperature swings are larger)
- Intermediate joints in very long or wide spaces: maximum **15m** floating panel (SPC can span longer than laminate before intermediate joints are needed)

**The conservatory rule:**
SPC is one of the few hard flooring products that is genuinely well-suited to conservatories and rooms with extreme temperature variation. However, even SPC has limits:
- In a glass conservatory in summer, floor surface temperature can reach 40–50°C
- At these temperatures, even SPC expands; if the perimeter gap is inadequate, buckling occurs
- Specify 12mm minimum gaps; consider glue-down flexible LVT as an alternative for extreme conservatory installations

### 11.3 Installation Sequence for Click SPC/WPC

1. Prepare subfloor (3mm/2m tolerance; DPM over concrete)
2. Install underlay if not pre-attached to board back
3. Set expansion spacers at starting wall
4. First row: board with groove to wall; lock end joints
5. Subsequent rows: angle-fold or push-lock per click system; stagger end joints minimum 300mm
6. Last row: measure; rip; pull bar to close; remove spacers
7. Fit skirting/beading to cover gap; fix to wall only

**The angle-fold vs push-lock on SPC:** SPC click profiles are engineered to work with either angled-fold or horizontal push depending on the product. Do not force an angled-fold system horizontally — the SPC core can crack. Always follow the specific product's installation method.

### 11.4 Stair Installation (SPC)

- SPC planks can be used on stair treads and risers
- The rigid core is more stable on stairs than flexible LVT
- Must be glued (construction adhesive) to stair tread; do not float on stairs
- Use stair nosing profile to cap the leading edge; must be adhered and mechanically fixed (face screw or brad nail)
- The hard, smooth surface of SPC on stairs may require anti-slip treatment; check product slip resistance coefficient

## 12. GLUE-DOWN INSTALLATION — DETAIL

### 12.1 Adhesive Selection for LVT Glue-Down

**PSA (Pressure-Sensitive Adhesive) / Tackifier:**
- The standard adhesive for residential and light commercial LVT
- Applied to subfloor; dries to a "dry tack" (no wet adhesive present when board is laid)
- Repositionable for approximately 20–30 minutes after laying
- Adequate bond for most residential applications
- Solvent-free; low VOC
- Brands: Karndean K-ADH, Amtico SF Adhesive, F. Ball Styccobond F41, Bostik Greenstar

**Full-bond acrylic dispersion:**
- Stronger, more permanent bond than PSA
- Required for: commercial applications; areas with heavy rolling loads; heavy-gauge LVT
- May not allow repositioning once pressed down
- Spread rate critical: too much = adhesive bleed-up through joints; too little = inadequate coverage

**High-temperature/conservatory adhesive:**
- Specifically formulated for high-temperature environments; maintains bond at elevated temperatures
- Standard adhesives can soften in temperatures above 35–40°C, allowing flexible LVT to "creep" (the floor shifts slowly in one direction)
- Required in conservatories and full-sun rooms

### 12.2 Adhesive Open Time and Tack

The most critical variable in glue-down LVT installation:

**Applying too early (wet adhesive):**
- Board floats in adhesive; adhesive squeezes between boards; board can be displaced
- Bond is good once cured but installation is messy

**Applying at correct tack (dry, tacky surface):**
- Board bonds firmly on contact; no adhesive squeeze-up; repositioning possible briefly
- Optimal result

**Applying too late (adhesive has dried beyond tack):**
- Board does not bond; hollow areas; adhesive bond fails
- Must remove adhesive and re-apply

**Testing tack:** Touch adhesive with knuckle — should feel slightly sticky but no adhesive should transfer to skin. This is the correct laying point.

**Factors affecting open time:**
- High temperature: shortens open time (adhesive dries faster)
- Low humidity: shortens open time
- High humidity: extends open time
- Absorbent subfloor: shortens open time (moisture absorbed quickly)
- Porous substrate (new concrete): prime first to reduce absorption and stabilise open time

### 12.3 Roller — Technique

- Use a 68kg 3-section roller (a flat rolling pin format; not a cylindrical roller)
- Roll in two directions (along and across the boards) immediately after each section is laid
- Re-roll after 20–30 minutes while adhesive is still active
- In commercial installation: re-roll after 24 hours as well
- A roller is NOT optional — it is a fundamental requirement for full adhesive contact

### 12.4 Joint Sealing in Wet Areas

For glue-down LVT in bathrooms, wet rooms, or other splash-risk areas:
- Flexible tile joint sealant (LVT-specific silicone or polyurethane) along all joints after adhesive has cured
- Silicone beading at all wall junctions (at the floor/wall meeting point)
- This creates a surface water barrier; it does not make the floor impermeable but prevents pooled water from sitting in joints and migrating beneath

## 13. UNDERLAY FOR LVT

### 13.1 Glue-Down LVT — No Underlay

Flexible glue-down LVT is installed directly to the subfloor; no underlay is used. Any underlay under a glue-down product would compromise the adhesive bond and allow the floor to move. The adhesive is the interface layer.

### 13.2 Floating SPC/WPC — Underlay Options

**Pre-attached (factory-bonded) foam underlay:**
- IXPE (Irradiation Cross-linked Polyethylene foam): the standard; good density; consistent performance
- EVA (Ethylene-Vinyl Acetate foam): softer; warmer; used in some WPC products
- Cork backing: used in some premium products; lower R-value (better for UFH); better acoustic
- Thickness: typically 1–2mm attached underlay; thin by design — thicker attached underlay increases R-value and reduces UFH efficiency
- Never use a separate underlay under a board with pre-attached underlay

**Separate underlay (no pre-attached underlay on board):**
- As per engineered wood and laminate underlays (Lexicon 03, Section 10 and Lexicon 04, Section 11)
- PE foam + DPM (standard residential)
- Cork with DPM (better acoustic and thermal; lower R-value)
- Acoustic rubber (apartment specification)
- Maximum thickness for SPC/WPC floating: 3mm — thicker underlay creates excessive flex in the rigid core and stresses click joints

### 13.3 DPM Requirement

Over concrete, a DPM is always required under floating LVT:
- Not because the LVT itself is damaged by moisture (it is not)
- Because moisture vapour rising through the subfloor will condense between the floor and the subfloor in cooler weather
- This condensation promotes mould growth, which can damage the subfloor and cause hygiene issues
- A polythene DPM (minimum 150-micron, ideally 300-micron) or DPM-integrated underlay is required in all floating LVT over concrete applications

## 14. UNDERFLOOR HEATING

### 14.1 LVT Over UFH — Advantages

LVT is one of the best floor coverings for use over underfloor heating:

1. **Very low thermal resistance:** Most LVT products at 2–5mm total thickness have an R-value of 0.01–0.05 m²K/W — substantially below the 0.15 m²K/W limit. The floor imposes almost no thermal penalty on the UFH system.

2. **No moisture sensitivity:** Unlike wood, LVT does not shrink, gap, or check as the heated room dries out in winter. The primary failure mode of wood over UFH (excessive drying) does not apply to LVT.

3. **Wide species options:** Any design effect can be used over UFH — there is no need to avoid wide boards or plain-sawn cuts as with solid wood.

### 14.2 Maximum Surface Temperature

- Maximum floor surface temperature for LVT: **27°C** (standard recommendation)
- Some manufacturers specify up to **28°C** or even **30°C** for SPC products — check specific product data sheet
- At temperatures above 27–28°C sustained:
  - PVC plasticisers can slowly migrate to the surface
  - Adhesive can soften in glue-down applications
  - Dimensional changes increase
- Programme the UFH thermostat with a floor temperature sensor (not air temperature) to control surface temperature precisely

### 14.3 UFH Type Compatibility

**Warm water hydronic (in screed):**
- Excellent; the most stable system; slow temperature rise prevents thermal shock
- Commission screed fully before installation; RH test after commissioning

**Electric mat (in adhesive bed/thin-set):**
- Compatible; the thin mat adds negligible R-value
- Use a floor temperature sensor thermostat — not a room air sensor
- Avoid rapid temperature cycling

**Electric foil:**
- Compatible; the foil is essentially R-value zero
- Ensure foil is flat before installing LVT over it
- With glue-down: the adhesive goes directly over the foil — ensure adhesive compatibility with the foil system

**UFH with glue-down LVT:**
- The preferred approach; maximum thermal efficiency
- Use a specifically UFH-rated adhesive (check product data sheet; temperature limits stated)
- Do not use standard PSA tackifier over UFH if the floor will regularly reach 25°C+ — standard PSA may soften; use a heat-resistant full-bond adhesive

## 15. ACOUSTIC PERFORMANCE

### 15.1 Impact Sound — LVT Systems

LVT is a hard surface; without acoustic treatment, it transmits impact sound (footfall) more than carpet but generally better than tile or stone. Approved Document E compliance is a concern in apartments.

**ΔLw values by LVT system:**

| System | Typical ΔLw |
|--------|------------|
| Flexible glue-down (no underlay) | 3–8 dB |
| Flexible glue-down + acoustic mastic | 14–18 dB |
| SPC click + 1.5mm IXPE attached underlay | 15–18 dB |
| WPC click + 2mm EVA attached underlay | 16–20 dB |
| SPC click + 3mm cork underlay (separate) | 18–21 dB |
| SPC click + 5mm acoustic rubber mat | 20–24 dB |
| Double-stick (acoustic mat + glue) | 19–23 dB |

### 15.2 Healthcare-Specific Acoustic LVT

Premium commercial LVT for healthcare (Tarkett iQ, Forbo Eternal, Altro Whiterock products) incorporates heavy backing compounds and acoustic cushion layers specifically engineered to:
- Reduce footfall noise in patient areas
- Reduce impact sound transmission to wards below
- Maintain the acoustic environment of clinical spaces (reduced noise = improved patient outcomes per NHS evidence)

## 16. EXPANSION GAPS AND MOVEMENT JOINTS

### 16.1 Flexible Glue-Down

- No perimeter expansion gap required — the adhesive bond controls all dimensional movement
- However: silicone bead at all wall junctions provides waterproofing in wet areas and accommodates any residual thermal movement
- The floor IS constrained by the adhesive; if it expands significantly (high temperature), the adhesive must absorb the stress — use heat-resistant adhesive in warm environments

### 16.2 SPC Click Floating

| Room dimension | Minimum expansion gap |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Up to 8m | 8–10mm |
| 8–12m | 10–12mm |
| 12–15m | 12–15mm |
| Over 15m | Intermediate T-bar; 10mm each panel |
| Conservatory | 12mm minimum regardless of room size |

**Maximum floating panel (SPC): 15–20m** in either direction before intermediate joint — significantly larger than laminate or engineered wood due to lower thermal expansion.

### 16.3 WPC Click Floating

WPC has higher thermal expansion than SPC:
| Room dimension | Minimum expansion gap |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Up to 6m | 10mm |
| 6–10m | 12mm |
| Over 10m | 12–15mm; consider intermediate joints |

**Maximum floating panel (WPC): 10–12m** — closer to laminate/engineered wood limits due to higher thermal expansion coefficient.

## 17. THRESHOLD DETAILS AND TRANSITIONS

### 17.1 LVT to Carpet

- LVT is typically 4–8mm; carpet + underlay is 15–22mm
- If carpet is higher: use a carpet gripper transition that steps down from carpet to LVT level
- If LVT is higher (unusual): use a reducer profile

### 17.2 LVT to Tile

- LVT typically 4–8mm; tile + adhesive typically 12–15mm
- LVT is almost always lower than tile; a ramp reducer transitions
- For flush/zero-threshold between LVT and tile: both floor coverings must be specified to achieve the same finished height — careful planning of adhesive/screed depths required

### 17.3 LVT to Wood (Engineered or Solid)

- Similar heights depending on installation methods
- T-bar for same level; reducer for height differential
- In bathrooms where LVT meets solid wood outside a shower enclosure: silicone bead at junction; the wood must be at risk assessment of any splash

### 17.4 LVT at Wet Room Boundaries

- At the boundary between a wet room (waterproof construction) and an adjacent LVT floor:
- The wet room tanking membrane must come up the wall and return beneath the LVT at the threshold
- Silicone bead over the junction
- Aluminium threshold strip

## 18. PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 18.1 Lifting Edges (Peaking / Tenting) — Glue-Down

**Symptoms:** Tile or plank edges lifting from the subfloor; visible curl at corners.

**Causes:**
1. **Adhesive failure** — inadequate tack at time of laying; floor not rolled; adhesive incompatibility with primer or subfloor
2. **Contaminated subfloor** — oil, wax, old adhesive residue preventing bond
3. **Moisture** — high RH under glue-down causing adhesive failure; DPM not installed
4. **Temperature extremes** — PSA tackifier softened in high-temperature room; adhesive bond released
5. **Wrong adhesive for product** — some LVT products have specific adhesive requirements; incompatible adhesive type

**Diagnosis:**
- Lift a corner: is there adhesive on the board back (bond failure) or no adhesive contact at all (adhesive failed to wet the surface)?
- Check subfloor for contamination or moisture
- Check if lifting correlates with high-temperature areas (near radiators, sunny spots)

### 18.2 Tenting / Buckling — Click Floating

**Symptoms:** Boards lifting from centre; panels rising from subfloor; more common in summer or high-temperature periods.

**Causes:**
1. **Insufficient expansion gap** — as with laminate; the dominant cause
2. **Excessive temperature** — boards expanded beyond the gap capacity
3. **Fixed objects on floor** — furniture or fittings constraining the floating panel
4. **No intermediate joint in large area**

**Action:** Same as laminate (Lexicon 04, Section 17.1) — check and free gaps; boards typically return flat when temperature normalises if gaps are freed promptly

### 18.3 Indentation from Heavy Loads

**Symptoms:** Permanent depressions in the LVT surface under heavy furniture, appliances, or equipment.

**Causes:**
- PVC is thermoplastic — it will deform under sustained heavy point loads, particularly at elevated temperatures
- Standard residential furniture: usually acceptable
- Piano, fridge-freezer, washing machine, heavy filing cabinet, etc.: may cause permanent indentation, particularly in warmer conditions

**Prevention:**
- Use floor protectors/castors with wide diameter (distribute load over larger area)
- Appliance feet: use appliance pads or a plywood panel under heavy items
- Commercial heavy loads: specify heavy-duty commercial-grade product; or use hard tile/vinyl composition tile for the area

**Indentation Recovery Test (EN ISO 24343):** The test method for measuring residual indentation after load removal. Commercial products specify maximum residual indentation values per EN ISO 10874 class.

### 18.4 Discolouration

**Symptoms:** Yellow, orange, or brown discolouration of the LVT surface; may be localised or widespread.

**Causes:**
1. **Rubber mat reaction** — rubber-backed entrance mats or anti-fatigue mats placed on LVT; sulfur compounds in rubber react with PVC, producing a permanent yellow/orange stain; irreversible
2. **Plasticiser migration from rubber** — softer rubber items (chair mats, gym mats) in prolonged contact
3. **Chemical contamination** — cleaning products with bleach at high concentration; some disinfectants
4. **Solar UV** — some older formulations fade under intense UV; modern products are UV-stable; check certification
5. **Adhesive bleed-through** — some adhesives, particularly older solvent-based types, can yellow the surface over time

**Prevention:** Only use purpose-made LVT/vinyl entrance mats (PVC-backed or no backing); do not use rubber-backed mats directly on LVT.

### 18.5 Joint Gaps — Click Floating

**Symptoms:** Visible gaps between boards; boards pulling apart at long or short side joints.

**Causes:**
1. **Thermal contraction** — cold weather; low RH; boards contracted; gaps open seasonally
2. **Boards not fully engaged during installation** — click joint was not completely locked
3. **Floating panel too large** — accumulated movement from thermal expansion/contraction
4. **SPC core cracking** — if the click joint was subjected to excessive force (subfloor hollow, heavy impact), the SPC core can crack at the click profile; the joint opens and cannot be re-closed

**Note on SPC click cracking:** Unlike HDF laminate (which also can crack), SPC click profiles can be more brittle. Heavy impact (e.g., dropping a heavy object on the joint) can shatter the click profile in SPC. This is a known limitation vs WPC (more flexible) and flexible LVT (no click joint). Replacement of the affected board is the only remedy.

### 18.6 Hollow Sound Underfoot

**Causes:**
- Glue-down: inadequate adhesive coverage (hollows where no adhesive contact)
- Floating: inherent to floating method — the air between underlay and subfloor amplifies sound
- Thin underlay or no underlay under floating product

**For glue-down:** Tap test the floor; localise hollow areas; re-bond by injecting LVT adhesive through a syringe from the edge (limited effectiveness) or by lifting and re-laying affected boards.

## 19. MAINTENANCE AND CARE

### 19.1 Routine Maintenance

- Regular sweeping/microfibre dust mopping — as with all hard floors; grit is the enemy
- Suction-only vacuuming; hard floor setting
- Felt pads under all furniture
- Do not use rubber-backed mats (see 18.4)
- Entrance matting at all external doors

### 19.2 Damp Mopping

LVT is waterproof — but this does not mean it should be wet-mopped aggressively:
- Excess water can ingress through click joints (floating) or tile joints (glue-down) and:
  - Pool under the floor (mould risk between floor and subfloor)
  - Affect the subfloor if wood-based (timber moisture rise)
- **Damp mop only; wring thoroughly; no standing water on the surface**
- Use pH-neutral LVT/vinyl-specific cleaner
- No abrasive cleaners; no solvent-based cleaners; no bleach at high concentration

### 19.3 Cleaning Products

- **Recommended:** Manufacturer-specific cleaner; Karndean LVT Cleaner; Amtico Clean; Forbo Cleaner; Altro Safe'n'Sure; general pH-neutral floor cleaner
- **Not recommended:** Bleach at high dilution (staining risk); solvent-based products (can soften PVC surface); abrasive powder cleaners; wax or polish (leaves residue; makes surface slippery)
- **Surface refresher/polish (where specified):** Some LVT manufacturers supply a compatible polish/refresher to restore sheen on commercial floors where the PU treatment has dulled through heavy use; use only manufacturer-compatible products

### 19.4 Deep Cleaning (Commercial)

- Machine scrubbing with low-speed pad; neutral detergent
- High-speed burnishing for gloss-finish commercial LVT
- Strip and re-seal (floor care programme): strip old polish/maintenance coat; apply fresh maintenance coat; used on commercial programme cleaning cycles
- IICRC S100 (carpet) and proprietary manufacturer deep-clean specifications apply

### 19.5 Anti-Scuff Treatment

- Commercial kitchen-grade LVT or heavily used public area LVT can be protected with a compatible anti-scuff maintenance coat
- Applied as a thin liquid layer over the PU surface; provides sacrificial protection
- Must be stripped periodically and reapplied

## 20. COMMERCIAL APPLICATIONS

### 20.1 Healthcare

**Why LVT dominates healthcare flooring:**
- Seamless or near-seamless (sheet or heat-welded tile) surface eliminates microbial harbour points
- Chemical resistance to hospital-grade disinfectants (subject to product specification)
- Comfort and acoustic performance in ward environments
- Slip resistance (anti-slip products comply with DIN 51130, HSE guidance)
- Warm aesthetics support patient wellbeing

**Healthcare-specific products:**
- **Altro Whiterock / Altro Aquarius:** Safety vinyl with integrated anti-slip quartz; the dominant UK standard for healthcare, education wet areas
- **Tarkett iQ Natural / Granit:** Heavy commercial homogeneous vinyl; weldable; chemical resistant
- **Forbo Eternal / Marmoleum (linoleum — see Lexicon 06):** Acoustic and comfort-focused commercial products
- **Karndean commercial / Amtico Signature commercial:** Luxury LVT adapted for healthcare aesthetics in private healthcare

**Chemical resistance testing:** EN ISO 26987 (staining agents); EN ISO 175 (immersion); critical in healthcare where concentrated disinfectants, bleach, and clinical chemicals are used.

**Heat welding:** In commercial applications, tile/plank joints are eliminated by heat welding — a hot-air gun melts a PVC welding rod into the joint, creating a seamless surface. Essential in clinical areas, food production, and wet rooms. See Section 20.4 below.

### 20.2 Education

- Heavy commercial product (Class 33–34 per EN ISO 10874)
- Anti-slip properties; slip rating R10 or better (DIN 51130)
- Chemical resistance to cleaning products
- Acoustic consideration per BB93 (reverberation time)
- Sustainability: low VOC; phthalate-free; recyclable at end of life

### 20.3 Hospitality

- **Hotel bedrooms:** Premium LVT with 0.7mm wear layer; wood-effect plank; EN ISO 10874 Class 32 minimum
- **Hotel corridors:** Class 33–34; heavy commercial LVT; high acoustic mat below
- **Restaurants/bars:** Stone or wood effect; slip resistance; heat resistance at droppings; spillage tolerance
- **Spas/wet areas:** Specialist anti-slip LVT; rated to DIN 51097 class B or C

### 20.4 Heat Welding

Heat welding is the process used to create seamless commercial LVT floors:

**Process:**
1. Lay all tiles/planks; allow adhesive to cure fully (minimum 24 hours)
2. Groove-cut along all joints using a proprietary groove cutter (creates a V-groove approx 2mm wide × 2mm deep along each joint)
3. Preheat the hot-air welding gun to approximately 350–400°C (product-specific)
4. Feed welding rod (matching PVC rod in correct colour/pattern) continuously through the groove while moving the gun at approximately 1m/30 seconds
5. The welding rod melts and fuses with the cut groove edges; the joint becomes monolithic PVC
6. Allow to cool; trim excess rod flush with surface using a spatula (first pass: above surface; second pass when cool: flush to surface)
7. The result: a fully seamless, hygienically clean, waterproof floor surface

**Welding rod selection:** Must exactly match the base product — same PVC compound, same colour. Mismatched rod is visible as a visible seam line of different colour/texture.

**Coved skirting:** In commercial and healthcare applications, LVT is often coved up the wall to create a continuous, cleanable surface without a floor/wall junction. Achieved using a purpose-made cove former and coved LVT material; the cove is welded at the joint between floor and cove.

## 21. LVT VS COMPETING PRODUCTS — HONEST COMPARISON

### 21.1 LVT vs Laminate

| Factor | LVT (SPC, 0.55mm WL) | Laminate (AC4, 12mm) |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Waterproof | Yes | No |
| Scratch resistance | Good | Very high (Al₂O₃) |
| Authenticity (visual) | Very good | Very good (EIR) |
| Thermal expansion | Very low (SPC) | Medium |
| UFH performance | Excellent | Good (with limits) |
| Suitability for bathrooms | Yes | No (standard) |
| Underfoot feel | Warmer; softer (with foam backing) | Slightly hollow; harder |
| Refinishing | Never | Never |
| Acoustic | Moderate (floating) | Moderate (floating) |
| Cost | Comparable | Lower to comparable |

**Verdict:** For any moisture-risk environment, LVT is clearly superior. For dry environments, laminate's superior scratch resistance (aluminium oxide) and typically firmer feel may give it a slight edge. For UFH, LVT's lower thermal resistance and stability advantage tilt toward LVT.

### 21.2 LVT vs Engineered Wood

| Factor | LVT (SPC, 0.55mm WL) | Engineered Wood (4mm WL) |
|--------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Authenticity | Very good | Genuine (real wood) |
| Waterproof | Yes | No (moisture management) |
| Refinishing | Never | Yes (2–4 times) |
| Lifespan | 15–25 years | 30–50+ years |
| UFH performance | Excellent | Very good (glue-down) |
| Maintenance | Easy | More care required |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Resale perception | Depends on market | Generally positive |
| Suitable for bathrooms | Yes | No |

**Verdict:** For long-term premium installations in dry areas, engineered wood remains the superior product aesthetically and in longevity. For bathrooms, kitchens, rental properties, or anywhere moisture management is difficult, LVT is the correct specification.

### 21.3 LVT vs Ceramic/Porcelain Tile

| Factor | LVT | Ceramic/Porcelain Tile |
|--------|-----|----------------------|
| Waterproof | Yes | Yes (grout permeable) |
| Hardness/durability | High | Very high |
| Underfoot warmth | Medium | Cold (without UFH) |
| Acoustic (footfall) | Better | Poor (hard) |
| Installation complexity | Low-medium | Medium-high |
| Cost | Lower | Medium-higher |
| Repairability | Individual board | Individual tile (if matched) |
| Slip resistance | Product-dependent | Product-dependent |
| Authenticity | Very good | Genuine |

**Verdict:** For wet rooms where visual authenticity of stone or tile is important and hardness/durability is paramount, real tile remains superior. For residential bathroom/kitchen where comfort, acoustic, and installation ease matter, LVT is a strong alternative. The visual quality of premium LVT tile effects is very high — distinction from real tile requires close inspection.

## 22. BRITISH AND EUROPEAN STANDARDS

**EN ISO 10874:2012 — Resilient, textile, and laminate floor coverings — Classification:**
- The primary usage classification standard for LVT; Classes 21–43
- Replaces the older EN 685 standard; the current reference for all commercial and residential specification

**EN ISO 24343-1:2011 — Resilient floor coverings — Determination of indentation — Residual indentation:**
- Tests the floor covering's resistance to permanent indentation under static load
- Critical for specifying LVT under heavy furniture and equipment

**EN ISO 24345:2012 — Resilient floor coverings — Determination of flexibility:**
- Relevant to flexible LVT; determines conformity to curved surfaces and subfloor irregularities

**EN ISO 26987:2010 — Plastics — Vinyl chloride homopolymer and copolymer resins — Determination of absorption of plasticizer:**
- Relevant to PVC formulation testing; not a direct user-facing standard but part of the manufacturing quality framework

**EN 13501-1 — Reaction to fire:**
- LVT fire classification; most standard residential LVT: DFL-s1 or CFL-s1
- Commercial applications: Bfl-s1 required in escape routes and some public areas
- For healthcare and high-occupancy buildings: verify specific fire classification requirement per Approved Document B

**EN 13893 — Slip resistance:**
- Dynamic coefficient of friction; ≥0.30 for general use
- Wet areas require higher values — reference DIN 51130 or Pendulum test (UKSRG)

**DIN 51130 (German) — R-value classification for workplaces:**
- R9: ≥μ0.30; minimum commercial
- R10: slightly higher; recommended commercial
- R11: wet area commercial (industrial kitchens, changing rooms)
- R12/R13: high anti-slip; specialist industrial

**DIN 51097 — A/B/C classification for barefoot wet areas:**
- Class A: minimum; changing rooms
- Class B: pool surrounds
- Class C: pool steps, ramps; highest anti-slip requirement

**BS 8203:2017 — Installation of resilient floor coverings:**
- The primary UK installation standard for LVT and all resilient flooring
- Subfloor preparation; moisture testing; adhesive application; surface flatness tolerances
- Essential reference for all LVT installation work

**EN 14041:2004+A1:2006 — Resilient, textile, and laminate floor coverings — Essential characteristics:**
- The CE marking standard for floor coverings; covers: reaction to fire; formaldehyde; pentachlorophenol; heavy metals; electrostatic propensity; slip resistance; thermal resistance; sound reduction
- LVT placed on UK/EU market must bear UKCA/CE mark confirming compliance

**REACH Regulation (EC) 1907/2006:**
- The primary chemical safety regulation; governs phthalate plasticisers (DEHP, BBP, DBP, DIBP restricted); other SVHCs
- All LVT for UK/EU market must demonstrate REACH compliance

## 23. SUSTAINABILITY AND ENVIRONMENTAL CONSIDERATIONS

### 23.1 PVC and Sustainability

PVC is a petroleum-derived polymer; its manufacture and disposal have significant environmental impact:

**Chlorine:** 57% of PVC by weight is chlorine, derived from the chlor-alkali process. While chlorine is abundant (from salt), the process is energy-intensive.

**Plasticisers:** Traditional phthalate plasticisers are classified as endocrine disruptors. Modern alternatives (DOTP, DINCH) have substantially improved toxicity profiles. Specify phthalate-free for all healthcare, education, and children's environments.

**VOC emissions:** PVC flooring can off-gas VOCs during and after installation. Modern UV-stable formulations with PU treatment emit very low VOCs. AgBB/DIBt certified products and those with FloorScore or GreenGuard Gold certification provide verified low-emission assurance.

### 23.2 End-of-Life

**Recycling — the principal challenge:**
PVC is technically recyclable but practically difficult:
- Mixed-material construction (PVC, glass fibre, foam) makes material separation difficult
- PVC containing plasticisers requires specialised processing
- UK PVC recycling infrastructure is limited compared to some European countries

**Industry initiatives:**
- **VinylPlus:** The European PVC industry's voluntary sustainability commitment; targets for recycled content and end-of-life recycling
- **Recofloor (UK):** Industry take-back scheme for unused offcuts and waste LVT; collected material processed into new products
- Some manufacturers (Tarkett, Forbo) operate product take-back schemes for end-of-life floors in commercial installations
- Tarkett's "ReStart" programme provides fully reclaimed material routes for their products

**Bio-based PVC:**
- PVC produced using bio-based vinyl chloride monomer (from ethanol) rather than fossil VCM
- Currently a small fraction of production; growing interest
- Identical performance to conventional PVC; lower carbon footprint for the polymer production phase

### 23.3 EPD and BREEAM

- EPDs (Environmental Product Declarations) are available from major LVT manufacturers (Tarkett, Forbo, Altro, Karndean, Amtico)
- Required for BREEAM Mat 01 (Life Cycle Impacts) and Mat 03 (Responsible Sourcing) credits
- LEED credits (v4 IEQ prerequisite: low-emitting materials) require GreenGuard Gold or equivalent certification

### 23.4 Key Sustainability Certifications

| Certification | What it covers |
|---|---|
| FloorScore | Low VOC emissions; SCS (Scientific Certification Systems) |
| GreenGuard Gold | Very low VOC; suitable for schools and healthcare |
| EU Ecolabel | Whole product lifecycle; substances; recyclability |
| Declare (Living Future Institute) | Full ingredients transparency; Red List chemical avoidance |
| EPD (EN 15804) | Third-party verified lifecycle assessment |
| Phthalate-free declaration | DEHP, BBP, DBP, DIBP free; verified by REACH compliance |
