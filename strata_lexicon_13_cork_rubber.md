## 1. CORK FLOORING — MATERIAL SCIENCE AND ORIGIN

### 1.1 What Cork Is

Cork is the bark of the **cork oak tree** (*Quercus suber*), a species native to the western Mediterranean basin — primarily Portugal, Spain, Algeria, Morocco, Italy, Tunisia, and France. Portugal alone produces approximately 50% of the world's cork supply and has done so for centuries.

The cork oak is remarkable: its bark can be harvested without felling the tree. The outer bark is stripped by hand every **9–12 years**, after which the tree regenerates a new cork layer. A healthy cork oak lives for 150–250 years and can be harvested 15–20 times over its lifetime. The first harvest (at approximately 25 years of age) produces "virgin cork" — irregular and not suitable for wine stoppers or flooring. Subsequent harvests produce "amadia cork" of increasingly high quality.

### 1.2 Cork Cell Structure

Cork's unique properties arise entirely from its cellular microstructure:

- Cork consists of approximately **40 million cells per cubic centimetre**
- Each cell is a 14-faced polyhedron (a tetrakaidecahedron) — an extremely efficient packing geometry
- The cell walls are composed of **suberin** (a waxy, water-resistant polymer), **lignin**, and **cellulose**
- Each cell is filled with a gas mixture similar to air; the cells are sealed by the suberin walls
- This structure makes cork approximately **50% air by volume** — hence its low density, compressibility, and thermal/acoustic insulation properties

**The physical consequences of this structure:**
- **Compressibility:** Cork compresses under load and springs back — the gas-filled cells act as tiny pneumatic cushions
- **Thermal insulation:** The trapped air in each cell is an excellent thermal insulator; cork has one of the lowest thermal conductivities of any natural solid
- **Acoustic insulation:** The compressible cell structure absorbs vibration and sound energy
- **Low density:** Approximately 120–240 kg/m³ (compare: water at 1,000 kg/m³; oak wood at 700 kg/m³)
- **Impermeability:** The suberin cell walls resist water, oil, and most liquids; cork does not rot
- **Fire behaviour:** Cork chars but does not sustain combustion; it self-extinguishes

### 1.3 From Tree to Floor

**Harvesting:**
- Stripped by hand with long-handled axes; skilled harvesters (tiradores) remove the bark without damaging the cambium beneath
- Planks of bark stacked and left to dry outdoors for 6 months (improves quality; reduces moisture)

**Processing for flooring:**
- Cork bark is boiled: softens the material; removes tannins and some volatile compounds; the cells expand and become more regular
- After boiling: bark is dried, sorted by quality, and cut
- **Wine stopper cork:** punched from the highest-quality solid bark planks — the offcuts and lower-quality bark go to flooring
- **Cork granulate:** remaining bark is ground into granules of different sizes
- **Agglomerated cork:** granules are compressed with adhesive (natural or synthetic) and baked; produces uniform slabs or rolls used in flooring, underlay, and insulation

## 2. CORK FLOORING — PRODUCT TYPES AND CONSTRUCTION

### 2.1 Solid Cork Tile (Traditional)

**Construction:** Agglomerated cork compressed into tiles; typically 4–6mm thick; sometimes with a veneer of decorative cork on the face.

**Dimensions:** Typically 300×300mm or 600×300mm

**Installation:** Glue-down only; direct adhesive bond to prepared subfloor

**Finish:** Raw cork requires site-applied finishing (wax, oil, or lacquer) after installation

**Characteristics:**
- The most traditional cork flooring format
- Uniform, natural cork appearance through the tile depth
- Can be sanded and refinished multiple times if the tile is thick enough
- The softest and most comfortable underfoot of all cork formats
- Less dimensionally stable than cork-topped engineered products

### 2.2 Cork-Faced Engineered Board (Click or Glue-Down)

**Construction:** A thin cork veneer (2–4mm) bonded to an HDF or wood-fibre core; either with a click locking system (floating installation) or flat-back for glue-down; cork veneer on the top surface; balancing cork or HDF layer on the base.

**Dimensions:** Typically 900×185mm or 900×295mm plank format; 4–12mm total thickness

**Advantages:**
- Click format allows floating installation — accessible DIY; no adhesive
- More dimensionally stable than solid cork tile
- Factory-finished options (UV lacquer or UV oil) — no site finishing required
- The HDF core provides flatness and dimensional precision

**Products:** Wicanders Cork Flooring (Amorim group — Portugal); Granorte; Corkart; USFloors NaturalCork

### 2.3 Cork Underlay (Non-Flooring but Relevant)

Cork granulate compressed into sheet form is extensively used as underlay beneath other floor coverings:
- Under floating wood and laminate: thermal and acoustic benefit; low R-value (better for UFH than foam)
- Under ceramic tile: limited application but cork tile underlay exists for some low-flex timber substrates
- The underlay function is distinct from the cork as a floor covering in its own right

### 2.4 WPC-Cork Hybrid

A newer format: cork granulate blended into a WPC (Wood Plastic Composite) core, with a cork veneer face:
- The cork in the core improves acoustic and thermal performance over standard WPC
- The WPC provides waterproofing and rigidity
- A genuinely waterproof cork-aesthetic product — suitable for bathrooms and kitchens where standard cork is problematic

## 3. CORK PROPERTIES — PERFORMANCE CHARACTERISTICS

### 3.1 Comfort and Acoustic Performance

**Underfoot comfort:**
- The air-filled cell structure absorbs impact as you walk — cork has a natural "give" that no rigid floor covering can replicate
- Anti-fatigue benefit: significant; cork flooring is among the most comfortable for prolonged standing (kitchens, workshops, studios)
- Warm underfoot: the low thermal conductivity means cork does not feel cold underfoot — a significant residential benefit, particularly for barefoot use in bedrooms and kitchens

**Impact sound reduction:**
- Cork tile (4–6mm) achieves ΔLw approximately 12–18 dB — competitive with standard foam underlay
- Cork-faced engineered (click, 10mm): ΔLw approximately 14–20 dB
- Very good for apartments; often exceeds equivalent-thickness LVT or laminate products in acoustic performance

**Airborne sound absorption:**
- The porous cell structure absorbs sound energy; a cork-floored room has measurably shorter reverberation time than a tile-floored room of equivalent dimensions
- Used in music studios and recording environments as part of an acoustic treatment package

### 3.2 Thermal Properties

**Thermal conductivity (λ):** approximately **0.04–0.065 W/mK** — among the lowest of any natural flooring material

**Thermal resistance (R-value):**
- 4mm cork tile: R = 0.004 ÷ 0.050 = **0.080 m²K/W**
- 6mm cork tile: R = **0.120 m²K/W**
- 10mm cork-engineered board: R ≈ **0.140–0.180 m²K/W**

**Implication for UFH:** Cork's low thermal conductivity is a double-edged sword:
- It makes cork warm and comfortable underfoot without UFH (an advantage in non-UFH rooms)
- Over UFH: a 6mm cork tile uses most of the 0.15 m²K/W budget alone; a 10mm cork-faced board may exceed it
- For UFH applications: specify the thinnest cork format (4mm tile or thin cork veneer on HDF) and confirm combined R-value

### 3.3 Durability and Wear

**Hardness:** Cork is softer than most floor coverings — the Brinell hardness of cork is approximately 0.3–0.6 (compare: oak wood ~3.0; porcelain ~8.0). This means:
- Cork will show indentations from heavy furniture legs (mitigated by wide furniture feet)
- High-heel damage is a risk in domestic applications
- Surface scratching from grit is more apparent than on harder surfaces
- Not suitable for high-traffic commercial areas without a hard protective finish

**Resilience:** The cellular structure means cork recovers from light to moderate compression remarkably well — furniture impressions left overnight typically recover within hours of the furniture being moved, particularly in wax-finished cork.

**Surface hardness varies by finish:**
- Wax-finished cork: softest surface; most susceptible to marks and water; most authentic feel
- Oil-finished cork: moderate protection; natural feel retained
- UV lacquered cork: hardest surface; best scratch resistance; reduces the natural "give" feel slightly but dramatically improves durability

### 3.4 Moisture and Water Resistance

**The paradox of cork and water:**
- The **cell walls** (suberin) are inherently water-resistant and impermeable
- However, the **joints between cork tiles** or the **edges of cork boards** are not sealed in the same way
- Water ingressing at joints will penetrate to the adhesive or HDF core; HDF in particular swells significantly on water contact

**Practical implications:**
- Cork tile (solid agglomerated): moderate moisture resistance if sealed; joints are the vulnerability
- Cork-faced HDF board: the HDF core is moisture-sensitive; not suitable in wet rooms without specific waterproof product
- WPC-Cork hybrid: genuinely waterproof core; suitable in bathrooms and kitchens
- Standard cork flooring: not suitable in wet rooms, shower areas, or areas with regular water pooling

**Sealing cork:** A good lacquer or polyurethane topcoat significantly improves moisture resistance of the tile surface; joints should be sealed with appropriate sealant in kitchen applications

## 4. CORK FLOORING — GRADING AND AESTHETICS

### 4.1 Natural Cork Appearance

Cork's natural colour is warm amber-brown, with subtle darker flecks from the cell structure variations. The agglomeration process creates a slightly granular, organic texture that is entirely unlike any manufactured floor covering.

**Colour variation:** Natural cork varies from pale honey to deep amber; the colour can also be influenced by the boiling and processing conditions.

**Cork veneer patterns:** In cork-faced engineered products, the veneer can be cut to show different patterns:
- **Random granular:** The standard agglomerated appearance; irregular, natural
- **Half-cork veneer:** Thin slices of natural cork showing the wood-grain-like annual growth structure; more linear and architectural

### 4.2 Stained and Coloured Cork

Modern cork flooring is available in a wide range of colours beyond natural amber:
- Staining: water-based stains applied to the cork surface before sealing; can produce greys, whites, dark browns, blacks, and a spectrum of naturals
- The stain penetrates the cell structure; the coloured cork retains its textural appearance
- White/grey stained cork: extremely popular in Scandinavian and contemporary interiors
- Dark/black cork: dramatic; contemporary

### 4.3 Surface Textures

- Smooth sanded: the standard finish; slightly granular from the aggregate structure
- Wire-brushed: opens the cork surface slightly; more pronounced texture
- Distressed: mechanical treatment for an aged appearance

## 5. CORK — SUBFLOOR REQUIREMENTS AND INSTALLATION

### 5.1 Subfloor Requirements

**Flatness:** Maximum **3mm under 2m straight-edge** for click-floating cork; maximum **2mm** for glue-down cork tile (as cork is thin and will telegraph irregularities)

**Moisture:** Maximum **75% RH** (concrete) / **14% MC** (timber)
- Cork is more moisture-sensitive than many floor coverings; elevated moisture causes swelling of the agglomerated mass and adhesive failure
- DPM essential over concrete

**Structural:** No spring or bounce; cork's cushioning means any subfloor flex is amplified underfoot

### 5.2 Glue-Down Cork Tile Installation

1. Prepare subfloor: flat, dry, clean; prime with appropriate primer
2. Apply pressure-sensitive adhesive (PSA) or contact adhesive (cork-specific)
3. Allow adhesive to reach correct tack
4. Lay tiles from the centre of the room outward (as per standard tile setting out)
5. Press firmly; roll with a 68kg roller
6. Allow adhesive to cure fully (24 hours minimum) before finishing
7. Sand lightly with orbital sander (80–120 grit) to level any minor tile height variation at joints
8. Apply finish (see Section 6)

**Expansion gap:** 8–10mm at all walls; cork expands slightly with humidity change

**Important:** Do not grout cork tile joints; leave a natural joint (approximately 1mm) or butt tightly — the joints are part of the aesthetic

### 5.3 Floating Cork (Click System)

- Lay underlay (DPM/thin PE foam) over concrete
- Click boards together as per standard floating installation (same mechanics as laminate/engineered wood)
- Expansion gap: 8–10mm at all walls
- Factory-finished boards: ready to use after installation (no site finishing)
- Unfinished boards: apply finish after installation

### 5.4 Acclimatisation

Cork must acclimatise before installation:
- 48–72 hours in the installation room; in original packaging but opened
- Room at living conditions (temperature 15–22°C; RH 40–60%)
- Cork responds to humidity; installing in the wrong MC condition leads to expansion (joints close; tiles buckle) or contraction (gaps develop)

## 6. CORK — FINISHING SYSTEMS

### 6.1 Site-Applied Finishes for Raw Cork Tile

After installation and light sanding, apply one of the following:

**Wax:**
- Traditional finish; the original cork finish
- Penetrates the cell structure; feeds and protects the surface
- Very natural feel; low sheen; warm appearance
- Requires regular re-waxing (every 1–3 years depending on traffic)
- Not water-resistant; not suitable for kitchens or bathrooms without accepting the maintenance commitment
- Carnauba wax (plant-based) or beeswax formulations available for a fully natural system

**Oil:**
- Similar to hardwax oil for wood floors (Lexicon 02)
- Penetrates and protects; natural feel; moderate sheen
- Better moisture resistance than wax but not waterproof
- Annual re-oiling recommended in residential

**Water-based polyurethane lacquer:**
- Hard film over the cork surface
- Best moisture and scratch resistance
- More protective but reduces the natural tactile quality of cork
- 2–3 coats; allow each coat to dry fully; light sand between coats
- Practical choice for kitchens and higher-traffic areas

**UV-cured lacquer (factory-applied only):**
- Applied in factory conditions; the hardest, most durable finish
- Not achievable on site; available only on factory-finished products

### 6.2 Maintaining the Finish

- The finish must be periodically renewed to maintain protection
- Wax and oil: spot repair is easy — re-apply in the worn area and blend
- Lacquer: spot repair is more difficult (gloss differential visible); periodic full re-coating more practical

## 7. CORK — UNDERFLOOR HEATING COMPATIBILITY

### 7.1 Cork Over UFH — Proceed with Caution

Cork is not the first choice for UFH applications, but it is not prohibited:

**R-value consideration:**
- 4mm cork tile: R ≈ 0.080 m²K/W — within the 0.15 limit with no underlay (glue-down)
- 6mm cork tile: R ≈ 0.120 m²K/W — within limit for glue-down; leaves minimal headroom
- 10mm cork-faced board with underlay: likely to exceed 0.15 m²K/W — check specific product data

**Thermal cycling effect on cork:**
- Cork is more tolerant of thermal cycling than solid wood (the cellular structure accommodates the slight dimensional changes)
- However, adhesive bond can be stressed by repeated thermal cycling; use a flexible adhesive specifically rated for UFH

**Maximum surface temperature:** 27°C as per all floor coverings

**Recommendation:**
- Over UFH: use the thinnest cork format (4mm glue-down tile); confirm R-value
- Or: consider cork underlay beneath another floor covering (the cork provides acoustic and thermal benefit without the R-value of a full cork floor)

## 8. CORK — MAINTENANCE AND CARE

### 8.1 Routine Maintenance

**Sweeping/dust mopping:** Daily in commercial; regular in residential
- Grit and abrasive particles damage the soft cork surface; removing them is the primary protective measure

**Vacuuming:** Suction only; no beater bar (would damage the cork surface)

**Mopping:**
- Damp mop only; well-wrung; no standing water
- pH-neutral cleaner specifically for cork or wood floors
- Excess water on cork can penetrate joints and cause swelling at tile edges (edge swell similar to laminate — Lexicon 04, Section 8.2)

**What to avoid:**
- Steam cleaners (excess moisture + heat)
- Abrasive cleaners
- Solvent-based products (can dissolve wax or soften some lacquers)
- Bleach at high concentration

### 8.2 Protective Measures

- Felt pads under all furniture: essential; cork is soft and will show furniture marks
- Entrance matting: prevents grit ingress
- Avoid rubber-backed rugs directly on cork (as with LVT — rubber can react with some cork finishes)
- Re-apply wax or oil finish as required; do not allow the finish to deplete completely before renewing

## 9. CORK — SUSTAINABILITY CREDENTIALS

### 9.1 The Case for Cork as the Most Sustainable Floor Covering

Cork flooring has a compelling sustainability narrative that goes beyond most other products:

**Renewable without harvesting the tree:**
- The cork oak is not cut down; only the bark is harvested
- The tree continues to grow; sequesters CO₂ throughout its life (150–250 years)
- Each harvest removes the bark; the tree regenerates; the cycle repeats every 9–12 years

**Carbon sequestration:**
- A cork oak forest (montado/dehesa) is one of the most carbon-rich ecosystems in Europe
- The harvesting of cork bark actually **increases the tree's CO₂ absorption rate** — the regenerating bark absorbs more CO₂ than a mature unharvested tree
- Portugal's cork forests sequester an estimated 4.8 million tonnes of CO₂ annually
- Cork flooring is effectively **carbon-negative** when the sequestration over the product's lifetime is accounted for

**Biodiversity:**
- The cork oak montado is one of Europe's most biodiverse ecosystems (Iberian lynx, black vulture, multiple endemic species)
- Demand for cork products directly funds the maintenance of this ecosystem; declining cork demand = montado abandonment = biodiversity loss
- Specifying cork flooring is an act of habitat conservation

**End of life:**
- Fully biodegradable: natural cork composites (without synthetic binders) biodegrade completely
- Some synthetic-binder agglomerated cork: partially biodegradable
- Cork can be granulated and recycled into new products (Amorim's ReCork programme)

**Certifications:**
- FSC certification available for cork products from certified forests
- GreenGuard Gold: some cork products certified for low VOC
- EU Ecolabel: available for qualifying cork products
- Global GreenTag: Wicanders and other premium brands hold Global GreenTag certification

### 9.2 The Binder Caveat

Not all cork flooring is purely natural:
- Agglomerated cork uses adhesive binders to hold the granules together
- Traditional binders: natural (tree resin, rubber latex) — fully natural product
- Modern binders: synthetic polyurethane or formaldehyde-based — check E1/E0 rating for formaldehyde
- For a fully natural cork floor: verify the binder composition with the manufacturer; specify natural binder or confirmed E0 formaldehyde-free synthetic

## 10. CORK — PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 10.1 Swelling at Tile Edges (Cork Equivalent of Edge Swell)

**Symptoms:** Raised ridges at tile joints; cork seems to have expanded at edges

**Causes:**
- Moisture ingress at joints (cleaning with too much water; bathroom splash; condensation)
- Agglomerated cork absorbs water; the granule mass swells irreversibly
- The HDF core of cork-faced boards swells identically to laminate edge swell (Lexicon 04)

**Action:**
- Identify and eliminate moisture source
- Minor swelling: allow to dry; may partially recover
- Severe swelling: tiles must be replaced; HDF core swelling is not reversible

### 10.2 Furniture Indentation

**Symptoms:** Permanent depression in the cork surface under furniture legs

**Cause:** Cork's softness means concentrated point loads exceed the elastic limit of the cells; permanent deformation

**Action:**
- Prevention: wide-base furniture feet; cork-specific furniture cups
- Mild indentation: warm with a damp cloth (steam); the cells may partially recover
- Severe indentation: replacement of affected tile(s)

### 10.3 Fading and Colour Change

**Symptoms:** Cork becomes darker or lighter over time; uneven colour under furniture vs open areas

**Causes:**
- UV exposure lightens some natural cork finishes; fading is normal
- Conversely, some cork darkens with oxidation of the natural compounds
- Areas under rugs or furniture are shielded from UV; colour differential becomes visible
- Natural cork, like wood, develops a patina with age

**Action:** Prevention — UV-filtering window film; consistent exposure (rotate rugs periodically); accept as natural character of the material

### 10.4 Surface Peeling (Lacquer Delamination)

**Symptoms:** The lacquer finish peeling or flaking from the cork surface

**Causes:**
- Insufficient surface preparation before finishing (cork not sanded; dust present)
- Moisture under the lacquer film
- Incompatible lacquer product (not suitable for porous cork surfaces)
- Finish applied too thickly (no adhesion to previous coat)

**Action:** Sand back to bare cork; address moisture if present; apply compatible cork-specific finish in thin coats

## 11. RUBBER FLOORING — MATERIAL SCIENCE

### 11.1 What Rubber Flooring Is

Rubber flooring consists of natural or synthetic rubber (or blends) vulcanised into sheet, tile, or stair tread form. It is one of the oldest synthetic floor coverings — rubber flooring has been manufactured since the late 19th century — and remains the dominant specification in demanding environments where its unique combination of properties cannot be matched by any other material.

### 11.2 Natural Rubber (NR)

**Source:** Latex harvested from the rubber tree (*Hevea brasiliensis*), native to Brazil but now grown primarily in Southeast Asia (Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia).

**Processing:**
- Latex is tapped from the tree (similar in concept to cork harvesting but the tree exudes latex continuously)
- Coagulated and processed into sheets (ribbed smoked sheet — RSS) or technically specified grades
- Compounded with fillers, pigments, antioxidants, and vulcanising agents (sulphur + accelerators)
- **Vulcanisation:** The critical process — sulphur bridges are formed between rubber polymer chains at elevated temperature; the result is a cross-linked, elastic, and durable polymer network
- Before vulcanisation: rubber is plastic and soft; after: elastic and resilient

**Properties of natural rubber:**
- Very high elasticity and elongation
- Excellent resilience (returns to original shape after deformation)
- High tensile strength
- Good abrasion resistance
- Susceptible to oxidation (ozone, UV) — antioxidants and antiozonants added during compounding
- Susceptible to petroleum-based solvents

### 11.3 Synthetic Rubber Types in Flooring

**SBR (Styrene-Butadiene Rubber):**
- The most widely used synthetic rubber in flooring
- Better UV and ozone resistance than natural rubber
- Lower elasticity than NR; good abrasion resistance
- Cost-effective; the primary constituent of most commercial rubber flooring
- Recycled SBR is used extensively in rubber safety matting and gym flooring (see Section 12.3)

**EPDM (Ethylene Propylene Diene Monomer):**
- Excellent UV and ozone resistance; suitable for external applications
- Good colour stability (can be bright-coloured without fading)
- Used in coloured granule layers in multi-layer rubber products; common in playground safety surfaces and gym flooring colour layers

**Nitrile Rubber (NBR):**
- Excellent oil and fuel resistance
- Used in flooring for garages, workshops, and industrial areas with petroleum product contact
- Often specified in aviation and automotive workshop contexts

**Recycled rubber (crumb rubber / SBR granulate):**
- Ground up tyres (primarily SBR); the raw material for many gym floors, sports surfaces, and playground safety surfaces
- Available in black (natural tyre colour) or coloured with EPDM granules on the surface
- The most sustainable rubber flooring option (waste product diverted from landfill)

### 11.4 Vulcanisation — Why It Matters for Performance

The vulcanisation process (temperature, time, sulphur loading, and accelerator chemistry) determines the final properties of the rubber:
- **Under-vulcanised:** Too soft; tacky surface; poor chemical resistance
- **Over-vulcanised:** Too hard; brittle; cracks under stress
- **Correctly vulcanised:** The optimal balance of hardness, elasticity, and durability; this is the manufacturer's formulation secret

The quality of vulcanisation cannot be assessed visually; it is a manufacturing quality parameter verified by physical testing (tensile strength, elongation at break, hardness — Shore A scale).

## 12. RUBBER FLOORING — PRODUCT TYPES AND FORMATS

### 12.1 Sheet Rubber

- Rolls: typically 1.2m or 1.4m wide; lengths to 10m+
- Thickness: 2mm (light commercial), 3mm (standard), 4–6mm (heavy duty), 9–12mm (gym/sports)
- Installation: glue-down with rubber-specific adhesive
- Surface finish: smooth, studded, ribbed, or patterned (see Section 12.4)
- Used in: corridors, laboratories, sports areas, hospitals, schools

### 12.2 Rubber Tile

- Standard sizes: 500×500mm, 1000×1000mm
- Thickness: 3–12mm depending on application
- Square or interlocking edges
- Advantages over sheet: easier to handle; easier to replace damaged sections; tile format suits raised access floors
- Glue-down installation (same as sheet) or loose-lay (for heavy interlocking tiles)

### 12.3 Rubber Gym Flooring (Fitness and Sports)

**Black SBR crumb rubber tile/roll:**
- The dominant gym floor product; made from recycled tyres
- Thickness: 6mm (yoga/stretching), 8–10mm (general gym), 15–25mm (weightlifting platform), 40mm+ (crossfit/heavy dropping)
- Excellent impact absorption (protects equipment and subfloor)
- Shock absorption for athletes
- Heavy; hard to damage; essentially indestructible in normal gym use

**EPDM-topped coloured rubber:**
- Base layer of SBR (black recycled rubber); coloured EPDM granules on the top surface
- Available in virtually any colour combination; used for colour-zoning in sports facilities
- The EPDM surface layer provides UV stability and improved appearance

**Interlocking rubber tiles:**
- Jigsaw-edge or straight interlocking edge; no adhesive required
- Can be repositioned; popular in home gym, exhibition, and temporary installation contexts
- Lower installation quality than glued sheet; joins can be felt underfoot

**Rolled rubber (vulcanised sheet):**
- Superior to tile for permanent installation; fewer joints; better surface uniformity
- Full vulcanised rubber; not recycled crumb; more expensive; higher performance

### 12.4 Rubber Surface Textures

**Smooth:**
- Cleanest aesthetic; used in healthcare and laboratory; easiest to clean and disinfect
- Lower inherent slip resistance than textured; check PTV for wet applications

**Studded (coin/button):**
- Regular raised studs across the surface; the classic "rubber floor" appearance
- Excellent anti-slip from the stud texture; drains water away from foot contact area
- Very common in commercial stairs, ramps, and entrance areas
- Slightly harder to clean (dirt lodges between studs); compensated by very durable finish

**Ribbed:**
- Parallel ribs running in one direction; directional appearance
- Good anti-slip in the cross-rib direction; less effective parallel to ribs
- Common in entrance matting and workshop areas

**Diamond:**
- Diamond pattern of raised points; decorative and functional
- Good omnidirectional anti-slip

**Safety (carborundum aggregate):**
- Abrasive aggregate incorporated in the rubber surface
- Highest anti-slip performance; used in stairs, ramps, and slip-critical areas
- DIN 51130 R11 or better achievable with carborundum-loaded rubber

### 12.5 Stair Treads and Nosings (Rubber)

Rubber stair treads are a significant product category in commercial construction:
- Pre-moulded with a nosing profile; installed on each stair tread
- Available with integral or applied carborundum nosing strip (anti-slip insert)
- Colours: full range; typically dark (practical) or colour-coded for wayfinding
- Fixed: contact adhesive to stair tread; mechanical fixing at the nosing (brad nail or screw through nosing)
- Standards: Approved Document M visual contrast requirement (50 LRV difference at nosing)
- Key brands: Nora (nora® stair treads), Gerflor, Mondo, Polyflor

## 13. RUBBER PROPERTIES — PERFORMANCE CHARACTERISTICS

### 13.1 Durability

Rubber flooring is among the most durable floor covering categories:
- Abrasion resistance: very high; rubber compresses under load and returns to shape; does not abrade in the same way as rigid materials
- Impact resistance: excellent; the elastic nature absorbs impact without cracking or chipping
- Tear resistance: good to excellent depending on formulation
- No pile to crush (unlike carpet); no surface layer to wear through (unlike LVT)
- Expected lifespan in commercial use: 20–40 years with appropriate maintenance — comparable to tile and stone

### 13.2 Comfort and Anti-Fatigue

- The elasticity of rubber means each step is cushioned
- Standing on rubber flooring for extended periods is measurably less fatiguing than standing on tile, stone, or concrete
- This is why rubber is specified in: retail checkout areas, laboratory standing workstations, industrial assembly lines, sports facilities
- Anti-fatigue rubber matting (distinct from floor covering rubber) is a specialist product with enhanced cushioning for extreme standing environments

### 13.3 Slip Resistance

- Inherently good slip resistance from surface texture
- Studded, ribbed, and carborundum-loaded surfaces: excellent
- Smooth rubber when wet: can be slippery — always check PTV for smooth rubber in wet areas
- DIN 51130 R11–R12 achievable with carborundum-loaded rubber
- R10 typical for standard ribbed/studded rubber

### 13.4 Chemical Resistance

Depends on rubber type:
- SBR/NR: resistant to dilute acids and alkalis; not resistant to petroleum solvents, oils, fuels
- Nitrile (NBR): excellent oil and fuel resistance
- EPDM: good chemical resistance; excellent UV and ozone resistance
- For specific chemical environments: always obtain chemical resistance data from the manufacturer

### 13.5 Acoustic Performance

- Rubber is an excellent acoustic material; the elastic nature absorbs impact sound
- Dense rubber tile/sheet: ΔLw 12–18 dB for 4–6mm product
- Thick gym rubber (15–25mm): ΔLw 22–28 dB — very significant acoustic contribution
- Rubber acoustic underlay (under other floor coverings): the same acoustic benefit without the aesthetic

### 13.6 Electrical Properties

- Standard rubber: electrical insulator
- Antistatic rubber: specific formulation with conductive carbon black; surface resistance 10⁵–10⁹ Ohm
- Conductive rubber: surface resistance <10⁵ Ohm; used in ESD-critical environments
- Electrical insulating rubber: specifically rated for electrical safety (switchrooms, electrical substations)

### 13.7 Thermal Properties

- Thermal conductivity: approximately 0.25–0.35 W/mK (denser and better conductor than cork but worse than tile)
- R-value for 4mm rubber: approximately 0.014 m²K/W — very low; excellent for UFH

## 14. RUBBER — COMMERCIAL APPLICATIONS

### 14.1 Healthcare

Rubber flooring (particularly Nora Systems products) is one of the primary healthcare floor coverings:

**Why rubber in healthcare:**
- Seamless or heat-welded (as per vinyl — Lexicon 06, Section 14)
- Excellent chemical resistance to hospital disinfectants
- Inherent slip resistance; no surface treatment needed to achieve R10
- Anti-fatigue: reduces clinical staff fatigue in long-shift environments
- Acoustic: quieter than vinyl or tile; reducing noise in patient-sensitive areas
- Anti-bacterial options: silver-ion or similar biocide incorporated in the rubber compound

**Nora (nora® systems):** The dominant premium healthcare rubber brand globally; manufactured in Germany; used in many major UK hospital projects. Their norament and norament 825 products are widely specified in NHS environments.

### 14.2 Education

- Corridors: rubber sheet; very high durability for student corridor traffic; excellent acoustic
- Gymnasiums: sprung floor with rubber safety surface layer, or dedicated sports rubber
- Science labs: chemical-resistant rubber; studded for anti-slip
- Libraries: rubber provides excellent acoustic performance; lower reverberation than hard floors

### 14.3 Sports and Fitness

As described in Section 12.3:
- Gyms: crumb rubber; thickness determined by activity
- Sports halls: BS EN 14904-rated rubber systems
- Running tracks: athletics rubber (IAAF-approved formulations)
- Playground safety surfaces: impact-attenuating rubber to EN 1177 (fall height protection)

### 14.4 Transport and Public Spaces

- London Underground: rubber flooring is the specification throughout the Underground network (Penrose design); extremely high durability; anti-slip; acoustic
- Railway stations, airports: rubber in many high-traffic areas; durable; easy to clean
- Buses and coaches: specialist rubber flooring rated for vehicle vibration and cleaning chemicals

### 14.5 Industrial

- Nitrile rubber for oil/fuel environments (garage workshops, aviation ground support)
- ESD rubber for electronics manufacturing
- Anti-fatigue rubber matting at assembly workstations
- Heavy-duty studded rubber in loading bays and warehouses

## 15. RUBBER — SUBFLOOR REQUIREMENTS AND INSTALLATION

### 15.1 Subfloor Requirements

**Flatness:** Maximum **3mm under 2m straight-edge** (rubber is slightly more tolerant than thin vinyl due to its inherent flexibility)

**Moisture:** Maximum **75% RH** / **≤4% CM** — rubber is relatively moisture-tolerant compared to wood but adhesive systems are moisture-sensitive; DPM required over concrete

**Surface condition:** Clean, free of oil and contamination; any oil contamination on concrete will prevent rubber adhesive bonding

### 15.2 Adhesive for Rubber

**Contact adhesive (solvent-based or water-based):**
- Applied to both the subfloor and the back of the rubber; allow both surfaces to become tacky; press together
- Immediate bond on contact; no repositioning
- Solvent-based: very strong; traditional; high VOC; requires ventilation and RPE
- Water-based contact adhesive: lower VOC; slightly lower initial grab; increasingly preferred for VOC compliance

**Pressure-sensitive adhesive (PSA):**
- As used for vinyl and LVT; applied to subfloor only; allows brief repositioning
- Lower bond strength than contact adhesive; adequate for most rubber tile applications in normal commercial use
- Not suitable for areas with heavy rolling loads or for stair treads (contact adhesive required)

**Mechanical fixing (stair treads):**
- Stair treads additionally mechanically fixed at the nosing with brass or stainless countersunk screws or brad nails through the nose profile
- This is in addition to adhesive, not instead of it

### 15.3 Installation — Sheet Rubber

1. Acclimate rubber rolls in the installation space (24 hours minimum; rubber is manufactured under heat and may need to relax)
2. Prepare subfloor to tolerance; prime if required
3. Cut sheets to size; leave 25mm excess at walls for final trimming
4. Apply contact adhesive to subfloor and rubber back (large areas: use squeegee application)
5. Allow adhesive to reach tack on both surfaces
6. Carefully fold rubber back over itself (adhesive face to adhesive face — dry side outward)
7. Place folded rubber in position; unfold progressively, pressing firmly
8. Roll with 68kg roller in both directions immediately
9. Trim edges; apply seam sealer at all joints

**Heat welding rubber:**
- The same principle as vinyl heat welding (Lexicon 06, Section 14) but with rubber welding rod
- Rubber welding rod must exactly match the product
- Temperature: approximately 300–350°C for rubber (slightly lower than PVC)
- Same technique: groove, weld, trim hot, trim cold

### 15.4 Seams in Rubber Sheet

- All seams in healthcare, food production, and wet areas: heat-welded for hygiene
- All seams in dry commercial: either heat-welded or cold-seam sealed with rubber-specific seam sealer
- Seam placement: as per vinyl (Lexicon 06, Section 16.1): away from traffic direction; not under doors

## 16. RUBBER — MAINTENANCE AND CARE

### 16.1 Routine Maintenance

**Sweeping/dust mopping:** Regular; rubber is relatively impervious to grit but dirt accumulates in texture
**Mopping:** pH-neutral to mildly alkaline cleaner (pH 7–10); damp mop
**Machine scrubbing:** The recommended periodic deep clean method; low-speed rotary machine with appropriate pad

**What to avoid on rubber:**
- **Petroleum-based solvents:** Dissolve natural and SBR rubber; damage the surface
- **Strong acids:** Attack rubber polymer; avoid toilet cleaners (often acid-based) on rubber
- **Abrasive cleaners or pads:** Scratch studded or smooth rubber surfaces
- **Wax and polish on safety rubber:** Fills the texture and reduces anti-slip performance (as per safety vinyl — Lexicon 06, Section 19.4)

**Periodic maintenance:**
- Strip and re-clean with alkaline stripper (pH 10–12) annually in heavily used areas
- Machine scrub with stiff brush head to clean aggregate texture on safety rubber
- Re-test PTV in safety-critical areas after cleaning programme changes

### 16.2 Rubber Blackening and Discolouration

A common issue: rubber floors in light colours (grey, beige, white) can become discoloured from:
- Rubber transfer from shoe soles (as per LVT — Lexicon 05, Section 18.4): rubber on rubber compounds interact; remove with isopropyl alcohol
- Tyre marks: rubber from vehicle tyres (in workshops, loading bays); specialist rubber mark remover
- Oxidation: rubber naturally oxidises; surface becomes slightly duller; polish or surface conditioner may help appearance but note: do not use on safety rubber surfaces

## 17. RUBBER — SUSTAINABILITY

### 17.1 Natural Rubber Sustainability

- *Hevea brasiliensis* is a renewable crop; plantations provide rubber for 25–30 years before replanting
- Southeast Asian rubber plantations: significant biodiversity concern if native forest is cleared; specify from certified sustainable sources (FSC equivalent for rubber: RSPO — Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil principles applied to rubber; Rainforest Alliance)
- Natural rubber: fully biodegradable; composting possible (though slow)

### 17.2 Recycled Rubber (SBR Crumb)

- Made from post-consumer tyre waste; the largest tyre recycling stream globally
- Diverts tyres from landfill and incineration
- Carbon-intensive original tyre production cost is amortised across the recycled product
- The most sustainable rubber product from an end-of-life perspective

### 17.3 Premium Rubber Brands and Sustainability

**Nora Systems (Interface Inc.):** Comprehensive sustainability programme; take-back scheme for old nora flooring; products made without heavy metals; Cradle to Cradle certified; carbon neutrality commitments

**Mondo (Italy):** Sports rubber; sustainability reporting; increasingly using recycled content

**Polyflor:** Some rubber products; broader sustainability framework

### 17.4 End-of-Life

- Premium vulcanised rubber: difficult to recycle into the same quality product; mechanical grinding for lower-grade applications (aggregate, playground surface)
- Crumb rubber recycling: ground and rebound into new crumb rubber products — a true circular loop
- Incineration with energy recovery: acceptable for rubber at end of life when recycling not viable (rubber has high calorific value)

## 18. RUBBER — PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 18.1 Lifting Edges and Bubbling

**Causes and action:** Identical to vinyl (Lexicon 06, Section 18.1) — inadequate adhesive coverage; moisture; contaminated substrate; adhesive not at correct tack

### 18.2 Surface Hardening and Cracking

**Symptoms:** Rubber surface becomes hard; loses flexibility; develops surface cracks

**Causes:**
- **Ozone attack:** Ozone (from UV, electrical equipment, urban air) attacks rubber double bonds; causes surface cracking ("ozone cracking" — visible as perpendicular cracks to stress direction)
- **Over-oxidation with age:** Natural rubber in particular oxidises with age; loses elasticity
- **Chemical attack:** Petroleum solvents swelling and then evaporating leave the rubber degraded

**Action:** Ozone cracking and over-oxidation are not repairable; replacement required. Ensure the specified product has appropriate antiozonant additives for the environment.

### 18.3 Swelling and Softening

**Symptoms:** Rubber surface becomes soft, sticky, or swollen

**Causes:**
- Contact with petroleum-based solvents or oils
- The solvent penetrates the rubber polymer network; causes swelling and plasticisation
- In nitrile rubber: swelling from non-oil-resistant compound in an oil environment

**Action:** Remove the chemical contact; the rubber may recover partially on drying. If significant swelling has occurred, the surface may have permanent damage; replacement of affected area.

### 18.4 Seam Failure

**Causes:**
- Cold seam sealer failure (not heat-welded in a wet or hygienically critical area)
- Heat weld performed at incorrect temperature (under-welded: rod not fused; over-welded: charring)
- Subfloor joint movement transmitted to the rubber seam

**Action:** Re-groove and re-weld the seam; address subfloor movement if present

## 19. CORK VS RUBBER — COMPARISON AND SELECTION GUIDE

| Property | Cork | Rubber |
|----------|------|--------|
| Primary application | Premium residential; sustainability-led commercial | Heavy commercial; sports; healthcare; industrial |
| Hardness | Soft | Medium to hard (formula-dependent) |
| Durability | Moderate | Very high |
| Underfoot comfort | Excellent (warm, cushioned) | Very good (cushioned, slightly cooler) |
| Acoustic performance | Good | Very good to excellent |
| Moisture resistance | Moderate (sealant-dependent) | Good (chemical-resistant) |
| Chemical resistance | Low to moderate | Good to excellent |
| UFH compatibility | Borderline (check R-value) | Excellent (very low R-value) |
| Sustainability | Outstanding (renewable; carbon-negative) | Good (recycled rubber); moderate (virgin rubber) |
| Maintenance | Moderate (finish renewal) | Low to moderate |
| Aesthetics | Warm, natural, organic | Industrial to contemporary; wide colour range |
| Cost | Mid to premium | Mid to premium |
| Fire class | Cfl-s1 typical | Bfl-s1 to Cfl-s1 typical |
| Suitable for wet rooms | Limited (sealed only) | Yes (with correct adhesive and welding) |
| Suitable for commercial heavy traffic | No | Yes |

**Choose cork when:**
- Sustainability is a primary driver
- Residential comfort and warmth are the priority
- The aesthetic is contemporary-natural or Scandinavian
- The area is dry and low-to-medium traffic

**Choose rubber when:**
- Heavy commercial traffic is expected
- Healthcare, laboratory, industrial, or sports specification
- Chemical resistance is required
- Anti-fatigue in standing-intensive environments
- Anti-slip performance in wet areas
- Acoustic performance is critical

## 20. BRITISH AND EUROPEAN STANDARDS

**EN 12104:2014 — Resilient floor coverings — Cork floor tiles — Specification:**
- Product standard for cork floor tiles; defines thickness, dimensions, abrasion resistance, residual indentation, and water absorption

**EN 16085:2012 — Resilient floor coverings — Cork-based floor coverings — Specification:**
- Product standard for cork-based composite floor coverings (including cork-faced engineered boards)

**EN ISO 10874:2012 — Resilient floor coverings — Classification:**
- Usage classification for cork and rubber (as for LVT and vinyl); Classes 21–43

**EN 12199:2014 — Resilient floor coverings — Specification for homogeneous and heterogeneous rubber floor coverings:**
- The primary product standard for rubber floor coverings; thickness, hardness, abrasion resistance, tear strength, dimensional stability

**EN 1817:2010 — Resilient floor coverings — Specification for homogeneous smooth rubber floor coverings:**
- Additional specification for smooth rubber

**EN 14904:2006 — Surfaces for indoor sports areas:**
- Referenced for sports rubber; force reduction and friction requirements

**BS 8203:2017 — Installation of resilient floor coverings:**
- The installation standard covering cork and rubber (as for all resilient floor coverings); subfloor requirements; adhesive application; moisture limits

**EN 13501-1 — Reaction to fire:**
- Cork: typically Cfl-s1 (natural cork chars; does not sustain combustion)
- Rubber: typically Bfl-s1 to Cfl-s1 depending on formulation; some premium rubber achieves Bfl-s1 without difficulty

**DIN 51130 — Slip resistance (workplaces):**
- R-value for rubber: R10 typical for standard studded; R11–R12 with carborundum aggregate

**EN 1177:2018 — Impact attenuating playground surfaces:**
- Critical fall-height protection for playground rubber safety surfaces; not flooring per se but relevant to rubber specification in school grounds
