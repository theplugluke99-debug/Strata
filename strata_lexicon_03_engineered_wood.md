## 1. WHY ENGINEERED WOOD EXISTS — THE ENGINEERING PRINCIPLE

### 1.1 The Problem with Solid Wood

As established in Lexicon 02, solid wood is hygroscopic — it moves dimensionally as its moisture content changes with ambient relative humidity. This movement is:
- **Predominantly across the grain** (tangential and radial) — very little along the grain
- **Significant in wide boards** — a plain-sawn 200mm oak board moves approximately 0.5mm per 1% MC change
- **Seasonal** — typically a 4–6% MC swing in a UK centrally heated interior between summer and winter
- **Cumulative** — in a 5m wide room with 200mm boards, total seasonal movement can exceed 10mm

This movement is manageable in narrow strip flooring (40–75mm) but becomes problematic in wide board formats and makes solid wood incompatible with the following situations:
- Over underfloor heating (which creates extreme low-humidity conditions)
- Below grade (basements) where moisture levels are variable
- Large commercial areas requiring wide planks
- Floating installation in wide formats

### 1.2 The Engineering Solution

Engineered wood solves the movement problem through **cross-ply construction** — bonding layers of wood together with alternating grain directions. When layers are oriented perpendicularly to each other:

- The movement of each layer is **constrained by the adjacent layer**, which would need to move in its own grain direction (which is very small — longitudinal movement in wood is approximately 0.01–0.03% per 1% MC)
- The net result is that the overall board movement is dramatically reduced — typically **50–80% less** than an equivalent solid wood board of the same width
- This allows the use of wide planks, floating installation over large areas, and installation over UFH that would be impossible with solid wood

**The engineering is in the core — not just the face.** The premium species veneer on the face is functionally identical to that of a solid wood board. The difference is entirely in what supports it.

## 2. CONSTRUCTION TYPES — CORE SYSTEMS

### 2.1 Three-Layer (3-Ply) Construction

**Structure:** Three layers bonded with alternating grain directions:
- **Face layer (top):** Hardwood veneer — the visible, finished surface
- **Core layer (middle):** A thick slab of softwood (typically pine or spruce), HDF, or single-species hardwood running perpendicular to the face
- **Backing layer (bottom):** A balancing veneer running parallel to the face to prevent bowing (the backing must balance the face layer's tension/compression response to MC change)

**Properties:**
- The most common construction for premium residential engineered flooring
- Thick core allows for significant total board thickness (14–21mm)
- The softwood core has a slightly lower density than multi-ply — marginally more "give" underfoot
- Good stability; suitable for all installation methods
- Suitable over UFH with appropriate adhesive and board width specifications

**Core variants in three-layer:**
- **Spruce/pine core:** Most common; cost-effective; good dimensional stability; some flexibility
- **Hardwood core (oak, beech, or birch):** Higher density; more rigid; excellent acoustic damping
- **HDF core:** High-Density Fibreboard; very dense and stable; uniform; good acoustic performance; slightly more brittle than wood core

### 2.2 Multi-Ply (5-Ply, 7-Ply, 9-Ply) Construction

**Structure:** Multiple thin layers (plies) of cross-banded hardwood or softwood veneer, bonded under high pressure, with a hardwood face veneer on top and a backing veneer beneath.

**Properties:**
- Superior dimensional stability compared to three-layer — more cross-plies = more movement restraint
- Thinner individual plies mean the MC equilibration across the board is faster
- High-ply boards are the most dimensionally stable engineered wood products
- Good for very wide boards (200–300mm+) and large open-plan areas
- Suitable for all installation methods; the preferred choice for UFH
- Widely used in commercial and specification-grade products
- Typically constructed from birch ply core (Baltic birch) — consistent, stable, widely available

**Ply count and stability:** Each additional ply adds cross-grain restraint. A 9-ply board is measurably more stable than a 5-ply board of the same species and width. For widths above 180mm over UFH, 9-ply or better is strongly recommended.

### 2.3 HDF Core Construction

**Structure:** Face veneer bonded to a High-Density Fibreboard core; balancing veneer beneath.

**Properties:**
- HDF has no grain direction — stability is isotropic (equal in all directions)
- Very uniform; no natural knots or defects in core
- High density (~800–950 kg/m³) — heavier than timber-core products
- Good sound absorption
- Less moisture-resistant than wood-core products — HDF can swell significantly if exposed to moisture; do not use in areas of high moisture risk without appropriate DPM
- Good for floating installation where acoustic performance is required
- Budget to mid-range product segment; also used in high-volume commercial

### 2.4 Plywood Core (Baltic Birch)

- Multi-ply construction using Baltic birch as the core ply species
- Baltic birch is a premium plywood material: consistent, knot-free veneers, strong glue bond
- The industry's preferred core material for high-stability specification-grade engineered flooring
- Provides excellent screw/nail holding for secret nail installation
- Typically 9–12 plies in a 15–18mm total thickness board
- Higher cost than softwood or HDF core

### 2.5 Strand-Woven Bamboo Core / All-Bamboo Engineered

- Not strictly an engineered wood product but sold alongside it
- Strand-woven bamboo face and core; extremely hard (Janka ~3,000 lbf for strand-woven)
- See bamboo notes in Lexicon 02; the same caveats apply regarding formaldehyde emissions

### 2.6 Glue Systems in Engineered Wood

The adhesive bonding the layers together is critical to the product's integrity:
- **UF (Urea Formaldehyde):** Traditional plywood adhesive; high formaldehyde emission potential; now largely replaced in quality products
- **MF (Melamine Formaldehyde):** Lower emissions than UF; still used in some budget products
- **PU (Polyurethane) glue:** Near-zero formaldehyde; water and heat resistant; premium products
- **MUF (Melamine-Urea Formaldehyde):** Reduced formaldehyde; used in E1/E0 rated products
- **Formaldehyde emission classes (EN 717-1 / EN 13986):**
  - **E1:** ≤0.1 ppm (0.124 mg/m³); the minimum standard for products sold in UK/EU
  - **E0:** ≤0.05 ppm; premium/low-emission specification
  - **California CARB Phase 2 / TSCA Title VI:** North American equivalent; ≤0.05 ppm for plywood panels
  - **REACH compliance:** Relevant for chemical substance exposure limits in the workplace

## 3. WEAR LAYER (FACE VENEER) SYSTEMS

### 3.1 What the Wear Layer Is

The face veneer is the real wood top layer — the surface you see, touch, and walk on. It determines the floor's appearance, its finishing options, and how many times it can be sanded and refinished over its lifetime.

**This is not the same as a laminate print layer** — it is real wood in all its depth and variation. The beauty, character, and patina of engineered wood is genuinely that of the species represented.

### 3.2 Wear Layer Thickness

| Thickness | Category | Sanding potential | Typical product tier |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 0.6mm | Very thin | Not sandable; one refinish only if light | Budget / entry-level |
| 1.0–1.5mm | Thin | One careful light sand possible | Mid-entry |
| 2.0–3.5mm | Standard | 1–2 sands over lifetime | Mid-range residential |
| 4.0–5.0mm | Thick | 2–3 sands | Premium residential |
| 6.0mm | Very thick | 3–4 sands | Premium/specification |
| 6.5–12mm | Extra thick | Multiple sands; approaches solid wood longevity | Luxury / specification |

**Practical rule of thumb:** 1mm of usable wear layer = approximately 1 sanding cycle (removing approximately 0.5–0.8mm per sand). A 6mm wear layer product can realistically be sanded and refinished 4–5 times over its lifetime.

**Minimum wear layer for commercial use:** 3.5mm minimum; 4mm+ preferred. Below this, sand-through risk in commercial applications within 5–10 years.

### 3.3 Veneer Cutting Methods

How the face veneer is sliced from the log determines its grain pattern, stability, and figure:

**Rotary-peeled veneer:**
- The log is mounted and rotated against a fixed blade; veneer peels off continuously like unwinding a roll
- Very economical — maximum yield from log
- Produces a wide, arching, "cathedral" grain — often very dramatic but less natural-looking
- Less stable than sliced veneer (cutting tangentially at the outermost log surface)
- Common in budget products; the grain can look "engineered" or unnatural to the trained eye
- The most common in budget to mid-range products

**Sliced veneer (flat-sliced / plain-sliced):**
- Log or flitch is advanced against a stationary blade; each slice produces one veneer
- Mimics plain-sawn solid wood appearance: natural arching grain
- Higher cost; more waste; more natural appearance
- The standard in quality residential and commercial products

**Quarter-sliced veneer:**
- Sliced perpendicular to the growth rings; produces straight, parallel grain
- In oak: produces ray fleck — the defining feature of quarter-sawn oak
- Maximum stability; minimum curvature; most consistent appearance
- Premium product; higher yield loss per log; commands premium pricing

**Rift-sliced veneer:**
- Sliced at 45° to growth rings
- Very straight grain; no ray fleck
- Wasteful; expensive; used in minimalist contemporary specification products

### 3.4 Veneer Matching Systems

When multiple veneer strips are assembled across a wide plank, the way they are matched creates different aesthetic effects:

**Book-matched:**
- Adjacent veneer sheets are "opened like a book" — one is flipped; they mirror each other
- Creates a symmetrical, butterfly pattern
- Can look highly formal or "manufactured"

**Slip-matched:**
- Adjacent veneers are slid sideways (not flipped) — same grain direction throughout
- More natural appearance; subtle variation across width
- Common in quality engineered products

**Random-matched:**
- Veneer strips selected without matching consideration
- Maximum natural variation; no repeating pattern
- The most natural appearance; common in rustic and character grades

**Note:** Wide boards (200mm+) cut from a single veneer piece avoid matching entirely — the entire face is one piece of wood. This is only possible in products with a very thick face veneer (6mm+) or with very wide logs. It commands a significant premium.

## 4. SPECIES, GRADING AND AESTHETICS

### 4.1 Species Availability

Every species listed in Lexicon 02 is available in engineered construction. The most commercially significant in the UK market:

**Dominant species:**
- **European Oak** — by far the most commercially dominant; available in every grade, colour treatment, and finish
- **American White Oak** — strong growth; popular for contemporary pale aesthetics
- **Ash** — ring-porous, dramatic grain; under supply pressure (Ash Dieback)
- **Walnut** — premium; rich chocolate tones; popular in high-end residential

**Secondary species:**
- Hard Maple, Hickory, Cherry, Herringbone Parquet (often in oak or walnut)

**Exotic/tropical species in engineered construction:**
- Jatoba, Cumaru, Ipe, Teak, Iroko, Merbau — available in engineered format
- The thick face veneer preserves the species' appearance and character
- Engineered construction is preferred for tropical species — reduces the volume of tropical timber used per m² compared to solid construction (conservation benefit)

### 4.2 Grading

Grading follows the same European system as solid wood (see Lexicon 02, Section 4):
- **Prime/Select (AB):** Minimal knots; consistent colour; clean grain
- **Natura/Classic (ABC):** Natural variation; occasional tight knots
- **Rustic/Character (ABCD):** Full character; knots, sap, and variation
- **Vintage/Antique:** Very high character; often combined with surface treatments (hand-scraping, wire brushing, smoking)

**Additional grade descriptors specific to engineered products:**
- **Live Natural / Unselected:** No grading selection; boards as they come from the log; maximum variation
- **White Nature:** Includes sapwood (the pale outer wood) alongside heartwood; creates two-tone variation; popular in contemporary Scandinavian-influenced interiors

### 4.3 Surface Treatments and Colour

**Brushed (wire-brushed):**
- Wire brushing removes the softer early-wood fibres, leaving the harder late-wood raised in relief
- Creates a tactile, textured surface; hides scratches and wear marks effectively
- Very popular in rustic/farmhouse and contemporary markets
- Particularly effective on oak (dramatic open grain) and ash

**Hand-scraped:**
- Mechanical or genuine hand process creating random surface undulation
- Mimics the appearance of historically scraped floors
- Hides high-heel marks and daily wear
- Strong character; suited to older property contexts

**Smoked/fumed:**
- As per solid wood (Lexicon 02, Section 2.1)
- Ammonia fuming of oak reacts with tannins throughout the wear layer depth
- If the floor is sanded back, the smoky colour remains through the full veneer depth (unlike a stained product which would reveal pale wood below)

**Thermally modified face veneer:**
- Heat treatment of the face veneer layer; creates consistent dark brown/caramel tones
- More dimensionally stable face layer (less hygroscopic)
- Good for contemporary designs requiring rich tones without staining

**White/whitewashed/lye treated:**
- Alkali treatment or white pigmented oil on the face
- Pale, airy, Scandinavian aesthetic
- UV stable if using hardwax oil system

**Vintage/aged treatments:**
- Combination of distressing (knot enhancement, hand-scraping, worm-hole simulation), smoking, and colour oiling
- Simulate century-old reclaimed appearance
- Very popular in barn conversion, heritage, and high-end residential contexts

## 5. BOARD DIMENSIONS AND PROFILES

### 5.1 Standard Dimensions

**Thickness (total board):**
- 10mm: thin; suitable for floating only; limited wear layer
- 12mm: common residential floating and glue-down product
- 14mm: standard residential; floating and glue-down
- 15mm: mid-premium; can be secret nailed
- 18mm: premium; approaches solid wood thickness; nailable; excellent stability
- 20mm+: thick/heavy specification product; luxury residential and commercial

**Width:**
- Narrow strip: 70–100mm
- Standard plank: 100–160mm
- Wide plank: 160–240mm
- Feature/architectural plank: 240–400mm (some manufacturers produce up to 500mm)

**Length:**
- Short: 400–600mm (classic parquet-like appearance)
- Standard: 600–1500mm
- Long plank: 1500–2400mm
- Continuous / feature length: 2400–4000mm+ (bespoke; sold as specific lengths)
- Longer boards = more architectural; fewer end joints = more open appearance

**Common format combinations:**
- 14mm × 190mm × 1900mm — the UK mid-range residential "standard"
- 20mm × 220mm × 2200mm — premium residential/specification
- 15mm × 150mm × 1800mm — accessible premium

### 5.2 Click (Locking) Systems

The locking profile in the tongue and groove determines the floating installation system:

**Standard T&G (non-click):**
- Traditional tongue and groove; boards must be tapped together using a tapping block
- Used in both floating (with or without PVA glue) and glue-down installations
- No mechanical locking — floating boards rely on glue bond or weight/perimeter to stay together

**Uniclic / 5G (Unilin / Quick-Step):**
- A patented angled-fold locking system; one board is angled into the next and folded down
- No tapping required; very fast installation; excellent joint tightness
- Very widely licensed across the industry; the dominant click system for premium engineered products

**2G / Loc systems:**
- Horizontal push-together systems; the tongue is inserted and snapped horizontally
- Faster for long runs; some systems have lower joint strength than angled systems
- Common in mid-range products

**Valinge 5G / I4F click:**
- Alternative angled-fold and multi-function lock systems
- Very common in the market; each manufacturer licenses one of the major lock systems

**Click system quality:**
- The precision of the click profile determines the floor's performance: tight tolerance = tight joints = no gaps, no rattle
- Poor-quality click profiles produce loose, rattling floors that develop visible gaps at joints
- A critical quality indicator often not visible until the floor is installed

### 5.3 Edge Profiles

Identical to solid wood (Lexicon 02, Section 5.3):
- Square edge
- Micro-bevel (most common)
- Bevel / V-groove
- Distressed / hand-scraped edges
- Eased round-over

**Important note on bevels and sanding:** When an engineered floor is sanded, factory micro-bevels or bevels may be partially or fully removed. If the bevel is an important aesthetic element, communicate to the customer that it will not survive multiple sanding cycles. Premium products with thick wear layers may retain a micro-bevel after 1–2 careful sands.

## 6. STABILITY SCIENCE — HOW ENGINEERED WOOD MANAGES MOVEMENT

### 6.1 Cross-Ply Physics

The dimensional stability of engineered wood derives from **orthotropic constraint**:

Wood moves significantly across the grain (tangential and radial directions) but barely at all along the grain (longitudinal direction is approximately 10–20 times less than tangential).

In a cross-ply construction:
- **Face veneer** wants to move in its grain direction (across its width)
- **Core layer(s)** run perpendicular — their grain direction is the face's across-grain direction
- The core's resistance to movement in its own grain direction (which is very stiff) constrains the face
- Equilibrium: the face is partially restrained; the core is partially stressed; the net movement is a fraction of what either layer would show alone

**The result:** A 200mm engineered board moves approximately 0.3–0.5mm across its width per 4% MC change versus approximately 2.0mm for an equivalent solid board.

### 6.2 Core Quality and Stability

The quality of the core material and the bonding between layers directly determines stability:
- **More plies** = more cross-grain restraint = more stable
- **Higher core density** = stiffer core = more restraint
- **Better adhesive bond** = layers act as a unit = more restraint (delaminated layers lose their cross-constraint)
- **Consistent core MC at manufacture** = less built-in stress

### 6.3 Face Veneer Thickness and Stability

A thicker face veneer has more mass wanting to move and therefore creates more stress on the cross-ply system:
- Very thick face veneers (8–12mm) on thin cores (total 14mm) may show more movement than a 4mm face on the same core
- The ideal is a thick face veneer supported by a proportionally substantial, high-ply core
- The wear layer to total thickness ratio matters: a 6mm face on a 20mm total thickness board is supported by 14mm of cross-ply core — very stable

### 6.4 Stability in Practice — Comparing Construction Types

| Construction | Relative stability | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| 3-ply softwood core | Good | Standard residential; all methods |
| 3-ply hardwood core | Very good | Premium residential; UFH |
| 5-ply Baltic birch | Excellent | Wide boards; UFH; light commercial |
| 7-ply Baltic birch | Excellent | Wide boards; UFH; commercial |
| 9-ply Baltic birch | Outstanding | Very wide boards; demanding UFH; heavy commercial |
| HDF core | Good (isotropic) | Floating; acoustic applications |

## 7. MOISTURE REQUIREMENTS AND ACCLIMATISATION

### 7.1 Target MC for Engineered Wood

- Target MC at installation: **8–10%** for UK centrally heated interiors (same as solid wood)
- Engineered wood is less sensitive than solid to minor MC deviations at installation — the cross-ply construction accommodates variation better
- However, gross deviation (boards significantly wetter or drier than the in-service environment) will still cause problems

### 7.2 Acclimatisation

**Required:** Yes, but less critical than for solid wood.
- Minimum: 48 hours in the installation room under living conditions, in open packaging
- For wide boards (200mm+): 72 hours minimum
- For boards being delivered in cold/humid conditions to a warm/dry site: longer; measure MC

**Why less critical than solid:** The cross-ply construction means the boards will not move as dramatically if slightly off-target. Nonetheless, acclimatising is good professional practice and manufacturer-required for warranty purposes.

**Room conditions:**
- Same requirements as solid wood — heating operational, wet trades complete, windows closed
- Target room RH: 40–65%
- Target room temperature: 15–22°C

### 7.3 Subfloor Moisture Limits

**Concrete:**
- Maximum **75% RH** for standard products
- Some manufacturers approve up to **80–85% RH** with their specific adhesive/DPM system — always check product technical data sheet
- At or above 80% RH: apply a two-component epoxy DPM regardless of whether manufacturer permits higher
- Over UFH concrete: after full commissioning cycle; test moisture post-commissioning

**Timber subfloor:**
- Maximum **14% MC** (pin meter reading)

## 8. SUBFLOOR REQUIREMENTS

### 8.1 Flatness

- Floating: maximum **3mm under 2m straight-edge** (BS 8203)
- Glue-down: maximum **3mm under 2m straight-edge**; tighter tolerance preferred (2mm) for wide boards as hollows can leave boards unsupported in the centre
- Wide boards (220mm+) glue-down: aim for **2mm under 2m straight-edge** — a hollow in the centre of a 220mm board creates a "loose" feel and stress on the click joint or veneer

### 8.2 Concrete Subfloor Preparation

As per Lexicon 02, Section 7.2:
- Grind laitance; remove contamination; fill cracks/holes with SLC
- RH test minimum 72 hours (BS 8203)
- Apply DPM if RH ≥75% (epoxy two-component preferred)
- Prime before adhesive if required by adhesive manufacturer
- Anhydrite screeds: sand; prime with anhydrite primer; then proceed

### 8.3 Timber Subfloor

As per Lexicon 02, Section 7.3:
- Nail down all loose boards; punch fixings below surface
- Overlay with plywood if significant gaps, deflection, or surface irregularity
- Check MC ≤14%
- Minimum 18mm plywood for nail-down installation

### 8.4 Raised Access Floors

- Engineered wood tile format (usually 600×600mm) or narrow plank on raised access floor
- Full bond adhesive to panel surface
- GlasBac or high-stability click products preferred
- Verify load rating of panels before specification

### 8.5 Screeds Over UFH

**Critical sequence:**
1. Commission UFH per protocol (see Section 13.3 below)
2. After cooling: RH test
3. Prepare surface: grind, prime, SLC if needed
4. Install DPM if required
5. Lay engineered wood per chosen installation method
6. Restart UFH per graduated protocol

## 9. INSTALLATION METHODS

### 9.1 Overview

Engineered wood is significantly more versatile in its installation options than solid wood:

| Method | Subfloor | Board width | UFH compatible | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------------|----------------|-------|
| Floating (click) | Any with underlay | Up to 220mm typically | Yes (with limits) | Fastest; most accessible; some acoustic limits |
| Floating (glued T&G) | Any with underlay | Up to 180mm typically | Yes (with limits) | More stable than dry-float |
| Full glue-down | Concrete or ply | Any width | Yes (preferred for UFH) | Maximum stability; best UFH performance |
| Secret nail/staple | Timber/ply | Up to 180mm typically | No (nailing = air gap = poor UFH) | Traditional; good for renovation over timber |
| Double-stick | Concrete with adhered underlay | Any width | Yes | Premium; stable; excellent acoustic |

### 9.2 Method Selection Guide

- **Over UFH:** Always glue-down; floating creates an air gap that impedes heat transfer and allows board temperature cycling to stress the click joint repeatedly → joint failure over time
- **Over concrete (no UFH):** Glue-down or floating; floating is faster and more accessible; glue-down is more stable long-term for wide boards
- **Over timber subfloor (renovation):** Secret nail or floating; nail if subfloor is sound and plywood-overlaid; floating if subfloor is irregular
- **Apartment / upper floor:** Check acoustic requirements first — floating with acoustic underlay is standard; some developments prohibit click floating (acoustic compliance); glue-down over acoustic mat may be required
- **Commercial:** Glue-down for stability and durability; check EN 1307 commercial classification of product

## 10. FLOATING INSTALLATION — DETAIL

### 10.1 Underlay Selection for Floating Engineered Wood

The underlay under a floating engineered floor is critical for:
- Acoustic performance (impact sound insulation)
- Moisture vapour barrier (over concrete)
- Minor subfloor irregularity accommodation
- Thermal resistance (must remain within limits for UFH)

**Standard residential (no UFH):**
- 3mm PU foam with integrated DPM (polythene sheet): adequate; cost-effective; widely used
- 3mm cork underlay with DPM: better acoustic; more comfortable; suitable over UFH (lower R-value than foam)
- 5mm acoustic PU foam with DPM: improved impact sound reduction; standard in apartments

**Acoustic (apartment / multi-occupancy):**
- High-density rubber acoustic mat (e.g., Regupol, Acoustilay): 3–5mm; very high impact sound reduction (ΔLw 19–22 dB); meets most specification requirements
- Combination underlay with heavy foil or rubber: addresses both airborne and impact
- See Section 16 for full acoustic specification

**Over UFH:**
- Maximum combined R-value (underlay + engineered board): **0.15 m²K/W** (some manufacturers permit 0.17 m²K/W)
- Cork underlay: approximately 0.04–0.09 m²K/W at 2–3mm — good choice
- Thin foam (2mm): very low R-value; good thermal performance but minimal acoustic benefit
- Check specific manufacturer R-value data; use the lowest practical thickness

**Underlay installation:**
- Lay perpendicular to board direction (underlay joints should not align with board joints)
- Overlap DPM layer by 200mm at joins; tape with polythene tape
- Turn DPM up the wall by 50mm; trim after skirting is fitted
- Do not lap underlay up the wall — the DPM flap is tucked behind skirting, not visible

### 10.2 Laying Sequence

1. Establish starting wall and direction
2. Check room for square (diagonal measurement method: if room is square, both diagonals are equal)
3. If room is significantly out of square: establish a chalk line reference perpendicular to intended board direction
4. Install underlay across entire room; tape joins; turn DPM up walls
5. Install expansion spacers at all walls (10–12mm for standard; wider for large rooms — see Section 17)
6. First row: boards along starting wall; tongue facing wall (groove facing room, ready to receive next row); or groove facing wall if first row needs to be ripped to width
7. Lock rows using click mechanism (angled-fold or horizontal push per system)
8. Stagger end joints: minimum 300mm between end joints in adjacent rows; minimum 1/3 of board length as starting offset
9. Use tapping block to close joints; never strike board directly with mallet
10. Last row: measure and rip to width; allow expansion gap at far wall
11. Fit skirting/beading to cover expansion gap; do not nail through the floor

**Direction of boards:**
- The same principles apply as solid wood (Lexicon 02, Section 8.2): run boards away from the primary light source for minimal visible seam shadow; run perpendicular to joists for structural support feel

### 10.3 Cutting Engineered Boards

- **Circular saw / mitre saw:** Most precise; face down to prevent chip-out on face veneer (or score with a knife first if cutting face up)
- **Jigsaw:** For curves, notches, and complex shapes; face down or with fine blade
- **Score and snap:** Not recommended — risk of cracking at click profile
- **Oscillating multi-tool:** For fitting around door frames and obstacles; undercut door casings

**Dust management:** Fine wood dust from cutting; respiratory PPE (FFP2 minimum); dust extraction.

### 10.4 Fitting Around Obstacles

- **Door frames/casings:** Undercut with jamb saw or oscillating tool to minimum of board thickness + underlay depth; slide board beneath
- **Pipes:** Drill oversize hole (pipe diameter + 12mm for expansion); fit pipe collar to conceal gap
- **Hearths and steps:** Measure precisely; scribe if necessary; leave expansion gap; cover with appropriate nosing/trim

### 10.5 Floating — Performance Limitations

**Why floating is inferior to glue-down in some scenarios:**
- A floating floor is only connected to the subfloor by gravity and the expansion gap constraint — it can "float" upward if upward pressure exceeds its weight
- In high-humidity conditions, floating boards can buckle if the perimeter gap is insufficient
- In commercial environments with heavy rolling loads (castor chairs, pallet trucks), floating floors transmit impact and can rattle/flex
- Over UFH: the air gap between underlay and board surface creates a temperature buffer — the floor surface is cooler than it would be with direct adhesion; less efficient
- Acoustic underlay adds R-value which reduces UFH efficiency

## 11. GLUE-DOWN INSTALLATION — DETAIL

### 11.1 Adhesive Selection

**Full urethane / MS polymer adhesive (standard):**
- As described in Lexicon 02, Section 10.2
- Elastic bond: accommodates remaining wood movement
- Moisture-curing; good coverage; single-component
- The industry standard for engineered wood glue-down
- Key brands: Mapei Ultrabond ECO 4 / P990, Sika T-54, Bostik Ultra Floor, F. Ball Styccobond F49, Bona R848 / R850

**Reactive PU adhesive (2K):**
- Two-component; mixing required
- Higher initial strength; shorter working time
- Used in commercial and sports flooring where maximum rigidity is specified

**Acoustic mastic adhesive:**
- Combines adhesive bond with high acoustic damping
- Thicker bead application; remains elastic; excellent impact sound reduction
- Used in apartment refurbishments and premium residential where impact sound must be minimised without a floating installation
- Applied in a grid or ribbed pattern (not full trowel application); the space between ribs provides acoustic decoupling
- Spread rate and pattern are critical — manufacturer's template pattern must be followed precisely

### 11.2 Trowel and Application

- V-notch trowel: 2.5×2.5×3mm for standard strip/plank
- A2 (square-notch): 3×3×3mm for wide boards
- Bead application (acoustic mastic): applied with specific notch head per manufacturer

**Coverage check:** Lift a board within the first 20 minutes; back face should show ≥80% coverage. If coverage is poor, use a larger notch or apply more adhesive.

**Open time management:**
- Each adhesive has a defined open time (working time) — the period between trowel application and the point where the adhesive will no longer bond effectively
- In hot/dry conditions: open time shortens (faster moisture absorption from air)
- In cold/humid conditions: open time extends
- Always lay within the open time; do not spread more adhesive than can be laid before open time expires

**Pressing and weighting:**
- After laying each board: press firmly down with hands; tap with rubber mallet across full width
- Use a roller or heavy weight (stacked timber) to maintain board contact during cure
- For wide boards: apply weights along board edges to prevent edge lifting during adhesive cure
- Do not walk on freshly laid boards for minimum 24 hours

### 11.3 Cleaning Adhesive

- Urethane adhesive squeeze-up between joints: remove immediately with mineral spirits/white spirit and damp cloth
- Cured urethane: extremely difficult to remove without mechanical abrasion; risk of surface damage
- Prevention: use correct spread rate; avoid over-application; apply tape to face of boards if adhesive bleed-up is a concern with a particular product

### 11.4 Double-Stick Method

A premium installation technique providing maximum stability and optimal acoustic performance:

**Process:**
1. Adhere acoustic mat/underlay to the subfloor using a full-coverage trowel adhesive
2. Allow underlay adhesive to cure fully (typically 24 hours)
3. Apply wood flooring adhesive to the top face of the underlay
4. Lay engineered boards in adhesive
5. Press and roll as per standard glue-down

**Advantages:**
- Acoustic mat provides excellent impact sound reduction (ΔLw 17–22 dB) while maintaining rigid adhesion
- No floating gap = maximum UFH efficiency
- Maximum stability — no movement of floor assembly possible
- Premium result in demanding situations

## 12. SECRET NAIL / STAPLE INSTALLATION

### 12.1 When to Nail Engineered Wood

Secret nailing (as per Lexicon 02, Section 9) applies to engineered wood on timber subfloors:
- Board must be minimum **15mm thick** for staple nailing; 18mm preferred
- Plywood subfloor recommended: 18mm minimum for adequate staple withdrawal resistance
- Baltic birch or structural plywood provides best nail holding

### 12.2 Nail/Staple Selection for Engineered Wood

- Same cleats and staples as solid wood (38–45mm cleats or 15.5-gauge staples)
- Less risk of tongue splitting in engineered boards than in solid (the core absorbs the impact differently)
- Pre-drilling less commonly required, but use for boards with thin (≤3mm) hardwood tongues

### 12.3 Limitations

- Maximum board width for nail-only fixing: approximately 180mm; wider boards benefit from supplementary adhesive
- Not suitable over UFH — air gap between boards and subfloor impedes heat transfer
- On concrete: generally not recommended without a timber subfloor overlay due to variable nail penetration into concrete

## 13. UNDERFLOOR HEATING — THE ENGINEERED ADVANTAGE

### 13.1 Why Engineered Wood is Superior to Solid Over UFH

Engineered wood over UFH is not just an acceptable compromise — it is the correct technical specification. Here is why:

1. **Lower movement:** Engineered boards move 50–80% less than solid boards of the same width. The low-humidity conditions caused by UFH heating make solid wide boards crack, gap and cup; engineered boards remain substantially more stable.

2. **Better thermal contact:** Glue-down installation (mandatory for UFH) ensures every board is in continuous contact with the warm screed. Solid boards secret-nailed over UFH create air gaps that impede heat flow.

3. **Thinner total depth possible:** A 14mm engineered board over 65mm UFH screed is a thinner system than a 20mm solid board. Less build-up height means less impact on door clearances and transition levels.

4. **More species options:** Many species that would be too unstable as solid boards over UFH (wide oak, walnut, maple) are perfectly suitable in engineered multi-ply construction.

### 13.2 R-Value Requirements

**Maximum combined thermal resistance:**
- **0.15 m²K/W** is the widely accepted limit for UFH efficiency
- Some manufacturers permit up to **0.17 m²K/W**; confirm with the UFH manufacturer's specification
- The floor covering contributes: typically 0.05–0.12 m²K/W for a 14–20mm engineered board
- The adhesive contributes negligibly
- No underlay in a glue-down installation — the adhesive IS the interface layer

**Calculating R-value of engineered board:**
> R-value (m²K/W) = thickness (m) ÷ thermal conductivity λ (W/mK)

Typical λ for oak-core engineered: 0.15–0.18 W/mK
- 14mm board: R = 0.014 ÷ 0.17 = 0.082 m²K/W ✓ well within limit
- 20mm board: R = 0.020 ÷ 0.17 = 0.118 m²K/W ✓ within limit
- 20mm board + 3mm underlay (0.04 m²K/W): R = 0.118 + 0.04 = 0.158 m²K/W — near limit; avoid

**Manufacturer certification:** Always obtain specific R-value data from the flooring manufacturer's technical data sheet; do not use generic calculated values for contract specification.

### 13.3 Maximum Board Width Over UFH

| Core type | Maximum recommended width |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| 3-ply softwood | 150mm |
| 3-ply hardwood | 180mm |
| 5-ply Baltic birch | 200mm |
| 7-ply Baltic birch | 220mm |
| 9-ply Baltic birch | 240mm+ (with manufacturer approval) |

### 13.4 UFH Commissioning Protocol

As detailed in Lexicon 02, Section 13.4 — this protocol is identical for engineered wood:
1. Commission UFH to maximum temperature minimum 28 days after screed installation
2. Switch off UFH 48 hours before installation
3. After installation: increase temperature 5°C per day to operating temperature
4. Maintain room humidity 45–65% RH year-round (humidification may be needed in winter)

### 13.5 Surface Temperature Monitoring

- Maximum floor surface temperature for engineered wood: **27°C** (same as solid)
- Verify with a surface thermometer, not the UFH controller temperature
- Programme UFH thermostat conservatively — actual surface temperature is what matters to the wood

## 14. PARQUET IN ENGINEERED CONSTRUCTION

### 14.1 Engineered Parquet Blocks and Strips

Traditional solid wood parquet (block, herringbone strip, chevron) is now predominantly produced in engineered construction for several reasons:
- Greater dimensional stability allows wider and longer strips without the gapping/cupping issues of solid parquet
- Suitable for installation over UFH (solid parquet over UFH is high-risk)
- Less expensive per m² in many cases due to thinner face veneer using less premium wood

**Engineered parquet formats:**
- **Herringbone strip:** Typically 70×280mm, 80×400mm, 90×450mm; 14–15mm total; 3–4mm wear layer; oak dominant
- **Chevron strip:** As herringbone but with angled ends; creates a pointed joint; more architecturally demanding
- **Block parquet (basketweave / brick):** 70×230mm blocks; as per traditional parquet dimensions
- **Large herringbone / grand herringbone:** 120×600mm+; more contemporary; dramatic scale

### 14.2 Laying Engineered Herringbone

**Establishing the centre line:**
- Snap a chalk line along the centre of the room in the intended direction of the herringbone "spine"
- Dry-lay a test row along the centre line to check scale and confirm the pattern looks correct
- The centre line is the starting edge of the first L-shaped pair of strips

**Sequence:**
1. Apply adhesive along the centre line strip
2. Place the first pair of strips forming the initial V or L; check for square against datum
3. Work outward from the centre in both directions simultaneously
4. The pattern should be symmetrical about the centre line
5. Border boards at the perimeter: cut to straight edge; may be full strips or partial strips depending on room proportions

**Adhesive for parquet:**
- Full urethane/MS polymer; trowelled onto subfloor
- Parquet blocks have significant face area — good adhesive coverage critical
- Working in small sections (approximately 0.5m²) to stay within open time

**Expansion gap:**
- Engineered herringbone requires the same perimeter expansion gap as plank (10–12mm minimum)
- The parquet pattern means the expansion gap is clearly visible — always finish with a matching solid oak border strip and skirting

### 14.3 Versailles Panel (Parquet de Versailles)

- A complex interlocking geometric panel: square panels containing diagonal basketweave pattern
- Traditionally solid large-format parquet; now widely produced in engineered construction
- Panel sizes: typically 800×800mm or 1000×1000mm
- Very high material cost and installation complexity
- Requires very flat, rigid subfloor; always glue-down

## 15. FINISHING SYSTEMS

### 15.1 Pre-Finished Engineered Wood

The majority of engineered wood products are sold pre-finished from the factory:

**Factory UV-cured lacquer:**
- Hard, durable; 6–10 coats; aluminium oxide particles typically added to the topcoat for scratch resistance
- Scratch-resistant but not scratch-proof; damage shows as white marks in the lacquer film
- Very consistent finish; no on-site disruption
- Re-finishing: site-applied lacquer can be applied over factory lacquer IF the factory lacquer is abraded and the systems are compatible — check with manufacturer

**Factory UV-cured hardwax oil:**
- Factory hardwax oil with UV cure; most popular premium finish in the UK currently
- Natural feel; excellent repairability; re-oiling with matching home care oil is easy
- Oil penetrates between factory oil molecules — no stripping required for maintenance top-up

### 15.2 Unfinished (Raw) Engineered Wood

Some products are sold unfinished — the face veneer is bare wood, ready for site-applied finishing. This allows:
- Complete control over finish colour and sheen level
- Custom staining before finishing
- The same finishing approach as sanding and finishing raw solid wood (see Lexicon 02, Sections 14 and 18)
- Site conditions must be fully controlled: no dust from other trades; temperature and humidity stable

**Key difference from solid wood:** The wear layer is finite (e.g., 4mm). The first sand before finishing removes a thin layer from the veneer. Over subsequent refinishes, the wear layer must not be fully consumed. A 4mm wear layer allows approximately 2–3 site sanding cycles over the lifetime.

### 15.3 Site Refinishing of Pre-Finished Products

- **Hardwax oil finish:** Clean thoroughly; apply compatible oil directly — no sanding typically required for top-up maintenance coats; light abrasion (white pad) if dulled
- **Lacquered finish:** The floor must be abraded (screened) before applying new lacquer to ensure adhesion between old and new film; if the old lacquer is failing or incompatible, full sanding is required

## 16. ACOUSTIC PERFORMANCE

### 16.1 Why Acoustics Matter

In apartment buildings and multi-occupancy developments, impact sound transmission between floors is a significant quality-of-life issue and a Building Regulations requirement:

**UK Building Regulations, Approved Document E (Resistance to the Passage of Sound):**
- New build: maximum **45 dB L'nT,w** (impact sound; lower = better)
- This is a field measurement that includes the influence of the whole building structure
- The floor covering's contribution is measured as **ΔLw** (weighted sound reduction improvement; higher = better)
- A floor covering with ΔLw of 20 dB will reduce impact sound transmission by 20 dB relative to bare concrete

**Pre-completion testing:** All new-build multi-occupancy dwellings require pre-completion acoustic testing (Approved Document E); the floor covering and installation method directly affect the test result.

### 16.2 Acoustic Performance by Installation Method

| Installation method | Typical ΔLw |
|---------------------|------------|
| Direct glue-down (no acoustic mat) | 0–3 dB |
| Standard 3mm foam underlay, floating | 12–16 dB |
| 5mm acoustic foam underlay, floating | 16–19 dB |
| High-density rubber mat (floating) | 19–23 dB |
| Acoustic mastic adhesive (glue-down) | 17–21 dB |
| Double-stick with acoustic mat | 20–24 dB |

**Note:** These are indicative values for the floor covering system alone; actual field performance (L'nT,w) depends on the building structure, flanking paths, and ceiling construction below.

### 16.3 Achieving Approved Document E Compliance

For new residential developments:
- The structural concrete deck typically provides the baseline performance
- The floor covering system (engineered wood + underlay/adhesive method) must be selected to contribute sufficient ΔLw to meet the overall target
- A pre-completion acoustic test is mandatory — do not assume the specification will pass without test data
- Obtain third-party acoustic test data (EN ISO 10140 or EN ISO 140-8 method) from the underlay/adhesive manufacturer for the specific system being used

### 16.4 BB93 — Education Buildings

- Building Bulletin 93 specifies acoustic performance for schools
- Reverberation time targets are important in classrooms: engineered wood on acoustic underlay can contribute to RT60 targets
- Impact sound and surface noise (footfall noise) are both relevant

## 17. EXPANSION GAPS AND MOVEMENT JOINTS

### 17.1 Perimeter Expansion Gap

Engineered wood still requires perimeter expansion gaps at all fixed structures — the cross-ply construction reduces but does not eliminate movement:

| Board width | Minimum perimeter gap |
|-------------|----------------------|
| Up to 100mm | 8–10mm |
| 100–180mm | 10–12mm |
| 180–240mm | 12–15mm |
| 240mm+ | 15–18mm |
| Large room (>8m) | Additional intermediate joints; minimum 12mm at perimeter |

**Floating floors:** The entire floating panel moves as a unit — the expansion gap accommodates the entire panel's movement. For a 10m floating room, the panel itself is very stiff (locked boards) but the entire assembly expands and contracts at the perimeter. In large rooms, intermediate joints (T-bars) divide the assembly into manageable panels.

**Glue-down floors:** Movement is absorbed locally by the elastic adhesive bond; perimeter gaps serve as a safety margin rather than accommodating total movement. Smaller gaps may be acceptable (8–10mm) in glue-down installations on stable substrates.

### 17.2 Maximum Floating Area Without Intermediate Joints

- **Standard guidance:** Maximum floating panel: **10m in any direction**
- Beyond 10m in either direction: install a T-bar movement joint to create separate floating panels
- In practice, 8m is a more conservative and preferred limit for click systems in residential use
- Long corridor installations: divide at 8–10m intervals with T-bars

### 17.3 Door Threshold Joints

Every doorway separating two rooms with floating floors requires a T-bar or expansion strip threshold:
- This separates the two floating panels; each room's floor can move independently
- Even between rooms with the same floor, if separated by a structural wall: mandatory joint
- Concealed: threshold profiles in matching wood or metal; let into subfloor if a flush threshold is specified

## 18. THRESHOLD DETAILS AND TRANSITIONS

Identical principles to solid wood (Lexicon 02, Section 16) with the following addition:

**Engineered wood to tile:**
- Engineered board is typically 12–15mm; with underlay/adhesive, may be 14–18mm above subfloor
- Tile and adhesive typically 12–15mm above subfloor
- Height difference is often small — a flush transition plate or slim reducer is used

**Engineered wood to LVT/vinyl:**
- LVT is typically 4–6mm — often significantly lower than engineered wood
- A stepped reducer (ramp) profile accommodates the height differential
- This is the most common transition in mixed-material domestic projects

## 19. PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 19.1 Delamination

**Symptoms:** Layers of the engineered board separating; hollow sound when tapped; boards lifting at edges; in severe cases, the face veneer separating from the core.

**Causes:**
1. **Manufacturing defect** — inadequate adhesive bond during manufacture; inadequate press time; contaminated surfaces
2. **Excessive moisture** — the glue bond failed due to sustained moisture exposure (flooding, persistent damp subfloor)
3. **Incompatible adhesive** — in glue-down installation, using a solvent-based adhesive that attacked the factory glue or the core material
4. **Thermal cycling over UFH** — repeated expansion and contraction over many years stressing the glue line; particularly in products not rated for UFH

**Diagnosis:**
- Tap-test: hollow sound vs solid thump indicates delamination beneath
- Lift a board (if floating) and examine: visible separation between layers is diagnostic
- Check moisture: is the subfloor moist? Is the delamination consistent with the moisture pattern?

**Action:**
- Isolated delamination: individual boards can sometimes be re-bonded with injected PVA/wood glue under a clamp — viable in floating floors
- Widespread delamination due to manufacturing defect: warranty claim with evidence; document with photographs
- Moisture-related: identify and remedy source first; replacement of affected boards

### 19.2 Joint Separation (Floating)

**Symptoms:** Visible gaps between boards that were previously tight.

**Causes:**
1. **Board contraction** — room RH too low (below 40%) in winter; boards dry out and shrink away from each other; the click joint has disengaged under tension
2. **Insufficient perimeter expansion gap** — gap consumed during expansion; subsequent contraction pulls boards away from each other as the floor "locks" against the wall
3. **Poor quality click profile** — insufficient mechanical engagement allows joints to open under thermal cycling or foot traffic
4. **Floating floor too large without intermediate joints** — accumulated movement across many boards

**Seasonal gaps:** If gaps open in winter and close in summer — normal seasonal behaviour within acceptable limits (up to ~0.5mm in standard planks). If gaps exceed 1mm or do not close seasonally — install problem.

### 19.3 Cupping in Engineered Wood

Engineered wood can still cup, though less dramatically than solid wood. Causes:
- Moisture differential between face and back (same mechanism as solid — Lexicon 02, Section 17.2)
- Common in glue-down installations over a moist concrete subfloor without adequate DPM
- HDF-core products in sustained moisture: HDF absorbs moisture and swells upward, forcing board to cup

**Action:** Same as solid wood — identify moisture source; allow to dry; assess whether boards recover. Minor cupping in engineered usually resolves when moisture is corrected. Severe or permanent cupping in HDF-core boards → replacement.

### 19.4 Surface Checking of Face Veneer

**Symptoms:** Fine cracks appearing in the face veneer, typically along the grain.

**Causes:**
- Very low RH conditions (below 35%) causing the face veneer to shrink and split
- Particularly common in: thin face veneers (under 2mm); wide boards; light-coloured pale finishes (checks more visible)
- Over UFH in very dry winter conditions without humidification

**Action:**
- Raise room RH immediately (humidifier)
- Minor checks in oil-finished floors: apply oil to fill and seal checks
- Lacquered floors: checks in lacquer film must be sanded and re-lacquered
- Severe checking: replacement if RH cannot be maintained; consider alternative species/finish for the application

### 19.5 Click Joint Failure / Rattling

**Symptoms:** Boards rattle, flex, or separate when walked on; joint is not tight.

**Causes:**
1. **Poor quality click profile** — insufficient tolerance in manufacture
2. **Subfloor too uneven** — boards bridging a hollow; foot traffic causes boards to flex and loosen joints
3. **Floating panel too large** — accumulated stress on joints in large areas
4. **Adhesive contamination in joint** — adhesive from adjacent tile grout or other source contaminating the T&G/click surface

**Action:**
- Check subfloor flatness; correct if necessary
- Check panel size against intermediate joint requirements
- For isolated boards: replace affected sections

## 20. SANDING AND RESTORATION

### 20.1 Sandability — Key Determination

Before sanding engineered wood, the installer must determine:
1. **Wear layer thickness:** Use a moisture meter or check product specification. If the wear layer is ≤1.5mm, sanding is effectively not possible without risk of sand-through.
2. **Position of face bevel:** If boards have a pronounced bevel (V-groove), the bevel will be removed after one sand, changing the floor's appearance.
3. **Whether the factory finish extends to the bevel face:** Some factory UV systems are applied to all exposed surfaces; the sanded area will need a full refinish.

**Minimum usable wear layer before sanding:** 2mm. Below this, sand-through risk is too high for a full sand. Light abrasion and re-finishing only.

### 20.2 Sanding Technique for Engineered Wood

- **Same equipment as solid wood** (see Lexicon 02, Section 18.2)
- **Key difference:** The wear layer is finite — use the minimum necessary grit to achieve a clean surface
- **Starting grit for engineered:**
  - Oiled or hardwax oil finish in good condition: 60–80 grit (remove existing oil; clean surface)
  - Lacquered floor in good condition: 40–60 grit (cut through lacquer film)
  - Heavily worn: 36–40 grit (but be very careful on thin wear layers)
- **Never sand in one direction only** across a herringbone pattern — use diagonal passes first, then along the grain direction
- **Edge sanding:** Extra care needed around perimeter; thin wear layer at edges is most at risk

### 20.3 Wear Layer Monitoring

After each sanding cycle on an engineered floor:
- Measure the wear layer thickness (drill a very small pilot hole in an inconspicuous area and use a depth gauge, or use a commercial wear layer indicator pin)
- Record for future reference
- Advise the customer of the remaining wear layer after each refinish

## 21. MAINTENANCE AND CARE

### 21.1 Routine Maintenance

Identical to solid wood (Lexicon 02, Section 19.1):
- Regular sweeping/microfibre dust mopping
- Suction-only vacuuming (no beater bar)
- Felt pads under all furniture legs
- Non-rubber-backed rugs at entrances
- Damp mop with pH-neutral hardwood cleaner; never wet mop
- No steam cleaners

### 21.2 Oil/Hardwax Oil Maintenance

- Re-oil annually (residential); more frequently in commercial
- Rubio Monocoat maintenance soap for cleaning; maintenance oil for periodic top-up
- Osmo Wash & Care for cleaning; Osmo Polyx Touch Up for spot repairs
- Spot repair: sand affected area lightly; apply matching oil; blend with surrounding floor

### 21.3 Lacquered Floor Maintenance

- Use only compatible lacquered-floor cleaner
- Maintenance lacquer top-coat (no sanding required on some systems) extends time between full sands
- No wax or polish on lacquered floors — reduces adhesion of future maintenance coats

### 21.4 Humidity Management

The single most important maintenance message for any wood floor:
- **Maintain interior RH between 40–65% year-round**
- UK winters with central heating can drop RH to 20–30% without humidification
- At RH below 35%: surface checking, joint separation, and excessive gapping are likely
- Simple hygrometer in the room; humidifier in winter if needed
- This applies equally to solid wood, engineered wood, and bamboo

## 22. COMMERCIAL APPLICATIONS

### 22.1 EN 1307 Classification for Engineered Wood

Engineered wood flooring is classified under EN 1307 (same framework as carpet — see Lexicon 01, Section 5.1) using the following classes relevant to wood:

| Class | Application | Typical specification |
|-------|-------------|----------------------|
| 21 | Light domestic | Bedroom |
| 22 | General domestic | Living room |
| 23 | Heavy domestic | Hall, stairs |
| 31 | Moderate commercial | Private office |
| 32 | General commercial | Office, hotel bedroom |
| 33 | Heavy commercial | Retail, hotel lobby |
| 34 | Very heavy commercial | Airport, department store |

For commercial engineered wood specification, the key performance tests:
- **EN ISO 10361 (Vettermann drum test):** Appearance change after simulated walking
- **EN 985 (Castor chair test):** Office environment with heavy rolling loads — critical for commercial
- **EN 13893 (Slip resistance):** Dynamic coefficient of friction ≥0.30 dry

### 22.2 Sports Flooring (Gymnasium / Dance)

**BS EN 14904 — Surfaces for indoor sports areas:**
- Specifies performance requirements for sports floors including wood
- Key parameters: force reduction (shock absorption), vertical deformation, friction coefficient, ball behaviour, surface regularity
- Maple is the traditional gymnasium species; beech and oak also used
- **Force reduction:** typically 25–75% class (P1–P3); floor must absorb impact to protect athletes' joints
- **DIN 18032-2 (German standard):** Widely cited for sports hall floors; often specified alongside EN 14904

**Sprung floor systems:**
- The typical sports floor is not a simple engineered board over concrete
- A **sprung floor** uses sleepers, battens, or a purpose-built resilient subfloor assembly beneath the hardwood surface to provide force reduction
- The wood is the wearing surface; the spring comes from the substructure
- Common sprung systems: Boen Harlequin, Junckers, Gerflor (see also Lexicon 12)

### 22.3 Hotel and Hospitality

- **Bedrooms:** 14–15mm engineered planks in premium species (oak, walnut); factory hardwax oil finish; EN 1307 Class 32 minimum
- **Corridors:** Class 33 minimum; solution-dyed or heavily factory-coated products; acoustic mat below to manage impact noise from adjacent floors
- **Public areas:** Class 33–34; heavy specification engineered or solid; consider herringbone or patterned formats for aesthetic differentiation
- **Fire performance:** EN 13501-1 floor classification required; most engineered wood achieves DFL/CFL-s1; check product certification

### 22.4 Healthcare

- Wood flooring in healthcare is limited: infection control requirements often favour impervious surfaces (vinyl, resin)
- Engineered wood is acceptable in:
  - Private patient rooms
  - Staff offices and rest rooms
  - Mental health environments (warm, calming aesthetic is therapeutic)
- Not recommended in: operating areas, wet rooms, high-hygiene clinical areas
- If specified: factory lacquer (impermeable) over hardwax oil; bleach-compatible finish required in some areas

## 24. SUSTAINABILITY AND ENVIRONMENTAL CONSIDERATIONS

### 24.1 Resource Efficiency

Engineered wood uses significantly less premium timber per m² than solid wood:
- A 4mm wear layer of oak over a birch ply core uses approximately **80% less premium oak** per m² than an 18mm solid oak board
- This makes engineered wood a substantially more resource-efficient use of high-value hardwood species
- Particularly important for: tropical hardwoods; slow-growing species (walnut, cherry); large-format boards

### 24.2 FSC and PEFC Certification

- The same FSC/PEFC requirements apply as for solid wood (Lexicon 02, Section 21)
- Both the face veneer species AND the core species must be certified
- A product can carry FSC Mix certification if one or more components are from FSC-certified sources

### 24.3 Formaldehyde and Indoor Air Quality

- E1 minimum; E0 preferred for residential and healthcare applications
- GREENGUARD Gold certification (UL) indicates very low VOC emissions; used in LEED specification
- EU Ecolabel for floor coverings covers engineered wood; limits harmful substances in adhesives and finishes
- Low-VOC adhesives for site installation: urethane MS polymer adhesives are generally low-VOC; solvent-based adhesives should be avoided in occupied or sensitive buildings

### 24.4 End-of-Life

- Engineered wood with wood core: can be chipped for biomass energy; some recycling to particleboard
- Engineered wood with PVC facing (not standard but exists in some products): more complex recycling
- The glue system in engineered cores makes material recycling to timber difficult — energy recovery is more practical
- Some manufacturers (e.g., Kahrs, Dinesen) have take-back schemes for commercial projects

### 24.5 BREEAM Mat Credits

- FSC or PEFC certification of both face and core species required for Mat 05 credit
- EPDs (Environmental Product Declarations) are increasingly available from major manufacturers (Boen, Kahrs, Quick-Step, Dinesen)
- EPDs provide third-party verified lifecycle assessment data for inclusion in BREEAM credits

| **EMC** | Equilibrium Moisture Content |
| **Engineered wood** | Multi-layer board with real wood face veneer over cross-ply core; the subject of this lexicon |
| **EPD** | Environmental Product Declaration; third-party verified lifecycle assessment |
