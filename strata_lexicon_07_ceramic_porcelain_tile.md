## 1. TILE BODY TYPES — CERAMIC VS PORCELAIN VS OTHER

### 1.1 The Fundamental Distinction

The tile market is divided into two primary body types: **ceramic** and **porcelain**. These are not marketing terms — they reflect genuinely different raw material compositions, manufacturing conditions, and resulting physical properties. Getting this distinction right is the foundation of correct specification.

### 1.2 Ceramic Tile

**Definition:** A tile body produced from a mixture of clays, minerals, and water, fired at relatively moderate kiln temperatures to produce a tile body that is not fully vitrified.

**Raw materials:**
- Ball clay: provides plasticity and binding
- Kaolin (china clay): white-burning; reduces shrinkage
- Feldspar and quartz: flux and filler; provide body
- Calcium carbonate: flux in some formulations
- Water: processing only; driven off in firing

**Firing temperature:** Typically 1,000–1,150°C

**Porosity (water absorption):**
- EN ISO 10545-3 defines tile groups by water absorption
- Ceramic floor tiles (Group BIIa and BIIb): water absorption 3–6% (BIIa) and 6–10% (BIIb)
- Some ceramic wall tiles: water absorption >10% (Group BIII)
- The higher porosity means ceramic is more susceptible to staining, freeze-thaw damage, and moisture-related movement

**Tile body colour:** Variable; typically buff, red, or brown depending on clay iron content; covered by glaze in glazed products

**Typical applications:**
- Wall tiling (residential and light commercial)
- Floor tiling in light domestic use
- Decorative borders and feature tiles
- Not suitable for external use in UK climate (freeze-thaw risk due to water absorption)

### 1.3 Porcelain Tile

**Definition:** A dense, vitrified tile body produced from very fine white-burning clay (typically kaolin-dominant) fired at high temperatures under high pressure to produce a near-zero porosity body.

**Raw materials:**
- Feldspar: 40–60%; the primary flux; gives vitrification at lower temperatures
- Kaolin (fine): 20–30%; provides pure white body
- Quartz: 20–30%; structure and hardness
- Other minerals and additives depending on manufacturer and product

**Pressing:** Dry-pressing at very high pressure (up to 400–500 kg/cm²) before firing; this produces a dense, uniform body even before firing

**Firing temperature:** 1,200–1,300°C — significantly higher than ceramic; at these temperatures the feldspar flux vitrifies (turns glassy), filling all pores and creating a near-monolithic ceramic-glass body

**Porosity (water absorption):**
- EN ISO 10545-3 Group AI: water absorption ≤0.5% — the defining characteristic of porcelain
- Some ultra-dense porcelain (double-fired, special composition): <0.1%
- This near-zero porosity means: frost-resistant; stain-resistant (no pores for staining agents to penetrate); suitable for external use

**Tile body colour:** White or near-white (low iron feldspar/kaolin); consistent through the body depth

**Mohs hardness:** 7–8 (porcelain body); resistant to most scratching agents

### 1.4 Porcelain vs Ceramic — Decision Guide

| Property | Ceramic | Porcelain |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| Water absorption | 3–10% | ≤0.5% |
| Frost resistance | No | Yes |
| Stain resistance | Moderate | Excellent |
| Hardness | Moderate | High |
| Weight | Lighter | Heavier |
| Internal floors | Yes (light-medium) | Yes (all uses) |
| External floors | No (UK climate) | Yes |
| Wet rooms/showers | With suitable grout | Preferred |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Cutting difficulty | Easier | Harder (harder body) |
| Available formats | Up to 600×600 typical | Up to 3200×1600 (slabs) |

**The professional rule:** Always specify porcelain for floor applications in residential and commercial contexts. Ceramic floor tiles are appropriate only for light residential use in dry interior areas. Any wet area, external area, or commercial application: porcelain only.

### 1.5 Other Tile Body Types

**Quarry tile:**
- Unglazed extruded ceramic tile; typically red, buff, or brown
- High iron content clay; fired to give a dense, hard body with some porosity
- Slip-resistant naturally (unglazed, textured surface)
- Used in: traditional British kitchens, pubs, farmhouse floors, external terraces
- Frost-resistant in dense formulations; check individual products
- Traditionally laid on sand/cement bed; some are compatible with modern adhesives

**Terracotta:**
- Unglazed fired clay; high porosity; warm red/orange tones
- Traditional Mediterranean and rustic aesthetic
- Must be sealed before use (and re-sealed periodically); the porous body will absorb staining agents without sealing
- Not frost-resistant unless specifically dense-fired; not for external UK use without testing
- Traditional installation: thick sand/cement bed; some products compatible with adhesive

**Encaustic tile (Victorian/geometric):**
- Pattern set into the tile body using different coloured clay inlays; the pattern runs to some depth (not just surface decoration)
- Made from cement-bound or fired clay compound
- The authentic Victorian floor tiles in period properties
- Reproduction cement encaustic tiles: widely available; porous; must be sealed; not suitable for heavy commercial without sealing programme
- Genuine period originals: very valuable; restoration rather than replacement preferred

**Glass tile:**
- Made from vitreous glass; transparent or semi-transparent; wall use primarily
- Floor use: limited to specific anti-slip glass tiles rated for floor application; not generally recommended for floor use due to hardness inconsistency under load and slip risk
- Wall tiling: decorative; mosaics; wet areas

**Cement tile (handmade):**
- Pressed from cement, marble powder, and mineral pigment; no firing
- Porous; must be sealed; pattern is cement-based not glaze
- Increasingly popular in artisan/boutique interior design
- Not suitable for outdoor use; requires careful maintenance

**Mosaic tile:**
- Small tiles (typically <100mm per piece) on mesh backing for easy installation
- Available in: glass, ceramic, porcelain, natural stone, metal
- Floor use: excellent slip resistance (many small tiles = many joints = texture)
- Pool tiling: glass mosaic is standard in swimming pool liners
- Complex layouts; pattern-matching; installation is more involved than large tiles

## 2. MANUFACTURING PROCESSES

### 2.1 Pressing

**Dry pressing (monoporosa / double-fired):**
- Powder clay is pressed in a hydraulic press at very high pressure
- Produces a precise, consistent tile body
- Standard for most ceramic and porcelain tiles
- After pressing: bisque (unglazed body) fired; glaze applied; second firing (for double-fired products)
- Single-fired (monoporosa): glaze applied before firing; single firing fuses body and glaze simultaneously; more efficient; lower cost; slightly less precise than double-fired

**Extrusion (for quarry tiles and some large format):**
- Wet clay forced through a die and cut to length
- Produces a tile with more surface texture variation
- Standard for quarry tiles, handmade-look products

### 2.2 Digital Printing Technology

The transformation of the tile industry in the past 15 years has been driven by digital inkjet printing:

**Traditional screen printing:**
- Silk screen applied glaze colours; limited colour range; short repeat
- Good for geometric and repeat patterns

**Digital inkjet printing (current standard):**
- Industrial inkjet printers apply ink directly to the tile surface before glazing
- Effectively unlimited colour range and resolution
- Can replicate any natural material: marble veining, wood grain, concrete texture, stone
- Long repeat: some products have 3–6m² before a tile appears again — undetectable repeat in most rooms
- EIR (Embossed in Register — see Lexicon 04): the embossed surface texture can be precisely registered to the printed design; the definitive quality indicator for realistic natural material effect

**The quality consequence:** Modern large-format digitally printed porcelain tiles are visually indistinguishable from the natural stone or marble they replicate when laid and viewed from normal standing distance. The primary remaining tell is the uniformly consistent format (real stone slabs are variable) and the lack of natural veining continuity across multiple tiles (premium products now use "book-matched" production to address this).

### 2.3 Rectification

**Rectified tile:** After firing, the tile is precision-ground on all four edges to achieve exact dimensional tolerance.

**Non-rectified tile:** Comes directly from the kiln; dimensions vary slightly from tile to tile due to natural firing shrinkage variation

**Why it matters:**
- Non-rectified tiles have dimensional variation of ±0.5–2.0mm; they must be laid with a wider grout joint to absorb this variation (minimum 3mm; typically 5–8mm)
- Rectified tiles have dimensional variation of ±0.1–0.3mm; they can be laid with much smaller grout joints (1–2mm is achievable)
- Large format tiles are almost always rectified — the dimensional precision is essential to prevent lippage with narrow joints
- The visual distinction: large rectified tiles with 1–2mm joints create a near-seamless surface; non-rectified tiles with wide joints create a more traditional "tiled" appearance

**Rectification cost:** Adds approximately 15–25% to the tile manufacturing cost; premium products are rectified as standard; budget tiles are non-rectified

## 3. TILE DIMENSIONS, FORMATS, AND CALIBRATION

### 3.1 Standard Tile Formats

Tile formats are defined as nominal dimensions (the face size); actual size may vary slightly:

**Mosaic formats:**
- 25×25mm up to 100×100mm; typically on 300×300mm mesh sheet

**Small tile formats:**
- 100×100mm, 150×150mm (traditional Victorian encaustic)
- 200×200mm, 150×300mm

**Standard residential formats:**
- 300×300mm
- 400×400mm
- 450×450mm
- 600×600mm (the dominant contemporary residential format)

**Large format (contemporary):**
- 750×750mm
- 800×400mm (large plank format)
- 900×900mm
- 1000×1000mm
- 1200×600mm
- 1200×1200mm

**Very large format / slabs:**
- 1600×800mm
- 2400×1200mm
- 3200×1600mm
- Sintered stone panels (Lapitec, Dekton, Neolith) in these formats

**Format selection considerations:**
- Larger format tiles make spaces appear larger
- Smaller formats suit period properties and more intricate design approaches
- Large format tiles require a flatter subfloor (the large rigid tile will bridge minor irregularities but lippage between tiles becomes an issue)
- Plank formats (e.g., 200×800mm, 300×1200mm) replicate wood plank aesthetics in tile

### 3.2 Calibration

**Calibre/Calibration:** The actual measured size of tiles within a production run. Even within a single production batch, the kiln firing process introduces slight dimensional variation.

**Why calibration matters for large tiles:**
- Non-rectified large tiles with ±1.5mm dimensional variation and a 2mm joint: the variation in tile size means the joint width must accommodate the variation — joints will visibly vary in width
- Rectified large format tiles: ±0.2mm variation; 1.5mm joint is achievable and consistent

**Matching calibres:** When purchasing tiles from different batches (e.g., topping up a supply), tiles from different calibre batches must not be mixed — the dimensional difference will be visible as irregular joint widths. Always specify the same calibre when ordering additional material.

**Shade sorting:** Tiles are also sorted by shade (L-value or similar). Tiles of the same calibre but different shade should be mixed when laying to distribute any colour variation naturally across the floor.

## 4. SURFACE FINISHES

### 4.1 Glazed

A glass-based coating fused to the tile body during firing. The glaze provides:
- Colour and design surface
- Stain resistance (non-porous glaze surface)
- The visible finish (matt, satin, gloss, textured)
- Additional hardness in some cases (titanium-reinforced glazes, etc.)

**Glaze characteristics vary by PEI (Porcelain Enamel Institute) hardness class — see Section 7.**

**Limitation of glazed tiles:** If the glaze is worn through (heavy commercial use, abrasive traffic), the tile body beneath is exposed. For glazed ceramic tiles, the body is porous; for glazed porcelain, the body is still stain-resistant but aesthetically different from the glaze surface.

### 4.2 Polished (Lappato / Full Polished)

- Full polished: the tile surface is machine-polished to a mirror-like finish after firing
- Lappato (semi-polished): partial polishing creates a textured effect with polished and unpolished areas
- Very reflective; maximises light; creates luxurious aesthetic
- **Significant limitation:** Polished porcelain is slippery when wet — unsuitable for wet areas without additional anti-slip treatment; check slip resistance data before specifying
- **Maintenance:** Polished surfaces show every mark, watermark, and scratch; require regular maintenance to retain appearance
- The polish is a surface refinement — the tile body is unaffected; porcelain's inherent stain resistance remains

### 4.3 Matt (Unpolished / Natural)

- Tile surface is not polished; the natural texture of the fired tile face is retained
- Better slip resistance than polished
- Hides marks and watermarks better than polished
- Industrial/contemporary aesthetic popular in current market
- Variety of textures available: smooth matt, coarse-textured, micro-textured

### 4.4 Textured / Structured

- Surface texture pressed into the tile during manufacturing (direct pressing into the die) or applied via transfer/stamping
- Ranges from fine micro-texture to deep relief resembling natural stone, wood, or concrete
- Better slip resistance from texture
- Harder to clean in very deep textures (soil lodges in recesses)

### 4.5 Anti-Slip Finishes

- **Grip surface:** Controlled texture specifically engineered for slip resistance; tested to DIN 51130 or DIN 51097
- **R-value tiles:** Porcelain and ceramic tiles available in R9–R12 classifications for commercial and wet-area use
- **Natural stone-look with anti-slip:** Digital print with grip texture; available in slip-rated versions of most popular visual effects

### 4.6 Technical Body Finishes (Unglazed Porcelain)

- Full-body porcelain: the pigment/colour runs through the entire tile body (like VCT for tile); if the surface chips, the same colour is present throughout
- Through-body porcelain: same concept; also called "full-body"
- **Significant for commercial use:** Through-body porcelain on stairs, step nosings, and high-wear areas is preferred — chips and edge damage do not reveal a different-coloured body

## 5. SLIP RESISTANCE

### 5.1 Critical Importance

Slip and fall incidents on tiled floors are one of the most common causes of personal injury claims and insurance losses in commercial premises. Slip resistance specification is not optional — it is a legal duty of care under the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 and the Occupiers Liability Acts.

### 5.2 Slip Resistance Standards (recap from Lexicon 06 context)

**UK — Pendulum Test (BS 7976-2 / EN 14231):**
- The primary UK test method for post-installation assessment and legal investigation
- PTV (Pendulum Test Value) ≥36 = low slip risk (UK Slip Resistance Group guidance)
- PTV 25–35 = moderate slip risk (requires risk assessment)
- PTV <25 = high slip risk (not acceptable)
- Measured wet unless stated otherwise; wet is the relevant condition for injury risk

**DIN 51130 — R classification (workplaces, Germany):**
- R9–R13 ramp test with oil contamination; widely specified in UK commercial
- **R9:** General commercial, dry-risk environments
- **R10:** Wet-risk commercial (hotel bathrooms, commercial kitchens — general areas)
- **R11:** Higher wet-risk (commercial kitchen with oils; industrial food processing)
- **R12/R13:** Specialist industrial

**DIN 51097 — A/B/C (barefoot wet areas):**
- **Class A:** Changing rooms, poolside (flat)
- **Class B:** Pool surrounds, shower areas, ramps
- **Class C:** Pool steps, steep wet ramps

**BS EN 13893 — Dynamic coefficient of friction (dry):**
- μ ≥0.30 minimum for general use

### 5.3 Specifying Anti-Slip Tiles

The relationship between a tile's visual appeal and its slip resistance is a fundamental tension in specification:

- Polished/smooth tiles: highest visual appeal; worst slip resistance
- Heavily textured tiles: best slip resistance; harder to clean; less sleek aesthetics
- Modern micro-textured and lappato tiles: the current sweet spot — a satin or micro-textured surface that meets R10 or better while remaining visually contemporary

**Practical specification guide:**

| Location | Recommended minimum | Notes |
|----------|---------------------|-------|
| Dry residential floor | R9 / PTV ≥36 | Polished acceptable in living room |
| Residential bathroom | R10 / PTV ≥36 wet | No polished tile on floor |
| Residential shower tray | R10–R11 / PTV ≥36 wet | Mosaic preferred (many joints = texture) |
| Hotel bathroom | R10 / PTV ≥36 wet | Operator liability |
| Commercial kitchen | R11 minimum | DIN 51130; oil contamination |
| Commercial swimming pool | DIN 51097 Class B/C | Barefoot wet |
| External entrance | R10–R11 | Wet rain exposure |
| Hospital floor | R10 / PTV ≥36 wet | HSE enforcement applies |

### 5.4 Anti-Slip Treatments

Post-installation anti-slip treatments can enhance the slip resistance of existing tiles:
- **Acid etching treatment:** Micro-etches the glaze surface to create texture; increases friction
- **Anti-slip coatings:** PU or acrylic coatings with aggregate; applied topically; requires maintenance
- These are remedial measures — correct initial specification is always preferable

## 6. LARGE FORMAT TILE TECHNOLOGY

### 6.1 Definition and Market Context

Large format tiles (LFT) are conventionally defined as tiles with any face dimension exceeding 600mm, though the term is used loosely for anything from 600×600mm upward. True large format begins at 900×900mm or 1200×600mm; ultra-large format includes 1200×1200mm, 1600×800mm, and slab products up to 3200×1600mm.

The market for large format tiles has grown dramatically — contemporary interior design strongly favours the seamless, near-joint-free aesthetic they provide.

### 6.2 Technical Challenges of Large Format

**Subfloor flatness:**
- A large rigid tile spanning 1200mm will bridge a hollow; if the hollow is significant, the tile is only supported at its edges. Under foot traffic load, the tile flexes; the adhesive bond stresses; the tile cracks.
- EN 13006 (adhesive standard) specifies: for tiles with any dimension >600mm, the subfloor must not deviate more than **3mm under a 2m straight-edge** and **1mm under a 200mm straight-edge** (checking short-range undulation)
- This is stricter than the standard 3mm/2m tolerance; the short-range tolerance is critical for LFT

**Adhesive coverage:**
- EN 12004 (adhesive standard) requires **minimum 80% coverage** for wall tiles and general floor tiles
- For tiles with any dimension ≥600mm or in wet areas: **minimum 95% coverage**
- For external tiles: **100% coverage** (no voids; voids collect water; freeze-thaw damage)
- The back-buttering technique (applying adhesive to both the subfloor and the tile back) is required for large format tiles to achieve adequate coverage

**Lippage:**
- Lippage is the height difference between the surface of adjacent tiles; any protrusion is both aesthetically visible and a trip hazard
- EN 13451 specifies maximum allowable lippage: general use ≤2mm; rectified tiles with <3mm joint ≤1mm
- With large format tiles and small grout joints (1–2mm), even a minor subfloor undulation or variation in adhesive bed depth creates visible and potentially dangerous lippage
- Achieving lippage control on 1200×1200mm tiles requires: flat subfloor; consistent adhesive bed; tile levelling system (see Section 13.7)

**Tile weight:**
- A 1200×600mm porcelain tile at 9–11mm thickness weighs approximately 15–20kg per tile
- Very large slabs (3200×1600mm): can weigh 100kg+; mechanical handling equipment required
- Adhesive performance under this weight: the adhesive must have sufficient initial (open-time) grab to hold the tile before it cures; heavy tiles require a full-mortar bed or a rapid-set high-performance adhesive

**Thermal movement of large format:**
- A 1200mm porcelain tile has a coefficient of thermal expansion approximately 6–8×10⁻⁶ /°C
- Over a 20°C temperature change: 1200mm × 7×10⁻⁶ × 20 = 0.17mm movement per tile
- Across a 5m floor (approximately 4 tiles at 1200mm): 4 × 0.17mm = 0.68mm
- This accumulated movement is absorbed by grout joints and movement joints — which is why eliminating grout joints completely in large format installations is technically dangerous without adequate movement joint provision

### 6.3 Sintered Stone (Lapitec, Dekton, Neolith)

A new category of ultra-large format panel that is distinct from both conventional porcelain and natural stone:

**What it is:** Ultra-compact sintered surfaces produced by applying extreme heat (1,200°C) and pressure (>20,000 tonnes) to a blend of glass, feldspar, quartz, clay, and other minerals. The result is a fully vitrified, non-porous panel with near-zero porosity.

**Key brands:**
- **Dekton (Cosentino):** The original category-defining product; marketed as superior to both porcelain and natural stone
- **Neolith (TheSize):** Sintered surface; wide range of designs including ultra-realistic marble effects
- **Lapitec (Lapitec S.p.A):** Full-body sintered surface; consistent colour through full depth

**Properties vs standard porcelain:**
- Porosity: effectively zero (<0.05%); slightly better than porcelain
- Scratch resistance: Mohs 8+ (harder than most porcelain)
- UV resistance: fully UV-stable; suitable for external use without colour fade
- Heat resistance: withstands direct heat contact (cutting boards, hot pans) without damage
- Thickness options: 4mm (ultra-thin), 8mm, 12mm, 20mm
- Format: up to 3200×1600mm; available as worktops, wall panels, and floors

**Installation differences from porcelain:**
- Requires specific adhesives rated for the product; some standard adhesives are incompatible
- The extreme hardness requires diamond-blade cutting with specialist tools
- 4mm ultra-thin panels: require full-coverage adhesive and perfectly flat substrate; cannot bridge any subfloor irregularity; specialist installation
- Movement joints: same requirements as porcelain; the product does not reduce movement joint needs

**Thermal shock:** Sintered stone (particularly Dekton) has very low thermal expansion and high thermal shock resistance — it can be installed in external applications with significant temperature variation; also suitable as cladding behind fires and around ovens

## 7. PERFORMANCE STANDARDS AND CLASSIFICATION

### 7.1 EN ISO 10545 — The Tile Test Standard Series

EN ISO 10545 is a series of standards that together define the complete test framework for ceramic and porcelain tiles. The key parts relevant to flooring specification:

**EN ISO 10545-3: Water absorption (porosity):**
- Group AI: ≤0.5% (porcelain)
- Group AIIa: 0.5–3%
- Group BIIa: 3–6% (typical ceramic floor tile)
- Group BIIb: 6–10%
- Group BIII: >10% (wall tile only)

**EN ISO 10545-4: Modulus of rupture and breaking strength:**
- The tile's resistance to cracking under load
- Porcelain floor tiles: minimum breaking strength typically >1,300N (EN 14411 Class S1)
- Larger tiles have higher breaking strength requirements

**EN ISO 10545-6: Resistance to deep abrasion (unglazed tiles):**
- Volume of material removed by standardised abrasion wheel
- Relevant for unglazed porcelain; through-body products

**EN ISO 10545-7: Resistance to surface abrasion (glazed tiles — PEI):**
- The Porcelain Enamel Institute (PEI) rating for glazed tile wear resistance
- PEI 0: No foot traffic; wall tiles only
- PEI 1: Bare feet only; bathrooms
- PEI 2: Light traffic; residential with soft-soled footwear
- PEI 3: Light to moderate foot traffic; residential; some light commercial
- PEI 4: Moderate to heavy traffic; residential and commercial
- PEI 5: Heavy commercial traffic; industrial applications

**EN ISO 10545-12: Frost resistance:**
- Mandatory for external and wet-area tiles
- Only Group AI porcelain (≤0.5% water absorption) is frost-resistant; ceramic is not

**EN ISO 10545-13: Chemical resistance:**
- Test using standardised household and commercial chemicals
- Class GBA/GBB: general household chemicals
- Class HA/HB: high concentration chemicals (swimming pools, industrial)

**EN ISO 10545-14: Stain resistance:**
- Classes 1–5: resistance to standardised staining agents

### 7.2 EN 14411 — Ceramic Tile Classification

The overarching European standard for ceramic tile classification, referencing all relevant EN ISO 10545 tests and defining minimum performance requirements for each tile group/class.

**Key for specification:**
- A tile must carry CE marking indicating compliance with EN 14411
- The technical data sheet must state the Group (AI, BIIa, etc.), PEI class, slip resistance, breaking strength, and other test results
- A specifier should always request and review the technical data sheet before specifying a tile in a critical application

### 7.3 Building Regulations Applicability

**Approved Document C (Site preparation and resistance to contamination):**
- External tiles must be frost-resistant (Group AI porcelain)

**Approved Document M (Access to and use of buildings):**
- Slip resistance requirements at accessible routes; changes in level ≤6mm flush threshold; contrast requirements for step nosings
- Relevant to: entrance lobbies, ramps, stairs, and accessible toilets

**Approved Document K (Protection from falling, collision, and impact):**
- Stair nosing visibility; trip hazard thresholds; relevant to tile selection on stairs

## 8. SUBFLOOR REQUIREMENTS

### 8.1 The Non-Negotiable Rule

Unlike flexible floor coverings, tiles are rigid. They cannot flex or accommodate movement. This creates a fundamental constraint:

**Any movement in the subfloor will crack the tiles, fail the adhesive bond, or both.**

The subfloor must be:
1. Structurally sound — no movement, spring, or flex
2. Flat to the required tolerance
3. Dry — within moisture limits
4. Clean — free of contamination
5. Compatible with the adhesive system

### 8.2 Concrete Subfloors

**Moisture limits:**
- Maximum **75% RH** (BS 8203 / BS 5385) before tiling
- For water-sensitive adhesives (standard cementitious): >75% RH will retard curing and weaken bond
- Flexible adhesives (C2S2 class) are more tolerant of moisture but still require ≤75% RH for standard installation
- Exception: if a suitable primer is applied and the adhesive is specifically rated for higher moisture conditions (some manufacturers approve up to 90% RH with their specific primer+adhesive system — always check data sheet)

**Surface preparation:**
- Grind off laitance (the weak surface layer)
- Fill all cracks, holes, and hollows with SLC or repair mortar
- Maximum deviation: **3mm under 2m straight-edge** for standard tiles; **3mm/2m and 1mm/200mm** for large format (>600mm)
- Remove all contamination: oil, paint, curing compounds, adhesive residue

**New concrete:**
- Minimum 28 days before tiling; test RH after 28 days
- Some fast-setting screeds can be tiled earlier — verify with manufacturer

**Anhydrite (calcium sulphate) screeds:**
- Very common with UFH; requires specific treatment
- Must be sanded to remove surface laitance
- Must be primed with a dedicated anhydrite primer before adhesive — do not apply cementitious adhesive directly to unsanded, unprimed anhydrite
- Allow primer to cure fully before tiling

### 8.3 Timber Subfloors — The Structural Challenge

**The problem with timber and tile:**
Timber is not an ideal tile substrate. It flexes, it moves seasonally with moisture, and it is subject to long-term structural movement. Any of these will crack tiles.

**However, timber subfloors can be successfully tiled if:**
1. The deflection is within acceptable limits
2. A decoupling membrane is used
3. The tile and grout systems are appropriate

**Deflection limits:**
- BS 5385-3 specifies: maximum subfloor deflection **L/360** (where L is the span between joists)
- For a 600mm joist span: maximum deflection = 600/360 = 1.67mm under static load
- This is very stiff; most standard timber floors deflect more than this
- In practice: minimum 18mm structural plywood over the joists (or over existing boards) with additional 6mm plywood overlay is the minimum starting point; a tile-able concrete topping or a dedicated tile backer board is preferred

**Acceptable methods for tiling timber floors:**

**Method 1: Plywood substrate:**
- Minimum 18mm structural plywood (or two layers: 18mm + 6mm or 12mm + 12mm) — total minimum 24mm
- All joints staggered; screwed at 200mm centres
- Surface flatness: check and correct before tiling
- Apply primer; tile with flexible C2S2 adhesive
- Movement joints at all perimeter edges and at maximum 3m centres within the floor
- **Risk:** If the subfloor deflects or moves seasonally, tiles will crack regardless of adhesive flexibility. The flexible adhesive helps but is not a complete solution for dynamic movement.

**Method 2: Decoupling membrane (Schlüter Ditra, Mapei Mapeguard, etc.):**
- A polymer membrane (often polyethylene with a fleece backing) bonded to the subfloor with tile adhesive; tiles are then bonded to the top of the membrane with adhesive
- The membrane uncouples the tile layer from the subfloor — minor subfloor movement is absorbed by the membrane without cracking the tiles
- Far more reliable than plywood alone on timber
- Schlüter DITRA: polyethylene sheet with studded back and fleece on top; the studs provide the uncoupling action
- Requires: flat subfloor (the membrane does not fill irregularities); compatible adhesive; specific tile adhesive onto the membrane top

**Method 3: Tile backer board (Hardiebacker, Wedi, Marmox, Schlüter Kerdi-Board):**
- Rigid boards screwed to the timber subfloor; tiles bonded to the board
- Hardiebacker (James Hardie): cement-fibre board; heavy; excellent tile substrate; water-resistant
- Wedi board: XPS (extruded polystyrene) with glass-fibre reinforced cement coating; lightweight; waterproof; excellent for wet areas
- Schlüter KERDI-BOARD: XPS + reinforced mortar coat; tile directly
- Tape all board joints with alkali-resistant (AR) glass fibre tape and adhesive before tiling

### 8.4 Wall Substrates for Floor Tiles (Adjacent Walls)

While this lexicon focuses on floor tiling, the adjacent wall tiling in bathrooms, wet rooms, and kitchens is integrally connected to the floor system:

- Plasterboard (standard drywall): NOT suitable for wet areas without additional waterproofing (see Section 9)
- Moisture-resistant plasterboard (MR board / Aquapanel): better than standard but still requires tanking in wet zones
- Tile backer boards (Wedi, Marmox, Hardiebacker): directly tileable without additional waterproofing in many applications
- Blockwork/brickwork: sound and stable; prime before tiling; check for contamination

## 9. WATERPROOFING AND TANKING SYSTEMS

### 9.1 The Waterproofing Principle

Grout is not waterproof — even with excellent grout, water will slowly penetrate a tiled floor or wall over time. In wet rooms and shower areas, this penetrating water will reach the substrate behind the tiles. If the substrate is not waterproof:
- Timber substrates: rot and structural failure
- Plasterboard: swelling, mould, structural failure
- Concrete blockwork: damp penetration to adjacent rooms

**The solution:** Waterproof the substrate before tiling, not after. Tiles and grout are the finish; the waterproofing membrane is the actual water barrier.

### 9.2 Tanking Systems

**Liquid-applied tanking (most common residential method):**
- A polymer-modified waterproof membrane applied by brush or roller to the substrate in multiple coats
- Typically 1–3 coats; full coverage; particular attention to corners and junctions
- Corners and junctions reinforced with waterproof tape embedded in the first coat
- Allow to cure between coats and before tiling (typically 4–24 hours per coat depending on product)
- Key products: BAL Tank It, Mapei Mapelastic, F. Ball Stopgap 1200 Plus, Weber.sys 815, Schlüter KERDI (sheet version)

**Sheet membrane tanking (Schlüter KERDI, Wedi):**
- Bonded sheet membrane applied to the substrate with unmodified adhesive (for Schlüter KERDI) or compatible adhesive
- Very reliable; coverage is consistent; tape joints between sheets
- The membrane is also the tile adhesive substrate
- Preferred for demanding wet room applications

**Combined board + waterproofing:**
- Wedi, Marmox, and similar XPS/cement boards are inherently waterproof in their core
- Board joints must be taped and sealed with waterproof sealant or tape
- Provides both the structural substrate and the waterproofing in one layer

### 9.3 Wet Room Waterproofing — Full Specification

A wet room (full walk-in shower with no tray; water falls on the floor over a wide area) is the most demanding application:

**Complete specification:**
1. Structural substrate (concrete floor or timber with 18mm+ ply overlay)
2. Liquid-applied tanking membrane over entire floor AND walls to minimum 500mm above shower head height (1800mm for wall tanking in most cases)
3. Tape all floor-to-wall junctions (the most vulnerable point; movement creates cracks here)
4. Tape all internal corners
5. Tile with C2S2 (flexible, rapid-setting) adhesive
6. Grout with epoxy grout or premium flexible grout with water repellent
7. Silicone sealant (not grout) at all floor-to-wall junctions and internal corners — these are movement zones

**The floor-to-wall junction rule:**
In any tiled wet area, the junction between floor tiles and wall tiles must NEVER be grouted — it must be filled with silicone sealant. The floor and wall are moving independently (thermal movement, structural movement) and a rigid grout joint at the junction will crack. This is one of the most commonly incorrectly executed details in domestic tiling.

## 10. TILE ADHESIVE SYSTEMS

### 10.1 EN 12004 Classification System

Tile adhesives are classified under EN 12004:2007+A1:2012, which provides a standardised performance classification:

**Bond type:**
- **C** — Cementitious (the most common; cement-based)
- **D** — Dispersion (pre-mixed acrylic; no cement)
- **R** — Reactive resin (epoxy or polyurethane based)

**Adhesion class:**
- **1** — Normal adhesion (standard)
- **2** — Improved adhesion (better bond; required for specific applications)

**Special characteristics:**
- **F** — Fast-setting (achieves 50% of adhesion within 4 hours; required for wall tiling where tiles may slide, and for floors requiring rapid return to service)
- **T** — Reduced slip (anti-slump; for wall and large format tiles)
- **E** — Extended open time (minimum 30-minute open time; required for large format tiles and complex layouts)
- **S1** — Deformable (transverse deformation 2.5–5mm; flexible adhesive)
- **S2** — Highly deformable (transverse deformation >5mm; highly flexible)

**Reading an adhesive classification example:**
- **C2S2T:** Cementitious, Improved adhesion, Highly deformable (flexible), Reduced slip
- This would be appropriate for: large format tiles over timber substrate; tiles in wet areas; tiles over UFH
- **C1:** Cementitious, normal adhesion — minimum spec; for standard tiles in dry internal areas only

### 10.2 Adhesive Selection Guide

| Application | Minimum adhesive class | Notes |
|-------------|----------------------|-------|
| Standard internal floor (dry) | C1 | Basic; suitable only for straightforward applications |
| Internal floor (wet area) | C2S1 | Flexible; improved bond |
| Wet room / shower | C2S2 | Highly flexible; maximum bond |
| Tiles over timber substrate | C2S2 | Flexibility essential for subfloor movement |
| Tiles over UFH | C2S2 | Thermal cycling requires flexible bond |
| Large format tiles (>600mm) | C2S2T or C2S2E | Anti-slump or extended open time |
| External tiles | C2S2 + frost resistant | Freeze-thaw cycling; must be specifically rated for external use |
| Swimming pool | R2 (epoxy) or C2 + pool-rated | Chemical and water resistance |
| Natural stone | C2 (white) | White adhesive prevents show-through on translucent stone |

### 10.3 Back-Buttering Technique

**Required for:**
- All tiles with any dimension ≥600mm
- All natural stone tiles
- All tiles in wet areas and external applications
- Any situation requiring >80% coverage

**Process:**
1. Apply adhesive to the subfloor with a notched trowel (appropriate notch size — see below)
2. Comb the adhesive to produce even ridges
3. Apply a skim coat of the same adhesive to the back of the tile (the back-butter coat)
4. Press the tile into the combed bed on the subfloor; the notch ridges collapse and merge with the back-butter coat
5. Tap the tile to ensure full contact; check with a level
6. Lift a tile periodically to verify coverage: should be ≥80% (general) or ≥95% (large format/wet area)

**Notch trowel selection:**
- Small tiles (up to 100mm): V-notch 3×3mm
- Standard tiles (100–300mm): V-notch or square-notch 6×6mm
- Medium tiles (300–600mm): square-notch 10×10mm or 12×12mm
- Large format (600mm+): square-notch 12×12mm or 15×15mm; back-butter required

### 10.4 Open Time and Working Time

- **Open time:** The period after applying adhesive to the subfloor during which a tile can still be successfully bonded (skin does not form on the adhesive surface)
- **Pot life** (for two-component adhesives): the period after mixing before the adhesive becomes unusable
- Standard cementitious adhesive: open time typically 20–30 minutes; varies with temperature
- Extended open time (E) adhesive: minimum 30 minutes; required for complex large format work
- High temperature accelerates skin formation; always allow for site conditions
- Tiling in summer or in a heated room: open time is shorter; use E-class or work in smaller sections

## 11. GROUT SYSTEMS

### 11.1 EN 13888 Classification

Grout is classified under EN 13888:

- **CG (Cementitious Grout):** Sand/cement or polymer-modified cement grout; most common
  - CG1: standard performance
  - CG2: improved abrasion resistance and reduced water absorption
  - **W** suffix: reduced water absorption (CG2W)
  - **A** suffix: high abrasion resistance (CG2WA) — for high-traffic commercial floors

- **RG (Reactive Resin Grout / Epoxy Grout):** Epoxy resin grout; two-component (resin + hardener)
  - Chemical resistant; stain resistant; waterproof; very durable
  - Required for: swimming pools; food production; commercial kitchens; anywhere exceptional chemical resistance or hygiene is needed
  - More difficult to apply than cementitious grout; very short working time; requires experienced installer
  - Fully haze-free cleaning after installation is critical — cured epoxy haze is extremely difficult to remove

### 11.2 Grout Joint Width

The grout joint width has both aesthetic and functional implications:

**Minimum joint widths:**
- Rectified tiles: 1.5–2mm (only achievable with rectified tiles to ±0.2mm)
- Standard non-rectified tiles: 3–5mm (allows for dimensional variation)
- Natural stone: minimum 3mm (compensates for irregular edges)

**Functional requirement:** The grout joint accommodates:
- Minor tile size variation
- Minor installation tolerance
- A small amount of thermal and moisture movement

**The "no grout joint" trend:** Some customers request tiles butted together with zero grout joint. This is technically incorrect and will result in:
- Tile-to-tile contact and edge chipping under movement
- No ability to accommodate lippage
- Movement stress concentrated in the adhesive bond
- **Never recommend zero grout joint regardless of customer preference**

### 11.3 Grout Colour Selection

- Matching grout (same colour as tile): minimises the visible grid; emphasises the tile material
- Contrasting grout: emphasises the tile format and pattern
- White grout in high-traffic areas: will discolour rapidly; use a CG2WA grout with stain resistance, or specify dark/mid grey grout
- Epoxy grout is available in a wide range of colours and will not discolour

### 11.4 Grout Sealing

**Cementitious grout should be sealed in:**
- Wet areas (bathroom, kitchen)
- Commercial high-traffic floors
- Anywhere with staining risk

**Grout sealers:**
- Penetrating silicone or fluoropolymer sealers: absorbed into the grout; repel water and staining agents; must be reapplied periodically (typically annually)
- Topical sealers: form a film over the grout; less durable; can peel
- Epoxy grout does not require sealing (it is inherently waterproof)

## 12. MOVEMENT JOINTS — THE MOST NEGLECTED SPECIFICATION ELEMENT

### 12.1 Why Movement Joints Fail

Movement joint omission is the single most common cause of tiled floor failure in both residential and commercial applications. It is also the element most commonly ignored by both DIY installers and, unfortunately, some professional tilers who see joints as aesthetically disruptive.

**The consequences of omitting movement joints:**
- Thermal expansion accumulates across large floors; the adhesive bond eventually fails under compressive stress; tiles debond and hollow; in extreme cases tiles crack
- Building structural movement (seasonal); differential movement between the tile system and the subfloor
- Movement joints are specified in BS 5385 — their absence when tiles fail is a professional liability issue

### 12.2 BS 5385 Movement Joint Requirements

**BS 5385-3 (Internal floors) and BS 5385-4 (External):**

**Maximum bay size without movement joint (internal):**
- 4.5m × 4.5m in any direction (for standard tiles in a heated internal environment)
- 3m × 3m at changes of level, at structural movement joints, and at heated floor/unheated boundary
- Over UFH: maximum 3m × 3m (or as recommended by adhesive manufacturer — typically smaller with thin-bed systems)

**Perimeter joints:**
- Mandatory at all perimeter edges (tiles to wall, tiles to column, tiles to fixed furniture)
- The joint allows the tile field to expand/contract without pushing against fixed structures
- Width: minimum 6mm; typically 8–10mm; filled with silicone sealant after tiling

**Structural movement joints:**
- Where the building has a structural movement joint, the tile system MUST have a corresponding movement joint at the same location; the structural joint must not be tiled over

**Movement joint construction:**
- The joint must extend through the full depth of the tile and adhesive to the screed or substrate
- Filled with a compressible backing rod (foam rod) and a compatible flexible sealant (silicone, polyurethane, or purpose-made floor joint sealant)
- Cover: an aluminium, brass, or stainless steel cover strip can be used in commercial applications; in residential, typically concealed with silicone matching the grout colour

**Visual management:**
- Movement joints can be designed to align with existing grout lines — they are visually invisible or nearly so when correctly designed into the tile layout
- Bay sizes: plan the tile layout so movement joints fall along natural division lines (room boundaries, column centres, etc.)

## 13. INSTALLATION METHODS AND TECHNIQUES

### 13.1 Thin-Bed Method (Standard)

The dominant installation method for floor tiles in residential and most commercial applications:
- Tile adhesive applied to the subfloor with a notched trowel (bedding course thickness typically 3–8mm depending on notch size)
- Tile pressed and tapped into adhesive
- Tiles levelled; spacers used for consistent joint width
- Allow full adhesive cure before grouting (typically 24 hours minimum; rapid-set adhesive 4 hours)
- Grout applied; worked into joints; excess removed; surface cleaned

### 13.2 Thick Bed / Sand and Cement Method (Traditional)

- A bed of semi-dry sand and cement (approximately 4 parts sharp sand: 1 part cement by volume) applied at 25–75mm thickness
- Tiles laid onto the semi-dry bed with a thin coat of pure cement slurry on the tile back (or adhesive)
- Allows adjustment of levels over irregular or uneven floors
- Very labour-intensive; requires skilled tradesperson
- Still used for: quarry tiles; terracotta; very large format tiles where level correction is needed; restoration work
- More forgiving of subfloor imperfection than thin-bed but heavier (structural loading consideration)

### 13.3 Floor Tile Layout Planning

**Setting out — the critical first step:**

1. **Find the centre of the room:** Snap chalk lines across the centre in both directions; intersection = the working datum
2. **Dry-lay a test run:** Lay tiles dry from centre to wall in both directions; assess border widths
3. **Adjust the starting point:** Borders at opposite walls should be equal; no border less than half a tile wide
4. **Check for square:** Are the walls square to each other? Use the 3-4-5 triangle method:
   - Measure 3 units along one chalk line from the centre
   - Measure 4 units along the other chalk line
   - If the room is square, the distance between the two measured points = 5 units
   - If not 5 units: the room is not square; adjust the chalk lines accordingly
5. **Consider dominant visual lines:** Floor should look balanced from the main room entrance; the most visible wall should have full or near-full border tiles

**Pattern layouts:**
- **Grid (standard):** Tiles in a regular grid; end joints align; most common
- **Brick bond (offset):** Each row offset by half a tile; less formal; more dynamic; reduces perception of any minor lippage
- **Diagonal (45°):** Tiles at 45° to walls; more material used (~15% wastage increase); enlarges visual space; complex cuts at perimeter
- **Herringbone:** Rectangular tiles in V-pattern; more complex to set out; increasingly popular

### 13.4 Cutting Tiles

**Angle grinder with diamond blade:**
- Most versatile; used on site for most cuts
- Dry or wet; dust control essential (silica dust — see Section 13.8)
- Straight cuts, curved cuts, notches, internal corners

**Manual tile cutter (snap cutter):**
- Score-and-snap method; fast; clean straight cuts; no dust
- Works for ceramic and soft porcelain up to approximately 600×600mm
- Not effective for hard porcelain or sintered stone (the score does not penetrate; tile shatters rather than snapping cleanly)

**Wet tile saw (table saw with diamond blade and water cooling):**
- Precision straight cuts; essential for large format tiles and hard porcelain
- Water cools the blade and suppresses dust
- Professional standard for all high-quality work

**Bridge saw:**
- Large workshop or site-mounted saw for very large format tiles and slabs
- Sintered stone and slabs require bridge saw cutting

### 13.5 Grouting Process

1. Allow adhesive to cure fully before grouting (24 hours minimum; check with installer and adhesive data sheet)
2. Clean all spacers and adhesive residue from joints before grouting
3. Mix grout to correct consistency (peanut butter consistency for floor grout)
4. Apply across tiles with a rubber grout float; work diagonally to force grout into joints; ensure joints are fully filled
5. Remove excess grout with a damp sponge (wrung out); work in circular motions; avoid removing grout from joints
6. Allow to set (typically 20–30 minutes); polish off haze with a dry cloth
7. Final clean 24 hours later with appropriate tile cleaner
8. Apply grout sealer after full cure (48–72 hours minimum)

**Epoxy grout application:**
- Shorter working time; typically 20–30 minutes at 20°C (faster in warmth)
- Apply and clean as above but with more urgency
- Haze removal: use manufacturer-specified epoxy haze remover within 1–2 hours of application; cured epoxy haze requires strong acid cleaning and considerable effort

### 13.6 Silicone Sealant at Junctions

**Always silicone (not grout) at:**
- All floor-to-wall junctions in wet areas
- All internal corners in wet areas
- All movement joints
- Perimeter expansion joints
- Junctions at different floor materials

**Silicone selection:**
- Sanitary (antifungal/mould-resistant) silicone: for bathrooms; resist mould growth
- Neutral-cure silicone: for marble and some stones (acetoxy-cure/vinegar-smell silicone is acidic; damages calcareous stones)
- Colour-matched silicone: available from most tile manufacturers to match grout colours
- Width: typically 6–10mm for perimeter joints; 3–5mm for internal corners

### 13.7 Tile Levelling Systems (Lippage Control)

For large format tiles, a tile levelling system is essential to control lippage during installation:

**How they work:**
- A clip is inserted under the edge of adjacent tiles before the adhesive cures
- A wedge is driven through the clip, pulling the two tile surfaces level
- After the adhesive cures, the clip is snapped off (using a rubber mallet or pull-tab); the bottom of the clip breaks at a scored point just below the tile surface and the stub remains in the grout joint

**Types:**
- **Clip and wedge systems (Raimondi, Litokol, BAL):** The most common; clip width matches grout joint width (1.5mm, 2mm, 3mm clips available); wedge driven through with a rubber mallet or wedge pliers
- **Twist-and-remove (spinner) systems:** A threaded cap screws down on a base fixed in the joint; unscrew to check level; remove with foot twist after cure
- **Suction cup adjustment tools:** Used alongside levelling systems for fine adjustments of large format tiles before clip is tightened

**Mandatory for:** Any tile dimension ≥600mm or any installation where grout joints ≤3mm

### 13.8 Silica Dust — Health and Safety

**This is a critical health and safety requirement, not optional compliance:**

Cutting ceramic and porcelain tiles generates respirable crystalline silica (RCS) dust. Prolonged exposure to RCS causes:
- Silicosis: an incurable, progressive, and potentially fatal lung disease
- Occupational asthma
- Lung cancer (IARC Group 1 carcinogen)

**Legal requirements:**
- Control of Substances Hazardous to Health (COSHH) Regulations 2002
- WEL (Workplace Exposure Limit) for RCS: **0.1 mg/m³** (8-hour TWA)
- HSE guidance: use water suppression or on-tool dust extraction; RPE (FFP3 mask minimum) when dust cannot be adequately controlled; regular health surveillance for professional tilers

**Practical controls:**
- Wet-cutting on a wet tile saw: dramatically reduces dust
- Angle grinder + diamond blade: use water-fed attachment or on-tool extraction; if neither possible: FFP3 mask and work in well-ventilated area
- Dry-cutting with snap cutter: low dust; acceptable
- Grinding and profiling: highest dust risk; always use water or extraction

## 14. TILING OVER UNDERFLOOR HEATING

### 14.1 Why Tiles and UFH Work Well Together

- Tiles have excellent thermal conductivity (thermal conductance of dense porcelain: ~1.5–3.0 W/mK vs wood at ~0.15 W/mK)
- Tile is effectively R-value zero for practical UFH purposes
- The tile surface heats evenly; the thermal mass of the tile+adhesive bed stores and radiates heat effectively
- Tile over UFH is often considered the most thermally efficient hard floor covering combination

### 14.2 Adhesive Requirements Over UFH

- **Mandatory: C2S2 adhesive** — highly flexible (S2 deformable class)
- The thermal cycling of UFH (switch on/off) causes expansion and contraction of both the screed and the tile
- A rigid adhesive (C1 or even C2S1) will crack and debond under repeated thermal cycling
- Some manufacturers specify their own dedicated UFH adhesive products

### 14.3 UFH Commissioning Before Tiling

As per Lexicon 02 (engineered wood) — the same protocol applies:
1. Commission UFH to maximum operating temperature (minimum 28 days after cement screed installation; 7 days for anhydrite)
2. Run through a full thermal cycle; switch off 48 hours before tiling
3. During tiling and adhesive curing: UFH switched off
4. After tiling and grout have fully cured (minimum 7 days): gradually increase UFH temperature at 5°C per day to operating temperature

**Why the commissioning matters:**
- Before commissioning, screed retains construction moisture; heating drives this out; if tiles are laid before this, the driven moisture weakens the adhesive bond
- The screed dimensions stabilise after commissioning; if tiled before, thermal movement of an unstabilised screed creates very high stress in the tile system

### 14.4 Movement Joints Over UFH

Movement joints are more important, not less, over UFH:
- Thermal cycling adds to the movement the tile system must accommodate
- Maximum bay size over UFH: **3m × 3m** (reduced from standard 4.5m × 4.5m)
- Perimeter joints: mandatory at all edges; minimum 8mm width for UFH installations

## 15. WET ROOMS AND SHOWER AREAS

### 15.1 Fall to Drain

Wet room floors must be correctly graded to drain:
- Minimum fall: **1:80** (12.5mm per metre) to the drain
- This fall must be built into the screed before tanking and tiling
- Two drainage approaches:
  - **Central drain:** Floor falls from all four walls to a central point/line drain; complex to tile symmetrically
  - **Linear drain (channel drain):** Floor falls to one side; all tiles run parallel to the fall; simpler to tile and more aesthetically compatible with large format tiles

**Tile format and fall:**
- Very large tiles (600×600mm+) on a wet room floor create a challenge: each tile must accommodate the fall; the slight tilt of each tile must be planned during setting out
- Linear drain allows one direction of fall; tiles can be laid flat along the non-fall axis
- Mosaic tile: accommodates fall easily due to small tile size and flexible mesh backing

### 15.2 Waste and Drain Compatibility

The drain and the tile system must be compatible:
- Schlüter KERDI-DRAIN, ACO drain, Geberit, Wedi tile drain: these purpose-designed drains integrate with tile systems
- The drain body must be level-adjustable; it must be set to the finished tile surface height
- Waterproofing membrane must seal to the drain collar — this is the critical waterproofing junction
- A poorly sealed drain-to-membrane junction is the most common source of water damage in wet rooms

## 16. EXTERNAL TILING

### 16.1 Technical Requirements for External Use

External tiling is significantly more demanding than internal:

**Frost resistance:**
- Only Group AI porcelain (water absorption ≤0.5%) is frost-resistant
- Ceramic tiles, terracotta, and porous natural stone will suffer freeze-thaw damage within years in the UK climate
- The test: EN ISO 10545-12 (frost resistance); only tiles with confirmed test data should be specified externally

**Adhesive:**
- External grade: C2S2 rated for external use (some S2 adhesives are specifically rated for external; confirm with manufacturer)
- Resistance to freeze-thaw cycling in adhesive bed
- Full coverage (100%) is mandatory for external tiling — voids collect water; freeze-thaw expands the water; tiles debond

**Grout:**
- CG2S or RG rated for external use
- Frost-resistant; UV-stable (cement grout discolours in UV; external grade grout has UV stabilisers)
- Wider joints than internal (5mm minimum for external to accommodate greater movement)

**Substrate:**
- Concrete or engineering brick: suitable
- Timber: not suitable for external tiling in UK climate
- Render/plaster: must be cement-based and fully cured; lime render: check compatibility

**Thermal movement — outdoor conditions:**
- UK climate: tiles experience approximately −10°C to +40°C temperature range
- A 600mm tile: 600mm × 7×10⁻⁶ /°C × 50°C = 0.21mm movement per tile
- Movement joints: every 3m maximum in external applications; every change of plane; every structural joint

### 16.2 Frost Heave Risk

In external applications over poorly drained substrates, ground moisture freezes and expands (frost heave), lifting tiles. Prevention:
- Adequate drainage beneath the substrate
- Free-draining bedding layer
- No trapped moisture under the tile system

## 17. ACOUSTIC CONSIDERATIONS

### 17.1 Tile and Impact Sound

Hard tile surfaces are acoustically harsh for impact sound — footfall on tile generates a sharp, loud impact noise. In apartment buildings this is a significant Approved Document E compliance issue.

**The dilemma:** Tile cannot be floated on a thick acoustic underlay as wood or LVT can (tiles are rigid and adhesive bonded). The acoustic performance of a tiled floor depends entirely on the construction below.

**Solutions:**
- **Acoustic screed system:** Resilient acoustic layer incorporated in the screed system (e.g., rubber granule layer under screed); tiles bonded to the screed conventionally above
- **Resilient tile adhesive mat (Schlüter DITRA-SOUND, Mapei Mapeguard):** An acoustic uncoupling mat bonded to the subfloor; tiles bonded to the top; provides both uncoupling (protects tiles from subfloor movement) and acoustic performance (ΔLw typically 14–20 dB depending on product)
- **Suspended acoustic ceiling below:** The most effective solution for compliance — acoustic ceiling absorbs impact sound; the tile floor is specified conventionally above

### 17.2 Tile Over Timber (Structural Acoustic)

In multi-storey buildings, tiling directly to a concrete structural deck provides the best acoustic. Tiling to a timber intermediate floor (as in house conversion to apartments) requires careful engineering of both structural and acoustic performance.

## 18. PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 18.1 Tile Debonding (Hollow Tile)

**Test:** Tap each tile with a knuckle or rod; a hollow, drum-like sound = the tile is debonded (fully or partially) beneath; a solid thud = full bond

**Symptoms:** Hollow tiles in a floor or wall; may progress to tiles cracking or lifting (particularly over UFH or in wet areas)

**Causes:**
1. **Insufficient adhesive coverage** — the dominant cause; voids beneath the tile; the unsupported areas flex under load until bond fails; eventually the tile is entirely unsupported
2. **Incorrect adhesive type** — rigid adhesive over UFH; the thermal cycling breaks the bond progressively
3. **Adhesive skin dried before tile laid** — a skin formed on the adhesive before the tile was pressed into it; bond is poor from the outset
4. **Contaminated substrate** — laitance, oil, paint, or old adhesive prevents bond formation
5. **Substrate movement** — structural movement in the subfloor is translated to the tile bond; progressive bond failure over time
6. **Wet screed** — RH above 75% when tiled; adhesive curing inhibited; bond never fully formed

**Action:**
- Isolated hollow tiles: can be re-bonded by injecting adhesive from the edge using a syringe (limited effectiveness for large areas)
- Widespread debonding: removal and reinstallation with correct adhesive and preparation

### 18.2 Cracked Tiles

**Causes:**
1. **Point load** — heavy impact; dropped object; high-heel concentration
2. **Subfloor movement or deflection** — the tile bridges a hollow; dynamic load causes flexure; the tile cracks
3. **Omitted movement joint** — compressive stress from thermal or moisture expansion; tiles crack or arch
4. **Insufficient adhesive coverage** — unsupported tile area cracks under load
5. **Adhesive not cured before trafficking** — adhesive not set; tile moves and cracks under load
6. **Existing crack in subfloor** — crack in the substrate transferred through adhesive to tile (reflective cracking)

**Assessment:**
- Isolated crack correlating with a heavy impact: point load; single board replacement
- Multiple cracks in a pattern (grid or diagonal): suggests systematic movement/stress failure; investigation of movement joints and adhesive specification required
- Cracks aligned with hidden subfloor joints: reflective cracking from subfloor

### 18.3 Grout Cracking and Staining

**Grout cracking:**
- At movement joints that should have silicone: indicates grout was used where sealant was required; grout is cracking from movement
- Widespread joint cracking: suggests overall movement stress in the tile system; check movement joint provision and adhesive specification

**Grout staining:**
- White grout in high traffic: progressive discolouration from foot soiling, cleaning product residue, and atmospheric particulates
- Remedy: specialist grout cleaner and acid wash (carefully — acid damages some tiles and should not be used on natural stone); followed by grout sealer
- Long-term remedy: specify CG2WA grout and seal correctly from installation

### 18.4 Lippage

**Causes:**
1. **Subfloor not flat enough** — the tile layer telegraphs the subfloor variation
2. **Adhesive bed not consistent** — different depths of adhesive create height variation
3. **No levelling system used** — for large format tiles; essential for tiles >600mm
4. **Tiles from different calibres mixed** — dimensional variation creates height difference at joints

**Severity assessment:**
- ≤2mm: within EN 13451 tolerance for general use; technically acceptable
- 1–2mm: at edge of tolerance; relevant for rectified tiles with tight joints
- >2mm: unacceptable; potential trip hazard; professional liability issue

### 18.5 Efflorescence

**Symptoms:** White powdery deposits appearing on the tile surface or grout joints; typically in the first weeks or months after installation.

**Cause:** Soluble salts in the cement adhesive or grout migrate to the surface with evaporating moisture; the salts crystallise on the surface as the water evaporates. More common in wet areas, over high-moisture substrates, and with improperly cured adhesive.

**Action:** Allow the floor to fully cure and dry (4–6 weeks); the efflorescence typically reduces significantly; clean residual deposits with mild acid wash or specialist efflorescence remover; do not use acid on natural stone or polished tile

## 19. REPAIR AND REPLACEMENT

### 19.1 Replacing Individual Tiles

**Process:**
1. Protect adjacent tiles with tape at joints
2. Remove grout from all joints around the damaged tile (oscillating tool with grout removal blade; manual grout rake)
3. Drill multiple holes through the tile using a masonry bit; weaken the tile body
4. Use a cold chisel and club hammer (or SDS chisel drill) to break and remove the tile in pieces; work from the drilled holes outward; be very careful not to damage adjacent tiles
5. Remove all adhesive residue from the substrate; clean and prepare the substrate
6. Apply new adhesive; press new tile into position; level with adjacent tiles
7. Allow adhesive to cure; apply grout; finish

**Matching the replacement tile:** Always keep spare tiles from the original installation. The same tile will often be discontinued within 2–5 years; finding an exact match is frequently impossible. For this reason, ordering 10–15% extra material and storing spares is strongly recommended professional advice to clients.

### 19.2 Removing Adhesive From Substrate

- After tile removal: old adhesive bonds firmly to the substrate
- Grinding with angle grinder or SDS chisel: remove bulk adhesive
- Final skim: SLC to create a flat, clean surface
- This is essential — laying new tile directly over old adhesive creates height variation and poor bond

## 20. MAINTENANCE AND CARE

### 20.1 Sealed Porcelain — Routine Maintenance

**Sealed (polished) porcelain:**
- Sweep or vacuum daily in commercial; regularly in residential
- Damp mop with pH-neutral cleaner; wring mop well
- No acid cleaners (damage the polish)
- No highly alkaline cleaners (can dull the surface)
- Clean spills promptly; polished porcelain stain-resists but prolonged contact may penetrate micro-scratches

**Unsealed matt porcelain:**
- More forgiving than polished; micro-texture holds cleaning agents
- Mop with neutral or slightly alkaline cleaner (pH 7–9)
- Grout lines: specialist grout cleaner; toothbrush for persistent staining

### 20.2 Natural Porcelain and Unglazed Products

- Porous: must be sealed before use and re-sealed periodically
- Sealing: penetrating impregnating sealer (silicone, siloxane, or fluoropolymer-based); not a topical film sealer
- Sealed natural porcelain: stain-resistant; clean with pH-neutral cleaner
- Re-seal: test periodically by placing a few drops of water on the surface; if it beads, the seal is intact; if it absorbs, re-seal

### 20.3 Ceramic Tile Maintenance

- As per matt porcelain; pH-neutral cleaner
- Glazed ceramic is relatively stain-resistant (the glaze is non-porous)
- Grout cleaning is the primary maintenance task

### 20.4 Commercial Tile Maintenance

- Machine scrubbing with low-speed rotary pad and neutral detergent
- High-speed burnishing for polished surfaces
- Specialist stain removal as required
- Anti-slip treatment renewal in slip-rated areas (check PTV periodically)

## 21. COMMERCIAL APPLICATIONS

### 21.1 Retail and Commercial Kitchens

- Porcelain at Class 33–34 (EN ISO 10874) minimum
- Anti-slip R10 minimum for retail; R11–R12 for commercial kitchen
- Large format (600×600mm+) in retail for visual impact and ease of cleaning
- Coved skirting (as per vinyl) in commercial kitchen for HACCP compliance — note: coved skirting in tiled kitchens is done with dedicated cove tile profiles, not sheet vinyl
- Full 100% adhesive coverage mandatory

### 21.2 Healthcare

- Porcelain with anti-slip; chemical resistance to hospital disinfectants (EN ISO 10545-13 Class GBA+)
- Epoxy grout in clinical areas for seamless, hygienic joints
- Coved tile skirtings with epoxy grout in operating theatres and ICUs
- Slip resistance per DIN 51130 R10 minimum; DIN 51097 in wet areas

### 21.3 Swimming Pools

- External and underwater: Group AI porcelain or glass mosaic
- Pool floor: DIN 51097 Class B/C (anti-slip)
- Epoxy grout or pool-grade cement grout (chemical resistance to chlorine)
- Movement joints essential (water at pool temperature; chemical expansion)
- Pool waterproofing system (tanking membrane) beneath the tile system

### 21.4 Entrance Lobbies and Public Buildings

- Heavy commercial: Class 33–34
- Large format porcelain for visual impact
- Anti-slip at entrance (rain/snow ingress): R10 minimum at the threshold
- BS 8300 (accessible design): level thresholds; slip resistance; contrast markings at level changes

**BS 5385-3:2014 — Wall and floor tiling — Code of practice for the design and installation of internal ceramic and similar tile floor coverings:**
- The primary UK installation standard for internal floor tile
- Subfloor requirements; adhesive selection; movement joints; wet area construction
- **Essential reference; every professional tiler and specifier should know this standard**

**BS 5385-4:2015 — Wall and floor tiling — Code of practice for tiling and mosaics in specific conditions:**
- External tiling; swimming pools; large format; critical conditions

**BS 5385-5:2009 — Wall and floor tiling — Code of practice for the design and installation of terrazzo tile and slab, natural stone and composition block flooring:**
- For natural stone tiling specifications (see also Lexicon 08)

**EN 14411:2016 — Ceramic tiles — Definitions, classification, characteristics and marking:**
- The overarching ceramic tile product standard; CE marking requirements

**EN ISO 10545 series — Ceramic tiles — Determination of [various properties]:**
- Parts 3, 4, 6, 7, 12, 13, 14 most relevant to flooring (see Section 7.1)

**EN 12004:2007+A1:2012 — Adhesives for tiles — Requirements, evaluation of conformity, classification and designation:**
- Tile adhesive classification (C1, C2, S1, S2, F, T, E designations)

**EN 13888:2009 — Grout for tiles — Requirements, evaluation of conformity, classification and designation:**
- Grout classification (CG1, CG2, CG2W, CG2WA, RG)

**EN 13451 — Swimming pools — Safety requirements:**
- Referenced for pool tiling slip resistance and structural requirements

**BS 7976-2 — Pendulum Test:**
- UK slip resistance test method; produces PTV value

**Building Regulations Approved Documents B, C, K, M:**
- Fire; site preparation; protection from falling; accessibility — all referenced in commercial tile specification

**HSE COSHH Regulations 2002 / WEL EH40:**
- Silica dust control during tile cutting — a legal obligation for all tilers

## 23. SUSTAINABILITY

### 23.1 Ceramics — Raw Materials and Energy

- Raw materials: clay, feldspar, quartz — abundant, naturally occurring minerals; no finite resource depletion concern comparable to petroleum
- **Energy:** Firing at 1,200–1,300°C is energy-intensive; the ceramic tile industry accounts for significant energy use in manufacturing
- Improvement: modern roller kilns are significantly more efficient than older tunnel kilns; gas use is reducing as electric kiln technology improves; solar thermal integration in some facilities

### 23.2 Lifespan

- The most significant sustainability argument for tile: **extraordinary longevity**
- A well-installed tiled floor in a dry internal environment will last 50–100+ years without replacement
- Tile floors in ancient buildings (Roman, Moorish) survive intact after 1,000+ years
- When compared over a full lifecycle: even a high-embodied-energy tile significantly outperforms products requiring replacement every 10–20 years when the carbon cost of manufacturing multiple replacement cycles is considered

### 23.3 Recycled Content

- Some manufacturers use recycled glass, industrial mineral waste, and recycled ceramics in tile production
- Recycled content of 10–30% is achievable; some manufacturers claim up to 40%
- Post-consumer recycled tile: limited; the hardness of ceramic makes recycling difficult but crushing for aggregate is viable

### 23.4 End-of-Life

- Ceramic tile can be crushed and used as aggregate (recycled content in future products or roadbase)
- Not biodegradable but inert — does not leach harmful substances in landfill
- Unlike carpet, vinyl, and wood products: ceramic tile does not release organic compounds or persist as microplastic

### 23.5 EPD and BREEAM

- EPDs available from major manufacturers (Porcelanosa, Johnson Tiles, Domus, RAK)
- BREEAM Mat 01 credits for lifecycle data; Mat 03 for responsibly sourced materials
- Low VOC: ceramic tile has zero VOC (it is an inert sintered mineral); contributes to LEED IEQ credits

## 24. TRADE TERMINOLOGY GLOSSARY

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Adhesive coverage** | Percentage of tile back surface area in contact with adhesive; minimum 80% general; 95% wet/large format |
| **Back-buttering** | Applying a skim coat of adhesive to the tile back before pressing onto the adhesive bed |
| **Bisque** | Unglazed, unfired or once-fired tile body before glaze application |
| **BS 5385** | British Standard Code of Practice for wall and floor tiling; the primary UK installation standard |
| **Calibre** | The actual measured size of a batch of tiles; tiles of different calibres must not be mixed |
| **Cementitious grout (CG)** | Cement-based grout; classified CG1, CG2, CG2W, CG2WA under EN 13888 |
| **C2S2** | Cementitious tile adhesive, improved adhesion, highly deformable; required for UFH, wet rooms, large format over timber |
| **Coefficient of thermal expansion** | The fractional dimensional change per °C; porcelain: ~6–8×10⁻⁶/°C |
| **Decoupling membrane** | Polymer mat bonded between subfloor and tile system; absorbs subfloor movement without cracking tiles |
| **Dekton** | Cosentino's sintered stone product; ultra-compact surface; used as large format tile and worktop |
| **Deformable (S1/S2)** | Adhesive classification for flexibility; S1: 2.5–5mm; S2: >5mm transverse deformation |
| **Digital inkjet printing** | High-resolution printing technology applied to tile surface; allows unlimited realistic designs |
| **Drop match** | Pattern matching where adjacent rows offset by a specified amount before pattern aligns |
| **Efflorescence** | White salt deposits migrating to tile/grout surface through moisture evaporation |
| **EIR** | Embossed in Register; surface texture registered precisely to printed design |
| **EN 12004** | European adhesive classification standard |
| **EN 13888** | European grout classification standard |
| **EN 14411** | European ceramic tile product standard; CE marking requirement |
| **EN ISO 10545** | Series of ceramic tile test standards |
| **Epoxy grout (RG)** | Two-component reactive resin grout; stain-proof; waterproof; chemical resistant; required for pools and food areas |
| **Frost resistance** | Property of Group AI porcelain (≤0.5% absorption); essential for external use |
| **Group AI** | EN ISO 10545-3 water absorption class; ≤0.5%; defines porcelain |
| **Homogeneous (tile)** | Not to be confused with LVT; in tile context: a tile whose colour/pattern runs through full body depth |
| **Laitance** | Weak surface layer of cement on concrete screed; must be ground off before tiling |
| **Large format tile (LFT)** | Tile with any dimension exceeding 600mm; specific subfloor and adhesive requirements |
| **Lapitec** | Sintered stone manufacturer; full-body sintered surface panels |
| **Lappato** | Semi-polished tile finish; partly polished, partly unpolished texture |
| **Levelling system** | Clip and wedge or spinner system to control lippage during large format tile installation |
| **Lippage** | Height difference between adjacent tile surfaces; maximum 2mm general; 1mm rectified tiles |
| **Monoporosa** | Single-fired ceramic tile manufacturing method; glaze applied before firing |
| **Movement joint** | Compressible joint filled with sealant; mandatory at all perimeters and at maximum bay size intervals |
| **Neolith** | TheSize's sintered surface product; large format panels |
| **Notched trowel** | Trowel with V or square profile notches; creates ridged adhesive bed of controlled thickness |
| **Open time** | Period after adhesive application during which tile can still be successfully bonded |
| **PEI rating** | Porcelain Enamel Institute glazed tile abrasion class; 0–5 (5 = most durable glaze) |
| **Pendulum test** | UK slip resistance test producing PTV; primary legal assessment method (BS 7976-2) |
| **Porcelain** | Dense, vitrified ceramic tile; water absorption ≤0.5% (Group AI); frost-resistant |
| **PTV** | Pendulum Test Value; ≥36 = low slip risk; the UK threshold |
| **R-value (slip)** | DIN 51130 ramp test classification; R9–R13; widely specified in commercial |
| **Rectified tile** | Tile precision-ground after firing to exact dimensional tolerance; enables tight joints |
| **Reflective cracking** | Crack in tile following a crack or joint in the substrate below |
| **RG (reactive grout)** | Epoxy grout classification under EN 13888 |
| **Shade sorting** | Distributing tiles from the same colour batch across the floor to manage natural colour variation |
| **Silica dust / RCS** | Respirable crystalline silica; generated by tile cutting; causes silicosis; COSHH regulated |
| **Sintered stone** | Ultra-compact surface from extreme heat and pressure; Dekton, Neolith, Lapitec |
| **Tanking** | Waterproofing a substrate (walls and floor) before tiling in wet areas |
| **Thin-bed** | Standard tile adhesive installation method; adhesive bed 3–8mm |
| **Through-body / full-body** | Tile where colour/pattern runs through full depth; chip damage does not reveal contrasting body |
| **Tile backer board** | Rigid substrate board (Hardiebacker, Wedi, KERDI-Board) screwed to timber before tiling |
| **UFH** | Underfloor Heating; requires C2S2 adhesive; maximum 3m × 3m movement joint bays |
| **Vitrification** | The glassy fusion of minerals at high temperature; what makes porcelain non-porous |
| **WEL** | Workplace Exposure Limit; RCS limit 0.1 mg/m³ (COSHH) |
| **Wet room** | Walk-in shower with no tray; full floor waterproofing (tanking) mandatory |
