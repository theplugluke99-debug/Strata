## 1. CARPET FIBRE TYPES

### 1.1 Wool

**Overview:** The benchmark natural fibre for carpet. Wool has been used in woven carpets for centuries and remains the reference point against which synthetic fibres are measured.

**Structure:** Wool fibres are composed of keratin protein with a natural crimp and a multi-layered cuticle (scales) that interlock with adjacent fibres. This crimp provides natural resilience — the ability to recover from compression. The cortex contains air pockets that contribute to thermal insulation.

**Key properties:**
- Natural soil-hiding ability due to light-scattering scales on the cuticle
- Inherent flame resistance — wool does not melt or drip; it self-extinguishes
- Natural moisture regulation — absorbs up to 30% of its weight in moisture before feeling wet, releasing it slowly; this buffers humidity in a room
- Good resilience (pile recovery after compression), though inferior to nylon
- Natural anti-static properties
- Susceptible to moth larvae (Tineola bisselliella) if untreated — modern wool carpets are almost universally Superwash or Mothproofed
- Susceptible to bleach and alkaline cleaners; pH of cleaning agents must be below 8.0
- UV degradation over prolonged direct exposure (fading and fibre weakening)
- More expensive than synthetics, particularly 100% wool products

**Common wool types used in carpet:**
- **New Zealand wool** — the dominant commercial grade; long staple, white, consistent quality, takes dye well
- **British wool** — shorter staple, coarser, more varied crimp; used in Axminster and some Wilton products; considered more sustainable due to reduced transport
- **Shetland wool** — finer, used in heritage and luxury products
- **Berber wool** — undyed or naturally coloured from specific breeds; earthy tones

**Wool grades (Bradford Count / Micron):** Carpet wool typically ranges from 28–34 microns. Finer microns (lower numbers = finer fibre) are used in luxury products. Coarser fibres (higher micron count) provide durability.

**Blends:** Wool is commonly blended with synthetic fibres to balance cost and performance:
- **80/20 Wool/Nylon** — the classic UK blend; the nylon (typically 20%) dramatically improves abrasion resistance and tuft withdrawal force while the wool provides handle, appearance and soil-hiding
- **80/20 Wool/Polypropylene** — lower cost than nylon blend; polypropylene contributes little resilience but reduces fibre cost
- **50/50 Wool/Nylon** — increasingly common in mid-range products; performance comparable to 80/20 in many tests
- **100% Wool** — used in top-end Axminster, Wilton, and hand-tufted products

---

### 1.2 Nylon (Polyamide)

**Overview:** The highest-performing synthetic fibre for carpet. Two principal variants are used commercially.

**Types:**
- **Nylon 6 (Polyamide 6 / PA6):** Single monomer (caprolactam). Slightly softer hand, good dye uptake, lower melting point (~220°C). More commonly recycled commercially. Brands: Aquafil ECONYL (recycled), Lanxess.
- **Nylon 6,6 (Polyamide 6,6 / PA6,6):** Dual monomer (hexamethylene diamine + adipic acid). Superior resilience and heat resistance. Harder wearing in heavy traffic. Brands: Invista ANTRON, Ascend.

**Key properties:**
- Highest abrasion resistance of all carpet fibres
- Excellent resilience — pile springs back well after compression
- Good soil resistance when treated with stain protection chemistry (e.g., DuPont Teflon, 3M Scotchgard)
- Susceptible to static unless anti-static treatment applied or anti-stat fibre incorporated
- Susceptible to fading under UV unless solution-dyed (where colour is in the polymer core)
- Solution-dyed nylon (SDN) has superior colourfastness — used extensively in commercial applications
- Accepts a wide range of dyeing methods: acid dye, disperse dye
- Tends to show shading/pooling more than wool or polypropylene

**Nylon variants and treatments:**
- **BCF (Bulked Continuous Filament):** Continuous strands thermally bulked (texturised). Smooth, luxurious appearance. Resistant to shedding. Used in cut pile and loop pile saxony products.
- **Staple (spun) nylon:** Short lengths spun into yarn like wool. Softer, slightly more soil-prone, used in some mid-range residential products.
- **Type 6 recycled (e.g., ECONYL):** Regenerated from pre- and post-consumer waste (fishing nets, industrial waste). Identical performance to virgin nylon 6.

---

### 1.3 Polypropylene (PP) / Olefin

**Overview:** The most widely used synthetic fibre by volume globally. Characterised by its low cost and inherent solution-dyed colour.

**Key properties:**
- Inherently stain-resistant — polypropylene has near-zero moisture absorption, so water-based stains cannot penetrate the fibre
- All polypropylene carpet is effectively solution-dyed — colour is part of the polymer; cannot be dyed after extrusion
- Very low cost relative to nylon or wool
- Lowest resilience of major carpet fibres — poor pile recovery after compression; track and crush easily in traffic areas
- Susceptible to oil-based soiling (lipophilic) — oil bonds to polypropylene readily
- Low melting point (~160°C) — vulnerable to friction burns; cigarette burns, hot iron contact
- UV resistant in solution-dyed form — good for conservatories and light-flooded spaces
- Good chemical resistance
- Lightweight

**Applications:**
- Berber loop pile constructions (flatweave appearance)
- Commercial needle-punch and flatweave
- Budget residential tufted carpets
- Outdoor and conservatory carpet
- Kitchen carpet tiles

**Limitations in context:**
- Not recommended for heavy-use domestic areas (stairs, hallways) due to poor resilience
- Matting in traffic lanes is a near-universal outcome in cut pile constructions

---

### 1.4 Polyester (PET) and Recycled Polyester (rPET)

**Overview:** Polyester is used principally in mid-range residential cut pile carpets. Growing sustainability profile due to recycled content from PET bottles.

**Key properties:**
- Good initial softness — softer hand than nylon or polypropylene
- Excellent colourfastness (naturally receptive to solution dyeing)
- Good stain resistance to water-based stains
- Poor resilience — the weakest resilience of major carpet fibres; significant pile crushing occurs with use
- Poor abrasion resistance relative to nylon
- Susceptible to pilling in some constructions
- rPET (recycled PET) has equivalent or marginally inferior performance to virgin PET

**Triexta (PTT — Polytrimethylene terephthalate):**
- A sub-variant marketed heavily in North America under brand names such as Mohawk SmartStrand
- Better resilience than conventional PET due to molecular spring properties
- High stain resistance due to low moisture absorption
- Limited market penetration in UK currently

**Applications:**
- Mid-range residential saxony and twist pile
- Where soft hand feel is a priority at lower cost than wool

---

### 1.5 Acrylic

**Overview:** Developed as a "wool substitute." Performance has been largely superseded by nylon but still present in some entry-level products marketed with wool-like appearance.

**Key properties:**
- Closest visual appearance to wool of all synthetics — natural crimp, similar colour response
- Moderate abrasion resistance (better than polypropylene, worse than nylon)
- Poor resilience — will crush in heavy traffic
- Susceptible to pilling
- Low soil resistance
- Not widely specified for UK commercial use

---

### 1.6 Sisal, Coir, Jute, Seagrass — Natural Fibre Carpets

**Overview:** Hard-wearing natural fibre floor coverings. Distinct from conventional carpet in feel and application.

**Sisal:**
- Extracted from Agave sisalana leaves (East Africa, Brazil)
- Strong, durable, good abrasion resistance
- Can be dyed — good colour range available
- Absorbs moisture — can swell and wrinkle if wet; unsuitable for bathrooms, kitchens, or damp areas
- Textured surface — can be uncomfortable underfoot in bare feet
- Moth-resistant naturally

**Coir:**
- Extracted from coconut husk fibre
- Coarser than sisal; doormat aesthetic
- Extremely durable
- Very poor moisture resistance — significant shrinkage and swelling
- Difficult to clean

**Jute:**
- Softer than sisal or coir; used principally as a backing fibre but available as a pile
- Poor durability and moisture resistance; unsuitable for high traffic
- Used in luxury seagrass/sisal blends for softness

**Seagrass:**
- Harvested from coastal paddies (China, Vietnam)
- Non-porous surface — excellent stain resistance; liquid beads on surface
- Cannot be dyed — restricted to natural greens and straws
- Slippery — safety concern on stairs; not recommended for stair use without non-slip treatment
- Susceptible to moisture damage; not suitable for kitchens/bathrooms

**Fitting considerations — natural fibres:**
- All natural fibres absorb moisture from adhesive; allow acclimatisation before fitting
- Natural fibre carpets should be fitted loose or with specialist natural fibre adhesive
- Avoid over-stretching; seagrass in particular has low stretch tolerance
- All joints must be bound or heat-sealed; raw edges fray rapidly

---

### 1.7 Recycled and Sustainable Fibre Systems

- **ECONYL (recycled nylon 6):** From fishing nets, carpet waste, industrial nylon. Performance = virgin nylon 6.
- **rPET carpet:** Varying recycled content claims; verify with manufacturer certification.
- **Cradle-to-Cradle certified products:** Third-party verified recyclability and material health. Relevant to commercial specification.
- **Carbon-offset wool products:** Schemes such as Campaign for Wool quality marks.

## 2. CARPET CONSTRUCTION METHODS

### 2.1 Tufted Carpet

**Overview:** The dominant carpet manufacturing method globally, accounting for approximately 90%+ of all carpet produced. Developed in the 1950s from hand-tufting traditions.

**Manufacturing process:**
1. A primary backing (typically woven polypropylene or polyester) is fed through a tufting machine
2. Needles (up to 2,400+ simultaneously) punch yarn through the primary backing at precise gauge
3. Loopers beneath the backing hold yarn loops; if cut pile is required, a cutting blade trims each loop
4. Latex compound is applied to the back of the primary backing, locking tufts in place
5. A secondary backing (typically woven jute or non-woven polypropylene) is adhered via the latex
6. The carpet passes through an oven to cure the latex
7. Final finishing: shearing, brushing, and any topical treatments applied

**Key construction parameters:**

**Gauge:** The distance between needle rows across the width (measured in needles per inch). Common gauges:
- 1/8 inch gauge (8 needles per inch) — standard for loop piles and level-loop commercial
- 1/10 inch gauge (10 needles per inch) — common in cut pile residential
- 1/13 inch gauge (13 needles per inch) — fine gauge; higher definition patterns
- 5/64 inch gauge — fine gauge used in high-definition commercial
- Finer gauge = more tufts per unit area = denser, heavier carpet

**Stitch rate (stitch density / stitches per inch along length):** Tufts per inch in the machine direction. Combined with gauge, determines total tuft density:
- Total density = stitches per inch × needles per inch × 2 (for cut pile — each loop counts as 2 ends)

**Pile height (pile wire height):** Measurement from the top of the backing to the pile tip:
- Cut pile residential: typically 8–20mm
- Loop pile: typically 4–10mm
- Commercial cut pile: 5–10mm

**Tuft weight (face weight / pile weight):** Weight of yarn in the face pile, measured in oz/yd² or g/m². Face weight excludes backing weight. Critical performance indicator:
- Light domestic: 30–40 oz/yd²
- Medium domestic: 40–50 oz/yd²
- Heavy domestic/light commercial: 50–60 oz/yd²
- Commercial: varies widely by end use

**Pile density:** The most critical single performance indicator. Calculated formula:
> Pile density (oz/yd³) = (Face weight × 36) ÷ pile height (inches)
- Higher density = better performance, appearance retention, and soil resistance
- Minimum density for general domestic: ~2,000 oz/yd³
- Premium domestic: 3,500–6,000 oz/yd³
- Heavy commercial: 4,000–8,000+ oz/yd³

---

### 2.2 Woven Carpet

**Overview:** The original carpet manufacturing method. Structurally superior to tufted carpet — pile and backing are woven simultaneously, making them integral.

**Types:**

#### Axminster
- Pile is inserted into the backing during weaving via a gripper-knife mechanism that cuts individual tufts and inserts them row by row
- Virtually unlimited colour combinations per row — allows complex patterns
- The pile is cut during manufacture; Axminster is always cut pile
- Traditional manufacturing centres: Axminster (Devon), Kidderminster (Worcestershire)
- Available as **Gripper Axminster** (most common today) or **Spool Axminster** (heritage method, very few active looms)
- High yarn efficiency — yarn is only inserted where required (unlike Wilton)
- Typically 80% wool / 20% nylon face fibre, though 100% wool and 100% nylon variants exist
- Widths: typically 4m and 5m broadloom; also made as carpet tiles
- Pattern repeats can be large — critical consideration for calculating requirements

#### Wilton
- A continuous weave using a series of frames carrying different coloured yarns
- Pile yarn runs continuously through the backing when not on the surface (forming "stored" pile beneath — "buried" yarns)
- This buried yarn adds weight and density but is non-functional
- Wilton can be cut pile or loop pile (cord Wilton)
- Typically limited to 5 colours in a pattern (5-frame Wilton) due to the frame system
- Solid colour Wilton (Brussels Weave, face-to-face Wilton) has no buried yarn limitation
- Denser and heavier than Axminster for equivalent appearance; the buried yarn adds structural mass
- Excellent performance, durability, and appearance retention
- Used in luxury hotels, period properties, commercial contracts

#### Flatweave / Dhurrie
- No pile — a flat interlaced construction
- Extremely durable
- Reversible
- Thin profile — suitable over underfloor heating
- Common fibres: wool, cotton, polypropylene, sisal
- Traditional designs: Kelim, Dhurrie, Scandinavian flat weave

**Woven carpet construction terms:**
- **Pitch:** Number of pile ends per 27 inches of width (historical UK measure)
- **Rows:** Number of rows of pile per inch in the length direction
- **Frame:** Number of different yarn supplies (= maximum number of pattern colours in Wilton)
- **Selvedge:** Woven edge of the carpet — should not be trimmed unless required

---

### 2.3 Needle-Punch Carpet

**Overview:** A non-woven construction in which fibres are mechanically entangled by barbed needles passing through a fibre batt at high speed.

**Manufacturing:**
1. Fibres (polypropylene, polyester, or blends) are carded and laid into a batt
2. Barbed needles punch through the batt repeatedly, physically entangling fibres
3. A backing may be added
4. May be impregnated with latex for stability

**Properties:**
- Low pile height or no pile — flat, felt-like appearance
- Very hard-wearing — used in exhibitions, indoor sports facilities, caravans, boat carpet
- Low cost
- No pile to crush or wear unevenly
- Some products have a ribbed or textured surface
- Available in broadloom and tile formats

**Applications:**
- Exhibitions and events (temporary)
- Commercial corridors and back-of-house
- Outdoor terraces and sports areas
- Caravan and vehicle interiors

---

### 2.4 Bonded Carpet (Fusion Bonded)

**Overview:** Pile fibres are electrostatically planted or adhesively bonded into a pre-coated backing — there is no weaving or tufting involved.

**Properties:**
- Extremely dense pile with no pile lean
- Very low pile height typically
- Used in carpet tiles and some velvet-effect residential products
- Cut pile only
- Common in heavy commercial applications

---

### 2.5 Hand-Tufted Carpet and Rugs

**Overview:** Artisan construction using a hand tufting gun or hand tools to insert yarn into a canvas backing, subsequently backed with latex and secondary hessian.

**Properties:**
- High labour cost; premium pricing
- Unlimited design flexibility
- Complex cut/loop combinations achievable
- Variable quality depending on maker
- Common in luxury residential and bespoke commercial projects
- Pile may be inconsistent compared to machine-manufactured products

---

### 2.6 Hand-Knotted Carpet and Rugs

**Overview:** Each individual tuft is hand-knotted around warp threads by skilled artisans. The original and most prestigious carpet construction method.

**Knot types:**
- **Turkish (Ghiordes) knot:** Symmetrical; yarn wrapped around two warp threads and both ends drawn through. Produces a dense pile. Used in Turkish, Caucasian, and some Persian traditions.
- **Persian (Senneh) knot:** Asymmetrical; yarn looped around one warp and under an adjacent warp. Allows finer detail. Dominant in Persian, Indian, and Chinese production.
- **Tibetan knot:** Loop pile construction; loops cut after completion.

**Knot count:** Knots per square inch (KPSI) or per square metre. Ranges from 16 KPSI (tribal) to 1,000+ KPSI (extremely fine Qum or Isfahan). Higher knot count = finer detail, more labour, higher cost.

**Pile fibres:** Wool (most common), silk (luxury; fine detail), cotton (used for warp/weft), jute (backing).

**Not a fitting product** — these are area rugs specified by interior designers; their relevant considerations are rug pad selection, edge treatment, and humidity requirements.

## 3. PILE TYPES AND CHARACTERISTICS

### 3.1 Cut Pile

Loops are cut during manufacture, leaving individual tufts standing.

#### Saxony (Plush / Velvet Saxony)
- Longer pile (typically 15–20mm)
- High twist in individual yarns, then heat-set so yarn tips are closed
- Softer, more formal appearance
- Susceptible to footprinting and shading — the pile deflection under footfall is visible
- Best suited to lower-traffic areas: bedrooms, formal living rooms
- Not recommended for hallways or stairs

#### Twist Pile (Twisted / Frieze)
- Yarns highly twisted and heat-set; tips remain "bloomed" and open
- More informal/textured appearance than saxony
- Significantly more resistant to footprinting due to random orientation of pile tips
- The most popular residential carpet pile in the UK
- Suitable for all domestic areas including hallways and stairs (when density is adequate)
- Common twist count: 4–6 twists per inch (TPI); higher twist = more informal, more soil-hiding

#### Velvet (Velour)
- Very short, dense, upright pile — minimal twist
- Ultra-smooth appearance; rich colour saturation
- Highly susceptible to shading and pile reversal (watermarking)
- Used in luxury applications; not suitable for busy areas
- Common in stair nosings of luxury hotels and formal dining spaces

#### Loop Cut Pile
- Combination of cut and uncut loops in the same product
- Creates a textured, multi-level surface
- Better soil-hiding than flat cut pile; pile reversal less visible
- See Patterned Cut-Loop below

---

### 3.2 Loop Pile

Loops remain uncut.

#### Level Loop (Brussels Weave / Commercial Loop)
- All loops at the same height — uniform, flat appearance
- Very durable — no exposed tuft tips to abrade
- Used extensively in commercial applications
- Susceptible to "snags" — if a loop is caught and pulled, a run can develop
- Not suitable in environments with dogs (claws catch loops) or wheelchair/walker use (tip hazard)

#### Multi-Level Loop (Berber / High-Low Loop)
- Loops at two or more heights create a textured, informal appearance
- "Berber" specifically refers to a chunky, multi-level loop, often in undyed or natural-effect tones
- Good soil-hiding
- Resilient and durable
- The name "Berber" is now applied broadly to chunky loop piles regardless of fibre or colour

#### Cut-Loop (Sculptured / Patterned)
- Combination of cut tufts and loop pile within the same construction
- Creates a two-dimensional textured pattern
- Good performance: footprinting less visible than pure cut pile; more interesting than pure loop
- Popular for family living rooms and open-plan spaces
- "Sculptured" refers to geometric or organic patterns created by the cut/loop contrast

---

### 3.3 Pile Orientation and Directionality

All carpet pile has a natural "lay" — a direction in which it tends to lie. This has significant practical implications:

**Pile direction:**
- Identified by running a hand across the pile — the direction in which it lies flat is "with the pile"; raking against the pile raises it
- Should always be noted before fitting; consistent pile direction throughout a room is critical
- Running pile away from the primary light source minimises shading contrast and gives a fuller appearance
- Running pile toward the light source gives a richer, deeper colour appearance

**Shading / Pile reversal (watermarking):**
- Occurs in cut pile products when individual tufts permanently change orientation
- Creates light/dark patches — an optical effect of light reflecting differently from differently-oriented tufts
- Not a manufacturing defect; inherent to cut pile carpet, particularly saxony and velvet
- More pronounced in higher pile, softer fibres
- Cannot be corrected; a matter of consumer expectation management

## 4. BACKING SYSTEMS

### 4.1 Primary Backing

The substrate through which tufts are inserted:
- **Woven polypropylene (Action Bac):** The most common primary backing. Dimensionally stable, moisture-resistant, lightweight.
- **Non-woven polyester:** Used in some products; softer, slightly less stable
- **Jute:** Traditional primary backing for woven carpets; may shrink in damp conditions

### 4.2 Secondary Backing

Applied over the latex coating to give dimensional stability and body:
- **Woven jute:** Traditional, breathable, premium feel; may shrink in high humidity
- **Non-woven polypropylene (Action Bac 2):** Moisture-resistant, dimensionally stable; the majority of modern residential carpet
- **Foam secondary backing:** Integral foam layer replacing separate underlay — budget products
- **Hessian:** Used in Wilton and some Axminster products; breathable

### 4.3 Latex Bonding Layer

- SBR (Styrene-Butadiene Rubber) latex: Standard; bonds tufts to primary backing and adheres secondary backing
- PU (Polyurethane) foam: Used in integrated cushion-back products
- Quality of latex critically affects tuft withdrawal force (resistance of tufts to being pulled out)

### 4.4 Cushion-Back (Foam-Back) Carpet

- Integral foam underlay bonded to the carpet backing
- Eliminates the need for separate underlay
- Budget-oriented product segment
- Cannot be fitted over gripper rod — the foam does not stretch; must be direct-stuck or loose-laid
- Lower dimensional stability than separately underlaid carpet
- Foam may degrade over time (hardening, crumbling)
- Not suitable for staircases

### 4.5 Action Bac and Variant Designations

"Action Bac" is a trade name (Shaw Industries) now used generically to describe woven polypropylene backing systems. Various manufacturers use proprietary equivalents. The designation matters for recycling and re-fitting purposes.

## 5. CARPET GRADING AND PERFORMANCE RATING SYSTEMS

### 5.1 European Classification System (EN 1307)

The primary classification system used in the UK and Europe. Defines carpet usage class based on measured performance criteria.

**Usage Classes:**
| Class | Description | Typical Application |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| Class 21 | Moderate domestic | Bedroom, guest room |
| Class 22 | General domestic | Living room |
| Class 23 | Heavy domestic | Hallway, living area |
| Class 31 | Moderate commercial | Private office, hotel bedroom |
| Class 32 | General commercial | Office, reception |
| Class 33 | Heavy commercial | Shop, hotel lobby |
| Class 34 | Very heavy commercial | Airport, department store |

Note: The EN 1307 class 3x (commercial) series requires carpet to withstand an additional drum test.

**Performance criteria measured to assign EN 1307 class:**
- Appearance retention after simulated walking (Vettermann drum test — EN ISO 10361)
- Colour fastness to light (EN ISO 105-B02)
- Colour fastness to rubbing (wet and dry) (EN ISO 105-X12)
- Dimensional stability (EN 986)
- Antistatic properties (EN 1815) — critical for commercial/IT environments

### 5.2 Wheelmark / Keymark

- Quality assurance marks for carpets made to EN 1307
- Administered through European carpet trade associations (ECTA / The Carpet Foundation in the UK)
- Assures end-user that the declared class has been independently verified

### 5.3 The Carpet Foundation Star Rating (UK Domestic)

The UK industry's domestic consumer grading system (operated by The Carpet Foundation):
- **1 Star:** Light use — bedrooms, occasional rooms
- **2 Stars:** Medium use — living rooms, dining rooms
- **3 Stars:** Heavy use — hallways, stairs, family living
- **4 Stars:** Very heavy domestic use
- **5 Stars:** Luxury residential / light commercial (dual classification)

This is a simpler consumer-facing interpretation of the EN 1307 framework. Products must be independently tested to carry the mark.

### 5.4 Commercial Performance Testing Standards

Beyond EN 1307 classification, commercial projects often specify:

**Vettermann Drum Test (EN ISO 10361):** A weighted drum with steel balls rolls over a carpet sample for 22,000 or 44,000 revolutions (representing a walking simulation). Appearance change assessed using photographic reference scales:
- Grade 5 = no change
- Grade 4 = slight change
- Grade 3 = moderate change (minimum acceptable for most commercial specs)
- Grade 2 = significant change
- Grade 1 = severe change

**Lisson Test:** An older UK drum test; still referenced in some older specifications.

**Castor Chair Test (EN 985):** Rolling heavy castor chair over carpet. Critical for open-plan offices.

**Heavy Contract:** Products passing EN 1307 Class 33/34 criteria used in the heaviest commercial applications.

### 5.5 Critical Residential Specification Criteria

When advising on residential carpet, the key measurables:
1. **Pile density** (oz/yd³ or kg/m³) — the most reliable indicator of durability
2. **Face weight** (oz/yd² or g/m²) — useful but less reliable alone without knowing pile height
3. **Fibre type and blend** — nylon > wool > polyester/PP for durability
4. **Twist level** — higher twist = more durable cut pile
5. **EN 1307 usage class** — minimum Class 23 for heavy domestic
6. **Warranty terms** — length, what's covered (wear vs. staining)

## 6. SUBFLOOR REQUIREMENTS AND PREPARATION

### 6.1 General Principles

A carpet installation is only as good as the subfloor beneath it. The subfloor must be:
- **Structurally sound** — no movement, bounce, or spring
- **Dry** — moisture content within limits
- **Smooth** — no ridges, lumps, or hollows exceeding tolerance
- **Clean** — free of grease, adhesive residue, or contamination
- **Level** — within acceptable tolerances for the installation type

### 6.2 Concrete Subfloors

**Moisture testing — mandatory before installation:**
- **Relative Humidity (RH) test (BS 8203 method):** Install in-situ hygrometers in insulated boxes for a minimum of 72 hours. Maximum permitted reading: **75% RH** for most carpets over concrete. Above this, moisture-related problems (mould, adhesive failure, dimensional instability of backing) will occur.
- **Calcium Chloride (anhydrous) test:** Measures moisture vapour emission rate (MVER). Less common in UK; predominantly used in North America.
- **Carbide bomb / CM meter test:** Rapid test; useful as site indication but not a substitute for RH testing.
- **Surface conductance test:** Electronic meter reading; useful screening tool only.

**Surface regularity (flatness tolerance):**
- BS 8203 specifies: maximum deviation of **3mm under a 2m straight-edge**
- Hollows or ridges greater than this must be filled (self-levelling compound) or ground down prior to fitting

**Surface preparation:**
- Remove all existing adhesive residue (grind, scarify, or dissolve with appropriate solvent)
- Fill all cracks, holes, and indentations with floor levelling or patching compound
- Allow levelling compound to cure fully and test moisture again if compound was water-based
- Remove any contamination (oil, wax, paint, dust) — affects adhesive bond and gripper nail penetration

**New concrete screeds:**
- Allow minimum 1mm drying per day under normal conditions (UK climate), or 1 day per 2mm under good drying conditions — **rule of thumb only**
- Underfloor heating screeds: minimum 28 days commissioning cycle before floor covering
- The RH test is always the definitive guide regardless of age

### 6.3 Timber Subfloors

**Types of timber subfloor:**
- **Solid timber floorboards (T&G — tongue and groove):** Traditional construction; springy; may flex
- **Plywood overlay:** Provides rigid, smooth base; preferred for carpet over timber
- **OSB (Oriented Strand Board):** Acceptable with correct thickness; surface can be rough — overskim if needed
- **Chipboard/particleboard:** Common in newer construction; moisture-sensitive; squeaks if not fixed

**Inspection requirements:**
- Walk entire floor; identify squeaks, movement, soft spots, spring
- Nail or screw down any loose boards or panels — punching fixings below surface
- Any movement will be transmitted through carpet and underlay; mechanical fixing is essential
- Check for rot or insect damage — particularly at perimeter boards near exterior walls
- Any gap between boards exceeding 3mm should be filled with flexible filler or covered with 6mm plywood overlay

**Moisture in timber:**
- Timber flooring should be at equilibrium moisture content for the environment
- Typical equilibrium MC for UK interior conditions: 8–12%
- Measure with a calibrated pin-type or RF moisture meter
- Excessive MC (>14%) in timber indicates a problem (rising damp, plumbing leak, condensation) — source must be identified and remedied before installation

**Plywood overlaying:**
- 6mm plywood is the minimum overlay over board subfloors prior to carpet installation
- Secure at 200mm grid with appropriate screws
- Stagger joints; joints should not coincide between overlay sheets and underlying boards
- Plywood species: FSC-certified birch or pine structural plywood preferred
- Do not use MDF — not dimensionally stable when damp

### 6.4 Underfloor Heating (UFH)

- All carpet over UFH must be assessed for combined thermal resistance (R-value)
- Maximum recommended combined R-value (carpet + underlay): **0.15 m²K/W** for efficiency (some manufacturers permit up to 0.20 m²K/W but efficiency is reduced)
- Natural fibre carpets and thick underlay significantly impede heat transfer
- Polypropylene and thin foam underlay perform best thermally
- Commission UFH to maximum operating temperature prior to installation to stabilise screed
- Switch UFH off 48 hours before installation; restart 48 hours after installation (avoids rapid drying of adhesive)
- After installation, gradually increase UFH temperature 5°C per day back to maximum

**R-value calculations:**
- Carpet R-value is typically stated by manufacturer
- Underlay R-value stated by manufacturer
- Combined: add both values
- Wool carpet over thick sponge underlay may exceed 0.15 — check before specification

### 6.5 Subfloor Levelling Systems

- **Latex-based self-levelling compounds (SLC):** Flowable; self-smoothing; cures hard and smooth. Use: general concrete and timber subfloors. Brands: Mapei Ultraplan, Ardex K 15, F. Ball Stopgap 1200.
- **Fibre-reinforced SLC:** For timber substrates with flexion; accommodates minor movement.
- **Calcium sulphate (anhydrite) screeds:** Increasingly common in UFH installations; special primers required before SLC application over anhydrite.
- **Epoxy levelling mortars:** For industrial/very smooth finish requirements; expensive.

## 7. INSTALLATION METHODS AND TECHNIQUES

### 7.1 Stretch-In (Gripper-and-Underlay) Method

The standard residential installation method in the UK. Carpet is stretched over separate underlay and attached at the perimeter by gripper rod.

**Process overview:**
1. Install gripper rod around the room perimeter
2. Install underlay
3. Seam underlay joins (where required)
4. Cut carpet with appropriate allowance (typically 75mm extra per dimension)
5. Position carpet, align pile direction
6. Attach one end to gripper rod
7. Stretch carpet with knee kicker and/or power stretcher across the room
8. Attach to opposite gripper rod
9. Repeat in the other direction
10. Trim excess at all edges
11. Tuck into gap between gripper and wall using a bolster/stair chisel

**Stretching — critical detail:**
- Carpet must be stretched, not merely laid. Unstretched carpet will ripple over time as backing relaxes.
- Stretch toward the far wall — not across
- Sequence: fix one length edge → stretch across width and fix → work down the length while stretching
- The power stretcher must be used in all rooms exceeding 4.5m in either direction; knee kicker alone is inadequate and will result in relaxation and rippling over time (this is the single most common cause of carpet rippling)
- Power stretcher: tube extends to push against wall via protector plate; head engages pile; lever forces stretch across room

**Knee kicker usage:**
- Used for initial positioning, tucking into edges, and small areas/stairs
- Cushion head adjustable for pile depth — ensure teeth grip tufts without damaging backing
- Kick with knee, not foot — controlled force

### 7.2 Direct Stick (Fully Adhered) Method

Carpet is adhered directly to the subfloor with no underlay. Used in commercial applications and some cushion-back residential products.

**Applications:**
- Commercial: offices, retail, hotels, healthcare
- Cushion-back residential carpet (mandatory)
- Natural fibre flooring
- Conservatories and areas with high stability requirement

**Process:**
1. Prepare and prime subfloor as required
2. Apply tackifier or full-bond adhesive
3. Allow adhesive to reach correct tack (open time) as specified by adhesive manufacturer
4. Roll carpet into position; press firmly
5. Roll with 70kg carpet roller or floor roller to ensure full contact

**Adhesive types:**
- **Tackifier (pressure-sensitive adhesive / PSA):** Repositionable; allows removal. Used in commercial loop pile and carpet tiles. Applied to subfloor only.
- **Contact adhesive:** Applied to both surfaces; immediate bond on contact. Used for natural fibres and some special applications.
- **Thermal (hot melt) adhesive:** Used with seaming tape. Not a full-bond method.
- **Releasable full-bond:** Moderate bond; allows eventual removal without major subfloor damage.
- **Permanent full-bond:** Aggressive; difficult removal. Used where maximum stability essential (healthcare, heavy commercial).

### 7.3 Double-Stick (Double-Bond) Method

Two layers of adhesive:
1. Underlay is adhered to subfloor
2. Carpet is adhered to underlay

This provides the performance (cushioning, noise, thermal) of underlay with the dimensional stability of full adhesion. Used in premium commercial installations and healthcare.

### 7.4 Loose Lay

Carpet laid without fixings; relies on its own weight and friction.
- Used for temporary installations, exhibitions, small rooms
- Not suitable for stairs or areas of high lateral force
- Some heavy commercial products (particularly needle-punch) are designed for loose lay
- Natural fibre rugs: loose lay with rug pad

## 9. GRIPPER ROD SYSTEMS

### 9.1 Standard Gripper Rod

- Thin wooden or metal strip with angled pins (grippers/teeth) that engage the carpet backing
- Positioned **approximately 6–8mm from the wall** — sufficient gap to tuck carpet edge
- Nailed to timber floors or secured with hammer-in pins to concrete
- **On concrete:** Powder-actuated nail gun (Hilti type) is preferred for speed and security
- Angle of pins: critical — pins should lean toward the wall (away from room) to grip carpet when stretched
- Pins should be sharp — replace gripper rod if pins are bent or blunt

### 9.2 Gripper Rod Types

**Standard timber gripper:** Pine strip with steel pins; nailed down. Most common.
**Concrete gripper (metal):** Metal bar with harder fixing pins for hammer driving into concrete without nail gun.
**Aluminium gripper:** Lighter weight; used in some commercial applications.
**Double-pin gripper:** Used at seams where carpet must be gripped from two directions.
**Tackless gripper (US term):** Generic term for the gripper system as a whole (as opposed to tack-down method).
**Hot-melt gripper:** Self-adhesive base with pins; used where drilling/nailing is not permitted (raised access floors, some commercial).

### 9.3 Doorways and Thresholds

Where carpet meets another floor covering or transitions at a doorway:
- **Carpet-to-carpet binder bar:** Z-section metal strip; carpet tucks into both sides
- **Carpet gripper transition bar:** One side grips carpet; other side flat for abutting hard floor
- **Threshold strip (aluminium carpet strip):** Single-side carpet retention; flat edge facing hard floor
- **Flush transition plates:** Ramp-profile plates for wheelchair access compliance (DDA considerations)
- **Carpet to solid wood / LVT transition:** Reducer profile; height depends on material thicknesses

**Door clearance:** Account for carpet and underlay height increase when specifying doors. Typical increase: 8–20mm depending on carpet/underlay combination. Doors may need to be planed or rehung.

### 9.4 Gripper Installation Sequence

1. Install all doorway threshold bars first
2. Install gripper rod at perimeter — start from doorway threshold, work around room
3. Leave a gap at door threshold — carpet must end at/before threshold bar, not ride over it
4. In curved bays or alcoves: cut gripper rod into short sections to follow contour
5. Around radiators, pipes, and obstacles: cut and fit individual pieces
6. Ensure all pins are protruding and angled correctly before installing underlay

## 10. UNDERLAY — TYPES, SELECTION AND SPECIFICATION

### 10.1 Purpose of Underlay

- Comfort underfoot (cushioning)
- Extending carpet life (by absorbing impact and flex rather than the carpet backing)
- Thermal insulation
- Acoustic insulation (impact sound reduction)
- Smoothing out very minor subfloor imperfections
- Moisture vapour barrier in some products

### 10.2 Underlay Types

#### Polyurethane (PU) Foam
- **Reconstituted foam (crumb foam / chip foam):** Made from recycled foam offcuts bonded together. Very common, cost-effective. Dense and supportive. Typically grey or black. TOG rating: 1.0–2.5 typical.
- **Prime foam:** Continuous cell foam; softer, more uniform feel. Often white or beige. Less durable than crumb foam under heavy traffic.
- **Memory foam underlay:** Slow-recovery foam; premium feel; not ideal under heavy furniture or commercial use.

**PU foam density:** Measured in kg/m³. Higher density = harder-wearing:
- Standard domestic: 75–100 kg/m³
- Heavy domestic: 100–130 kg/m³
- Light commercial: 130+ kg/m³

#### Rubber Underlay
- **Natural rubber (Dunlop process):** Dense, durable; excellent for heavy commercial use. Heavy. Good dimensional stability. Not suitable under UFH (adds significant thermal resistance).
- **Synthetic rubber (SBR):** Adequate performance; lower cost. May outgas slightly when new.
- **Waffle rubber:** Traditional profile; open cell; adequate performance; now largely superseded by denser products

#### Felt Underlay
- **Wool felt:** Premium product; natural; good thermal and acoustic performance; best performance under UFH (lower R-value than foam)
- **Synthetic felt (needled polyester or polypropylene):** Good firmness; used under commercial carpet; very thin profile (3–5mm)
- **Hair and jute (horsehair and jute):** Traditional product; still used in restoration projects and luxury installs; premium feel

#### Specialist Underlay
- **Thermal underlay (foil-faced):** Reflective foil on top surface; claimed to reflect heat upward; marginal improvement in efficiency; reduces heat transmission into subfloor
- **Acoustic underlay:** High-density products specifically designed for impact sound (Ln,w) and airborne sound (ΔRw) reduction; used in flats and multi-occupancy buildings
- **Anti-bacterial underlay:** Silver-ion or other treatment; used in healthcare
- **Damp-proof underlay (DPM underlay):** Closed-cell foam or polythene-faced; acts as a vapour barrier over concrete with high moisture readings — NOTE: only acceptable as a risk-mitigating measure; RH readings above 75% require a properly bonded DPM membrane, not an underlay workaround

### 10.3 Underlay TOG Rating

The UK carpet industry uses the TOG rating system for thermal resistance:
- 1 TOG = 0.1 m²K/W
- Standard residential underlay: 1.5–3.0 TOG
- For UFH: maximum underlay TOG should keep combined carpet+underlay below 1.5 TOG total (0.15 m²K/W)
- Confirm TOG values from manufacturer datasheets

### 10.4 Underlay Thickness

- Standard domestic: 7–11mm
- Heavy domestic: 9–11mm
- Stairs: maximum 9mm (thicker underlay can cause carpet to lift from step)
- Commercial: 3–8mm (firmer products preferred to avoid excessive carpet compression)
- Over UFH: manufacturer-specified maximum (typically 3–5mm)

### 10.5 Underlay Installation

- Butt all joins (do not overlap — creates a ridge visible through carpet)
- Tape joins with specialist underlay tape (hessian-backed or polypropylene)
- Do not allow underlay to overlap gripper rod pins — cut flush to gripper rod inner edge
- Grip underlay lightly with staple gun on timber subfloors (prevents shifting during carpet stretch)
- On concrete: double-sided tape or underlay adhesive at perimeter and joins

## 11. ADHESIVES AND SEAMING

### 11.1 Seaming Techniques

**Hot-melt tape method (standard):**
- Most common seaming method in UK residential and commercial
- Lay tape adhesive-side up along centre of seam line
- Position carpet edges over tape (not touching each other)
- Slide pre-heated iron under one carpet edge; draw toward the other edge simultaneously pressing edges together
- Work continuously — do not allow iron to dwell or backing will scorch
- Hold pressed seam for 20–30 seconds as adhesive cools; weight with heavy objects if needed
- Seam must not be walked on until fully cool (minimum 30 minutes)

**Contact cement seaming:**
- Apply contact cement to both carpet edges and allow to become tacky
- Press edges together; bond is immediate — no repositioning possible
- Use for natural fibres and specialist products

**Seam placement principles:**
- Never place seams under doors (constant flex stress)
- Place seams parallel to the main light source (seam shadow less visible)
- Pile direction must be consistent across seam — both pieces must run the same direction
- Pattern-match across seam for Axminster and patterned products
- Allow pattern repeat wastage in material estimates
- Machine-direction seams (seam parallel to machine direction / along length) are generally stronger than cross-seam cuts

**Seam strength:** Seam must withstand the same tensile load as the unsupported carpet; hot-melt seam should equal or exceed the carpet's tensile strength.

## 12. STAIR CARPETING

### 12.1 Types of Stair Carpet Installation

**Wall to wall (fitted):**
- Carpet fitted across full width of stairs
- Gripper rod on each tread/riser junction (on tread) and at top of each riser
- Underlay cut for each tread individually; tacked or stapled to tread
- Most common domestic method

**Runner:**
- Strip of carpet down the centre of stairs; treads visible at each side
- Traditional; emphasises stair architecture
- Fitted loose in wooden rings or with rods, or direct-stick

**Cap and band:**
- Carpet on tread only; contrasting material (paint, wood) on riser
- Requires different carpet fixing on tread

### 12.2 Stair Gripper Placement

- One strip on the back of each tread (angled so pins grip carpet on the riser)
- One strip on each riser near the base (angled so pins grip carpet on the tread)
- This "L" formation at each step holds carpet firmly at the transition point
- Alternative: specialist stair nosing gripper (combined tread/riser gripper in one profile)
- Concrete stairs: nail gun or adhesive-fixed gripper

### 12.3 Stair Underlay

- Cut to fit tread (not riser)
- Tacked or stapled to rear of tread, up to riser
- Maximum thickness: 9mm; thicker underlay causes carpet to lift from nosing
- Some fitters use no underlay on stairs where Axminster or high-pile woven is specified — carpet mass is sufficient

### 12.4 Calculating Stair Carpet Requirements

**Standard method:**
- Measure tread + riser depth of each step (typically 230mm tread + 190mm riser = 420mm per step)
- Add 50mm per step for turning around nosing
- Total length = (tread + riser + 50mm) × number of steps + allowance for top landing and bottom tread
- Width = width of stair + 50mm each side for tucking

**Pattern carpet:**
- Pattern repeat must be considered — may need to advance one repeat per step
- This can significantly increase material usage
- "Drop match" pattern: each step must be offset; calculate carefully

### 12.5 Stair Safety

- EN 13893: Coefficient of friction for floor coverings — minimum requirement for slip resistance
- Stair carpet must achieve a minimum μ of 0.30 dry and 0.30 wet
- Seagrass and smooth natural fibres may fail this test on stairs — must be avoided
- Low-pile, dense carpet is recommended for stairs for safety

### 12.6 Angled / Feature Stairs

**Winding stairs:**
- Stairs that change direction (quarter-turn or half-turn)
- The tread width varies (narrow at centre of curve, wide at outer edge)
- Carpet must be cut to accommodate the winder — pieces often need paper templates
- Seaming at winders is common and must be expertly done

**Open-riser stairs:**
- No riser; carpet fits on tread only
- Wrapped around the tread nosing and tucked under
- Requires bonding or stapling to underside

## 13. COMMERCIAL CARPET INSTALLATION

### 13.1 Carpet Tiles

**Overview:** The dominant commercial carpet product. Standard tile size: 500mm × 500mm. Also available: 600×600mm, 250×1000mm planks.

**Construction types:**
- **Tufted tiles:** Tufted pile on a multi-layer bitumen or PVC tile backing
- **Woven tiles (Axminster):** Woven face pile on a rigid backing; higher cost; used in prestige commercial projects
- **Needle-punch tiles:** Non-woven; very flat; used in exhibitions and low-cost commercial
- **Fusion-bonded tiles:** Dense, flat pile; used in heavy commercial

**Tile backing systems:**
- **Bitumen (thermoplastic):** Standard; stable; heavy; cold = brittle; widely recycled
- **PVC / vinyl backing:** Lighter; more flexible; good dimensional stability
- **GlasBac (fibre-glass reinforced):** Excellent dimensional stability; thin; used in raised access floor environments
- **Cushion tile:** Integral foam cushion on tile; used in long-standing-use environments (retail)

**Installation layout:**
- **Ashlar (brick bond):** Each row offset by half a tile; reduces pattern visibility, good for plain tiles
- **Quarter turn (brick-work):** Each tile rotated 90° alternately; creates a varied shading effect from pile direction change; used with plain commercial tiles
- **Monolithic:** All tiles in same direction; creates directional appearance; used with patterned tiles
- **Parquet:** Checkerboard rotation; creates geometric effect

**Finding the centre:**
- Snap chalk lines at mid-points of all four walls; their intersection = room centre
- Dry-lay tiles from centre to assess border widths; adjust centre if border tiles would be less than half a tile wide
- Work outward from centre in all directions

**Adhesive:**
- Pressure-sensitive tackifier is standard — allows repositioning for replacement of individual tiles
- Full-bond adhesive used in high-traffic zones (airports) or where tiles must not shift under impact/castor chairs
- Tackifier application: notched trowel at specified spread rate; allow to dry to clear (typically 20–30 minutes before laying)

**Raised access floors:**
- Tiles must be dimensionally stable — GlasBac tiles preferred
- Avoid moisture-sensitive backings
- Full bond usually required to prevent tiles lifting under flexion
- Check load-bearing capacity of access floor panels

### 13.2 Broadloom Commercial Carpet

- Used in corridors, hotel bedrooms, and areas requiring seam-free appearance
- Installation: typically direct-stick or double-stick method
- EN 1307 Class 33/34 carpet required for heavy commercial
- Solution-dyed nylon BCF is the dominant fibre for heavy commercial broadloom
- Seams managed as per residential but with greater emphasis on seam strength (castor chairs)

### 13.3 Healthcare and Education Environments

**Healthcare-specific requirements:**
- Infection control: carpet must be cleanable with disinfectants (pH range 6–10 typically); check fibre and backing compatibility
- Solution-dyed fibres are essential for bleach tolerance
- Carpet tiles with impermeable PVC or polyurethane backing prevent liquid penetration to subfloor
- Some products achieve **MRSA-resistant** certification (antimicrobial treatment)
- Slip resistance: EN 13893 compliance is mandatory in patient-facing areas
- Low VOC: BREEAM or NHS SHTM specification may require low-emission products

**Education:**
- Heavy commercial (EN 1307 Class 33) minimum for classroom/corridor
- Carpet tiles preferred for easy replacement of damaged areas
- Acoustic benefit: significant reduction in reverberation time vs hard floors — benefits communication and concentration
- Durability: withstand heavy furniture, castor chairs, constant foot traffic

### 13.4 Hospitality Carpet

- **Hotels:** Axminster woven broadloom is the prestige specification for public areas; bespoke patterns common; pile typically 80/20 wool/nylon
- **Restaurants:** Hard-wearing; cleanability paramount; solution-dyed; modular tile or direct-stick broadloom
- **Corridors:** Long narrow spaces; seams minimised; EN 1307 Class 33+; fire performance compliance essential
- **Bedroom corridors:** Often Axminster or quality tufted; aesthetic more prominent than bedroom carpet

**Fire performance requirements (commercial):**
- BS EN 13501-1 fire classification — carpet must achieve minimum Cfl-s1 (commercial hospitality/public buildings) or Bfl-s1 (most critical applications)
- For UK buildings subject to Building Regulations Approved Document B: floor coverings must be at minimum Class 1 critical radiant flux
- For the Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005: carpets in all commercial premises must be assessed as part of fire risk assessment
- **Cfl** designation refers to the floor covering fire classification (f = floor)
- Key tests: ISO 9239-1 (critical radiant flux test)

## 14. PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 14.1 Rippling and Buckling

**Symptoms:** Waves or ridges appearing in the carpet surface, typically after some time in use.

**Causes:**
1. **Insufficient stretching during installation** — the most common cause; carpet was positioned rather than stretched; as the carpet relaxes, excess backing forms ridges. Fix: re-stretch with power stretcher.
2. **High humidity** — carpet backing (particularly jute) absorbs moisture and expands; more common in spring when indoor heating reduces and humidity rises. Often disappears in dry weather; persistent rippling in high humidity may require re-stretching.
3. **Heavy dragging of furniture** — particularly with foam-backed carpet; can distort backing permanently.
4. **Delamination of secondary backing** — the latex bond between primary and secondary backing has failed; backing layers move independently. Fix: replacement.
5. **Underlay shifting** — if underlay was not secured and has migrated, creating lumps under carpet.
6. **Carpet laid without underlay** — direct-laid carpet over wood floors can move with subfloor expansion.

**Distinguishing cause:**
- Re-stretch and monitor: if ripples return rapidly → humidity or backing delamination
- Feel the back of carpet at ridge: two separate layers → delamination
- Examine underlay during re-stretch: check for migration

### 14.2 Pile Shedding and Fluffing

**Symptoms:** Short fibres or fluff appearing on carpet surface, especially after vacuuming.

**Causes:**
- Normal in new carpets, particularly wool and wool-blend products — residual processing fibres from cutting
- Spun (staple) fibres will shed more than BCF (bulked continuous filament)
- In wool, shedding typically diminishes to negligible levels after 4–8 weeks of regular vacuuming
- Persistent, heavy shedding beyond 6 months may indicate poor tuft withdrawal force — a manufacturing defect

**Advice:** Advise customers to vacuum frequently (daily for first 4 weeks) with a suction-only vacuum (no beater bar on new carpets for the first month).

### 14.3 Pile Crushing and Matting

**Symptoms:** Pile becomes flat, does not recover, particularly in traffic lanes.

**Causes:**
1. **Wrong carpet for the application** — pile weight and density insufficient for usage level; particularly polypropylene or low-density polyester cut pile
2. **Inadequate underlay** — compressed underlay reduces resilience
3. **Normal wear** — any carpet will eventually show traffic patterns; the rate depends on pile type, fibre, and density

**Diagnosis:**
- Check EN 1307 class vs application
- Check face weight and pile density against recommended minimums for usage class
- Crush test: press firmly with thumb; release. Rate of recovery indicates current resilience.

**Mitigation:**
- Regular vacuuming maintains pile by lifting it
- Pile raising with a stiff brush or pile rake
- Not reversible once significant permanent compression has occurred

### 14.4 Shading and Pile Reversal (Watermarking / Pooling)

**Symptoms:** Light and dark patches in the carpet, often in apparently random patterns, particularly in cut pile.

**Causes:**
- Permanent change in pile direction — individual tufts lean in different directions
- An optical effect; not a staining issue
- More pronounced in: high pile height, soft fibres, luxurious saxony constructions, light colours
- Can develop over time or be present from early use
- Neither a manufacturing defect nor an installation error — an inherent characteristic of cut pile carpet

**Communication:** Critically important to set customer expectations before purchase, particularly for light-coloured saxony carpets. Show sample of pile reversal if possible.

### 14.5 Staining and Soiling

**Types:**
- **Surface soiling:** Loose particles in pile; removed by vacuuming. Primary source: foot traffic bringing in external particles.
- **Oily soiling:** Skin oils, cooking vapour, food fats — attracted to pile; cannot be vacuumed; requires detergent cleaning.
- **Spot stains:** Specific spills (red wine, coffee, ink, etc.)
- **Pet stains:** Urine, faeces — both immediate cleaning and odour treatment required
- **Wicking:** A stain that appears to be removed but reappears after drying — the stain penetrated to backing/underlay; drying draws it back to surface

**Wicking treatment:**
- Clean surface
- Saturate area with clean water to dilute and push contamination into dry cloth/paper towels by pressing
- Repeat multiple times
- May need to lift carpet, treat underlay/subfloor, and dry both before re-laying

**First aid stain treatment (general principles):**
1. Blot (do not rub) immediately — rubbing spreads and works stain deeper into pile
2. Work from outside of stain toward centre
3. Use appropriate chemical (see 15.2 below)
4. Rinse with clean water to remove cleaning agent
5. Blot dry; do not over-saturate (risk of wicking and backing delamination if soaked)
6. Use white cloth only — coloured cloths may transfer dye

### 14.6 Mould and Mildew

**Symptoms:** Black spots or musty odour at base of pile or backing.

**Causes:**
- Excess moisture — from flooding, cleaning over-saturation, or high subfloor RH
- Particularly affects jute-backed products and natural fibre carpet in damp conditions
- Foam backing can support mould growth in damp conditions

**Action:**
- Identify and eliminate the moisture source
- If mould is visible on backing: carpet almost certainly requires replacement (mould in backing cannot be effectively treated without off-site specialist cleaning)
- Treat subfloor with biocide; allow to dry fully; install DPM if RH above 75%
- Replace with moisture-resistant product if source of moisture is ongoing (e.g., ground-floor slab without DPM)

### 14.7 Static

**Symptoms:** Static electric shocks when walking on carpet and touching metal objects or other people.

**Causes:**
- Low-humidity environment + non-anti-static carpet
- Polypropylene carpet is particularly prone
- Carpets without anti-static fibre (carbon or stainless steel fibre incorporated in a fraction of tufts) will generate static above certain humidity thresholds

**Solutions:**
- Anti-static carpet specification (EN 1815 compliance: ≤2.0 kV in commercial environments)
- Topical anti-static spray treatment (temporary, requires re-application)
- Raise room humidity above 40% RH (if practical)

### 14.8 Fading and Colour Change

**Causes:**
- **UV fading:** Direct sunlight — particularly affects acid-dyed wool, vat-dyed nylon; solution-dyed fibres are most resistant
- **Ozone bleaching:** Air pollution — affects some dyes; most common near urban roads
- **Cleaning chemical damage:** Alkaline cleaners on wool (pH >8 degrades wool protein); bleach on non-bleach-resistant fibres
- **Topical treatment interaction:** Some stain-protection sprays can alter appearance of certain dye systems

**Assessment:**
- Compare faded area against unexposed area (under furniture)
- Check cleaning history — chemical damage or UV?
- Issue with solution-dyed vs. piece-dyed products

### 14.9 Gaps Between Gripper Rod and Wall

**Symptoms:** Edge of carpet visible, not tucked in; carpet "lifting" at edge.

**Causes:**
- Gripper rod installed too far from wall — gap too wide for tucking
- Carpet stretched insufficiently
- Plinth/skirting removed after installation; gap now exposed
- Baseboard installer trim applied on top of carpet edge

**Fix:**
- Re-stretch carpet if not already fully stretched
- Add narrow timber fillet to reduce gap if gripper cannot be re-positioned
- Tuck with bolster chisel; may need to re-stretch first to create sufficient surplus

## 15. MAINTENANCE AND CLEANING

### 15.1 Routine Maintenance

**Vacuuming:**
- The single most important maintenance activity
- Removes dry soil before it embeds in pile and causes abrasive wear
- Frequency recommendation: residential — minimum 2× per week in traffic areas; commercial — daily in public areas
- **Upright vacuum with beater bar/brush roll:** Most effective for cutting pile; agitates pile and lifts embedded soil; not recommended for loop pile (risk of snagging) or very new carpets
- **Cylinder/canister with suction head:** Suitable for all carpet types; gentler
- Slow, overlapping passes in traffic direction; then cross-direction pass periodically

**Pile raking / grooming:**
- Restore pile direction and appearance after heavy use
- Pile rake or carpet grooming brush drawn in pile direction
- Particularly effective after cleaning and before receiving visitors (hospitality use)

**Entrance matting:**
- The most cost-effective maintenance measure for any carpeted space
- Matting at all entries traps soil before it reaches carpet
- "Walk-off" principle: a minimum of 4.5 metres of entrance matting significantly reduces soil ingress
- Matting should be cleaned more frequently than carpet itself

### 15.2 Spot and Stain Cleaning

**Cleaning agents by stain type:**

| Stain Type | First Response | Chemical Agent |
|---|---|---|
| Water-based (coffee, tea, wine, juice) | Blot immediately | Water + neutral detergent; rinse |
| Red wine | Blot; apply cold water | Specialist wine/red stain remover; enzymatic cleaner |
| Grease/oil | Blot; do not spread | Dry cleaning fluid (non-aqueous); or specialist degreaser |
| Chewing gum | Freeze with aerosol | Peel off frozen gum; residue with dry cleaning solvent |
| Urine (pet) | Blot immediately | Enzymatic cleaner (breaks down uric acid salts) — essential to prevent re-soiling |
| Blood | Cold water only — never hot | Cold water blotting; enzymatic cleaner |
| Ink (ballpoint) | Do not rub | Isopropyl alcohol; dab and blot |
| Emulsion paint (wet) | Remove immediately | Water; blot; rinse |
| Nail varnish | Blot | Non-acetone nail varnish remover; acetone only on synthetics (test first) |

**pH considerations:**
- Wool carpet: only pH 5–8 cleaning agents; alkaline agents (bleach, ammonia, washing soda) will damage wool permanently
- Polypropylene: tolerates wider pH range; stain resistance reduces need for active cleaning

### 15.3 Deep Cleaning Methods

**Hot water extraction (HWE / "steam cleaning"):**
- Most effective deep cleaning method for residential and commercial carpet
- Injects heated water (with or without detergent) into pile at pressure; immediately extracted by powerful vacuum
- Temperature: typically 60–80°C (referred to as "steam" but is hot water/steam mix)
- Suitable for most tufted and woven carpets
- Not recommended for natural fibre (jute-backed, sisal, seagrass) — moisture causes backing shrinkage
- Dry time: 4–12 hours depending on carpet weight, humidity, and airflow
- Detergent residue if not rinsed can cause rapid re-soiling — always rinse after detergent extraction
- Low-moisture variants (dry steam) exist for sensitive backings

**Dry compound cleaning (HOST / Capture):**
- Absorbent powder mixed with cleaning agents worked into pile
- Powder absorbs soil; vacuumed out after drying period (20–30 minutes)
- Low moisture — suitable for natural fibres and moisture-sensitive products
- Not as effective for heavy soiling
- Residual powder is a concern if vacuum does not fully extract

**Encapsulation cleaning:**
- Cleaning solution applied; dries to a crystalline polymer that encapsulates soil
- Soil-polymer crystal is vacuumed out after drying
- Suitable as interim maintenance clean between deep extractions
- Popular in commercial maintenance contracts; low dry time
- Does not deep-clean — soil near pile base is not removed
- Can cause build-up if over-used

**Bonnet/absorbent pad cleaning:**
- Rotating pad soaked in cleaning solution agitates and absorbs soil
- Surface cleaning only — does not reach pile base
- Risk of pile distortion from rotating head
- Low quality result; not recommended as primary method

**Shampooing:**
- Older method; generates foam; soil trapped in foam dried and vacuumed
- Risk of residue and rapid re-soiling; largely superseded by HWE and encapsulation
- Still used in some contexts for pre-treatment

### 15.4 Cleaning Frequency (Commercial)

| Application | Interim Vacuum | Encapsulation | HWE |
|---|---|---|---|
| Office (light traffic) | Daily | Every 4–8 weeks | 1–2× per year |
| Office (heavy traffic) | Daily | Every 2 weeks | 3–4× per year |
| Hotel public area | 2× daily | Weekly | Every 3 months |
| Retail | Daily | Weekly | Quarterly |
| Healthcare | Daily | Weekly minimum | Per infection control schedule |

### 15.5 Professional Cleaning Standards

- IICRC (Institute of Inspection, Cleaning and Restoration Certification) — S100 Standard and Reference Guide for Professional Carpet Cleaning
- NCCA (National Carpet Cleaners Association, UK) — membership and training standard
- CRI (Carpet and Rug Institute) — Seal of Approval for cleaning equipment and chemicals

## 16. BRITISH AND EUROPEAN STANDARDS

### 16.1 Installation Standards

**BS 5325: Installation of textile floor coverings — Code of Practice**
- The primary UK standard governing carpet installation
- Covers: subfloor requirements, moisture testing, fitting methods, tools, edge finishing, and seaming
- Compliance with BS 5325 is the benchmark for all professional carpet installations
- References BS 8203 for subfloor moisture testing

**BS 8203: Code of practice for installation of resilient floor coverings**
- Subfloor preparation requirements (also referenced for carpet)
- Moisture testing protocols: RH threshold 75%
- Surface regularity: 3mm under 2m straight-edge

**BS 6263-2: Care and maintenance of floor surfaces — Code of practice for textile flooring**
- Maintenance, cleaning frequency, and cleaning methods for carpet
- Used in commercial maintenance contract specifications

### 16.2 Product Standards

**BS EN 1307: Textile floor coverings — Classification of pile yarn floor coverings**
- Defines performance testing criteria and usage classes
- Tests: appearance retention (Vettermann drum), pile fibre content, pile yarn count, pile height, pile density, colour fastness
- The European standard replacing older national standards

**BS EN 1470: Textile floor coverings — Classification of needle punched floor coverings (non-woven)**
- Performance classification for needle-punch carpet

**BS EN 1963: Textile floor coverings — Specification for machinery woven axminster carpet**
**BS EN 1964: Textile floor coverings — Specification for machinery woven Wilton pile carpet**

**BS EN ISO 10361: Textile floor coverings — Vettermann drum test for appearance change**
- The standard test for pile appearance change (crush/matting/pile reversal)

**BS EN 985: Textile floor coverings — Determination of castor chair resistance**
- Used for office carpet specification

**BS EN 1815: Textile floor coverings — Assessment of static electrical propensity**
- Determines electrostatic generation; maximum 2.0 kV for EN 1307 commercial compliance

**BS EN 13893: Floor coverings — Measurement of dynamic coefficient of friction on dry floor surfaces**
- Slip resistance measurement; relevant to safety assessments

**BS EN ISO 11058: Geotextiles and geotextile-related products — Determination of water permeability characteristics normal to the plane**
- Relevant to carpet underlay permeability testing

### 16.3 Fire Standards

**BS EN 13501-1: Fire classification of construction products and building elements**
- The European fire classification framework
- Floor coverings are assigned classes: Afl, Bfl, Cfl, Dfl, Efl, Ffl (fl = floor)
- Additional smoke classification: s1 (limited smoke), s2 (moderate smoke)
- Minimum requirement for commercial carpet in UK: Cfl-s1 (most applications); Bfl-s1 (escape routes, higher risk)

**BS 4790: Method of determination of the effects of a small source of ignition on textile floor coverings (hot metal nut test)**
- Tests resistance to small ignition sources (cigarette, ember)
- Relevant to hospitality and public building specification

**Building Regulations Approved Document B (Fire Safety):**
- UK building regulation; references EN 13501-1 classification requirements for floor coverings
- Class 1 critical radiant flux minimum for circulation areas in public buildings
- Escape routes (including hotel corridors) require higher performance

### 16.4 Sustainability Standards

**BS EN ISO 14001:** Environmental management systems — relevant to manufacturer environmental credentials
**The Carpet Foundation Responsible Fibre Standard:** UK industry sustainability certification
**EU Ecolabel for Floor Coverings:** Limits on harmful substances in carpet production
**Oeko-Tex Standard 100:** Tests for harmful substances in textiles; relevant to children's environments

### 16.5 Acoustic Standards

**BS EN ISO 717-1 / 717-2:** Acoustics — rating of sound insulation; impact and airborne sound
**BS EN ISO 10140:** Laboratory measurements of sound insulation of building elements
**BB93 (Building Bulletin 93):** Acoustic design of schools — specifies reverberation time requirements; carpet significantly assists compliance

## 17. SUSTAINABILITY AND ENVIRONMENTAL CONSIDERATIONS

### 17.1 End-of-Life and Recycling

- Carpet is one of the largest contributors to landfill in the construction sector
- Annual UK carpet waste: approximately 400,000 tonnes
- Recycling routes:
  - **Nylon 6:** Chemical depolymerisation back to caprolactam monomer (ECONYL process); closed loop
  - **Nylon 6,6:** Mechanical recycling; limited chemical recycling currently
  - **Polypropylene:** Mechanical recycling; energy recovery
  - **Wool:** Composting (natural fibre); wool back to fibre (specialist shoddy industry)
  - **Bitumen tile backing:** Energy recovery; limited material recycling

**CARE (Carpet Recycling UK):** Industry body administering UK carpet recycling schemes. The Carpet Recycling UK scheme diverts carpet from landfill via a network of recyclers and reuse schemes.

### 17.2 Environmental Product Declarations (EPD)

- ISO 14025 / EN 15804 compliant EPDs provide verified life-cycle assessment (LCA) data
- Used in BREEAM and LEED green building assessments
- Growing requirement in commercial specification

### 17.3 VOC Emissions

- Carpet and adhesives can off-gas VOCs (volatile organic compounds) after installation
- Primary sources: latex backing compounds, adhesives, topical treatments
- UK Air Quality standards (DEFRA) and CEN requirements
- AgBB / GUT and EU Ecolabel certifications indicate low VOC content
- Ventilate premises thoroughly for 48–72 hours after installation of new carpet
- Low-VOC adhesive options widely available for direct-stick applications

### 17.4 Wool as a Sustainable Fibre

- Renewable: sheep produce wool annually
- Biodegradable: fully biodegrades in soil, releasing nitrogen (a soil nutrient)
- Carbon sequestration: sheep graze grasslands that sequester carbon
- Campaign for Wool: HRH King Charles III-founded initiative promoting wool as a sustainable fibre
- British Wool Marketing Board: grades and markets UK fleece; provides provenance labelling

## 18. SPECIALIST APPLICATIONS

### 18.1 Carpet Tiles — Raised Access Floors

- Tiles must be GlasBac or equivalent dimensionally stable backing
- No underlay — tiles laid directly on access floor panels
- Full bond adhesive recommended to prevent tile lift from panel flexion
- Panel access: individual tiles can be lifted cleanly for cable management

### 18.2 Outdoor and Conservatory Carpet

- UV-resistant fibres essential: solution-dyed polypropylene
- Drainage backing (perforated or open-back) for areas prone to moisture
- Anti-mould treatment
- Natural fibres are unsuitable for true outdoor or wet environments
- Some needle-punch products are UV-stable and moisture-resistant for covered outdoor terraces

### 18.3 Marine and Vehicle Carpet

- Typically needle-punch polypropylene or flatweave
- Adhesive bonded to fibreglass or GRP hull surfaces
- Waterproof; mould-resistant
- Cut to template and bonded with contact adhesive

### 18.4 Acoustic Treatment Carpet (Performance Venues)

- Acoustic carpet engineered to absorb specific frequency ranges
- Used in concert halls, recording studios, home cinema rooms
- Thick pile, high density wool carpet provides broad-spectrum absorption
- Often specified with dense felt underlay for maximum absorption coefficient
- NRC (Noise Reduction Coefficient) ratings available from manufacturers

### 18.5 Anti-Fatigue Carpet

- Thick cushion-backed or double-stuck carpet with significant pile depth and soft foam underlay
- Used in environments requiring long standing periods: retail checkout areas, manufacturing
- EU Manual Handling Directive considerations

## APPENDIX B: PATTERN REPEAT CALCULATION

For Axminster and patterned carpets, material requirement must account for pattern matching:

**Formula:**
> Required length (m) = [(Room length ÷ Pattern repeat, rounded up to whole number) × Pattern repeat] + 300mm fitting allowance
> Repeat this for each drop/piece

**Example:**
- Room: 4.8m long
- Pattern repeat: 640mm
- Number of repeats required: 4.8 ÷ 0.64 = 7.5 → round up to 8
- Required length: 8 × 0.64 = 5.12m + 0.3m = 5.42m

**Across-the-width pattern matching:**
- For patterns that must also match across seams, the same calculation applies laterally
- Total material waste can be 10–20% for large repeat patterns in irregularly shaped rooms
