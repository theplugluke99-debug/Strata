## 1. UNDERFLOOR HEATING — OVERVIEW AND MARKET CONTEXT

### 1.1 The Growth of UFH in the UK

Underfloor heating has moved from a luxury or specialist installation to a mainstream building service in the UK over the past 20 years:

- Virtually all new-build residential properties with a specification above entry-level now include UFH, particularly on the ground floor
- Premium residential renovation: UFH specified in the majority of kitchen extensions, open-plan ground floor refurbishments, and master bathroom upgrades
- Commercial: increasingly specified in high-end retail, hotel lobbies, office reception areas, and glazed atrium spaces
- The growth has been driven by: improvements in pipe and cable technology; falling installation costs; the widespread adoption of heat pumps (which work optimally with low-temperature UFH systems); and increased consumer awareness of the comfort advantages

### 1.2 Why UFH Matters to Flooring

UFH fundamentally changes the thermal and moisture environment that floor coverings experience:

**Thermal cycling:** The floor covering experiences daily temperature changes (from ambient when the UFH is off or set back, to the operating temperature when heating is active). This is fundamentally different from a conventionally heated room where the floor is always at ambient temperature.

**Low humidity in winter:** When a heated room dries out in winter (a universal phenomenon), the low humidity causes wood to lose moisture and shrink. With UFH, the drying effect is more intense near the floor surface — the heated floor accelerates moisture evaporation from wood and from the room air. This is the primary cause of gapping in wood floors over UFH.

**Adhesive stress:** Thermal cycling stresses adhesive bonds in glue-down floor coverings. Rigid adhesives that work well in static-temperature applications may fail progressively under thermal cycling; flexible adhesives are required.

**Floor covering selection constraint:** Not all floor coverings are suitable over UFH. The thermal resistance of the floor covering must remain within limits for UFH to function efficiently; some floor covering types have specific maximum thicknesses or structural limitations over UFH.

## 2. SYSTEM TYPES — WARM WATER (HYDRONIC) UFH

### 2.1 How Hydronic UFH Works

Warm water UFH circulates heated water through a network of plastic pipes embedded in or beneath the floor structure. The pipes give off heat to the surrounding material (screed, concrete, or dry system components), which in turn heats the floor surface and the room above by radiant and convective heat transfer.

**Components:**
- **Manifold:** The central distribution unit; water from the boiler or heat pump enters and is distributed to individual circuits (zones); each circuit has a flow and return pipe; the manifold typically has flow meters and actuators for zone control
- **Pipes:** PEX-A (cross-linked polyethylene), PEX-B, PERT (Polyethylene of Raised Temperature), or multilayer composite (PEX/aluminium/PEX) pipe
- **Pipe spacing:** Typically 100–200mm between pipe runs; closer spacing gives more even heat distribution and faster response; wider spacing reduces material cost but may create temperature zebra-striping (visible as alternating warm/cool strips in the floor)
- **Insulation layer:** Rigid insulation below the UFH pipes; prevents downward heat loss; polystyrene (EPS) boards with pre-formed channels or overlays; typically 50–100mm thick
- **Screed or dry system:** The heat-emitting medium in direct contact with the pipes

### 2.2 Heat Sources for Hydronic UFH

**Gas condensing boiler:**
- The most common heat source in existing UK installations
- Flow temperature to UFH: typically 45–55°C (blended down from the boiler flow temperature by a mixing valve/manifold; the boiler itself may run at 65–80°C)
- UFH is more efficient than radiators with a condensing boiler because the lower return temperature (25–35°C from UFH) allows better condensing efficiency

**Heat pump (air source or ground source):**
- UFH is the ideal emitter for a heat pump; heat pumps produce heat at lower temperatures (35–50°C flow) most efficiently; this exactly matches UFH requirements
- Heat pumps and UFH are the designed combination for modern low-carbon heating; a heat pump feeding radiators is inefficient; a heat pump feeding UFH is optimal
- Floor coverings over heat pump UFH must have very low thermal resistance — the lower the floor resistance, the more efficiently the heat pump operates

**District heating:**
- Pre-heated water from a central plant; distributed at various temperatures depending on the network; UFH compatible if the flow temperature is appropriate

### 2.3 Water Temperature in the Screed vs Floor Surface Temperature

A critical understanding:
- The water temperature in the pipes (flow temperature) is typically 45–55°C
- The screed surrounding the pipes equilibrates to an intermediate temperature (30–40°C)
- The **floor surface temperature** is what matters for floor covering compatibility: typically 22–29°C in operation
- The surface temperature is always significantly lower than the water temperature; this is why 55°C water does not damage a wood floor
- The **maximum permitted floor surface temperature** for most floor coverings is **27°C** — this must be verified by a surface thermometer, not estimated from the flow temperature

### 2.4 Screed UFH (Wet System) — The Standard

The dominant UK residential system:
- Pipes laid on insulation boards; secured with clips, staples, or pre-formed channels
- Screed poured over the pipes to a minimum depth above the pipe crown (see Lexicon 10, Section 18)
- The screed acts as a thermal mass: it absorbs heat from the pipes; it stores that heat; it releases it slowly and evenly through the floor surface

**Response time:** Wet screeded UFH has a slow response — the screed must heat up before the room benefits; typically 2–6 hours from switching on to reaching operating temperature. This makes it unsuitable for highly intermittent use; it works best with continuous or extended-period operation.

**Thermal mass benefit:** The slow response is also an advantage — the screed stores heat; when the boiler/pump switches off, the room continues to receive heat from the stored thermal mass for hours.

### 2.5 Dry (Overlay) Hydronic Systems

Increasingly available for renovation applications where screed installation is not possible:

- **Routed timber systems:** Pipes routed into engineered timber panels or MDF overlay boards; very thin profile (typically 18–30mm total); limited thermal mass; faster response than screed
- **Aluminium heat spreader systems:** Pipes placed in aluminium-faced channels; the aluminium spreads heat across a wider area, improving efficiency without the thermal mass of screed
- **Suspended floor systems:** Pipes clipped between joists; insulation below; heat emitted upward through the floor deck above

**Performance comparison vs screed systems:**
- Lower thermal mass: faster response time but less even heat distribution
- Less efficient per unit area: aluminium spreaders help but cannot match the thermal contact of a fully embedded screed system
- Lower installation disruption in renovation contexts

## 3. SYSTEM TYPES — ELECTRIC UFH

### 3.1 Types of Electric UFH

**Mat system (electric resistance cable on mesh):**
- Pre-spaced cable woven into a mesh mat; unrolled on the subfloor and embedded in tile adhesive or self-levelling compound
- Very thin profile (2–3mm including tile adhesive bed): minimal floor build-up
- Primarily used under ceramic or porcelain tile
- Heats relatively quickly (within 30–60 minutes); suitable for intermittent use (bathroom floor)
- Higher operating cost than hydronic (direct electricity vs heat pump)

**Loose cable system:**
- Individual cable installed in custom patterns; allows different spacing for different heat output needs
- Can be embedded in screed (thin screed) or in adhesive bed
- More flexible layout than mat system; appropriate for irregular room shapes

**Electric foil system:**
- Thin foil film heating element; very flat (0.5–1.5mm)
- Laid directly on the subfloor (no adhesive bed needed for some systems); floor covering installed over it
- Used primarily under floating floors (LVT, laminate, engineered wood)
- Very fast response; the foil heats within minutes; suitable for rooms used intermittently
- The foil must be absolutely flat before floor covering installation; any ripple in the foil creates hot spots

**Heating mat under tile:**
- Mat embedded in the adhesive bed; the tile and adhesive conduct heat to the floor surface
- The adhesive bed is the primary thermal path; thick adhesive beds reduce efficiency

### 3.2 Electric vs Hydronic — The Flooring Specifier's Comparison

| Factor | Electric | Hydronic (wet) |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| Installation cost | Lower | Higher |
| Running cost | Higher (direct electricity) | Lower (boiler/heat pump) |
| Response time | Fast (minutes) | Slow (hours) |
| Best for | Bathrooms; single rooms; renovation | Whole house; new build |
| Profile thickness | Very thin (mat: 2–3mm) | Thicker (screed system) |
| Floor covering compatibility | Any compatible with surface temp | Any compatible with R-value limit |
| Repair difficulty | Relatively accessible | Pipes in screed — very difficult |
| Control precision | High | Moderate to high |

## 4. THERMAL PHYSICS — HOW UFH HEATS A ROOM

### 4.1 Radiant vs Convective Heat Transfer

UFH heats a room primarily by radiation and secondarily by convection:

**Radiant heat:**
- The warm floor surface emits infrared radiation; this radiation is absorbed by objects in the room (furniture, people, walls) and heats them directly
- Radiant heat feels comfortable because the human body absorbs it directly — the sensation is similar to sunshine warmth
- Radiant heat works even in a room with cold air; the air temperature can be 1–2°C lower than a conventionally heated room while maintaining the same perceived comfort level

**Convective heat:**
- The warm floor heats the air immediately above it; this warm air rises; cooler air replaces it; creating a gentle convective air movement
- The convective contribution is secondary in UFH but still significant
- The airflow pattern in a UFH-heated room is different from a radiator-heated room: UFH creates gentle floor-level warmth rising evenly; radiators create strong convective currents from a localised source

### 4.2 The Floor Surface Temperature and Comfort

Research (ISO 7730, ASHRAE 55) establishes the relationship between floor surface temperature and comfort:

- **Optimal floor surface temperature for occupied spaces:** 19–26°C
- **Maximum comfort limit:** 29°C; above this, floors feel uncomfortably hot underfoot
- **Minimum comfort in bare-foot areas:** 21–23°C (bathroom, bedroom)
- **The 27°C maximum for floor coverings** aligns very closely with the upper comfort limit; the floor covering protection limit and the human comfort limit reinforce each other

### 4.3 Heat Output of UFH

The heat output from a UFH system depends on:
- **ΔT (Delta T):** The temperature difference between the floor surface and the room air; greater ΔT = more heat output
- **Floor surface emissivity:** Hard surfaces (tile, stone) emit heat more readily than soft surfaces (carpet)
- **Pipe spacing:** Closer spacing distributes heat more evenly; reduces zebra-striping
- **Screed depth:** Thicker screed above the pipes increases surface temperature uniformity but reduces peak surface temperature

**Typical heat output ranges:**
- Ceramic/stone over UFH: 100–130 W/m² at maximum setting
- Engineered wood over UFH: 60–80 W/m²
- Carpet over UFH: 40–60 W/m²
- The output difference explains why UFH under carpet rarely produces a noticeably warm floor; the carpet and underlay resistance is too high

## 5. THE R-VALUE — THERMAL RESISTANCE OF FLOOR COVERINGS

### 5.1 What the R-Value Means

The **R-value (thermal resistance)** of a floor covering quantifies how much it impedes the flow of heat from the UFH system to the room. It is measured in **m²K/W** (square metres Kelvin per Watt).

The higher the R-value, the more the floor covering insulates — which is desirable for thermal comfort in a cold room, but undesirable for UFH performance because it reduces the amount of heat that reaches the room.

**The 0.15 m²K/W universal limit:**
This is the maximum combined thermal resistance (floor covering + underlay, if any) that should be installed over a UFH system for the system to function efficiently. It is the value consistently specified across:
- EN 1264-4 (hydronic UFH performance specification)
- BS EN 15377 (heat pump and UFH performance)
- Most major UFH manufacturers' installation guidelines
- Building Regulations Approved Document L (energy efficiency)

**What exceeding 0.15 m²K/W means:**
- The UFH system must run at a higher flow temperature to achieve the same room temperature
- This reduces the efficiency of heat pump systems (efficiency drops sharply as flow temperature rises)
- For boiler systems: higher energy consumption
- The floor surface temperature required to deliver adequate heating may exceed the 27°C maximum, causing floor covering damage or discomfort

### 5.2 The R-Value Formula

> **R = d ÷ λ**

Where:
- **d** = thickness of the material in metres
- **λ** = thermal conductivity of the material in W/mK

**Thermal conductivities (λ) of common flooring materials:**

| Material | Typical λ (W/mK) |
|----------|-----------------|
| Dense porcelain tile | 1.3–2.0 |
| Natural stone (granite) | 2.5–3.5 |
| Natural stone (limestone) | 1.3–2.0 |
| Concrete/screed | 1.0–1.8 |
| Ceramic tile | 0.9–1.3 |
| Engineered wood (oak) | 0.13–0.18 |
| Solid wood (oak) | 0.13–0.17 |
| Laminate | 0.10–0.14 |
| LVT (SPC, dense) | 0.17–0.22 |
| LVT (WPC, foamed) | 0.10–0.17 |
| Carpet (dense, short pile) | 0.06–0.12 |
| Carpet (thick, long pile) | 0.04–0.07 |
| PU foam underlay (25mm) | 0.04–0.06 |
| Cork underlay (3mm) | 0.05–0.07 |
| PE foam underlay (3mm) | 0.03–0.04 |
| Rubber underlay (3mm) | 0.13–0.17 |

### 5.3 Calculating Combined R-Value

For a floating floor system (floor covering + underlay):
> **R_combined = R_covering + R_underlay**

Where each R value is calculated from d/λ.

**Example — Engineered wood (floating) over UFH:**
- 15mm engineered oak: R = 0.015 ÷ 0.15 = **0.100 m²K/W**
- 3mm cork underlay: R = 0.003 ÷ 0.065 = **0.046 m²K/W**
- Combined: 0.100 + 0.046 = **0.146 m²K/W** ✓ (below 0.15 limit)

**Example — Engineered wood with foam underlay:**
- 15mm engineered oak: R = **0.100 m²K/W**
- 5mm PU foam underlay: R = 0.005 ÷ 0.045 = **0.111 m²K/W**
- Combined: 0.100 + 0.111 = **0.211 m²K/W** ✗ (significantly over limit)

**Example — Carpet over UFH:**
- 10mm twist pile carpet: R = 0.010 ÷ 0.08 = **0.125 m²K/W**
- 8mm PU foam underlay: R = 0.008 ÷ 0.045 = **0.178 m²K/W**
- Combined: 0.125 + 0.178 = **0.303 m²K/W** ✗ (double the limit)

### 5.4 The TOG System

In the UK carpet industry, thermal resistance is expressed in TOG rather than m²K/W:
- **1 TOG = 0.1 m²K/W**
- The 0.15 m²K/W limit = **1.5 TOG combined** (carpet + underlay)
- Manufacturers state TOG values for carpet and underlay products

**Checking carpet over UFH:**
- Light carpet (1.0 TOG) + thin underlay (0.5 TOG) = 1.5 TOG total ✓ — borderline acceptable
- Standard carpet (1.5 TOG) + standard underlay (1.5 TOG) = 3.0 TOG total ✗ — not suitable

## 6. R-VALUE CALCULATIONS BY FLOOR COVERING TYPE

### 6.1 Tile and Stone Over UFH — The Best Performers

Ceramic, porcelain, and natural stone have very high thermal conductivity; their R-value is negligible:

| Product | Thickness | λ | R-value |
|---------|-----------|---|---------|
| 10mm porcelain | 0.010m | 1.5 | 0.007 m²K/W |
| 20mm porcelain | 0.020m | 1.5 | 0.013 m²K/W |
| 20mm granite | 0.020m | 3.0 | 0.007 m²K/W |
| 20mm limestone | 0.020m | 1.5 | 0.013 m²K/W |

**Tile and stone over UFH leave virtually all of the 0.15 m²K/W budget available for adhesive bed; the adhesive bed itself contributes a negligible additional R-value.**

This is why tile and stone are the ideal floor coverings over UFH from a thermal efficiency perspective — the floor covering contributes almost no impediment to heat flow.

### 6.2 LVT Over UFH

LVT typically has a low R-value:

| Product | Thickness | λ | R-value |
|---------|-----------|---|---------|
| 4mm SPC LVT | 0.004m | 0.19 | 0.021 m²K/W |
| 5mm SPC LVT | 0.005m | 0.19 | 0.026 m²K/W |
| 6mm WPC LVT | 0.006m | 0.14 | 0.043 m²K/W |

LVT leaves substantial budget for underlay, but even then most underlays should be kept thin (≤2mm) to remain within 0.15 m²K/W combined.

**Glue-down LVT over UFH:** No underlay; the LVT itself is the only resistor. R-values are well within limit. **The preferred installation method for LVT over UFH.**

### 6.3 Laminate Over UFH

Laminate has a slightly higher R-value than LVT due to HDF core:

| Product | Thickness | λ | R-value |
|---------|-----------|---|---------|
| 8mm laminate | 0.008m | 0.12 | 0.067 m²K/W |
| 10mm laminate | 0.010m | 0.12 | 0.083 m²K/W |
| 12mm laminate | 0.012m | 0.12 | 0.100 m²K/W |

A 12mm laminate alone uses two-thirds of the 0.15 m²K/W budget; the underlay must be very thin:
- 12mm laminate (0.100) + 2mm cork underlay (0.031) = **0.131 m²K/W** ✓
- 12mm laminate (0.100) + 3mm PE foam (0.067) = **0.167 m²K/W** ✗

**Key message:** With laminate over UFH, always specify the thinnest compatible underlay and verify combined R-value.

### 6.4 Engineered Wood Over UFH

Engineered wood R-values are significant due to the relatively poor conductivity of wood:

| Product | Thickness | λ | R-value |
|---------|-----------|---|---------|
| 12mm engineered oak | 0.012m | 0.15 | 0.080 m²K/W |
| 14mm engineered oak | 0.014m | 0.15 | 0.093 m²K/W |
| 18mm engineered oak | 0.018m | 0.15 | 0.120 m²K/W |
| 20mm engineered oak | 0.020m | 0.15 | 0.133 m²K/W |

A 20mm engineered board uses 89% of the 0.15 budget — almost no underlay can be added:
- 20mm engineered (0.133) + 2mm cork (0.031) = **0.164 m²K/W** ✗

For 20mm engineered over UFH: **glue-down installation only** (no underlay); the adhesive bed is the interface layer and contributes negligible R-value.

**The thinner the engineered board, the more UFH-efficient the installation.** A 12mm board with 2mm cork underlay (0.080 + 0.031 = 0.111) is well within limits and can be floated.

### 6.5 Carpet Over UFH — A Detailed Assessment

Carpet and underlay combined represent the most challenging category for UFH compatibility:

**The TOG limit calculation:**
- Maximum combined: 1.5 TOG (0.15 m²K/W)
- Most carpet-underlay combinations exceed this significantly

**What works over UFH (carpet):**
- Very short pile carpet (loop pile, commercial-style): low TOG (0.7–1.0 TOG carpet)
- Combined with: thin rubber or felt underlay (0.4–0.6 TOG)
- Total: 1.1–1.6 TOG — borderline; some products certified as UFH-compatible

**What does not work over UFH:**
- Thick twist pile carpet + standard foam underlay: 2.5–4.0 TOG combined — far over limit
- Saxony or deep pile carpet: 1.5–3.0 TOG for carpet alone — exceeds limit before adding underlay

**The honest assessment:** Carpet over UFH is technically achievable but represents a significant thermal efficiency penalty. In most cases, it negates the benefit of UFH as a heating system for the space. The better approach is to specify a low-resistance floor covering (tile, stone, LVT, wood) in UFH-heated areas and carpet in areas without UFH.

**The exception:** Some clients specify carpet in bedrooms over UFH for comfort and acoustic reasons. A short pile, UFH-rated carpet (manufacturer confirmed) with a thin rubber underlay is acceptable — but the UFH output and efficiency will be significantly lower than with a hard floor covering.

## 7. MAXIMUM SURFACE TEMPERATURE — THE UNIVERSAL LIMIT

### 7.1 The 27°C Rule

**27°C is the universally accepted maximum floor surface temperature for occupied areas with floor coverings.**

This value is specified in:
- EN 1264-4 (design of water-based surface embedded heating and cooling systems)
- Most floor covering manufacturer warranties (wood, LVT, laminate, carpet)
- BS EN 15377 (heating systems in buildings)

**Why 27°C:**
- Human comfort: floors above 29°C are perceived as uncomfortably hot by most people; 27°C is a safety margin below the discomfort threshold
- Wood: above 27°C sustained, wood begins to lose moisture more rapidly than it can equilibrate; checking, cracking, and gap formation accelerate
- Adhesives: most flexible wood and LVT adhesives have a rated temperature limit; many are 28–30°C; above this, adhesive creep can occur
- PVC/LVT: plasticisers in PVC begin to migrate more actively above 27–28°C

### 7.2 Measuring Floor Surface Temperature

**Surface thermometer (non-contact IR thermometer):**
- Aim at the floor surface from 15–30cm; reads the surface temperature directly
- Fast; convenient; the standard site measurement tool
- Must be calibrated; accuracy typically ±1–2°C
- Take multiple readings at different points across the floor; particularly near pipe centres (highest temperature points)

**Type K thermocouple:**
- Contact measurement; tape probe to floor surface; read with handheld meter
- More accurate for sustained monitoring

**The thermostat is NOT a reliable surface temperature indicator:**
- Room air temperature thermostats control air temperature; the floor surface may be considerably different
- Floor temperature sensors (thermistor probes) placed in the floor are more reliable for controlling surface temperature
- Even a floor sensor may not reflect the actual surface temperature across all points if the pipe spacing is uneven

### 7.3 What to Do If Surface Temperature Exceeds 27°C

1. **Reduce the UFH flow temperature at the manifold** — the mixing valve or blending valve controls the water temperature entering the floor loops; reduce this to lower the surface temperature
2. **Check the UFH controls:** Is the thermostat set too high? Is a floor-limiting sensor installed?
3. **Check the R-value:** If the floor covering is within specification, the surface temperature should not exceed 27°C at normal flow temperatures; if it does, the UFH system flow temperature may need re-commissioning
4. **Install a floor-limiting thermostat** (see Section 10.3) if not already present

### 8.2 The Solid Wood Exception

Solid wood over UFH is technically possible but represents a high risk and requires very careful management:

**The challenges:**
- Solid wood moves significantly with MC changes; UFH creates the most extreme MC fluctuation a wood floor will experience
- In summer: high humidity; wood gains MC and expands
- In winter with UFH: very low humidity; wood loses MC rapidly (the UFH accelerates evaporation) and contracts sharply
- This extreme seasonal swing creates: wide gaps in winter; compression and potential cupping in summer

**Where solid wood over UFH can be acceptable:**
- Narrow strip or plank only (maximum 90mm width — some manufacturers say 70mm)
- Quarter-sawn only (minimum movement coefficient)
- Fully glued down (adhesive controls movement and maintains contact with warm substrate)
- In a room with humidity control (humidifier in winter; dehumidifier in summer) maintaining 45–60% RH year-round
- With a maximum surface temperature of 25°C (not 27°C) for greater safety margin

**In practice:** Most flooring professionals recommend engineered wood over UFH rather than solid; the performance difference is significant and the risk profile is much better.

## 9. UFH COMMISSIONING PROTOCOL — THE DEFINITIVE SEQUENCE

### 9.1 Why the Protocol Exists

The commissioning protocol is one of the most misunderstood and most frequently ignored aspects of UFH and flooring. Understanding why it exists makes compliance more likely:

**Before the floor covering is installed, the screed contains:**
- Construction water from the screed mix (significant quantity; can take months to dry without UFH)
- Any moisture absorbed during construction (wet trades operating above the screed; rain ingress)

**If the UFH is not commissioned before floor covering installation:**
- The floor covering is installed over a damp screed
- When the UFH is first switched on, the heat drives this moisture upward toward the floor covering
- Wood: sudden moisture arrives; boards expand rapidly; buckling, cupping, and joint failure
- Tile and stone: moisture vapour pressure weakens adhesive; debonding occurs
- LVT: moisture collects beneath the floor covering; mould

**If the commissioning is done correctly:**
- All construction moisture is driven out before the floor covering is installed
- The screed dimensions stabilise (the screed shrinks slightly as it dries and heats; this stabilisation must occur before tiles are laid or gapping will result)
- The RH test after commissioning confirms the screed is ready for floor covering

### 9.2 The Standard Commissioning Protocol

**For warm water (hydronic) systems in screed:**

**Stage 1: Initial operation (minimum 7 days after screed pour for anhydrite; minimum 28 days for sand and cement)**
1. Fill the UFH system with water; pressurise and leak-test all circuits
2. Set the flow temperature to **20°C** (do not start at operating temperature; the thermal shock of going from ambient to full operating temperature causes screed cracking)
3. Operate at 20°C for **3 consecutive days**

**Stage 2: Gradual temperature increase**
4. Increase the flow temperature by **5°C per day** until the maximum operating temperature is reached (typically 45–55°C)
5. Maintain at maximum operating temperature for **minimum 7 days** (this drives out the construction moisture; the drying rate peaks at maximum temperature)

**Stage 3: Cool-down**
6. Reduce the flow temperature by **5°C per day** back to ambient
7. Allow the floor to cool to normal room temperature

**Stage 4: Moisture testing**
8. Conduct RH testing (minimum 72 hours with calibrated hygrometers as per BS 8203)
9. If ≤75% RH: proceed to floor covering installation
10. If >75% RH: continue drying (dehumidifiers + further UFH operation); re-test

**Stage 5: Preparation for floor covering**
11. Switch UFH **off completely** 48 hours before floor covering installation begins
12. The floor must be at normal room temperature during installation (adhesive, levelling compound, and acclimatisation all require stable temperature)

**Stage 6: Restart after installation**
13. After installation and initial cure period (as specified for the floor covering type — typically 24–48 hours minimum; 7 days for tile):
14. Set flow temperature to **20°C** again
15. Increase by **5°C per day** to operating temperature
16. Do not rush this stage — the floor covering, adhesive, and grout all continue to cure during the initial days; thermal shock can cause premature failure

### 9.3 Electric UFH Commissioning

For electric mat or foil systems:
- No lengthy screed drying procedure required (no significant construction moisture in thin adhesive bed)
- After installation and adhesive/tile cure: run at low setting (30% power) for 24 hours; increase to full operation after 48 hours
- The tile or floor covering must be fully cured before any heat is applied

### 9.4 The "Contractor Left Without Commissioning" Scenario

Very common in practice: the UFH contractor has installed the pipework and screed; left the site; no commissioning protocol has been followed. The floor covering contractor arrives to find the screed has not been commissioned, has been heated intermittently (workers using the boiler for comfort heating), or has been partially commissioned.

**What the flooring contractor should do:**
1. Measure RH; if above 75%, refuse to proceed with floor covering installation
2. Document the moisture readings
3. Communicate to the client/main contractor that commissioning must be completed
4. Re-test after proper commissioning; document the pass reading
5. If the client insists on proceeding despite elevated moisture, issue a written statement declining responsibility for any moisture-related floor covering failures

## 10. CONTROLS, THERMOSTATS, AND SENSORS

### 10.1 The Importance of Correct Controls for Floor Coverings

UFH controls determine whether the floor surface temperature stays within the 27°C limit. Incorrect controls (or no controls) are a direct risk to the floor covering:

- A room air thermostat set to 22°C in a cold room may require a floor surface temperature of 30°C+ to achieve that air temperature — damaging all floor covering types
- A floor temperature limiting thermostat prevents this by cutting power once the floor surface reaches the set limit (typically 27°C)

### 10.2 Types of UFH Thermostat

**Room air thermostat:**
- Senses air temperature; controls the UFH to achieve the set air temperature
- The most common type; does not directly control floor surface temperature
- Safe for tile and stone (the floor surface temperature will naturally self-limit as the room air warms)
- Risk for wood, LVT, and laminate in a cold room or draughty space

**Floor temperature limiting thermostat (dual-input):**
- Has both a room air sensor and a floor temperature sensor
- The floor sensor is a thermistor probe placed in the floor (typically between tiles or in a conduit in the adhesive bed under LVT or wood)
- Controls the UFH to achieve the target air temperature but with a maximum floor surface temperature limit (e.g., limited to 27°C)
- **Recommended for all wood, laminate, and LVT installations over UFH**
- Also recommended for bathrooms and wet rooms where both comfort and safety are relevant

**Programmable thermostat:**
- Time-based programming; sets different temperatures at different times of day
- Combined with a floor sensor: the ideal control for occupied residential spaces
- Reduces energy consumption by lowering temperature during unoccupied periods

**Smart thermostat (Nest, Hive, Honeywell Evohome):**
- Learning and remote-control capability; integrates with smart home systems
- The floor sensor integration varies by brand; confirm floor sensor compatibility before specifying for wood/LVT applications

### 10.3 Floor Temperature Sensor Installation

**For electric systems:**
- The sensor probe (thermistor) is installed in a conduit in the adhesive bed, positioned between heating cables or between tiles
- The conduit allows the sensor to be withdrawn and replaced if it fails without lifting the floor
- Position: typically 150–200mm from the wall, between two cable runs (measures the average temperature between cables, not directly over a cable)

**For wet systems:**
- The sensor is typically placed in a sealed pocket or conduit in the screed, 50–75mm from the wall
- Position: between two pipe runs at mid-span (same logic as electric — average temperature)
- The sensor must be accessible for replacement

## 11. UFH AND CONCRETE SCREEDS

### 11.1 Screed Depth Over UFH Pipes

The depth of screed above the UFH pipe crown determines:
- Surface temperature uniformity (thicker = more even)
- Response time (thicker = slower)
- Total floor build-up

**Minimum depths:**
- Sand and cement floating screed: minimum **65mm above insulation** (or minimum **75mm above pipe crown** if pipes are at the base of the screed)
- Anhydrite flow screed: minimum **30–40mm above pipe crown** (the fluid nature gives better pipe encapsulation at lower depths)
- Some UFH manufacturers specify their own minimum depths; always follow the UFH system designer's specification

### 11.2 Screed Cracking Over UFH

Thermal cycling (expansion and contraction during UFH operation) subjects the screed to ongoing stress. To manage this:

**Reinforcement:**
- Steel mesh or polypropylene fibres: as specified in Lexicon 10, Section 5.3
- Required in all floating screeds over UFH

**Expansion joints in the screed:**
- At all room boundaries (the screed must not be continuous between rooms; it must have a joint at doorways)
- At bay boundaries: maximum bay size for tiled or stone floors over UFH: 3m × 3m
- Expansion joint positions must be coordinated with the UFH pipe layout: pipes should not cross an expansion joint without a conduit sleeve (the expansion joint must be free to move; a pipe crossing it without a sleeve will be stressed and may fail)

### 11.3 Anhydrite Screed Over UFH — Additional Considerations

- Anhydrite screeds over UFH have very good thermal performance (good contact with the pipes; low void risk due to liquid installation)
- The commissioning of anhydrite is slightly faster than sand and cement (anhydrite dries and stabilises faster in some respects)
- The preparation protocol (sanding, priming) is the same as without UFH but is even more critical because the UFH creates temperature cycling that stresses any poorly bonded floor covering

## 12. UFH AND TIMBER SUBFLOORS

### 12.1 UFH in Timber Joist Floors (Dry Systems)

When UFH is retrofitted into existing timber joist floors or installed in new-build timber upper floors:

**Between-joist systems:**
- Pipes or cables clipped between the joists from below; insulation below the pipes; heat emitted upward through the floor deck
- The floor deck (typically chipboard or plywood) acts as the primary heat-emitting surface
- Performance: significantly lower output than screed-embedded systems due to the air gap between pipes and deck and the lower thermal conductivity of timber vs screed
- The aluminium heat spreader panels are often fitted between the joists to improve lateral heat distribution

**Overlay systems (dry fix):**
- Purpose-designed pre-routed timber panels or aluminium panel systems installed on top of the existing floor
- Pipes laid in the routed channels; covered with a thin covering board
- Total additional depth: 18–30mm
- Good for renovation where screed is not possible
- Door clearances and step heights affected; must be assessed

### 12.2 Floor Covering Over Timber UFH Systems

- The timber-based system inherently adds thermal resistance: the timber deck above the pipes has a significant R-value
- Floor coverings must be thin and have low R-values
- **Tile and stone:** The ideal covering; low R-value; but the structural flexibility requirement (L/360 deflection) must be met; decoupling membrane recommended
- **LVT (glue-down):** Good choice; thin; low R-value
- **Engineered wood (thin):** Maximum 14mm; glue-down; the combined R-value of timber system + engineered wood may be close to the limit; calculate carefully
- **Carpet:** Not suitable in most cases; R-value too high

## 13. MOVEMENT JOINTS OVER UFH

### 13.1 The Critical Importance

Movement joints over UFH are more important than in non-UFH situations:
- Thermal cycling adds daily/seasonal movement to the normal structural movement
- The screed expands and contracts with temperature; the tile or stone above must be able to accommodate this
- Inadequate movement joints over UFH is the most common cause of tile debonding, cracking, and floor covering failure in newly built properties

### 13.2 Movement Joint Requirements Over UFH

| Floor covering | Maximum bay size | Perimeter joint width | Notes |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------------|-------|
| Ceramic/porcelain tile | 3m × 3m | 8–10mm | Reduced from standard 4.5m × 4.5m |
| Natural stone | 3m × 3m | 8–10mm | As tile |
| Engineered wood | Not applicable (adhesive accommodates) | 12–15mm perimeter | Movement in adhesive bond |
| LVT (floating) | 8–10m in any direction | 8–10mm | SPC; limited expansion |
| Vinyl/linoleum (glue-down) | N/A (adhesive controls) | 6–8mm | Silicone perimeter bead |

**The perimeter joint with UFH:**
- The screed will expand toward the walls when heated; the perimeter gap must accommodate this movement in addition to normal thermal movement of the floor covering
- For tile over UFH: minimum **8mm** perimeter gap (not the standard 6mm); filled with neutral-cure silicone sealant (not grout)

### 13.3 Pipe Crossings at Movement Joints

When UFH pipes must cross an expansion joint:
- Install the pipe in a protective conduit sleeve across the joint; the sleeve allows the pipe to move freely as the joint opens and closes
- The conduit extends minimum 300mm each side of the joint
- Failure to sleeve pipes at joints will result in progressive fatigue and eventual pipe failure — a very expensive repair in a finished floor

## 14. UFH IN WET ROOMS AND BATHROOMS

### 14.1 The Ideal Application

Bathroom and wet room UFH is arguably the most comfortable and practical UFH application:
- The floor warming effect is most appreciated in bare-foot wet areas
- The warm floor accelerates drying of wet surfaces after bathing
- The floor surface temperature in bathrooms is typically set higher (24–26°C) for comfort when wet

### 14.2 Specific Considerations

**Waterproofing and UFH:**
- The tanking membrane (waterproofing — see Lexicon 07, Section 9) must be installed over the UFH screed before tiling
- The UFH must be commissioned before tanking — the commissioning drives moisture out; if tanking is applied before commissioning, the moisture cannot escape and will be trapped
- Sequence: screed pour → commission UFH → RH test → tanking membrane → tile adhesive and tiles

**Controls in bathrooms:**
- Electric mat UFH under bathroom tile: a floor temperature sensor (thermistor) is embedded in the tile adhesive bed; the thermostat limits floor temperature to 27°C and allows the room to be set to a comfort temperature
- Warm water UFH in bathroom: as per other rooms; floor sensor recommended

**Tile selection:**
- Anti-slip specification particularly important in a heated bathroom floor — polished tiles that might be borderline R10 when cool may become slightly more slippery as the surface warms
- Always specify honed or micro-textured tile in bathroom floors; verify PTV wet result

## 15. UFH IN COMMERCIAL APPLICATIONS

### 15.1 Commercial UFH Considerations

In commercial applications, UFH introduces additional specification layers:

**Load capacity:**
- Commercial UFH pipe systems must be rated for the floor loading above
- In retail and hospitality: fork lift trucks, pallet movements, heavy shop fixtures — the pipe system and screed must be specified for the imposed load
- Some commercial UFH systems use higher-strength pipe (multilayer composite rather than single-polymer) for additional protection

**Controls:**
- Commercial buildings often have complex BMS (Building Management Systems)
- UFH must integrate with the BMS; zone valve actuators at the manifold controlled by the BMS
- Floor sensor data fed to the BMS to monitor floor temperature across the building

**Large area considerations:**
- Commercial floors are often very large; the 3m × 3m bay size for tiled floors over UFH creates a dense grid of movement joints
- These joints must be designed into the tile layout at the specification stage; not retrofitted
- Visible movement joint cover strips (aluminium or stainless steel) may be preferable aesthetically in commercial contexts

### 15.2 Retail and Hospitality

- Hotels: marble or tile over UFH in lobbies; the thermal mass of the stone combined with UFH provides very even, comfortable warmth
- Retail: the warm floor experience significantly improves customer dwell time in colder months
- Restaurants: tile or polished concrete; good thermal efficiency; comfort during service periods

## 16. RETROFITTING UFH

### 16.1 The Retrofit Challenge

Retrofitting UFH into an existing building is significantly more complex than new-build installation:

**Screed systems in existing buildings:**
- Raising the floor level to accommodate insulation + screed + floor covering: typically adds 100–150mm to the floor
- Door frames, stair heights, threshold levels, and kitchen unit toe-kicks all affected
- Often impractical in multi-storey properties with low ceiling heights

**Dry system retrofit:**
- The preferred approach in most renovation situations
- Overlay panels or between-joist systems as described in Section 12.1
- Total build-up: 18–30mm for overlay systems — less disruptive than screed

**Electric mat retrofit:**
- The thinnest option (2–3mm); suitable for bathrooms and small rooms
- Can be installed under new tile without significant floor level increase
- Higher running cost than hydronic; acceptable for supplementary heating in individual rooms

### 16.2 Assessment Before Retrofit

Before specifying UFH retrofit:
- Structural assessment of existing floors (particularly for wet screed systems)
- Existing floor level: measure door clearances; check threshold heights; assess implications of raising the floor
- Existing subfloor condition: moisture, flatness, structural soundness
- Heating system compatibility: does the existing boiler/heat pump have the capacity to supply additional UFH zones?
- Insulation below: without insulation, the heat goes downward into the structure rather than upward into the room; insulation efficiency is critical

## 17. PROBLEM DIAGNOSIS

### 17.1 Wood Floor Gapping (Over UFH)

**Symptoms:** Gaps developing between boards in winter; gaps closing in summer; or gaps that do not close.

**Causes:**
1. **Normal seasonal movement:** If gaps appear in winter (when UFH is running and room RH is low) and close in summer — this is normal; the floor is responding correctly to humidity change; manage with humidification (see Section 18)
2. **Gaps that do not close in summer:** The floor was installed at too low an MC or the MC swing is too extreme; possibly UFH was not commissioned before installation; permanent gap
3. **Boards not glued down:** A floating wood floor over UFH where glue-down was required; boards move as a panel in large jumps rather than individual board movement

**Action:**
- Seasonal gaps: humidification; maintain RH 45–60% year-round; educate the client
- Permanent gaps: investigate cause; if due to commissioning failure, this is a defect; re-laying may be required

### 17.2 Tile Cracking or Debonding Over UFH

**Symptoms:** Tiles cracking along joints or mid-tile; or hollow/debonded tiles appearing weeks or months after installation.

**Causes:**
1. **Movement joints omitted or too small:** Thermal cycling stresses exceed the adhesive bond or tile strength; tiles crack or debond; the most common cause
2. **Insufficient adhesive coverage:** Unsupported tile areas crack under thermal stress; coverage below 95% creates high-stress points
3. **UFH switched on too quickly after installation:** Adhesive not fully cured when thermal stress applied; adhesive fails
4. **Rigid adhesive (C1):** Not rated for thermal cycling; bond fails progressively
5. **Commissioning failure:** Screed not commissioned before tiling; construction moisture drove adhesive failure when UFH was first switched on

**Action:**
- Investigate movement joint provision; map the cracked/hollow tiles and correlate with movement joint positions
- Lift samples; assess adhesive coverage; check adhesive classification
- Review commissioning records

### 17.3 LVT Expansion/Tenting Over UFH

As Lexicon 05, Section 18.2 — tenting in summer when the floor is not heating; the LVT expanded more than expected.

**Additional UFH-specific cause:** The underlay was too thick (high R-value); the LVT surface temperature fluctuated more than expected between heating on/off cycles; the repeated expansion/contraction across many cycles caused the floating panel to slowly migrate and eventually buckle.

**Action:** Check the underlay R-value; confirm maximum bay size was not exceeded; check perimeter gaps.

### 17.4 UFH System Not Heating Adequately (Floor Covering Barrier)

**Symptoms:** The UFH system is operating correctly but the room does not reach the target temperature; or the floor surface never feels warm.

**Cause:** Floor covering thermal resistance too high; the R-value of the installed floor covering (or floor covering + underlay) significantly exceeds 0.15 m²K/W.

**Diagnosis:** 
- Calculate the actual R-value of the installed system from product data sheets
- If > 0.15 m²K/W: the floor covering is the problem
- Confirm by checking whether an adjacent room with tile or stone heats more readily

**Action:**
- Increase UFH flow temperature (if within system limits and if surface temperature does not exceed 27°C)
- Or: replace the floor covering with a lower-R-value option
- Educate the client that the floor covering choice was the cause

## 18. HUMIDITY MANAGEMENT WITH UFH

### 18.1 Why UFH Creates a Low-Humidity Environment in Winter

When UFH heats a room in winter:
- The heated floor warms the air above it
- Warm air has a higher capacity to hold moisture
- If the absolute moisture content of the air remains constant (no moisture is added to the room), the relative humidity drops as the temperature rises
- In winter, external air is cold and holds very little moisture; heated and dried air from UFH reduces interior RH dramatically
- Typical winter RH in a centrally heated UK home: 30–40% RH
- With UFH and no humidification: can be as low as 20–25% RH

### 18.2 Effects of Low Humidity on Floor Coverings

**Wood floors:**
- MC drops as RH falls; boards lose moisture and shrink; gaps open between boards
- At 25% RH, solid 150mm oak boards may shrink by 1.5–2mm per board — cumulative across a room, very visible gaps
- Checked (surface cracking) and cupped boards can result from severe or rapid moisture loss

**Laminate:**
- Less sensitive than solid wood due to HDF core stability; but very low RH (below 30%) can cause click joints to stress and open

**LVT:**
- Largely unaffected by humidity changes (PVC has no hygroscopic response); the subfloor may be affected, however

### 18.3 Humidification Solutions

**Standalone humidifier:**
- Portable humidifier placed in the room; monitors RH and adds moisture as needed
- Size to room volume; run continuously in winter in wood-floored spaces
- Maintain RH 45–65%

**Whole-house humidification:**
- Integrated into the central heating/ventilation system (MVHR — Mechanical Ventilation with Heat Recovery)
- MVHR systems recover heat from exhaust air; they also recover moisture; significantly reduces winter dryness compared to conventional extract ventilation
- Increasingly specified in new-build; the most effective whole-house humidity solution

**The RH monitoring requirement:**
- A hygrometer should be installed in any wood-floored room with UFH
- The customer/occupant should monitor RH and activate humidification when RH drops below 40%
- Some smart thermostats include humidity monitoring; this should be specified where wood floors are present

## 19. BRITISH AND EUROPEAN STANDARDS

**EN 1264-1 to 1264-5 — Water based surface embedded heating and cooling systems:**
- The comprehensive European standard for hydronic UFH design, installation, and performance
- Part 4 (EN 1264-4): installation; includes the 0.15 m²K/W maximum thermal resistance requirement for floor coverings
- Part 5 (EN 1264-5): heating and cooling surfaces embedded in floors; performance calculations

**BS EN 15377 — Heating systems in buildings — Design of embedded water based surface heating and cooling systems:**
- UK/European standard for design; references EN 1264

**BS EN 14037 — Ceiling radiant heating panels:**
- Referenced for design comparison; confirms the UFH thermal physics approach

**Approved Document L (Building Regulations 2013, as amended 2021) — Conservation of fuel and power:**
- Specifies that heating systems must be energy-efficient; UFH with heat pump is a preferred combination; floor covering R-value affects system efficiency and therefore compliance

**BS 8204-1:2003+A1:2009 — In-situ floorings — Concrete bases and screeds:**
- Commissioning and screed depth requirements for UFH screed systems referenced in this standard

**ISO 7730 — Ergonomics of the thermal environment:**
- Specifies comfortable floor surface temperatures (19–29°C); the basis for the 27°C maximum floor covering guideline

**Manufacturer installation guidelines:**
- For each floor covering type, the specific manufacturer's UFH guidelines govern warranty and recommended practice
- Always request and retain the manufacturer's UFH-specific installation document before proceeding
