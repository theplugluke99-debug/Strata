# STRATA FLOORING — DIAGNOSIS DECISION TREE
## Symptom → Cause → Solution
### Version 1.0 | Strata Premium Flooring Platform | UK Market

---

> **Purpose:** This guide enables Strata's AI assistant to diagnose flooring problems accurately when customers describe symptoms. It is structured around what customers actually say — the symptom they observe — and works through the diagnostic logic to the most probable cause and the appropriate solution. The guide covers every major floor covering type and every common failure mode. For each problem, it provides: the diagnostic questions to ask, the most likely causes in order of probability, how to distinguish between them, and what action is required. Cross-references to the relevant lexicon section are provided for deeper technical context.

---

## HOW TO USE THIS GUIDE

**Step 1:** Identify the floor covering type from the customer's description
**Step 2:** Find the symptom category
**Step 3:** Follow the diagnostic questions to narrow the cause
**Step 4:** Deliver the solution with appropriate confidence and caveats

**Confidence language:**
- **"This is almost certainly..."** — when the symptom-cause match is highly specific
- **"This is most likely..."** — when the cause is probable but not certain
- **"This could be..."** — when multiple causes are possible and further investigation is needed
- **"I'd recommend having a professional assess this because..."** — when the diagnosis requires physical inspection

## SECTION A — CARPET PROBLEMS

### A1. Carpet is rippling or has waves/ridges

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "When did you first notice the rippling — soon after installation, or has it developed over time?"
2. "What time of year is it most noticeable?"
3. "Was the carpet professionally fitted with a power stretcher, or was it a DIY installation?"

**Most likely causes (in order of probability):**

**Cause 1: Insufficient stretching during installation (most common)**
- The carpet was positioned rather than properly power-stretched
- *Distinguishing sign:* Rippling is present year-round; not seasonal; appeared within months of installation
- *Solution:* Re-stretch using a power stretcher. This is a professional job — knee kicker alone is inadequate for a room over 4.5m. Contact the installer; if within warranty, this should be remedied at no cost.

**Cause 2: Seasonal humidity (very common, especially spring)**
- Jute-backed carpet absorbs moisture in humid conditions and expands
- *Distinguishing sign:* Rippling appears in spring/early summer when heating reduces and humidity rises; disappears in winter when heating returns
- *Solution:* In most cases this resolves naturally when humidity drops. If persistent: have the carpet re-stretched in the most humid conditions to prevent recurrence.

**Cause 3: Delamination of secondary backing**
- The latex bond between primary and secondary backing has failed
- *Distinguishing sign:* Press on the ridge — if you can feel two separate layers moving independently, delamination has occurred
- *Solution:* Replacement. Delamination is a manufacturing defect; contact the supplier with evidence (photos; date of installation). Warranty claim likely.

**Cause 4: Underlay migration**
- Underlay has shifted, creating a lump beneath the carpet
- *Distinguishing sign:* The ridge is firm to the touch; pressing it down reveals a hard lump rather than a soft ripple
- *Solution:* Lift the carpet at the nearest edge; reposition and secure the underlay; re-lay and re-stretch.

*See Lexicon 01, Section 14.1 for full technical context.*

---

### A2. Carpet is shedding fluff / small fibres on the surface

**Most likely cause:** Normal shedding from a new carpet
- Cut pile carpets (especially wool and wool-blend) shed loose processing fibres for the first 4–8 weeks
- *Solution:* Vacuum frequently — daily for the first four weeks with a suction-only vacuum (no beater bar initially). Shedding diminishes to negligible levels. Not a defect.

**If shedding persists beyond 3 months:**
- May indicate poor tuft withdrawal force — a manufacturing quality issue
- *Solution:* Contact supplier; provide a sample; request technical assessment. Warranty claim may be appropriate.

*See Lexicon 01, Section 14.2.*

---

### A3. Carpet has light and dark patches / looks different from different angles

**This is almost certainly pile reversal (watermarking / shading).**
- Individual tufts have permanently changed orientation, creating optical light/dark patches
- *Distinguishing sign:* The patches are not stains (they don't wipe off); the carpet looks different when viewed from different directions
- *Important:* This is NOT a manufacturing defect. It is an inherent characteristic of cut pile carpet, particularly saxony and velvet constructions.
- *Solution:* There is no remedy. This is permanent. The customer needs to understand this is inherent behaviour. For future reference: recommend loop pile or dense twist pile constructions if this is unacceptable.

*See Lexicon 01, Section 14.4.*

---

### A4. Carpet has a stain that won't come out

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "What caused the stain?"
2. "Has it been cleaned already — and if so, with what product?"
3. "Is the stain a distinct mark or a generalised discolouration?"

**Cause map by stain type:**

| Stain type | Best treatment | What not to do |
|------------|----------------|----------------|
| Red wine (fresh) | Blot; cold water; specialist wine remover | Do not rub; do not use hot water |
| Red wine (dried) | Warm water + enzyme cleaner; hydrogen peroxide on light carpet | Do not use bleach on wool |
| Coffee/tea | Cold water blotting; neutral detergent | Do not scrub |
| Grease/oil | Dry cleaning fluid; specialist degreaser | Do not use water first (spreads oil) |
| Pet urine | Enzyme cleaner (essential — breaks down uric acid); then neutralise | Do not use ammonia (smells like urine; attracts repeat soiling) |
| Ink (ballpoint) | Isopropyl alcohol; dab and blot | Do not rub |
| Chewing gum | Freeze with aerosol; peel; dry cleaning solvent for residue | Do not pull warm gum (spreads) |
| Bleach mark | Cannot be reversed — colour is gone | No treatment restores bleach damage |

**Wicking (stain reappears after drying):**
- The stain has penetrated to the backing or underlay; as the carpet dries, it draws the stain back to the surface
- *Solution:* Saturate the area repeatedly with cold water; extract with a wet vac or press with thick towels; repeat until clean water runs clear. May need to lift carpet and treat underlay/subfloor.

*See Lexicon 01, Sections 14.5 and 15.2.*

---

### A5. Carpet pile is flattened / matted in traffic areas

**Most likely cause:** Normal wear in an under-specified carpet
- *Distinguishing sign:* Flat areas correspond exactly with the traffic path; no pile recovery when pile is raked
- *Cause:* The carpet was under-specified for the usage level; polypropylene cut pile is particularly prone
- *Solution:* If recent: regular vacuuming and pile raking can partially restore. If established: the carpet has reached the end of its useful life in those areas.
- *Prevention:* Specify higher density carpet (minimum EN 1307 Class 23 for hallways; nylon or wool-nylon fibre).

## SECTION B — WOOD AND ENGINEERED WOOD PROBLEMS

### B1. Gaps between wood floor boards (floor is gapping)

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "When did the gaps appear — recently after installation, or has the floor been down for a while?"
2. "Do the gaps appear in winter and close in summer, or are they present year-round?"
3. "Do you have underfloor heating?"

**Cause 1: Normal seasonal movement (most common)**
- In winter: heating reduces interior humidity; wood loses moisture and shrinks; gaps open
- In summer: humidity rises; wood gains moisture; gaps close
- *Distinguishing sign:* Gaps are consistent with winter/summer cycle
- *Solution:* This is normal. Humidify the room in winter to maintain 45–60% RH. If the seasonal gap is very wide (>2mm on a standard plank), consider a humidifier.

**Cause 2: Installed too wet / insufficient acclimatisation**
- Boards were installed above their equilibrium MC; as they dried, they shrank
- *Distinguishing sign:* Gaps appeared shortly after installation (within first year); may not fully close in summer
- *Solution:* If gaps are year-round permanent: the floor was incorrectly acclimatised. This is an installation defect. Contact the installer.

**Cause 3: UFH drying the floor excessively**
- UFH in winter without humidification creates very low RH; dramatic MC loss in wood
- *Distinguishing sign:* Customer has UFH; gaps are very wide in winter; surface checking may also be present
- *Solution:* Install a room humidifier; programme UFH to gradual temperature changes; maintain 45–60% RH.

**Cause 4: Floating floor — no expansion gap / panel too large**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Customer has a floating floor; gaps open internally between boards (the floor has expanded outward and is pulling boards apart internally)
- *Solution:* Check perimeter gaps; if insufficient, removing skirting to free the expansion gap may allow boards to relax back. Professional assessment required.

*See Lexicon 02, Section 17.1; Lexicon 11, Sections 17.1 and 18.*

---

### B2. Wood floor boards are cupping (edges higher than centre)

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "Is the floor glued down, nailed, or floating?"
2. "When did cupping appear?"
3. "Has there been any water exposure — flooding, steam cleaning, wet mopping?"
4. "Have you tested the moisture content of the subfloor?"

**Cause 1: Moisture differential between face and back of board**
- The most common cause; moisture from the subfloor reaching the board back while the face dries out
- *Distinguishing sign:* Cupping is most pronounced near the edges of the room (closer to the moisture source); boards are concave on the face (edges higher)
- *Solution:* Identify and eliminate the moisture source; allow the floor to dry and equalise. DO NOT SAND A CUPPED FLOOR — sanding the high edges will leave a permanently crowned floor when boards recover.

**Cause 2: Surface moisture (wet cleaning, flooding)**
- Excessive water on the face dried faster than the back; face contracted; cup
- *Distinguishing sign:* Cupping appeared after a flood, steam cleaning, or period of wet mopping
- *Solution:* Dry the room thoroughly; use dehumidifiers; allow weeks to months for equalisation before assessing whether sanding is needed.

**Critical rule:** Never sand a cupped floor. Wait for full stabilisation (weeks to months). Sand only when the floor is demonstrably flat.

*See Lexicon 02, Section 17.2.*

---

### B3. Wood floor is squeaking or creaking

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "Is the squeak localised to one area or widespread?"
2. "Is it a glued-down, nailed, or floating floor?"
3. "Can you press the squeaking board firmly down — does the squeak stop?"

**Cause 1: Nails have worked loose (secret nailed floors)**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Localised squeak; pressing the board firmly eliminates it temporarily; felt on the face of the board at the nail location
- *Solution:* Drive a screw through the face adjacent to the squeak point; fill with matching timber plug.

**Cause 2: Board-to-board friction (floating floors)**
- The T&G joint between boards is creating noise as boards flex independently
- *Distinguishing sign:* Floating floor; squeak from a join between two boards; pressing down across the joint reduces it
- *Solution:* Minor: apply powdered graphite or talcum powder into the joint. Significant: may require professional assessment; the joint may need to be lubricated or the floor partially re-laid.

**Cause 3: Hollow subfloor beneath the board**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Squeak is in the subfloor itself, not the wood; tapping the floor reveals a hollow sound in the squeaking area
- *Solution:* If accessible from below: drive screws up through the subfloor into the board (pulling board down to subfloor). If not accessible: inject construction adhesive through a drilled hole.

**Cause 4: Debris in expansion gap**
- Small stones, grit, or off-cuts of wood in the perimeter expansion gap prevent free movement; boards grind against them
- *Solution:* Remove skirting at the squeaking wall; clear the gap; replace skirting.

*See Lexicon 02, Section 17.5.*

---

### B4. Wood floor surface is scratched or dull

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "Is the finish oil, wax, or lacquer?"
2. "Are the scratches surface scratches or are they cutting through to bare wood?"

**Surface scratches in lacquered floor:**
- *Solution:* Surface scratches in lacquer appear as white marks. Minor: a floor repair pen or coloured wax may disguise them. Significant: localised re-lacquering is difficult to match invisibly. Full re-sand and re-lacquer of the room is the professional solution.

**Surface scratches in oiled/hardwax oil floor:**
- *Solution:* Sand the affected area lightly; apply matching oil; blend into surrounding floor. Oil finishes are the most repairable — spot repair is genuinely effective and invisible.

**General dullness (entire floor):**
- *Cause:* Build-up of cleaning product residue; oil finish is depleted; lacquer film is worn to haze
- *Solution (oiled):* Clean thoroughly with the manufacturer's recommended cleaner; apply a maintenance oil coat
- *Solution (lacquered):* Clean; screen/buff the floor; apply a maintenance lacquer coat — this can often be done without full sanding if the lacquer has not worn through to bare wood

*See Lexicon 02, Sections 18 and 19.*

---

### B5. Engineered wood floor has delaminating layers (layers separating)

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "Is the floor floating or glued down?"
2. "Has there been any moisture exposure?"
3. "How old is the floor?"

**Cause 1: Manufacturing defect (inadequate adhesive bond)**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Delamination affecting multiple boards in a random pattern; no history of moisture; within warranty period
- *Solution:* This is a warranty claim. Photograph affected boards; contact the supplier with the purchase documentation. The manufacturer is liable.

**Cause 2: Moisture damage**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Delamination concentrated near areas of known moisture exposure (near patio doors, near a bathroom, over a damp subfloor)
- *Solution:* Identify and eliminate moisture source; affected boards must be replaced. The moisture source must be resolved first.

**Cause 3: Thermal cycling damage (over UFH)**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Delamination in a floor over UFH that was not rated for it, or where surface temperature has exceeded 27°C
- *Solution:* Check whether the product was approved for UFH; check surface temperature. If the UFH exceeded the product's temperature rating, the manufacturer's warranty is void.

*See Lexicon 03, Section 19.1.*

## SECTION C — LAMINATE PROBLEMS

### C1. Laminate floor is buckling / boards lifting from centre

**This is almost certainly an expansion gap problem.**

- *Cause:* The floor expanded (humidity; temperature) and had no room to expand at the perimeter; the energy went upward
- *Diagnostic question:* "Have you checked the gap between the floor and the skirting board?"
- *Solution:*
  1. Remove skirting boards or beading at the affected wall
  2. Check the gap — if the floor is pressed hard against the wall with no gap, this confirms the cause
  3. If boards are not permanently deformed: removing the skirting to free the gap may allow the floor to settle back flat over 2–7 days
  4. If boards are permanently bowed: affected section must be replaced with correct expansion gaps

*See Lexicon 04, Section 17.1.*

---

### C2. Laminate has raised edges at joints (lipping/edge swell)

**This is almost certainly moisture damage.**

- *Cause:* Water has penetrated the click joint and swollen the HDF core at the board edge; the swelling is not reversible
- *Distinguishing sign:* Raised ridge along the joint line; feels firm; board edge may be slightly lighter in colour
- *Likely source:* Wet mopping; spillage that was not cleaned immediately; bathroom or kitchen moisture ingress; damp subfloor
- *Solution:* The damage is permanent. Affected boards must be replaced. Before replacing:
  1. Identify the moisture source
  2. If wet mopping: change cleaning habits
  3. If subfloor moisture: test RH; apply DPM before re-laying
  4. If in a kitchen or bathroom: specify moisture-resistant core laminate or switch to LVT

*See Lexicon 04, Section 17.2.*

---

### C3. Laminate has gaps opening between boards

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "Are the gaps seasonal (open in winter, close in summer) or permanent?"
2. "How large are the gaps — hairline or clearly visible?"

**Seasonal gaps (hairline, close in summer):** Normal behaviour; acceptable
**Permanent gaps (year-round):** Installation problem

- *Cause of permanent gaps:* Boards were not fully clicked together during installation; or the floating panel is too large without intermediate joints; or the RH in the room is consistently too low
- *Solution:* Seasonal: maintain room humidity 45–60%. Permanent: if accessible, attempt to close gaps with a pull bar. If widespread: professional re-lay required.

*See Lexicon 04, Section 17.3.*

---

### C4. Laminate surface is scratched

**The scratches cannot be repaired.** This is the fundamental limitation of laminate.
- The melamine wear layer is damaged; there is no sanding option
- *Temporary cosmetic fix:* Laminate repair pen (colour-matched wax pencil); conceals minor scratches
- *Long-term:* If scratching is widespread, the floor has reached end of life and requires replacement
- *Prevention advice:* Felt pads under all furniture; entrance matting to trap grit; consider an AC4 or AC5 product for the next installation

## SECTION D — LVT / VINYL PROBLEMS

### D1. LVT floor is bubbling / has raised blisters

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "Is the floor glued down or floating (click)?"
2. "Are the blisters distributed across the floor or concentrated in particular areas?"
3. "Was a roller used during installation?"

**Glue-down LVT — blistering:**

**Cause 1: Moisture from the subfloor (most common)**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Blisters contain a milky fluid or feel damp when cut open; concentrated near drains, walls, or areas of known moisture risk
- *Solution:* Cut open blisters; allow to dry; test subfloor RH. If elevated: epoxy DPM required before re-bonding. Replace affected tiles/planks after DPM application.

**Cause 2: Inadequate roller or no rolling during installation**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Air-filled blisters; distributed randomly; appeared within days of installation
- *Solution:* In early stages, rolling with a 68kg roller may re-bond some areas. Persistent blisters: lift and re-lay with full rolling protocol.

**Cause 3: Wrong adhesive or adhesive applied incorrectly (too wet or too dry)**
- *Solution:* Lift affected area; assess adhesive condition; re-lay with correct adhesive at correct tack.

*See Lexicon 05, Section 18.1; Lexicon 06, Section 18.1.*

---

### D2. LVT floor has yellow or brown staining

**This is almost certainly rubber discolouration.**
- Rubber-backed mats or rubber furniture feet have reacted with the PVC surface
- *Distinguishing sign:* The discolouration pattern exactly matches the shape of a mat or rubber foot; appears over weeks to months of contact
- *Important:* This staining is permanent and cannot be removed. The discolouration is a chemical reaction within the PVC surface.
- *Solution:* Remove the rubber item immediately. For existing staining: no reliable remedy; replacement of affected tiles/planks is the only solution.
- *Prevention:* Only use felt pads or purpose-made LVT/vinyl mats (PVC-backed or fabric-backed). Never rubber-backed mats.

*See Lexicon 05, Section 18.4.*

---

### D3. LVT click floor is tenting / buckling (floating)

**Same cause as laminate buckling (Section C1) — insufficient expansion gap.**
- For SPC LVT: lower thermal expansion than laminate means this is less common but still occurs
- *Solution:* As per laminate; free the expansion gap; floor should settle

*See Lexicon 05, Section 18.2.*

---

### D4. Vinyl sheet has bubbles or is lifting at edges

**Lifting edges:**
- *Cause:* Insufficient adhesive at perimeter; adhesive failure from moisture; thermal movement in perimeter-bonded only installation
- *Solution:* Re-bond with appropriate vinyl adhesive; press and weight until cured

**Bubbles in field:**
- *Cause:* Air pockets from inadequate rolling at installation; or moisture blistering (as above)
- *Solution:* Minor air bubbles: try rolling with a 68kg roller; if recently laid, may re-bond. Established bubbles: lift and re-lay.

*See Lexicon 06, Section 18.1.*

---

## SECTION E — TILE AND STONE PROBLEMS

### E1. Tiles are hollow when tapped (drum sound)

**This is debonding — tiles have lost their adhesive bond to the subfloor.**

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "How many tiles are hollow — isolated or widespread?"
2. "Has there been any water exposure or UFH installed?"
3. "How old is the tiled floor?"

**Cause 1: Inadequate adhesive coverage (most common)**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Hollow tiles appear randomly across the floor; no pattern linked to moisture or movement
- *Solution:* Isolated hollow tiles: inject epoxy adhesive through a drilled hole; may stabilise. Widespread debonding: re-tiling required.

**Cause 2: Moisture under the tiles (adhesive failure from damp)**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Hollow tiles concentrated near moisture sources (drains, external walls, over a damp slab)
- *Solution:* Identify and resolve moisture source; test subfloor RH; apply DPM if required; re-tile.

**Cause 3: Movement joints omitted (tiles debonded from thermal or moisture stress)**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Hollow tiles at regular intervals in a large floor; tiles may be cracking
- *Solution:* Professional inspection; determine movement joint requirements; re-tile with correct expansion provision.

*See Lexicon 07, Section 18.1.*

---

### E2. Tiles are cracking

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "Is the crack in one tile only or do multiple tiles show cracks in the same alignment?"
2. "Is the crack at the grout joint (edge crack) or through the tile body (mid-tile crack)?"
3. "Are the cracks appearing over UFH?"

**Cause 1: Impact (single tile; random crack pattern)**
- *Solution:* Replace the individual tile. Match the calibre and shade; check with the supplier whether the same batch is still available.

**Cause 2: Reflective cracking from a subfloor crack**
- *Distinguishing sign:* The crack in the tile follows a straight line that continues across the grout joint into adjacent tiles; there is a crack or joint in the subfloor below at the same location
- *Solution:* Remove and replace tiles; fill the subfloor crack with flexible epoxy filler; reinstall with a movement joint above the subfloor crack.

**Cause 3: No movement joints (compressive cracking)**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Tiles cracking in a pattern consistent with thermal or moisture expansion; cracking may follow a grid pattern or occur at bay boundaries
- *Solution:* This is a specification/installation failure. Professional assessment; re-tiling with movement joints is required.

**Cause 4: UFH — thermal cycling stress**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Cracking in a UFH-heated floor; cracks at grout lines; debonding may also be present
- *Solution:* Check whether C2S2 adhesive was used; check whether movement joints at 3m × 3m were installed; these are the primary causes of tile failure over UFH.

*See Lexicon 07, Section 18.2.*

---

### E3. Grout is cracking or falling out

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "Is the cracking at specific locations (perimeter, doorways, internal corners) or throughout the floor?"
2. "Was the floor recently tiled or has it been down for years?"

**Cause 1: Grout at a movement joint location (where sealant should be)**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Cracking concentrated at perimeter edges, internal corners, or floor-to-wall junctions
- *Solution:* Remove the grout from these locations entirely. These are movement joints — they must be filled with flexible silicone sealant, not grout. Grout at a movement zone will always crack.

**Cause 2: Building movement or substrate movement**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Cracking along a consistent line through the floor; associated with a hairline crack in the tile
- *Solution:* Structural assessment if significant; inject and stabilise the subfloor; re-grout or introduce a movement joint.

**Cause 3: Inadequate adhesive cure before grouting**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Grout cracks appearing within weeks of installation; floor may also have hollow tiles
- *Solution:* Re-grout after allowing the adhesive to fully cure; address any hollow tiles.

*See Lexicon 07, Section 18.3.*

---

### E4. Natural stone has dull patches / etching marks

**This is almost certainly acid etching.**

- *Cause:* An acidic substance (lemon juice, vinegar, wine, cola, some cleaning products) has dissolved the calcium carbonate surface of marble or limestone
- *Distinguishing sign:* The dull patches correspond to areas of spillage; the patches feel smooth (no surface texture change); they cannot be wiped off — they are dissolved stone
- *Important:* This is not a stain. It is physical material removal. It cannot be cleaned away.
- *Solution:* The only remedy is mechanical — professional re-polishing or re-honing of the affected area. For a small area: a stone restoration specialist can hone and re-polish just the affected patch. For widespread etching: the whole floor may need refinishing.
- *Prevention going forward:* Coasters; immediate cleaning of any spill; pH-neutral stone cleaners only.

*See Lexicon 08, Section 18.1.*

---

### E5. Natural stone has a white powdery deposit on the surface

**This is efflorescence.**

- *Cause:* Soluble salts in the adhesive, grout, or subfloor are migrating to the surface dissolved in moisture; when the moisture evaporates, the salts crystallise on or just beneath the stone surface
- *Distinguishing sign:* White powdery or crystalline deposit; brushes off easily but returns; most common in the first weeks/months after installation
- *Solution:*
  1. Allow the installation to fully cure and dry (4–6 weeks) — efflorescence often resolves as construction moisture dries out
  2. Brush off deposits with a dry brush; vacuum
  3. If persistent: use a specialist efflorescence remover (pH-neutral; not acid on limestone/marble)
  4. If very persistent: investigate the moisture source in the subfloor

*See Lexicon 07, Section 18.5; Lexicon 08.*

---

### E6. Stone floor surface is stained

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "What type of stone is it?"
2. "What caused the stain?"
3. "Has the floor been sealed?"

**Treatment by stain type (natural stone):**

| Stain | Treatment | Caution |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| Oil (food, cooking) | Poultice: chalk powder + acetone; leave 24–48 hours; remove | Test in inconspicuous area first |
| Red wine | Poultice with hydrogen peroxide (light stone) or specialist remover | Never acid on limestone/marble |
| Rust/iron | Specialist rust remover (oxalic acid) — ONLY on granite/sandstone | Never acid on limestone, marble, travertine |
| Coffee | Enzyme cleaner; or hydrogen peroxide on light stone | Test first |
| Paint | Plastic scraper (do not scratch surface); then specialist paint remover compatible with stone | Test compatibility |
| Permanent marker | Specialist remover; acetone on hard stone | Test first |

**If the stone is unsealed:** The staining agent has penetrated into the body of the stone. Poultice treatment draws it back out. Seal after treatment.

**If the stone was sealed:** The sealant has failed to protect (sealant depleted or the staining agent exceeded the sealant's resistance). Re-seal after treatment.

*See Lexicon 08, Section 18.1.*

## SECTION F — RESIN AND SPECIALIST FLOOR PROBLEMS

### F1. Resin floor has blisters or bubbles

**Cause 1: Moisture osmosis from subfloor (most common)**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Blisters contain milky or clear fluid; concentrated in areas of known moisture ingress
- *Solution:* Cut and dry affected area; test subfloor CM/RH. Widespread blistering from moisture: the entire system may need to be removed and reapplied with an epoxy DPM.

**Cause 2: Air bubbles from inadequate de-aeration**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Pinholes or small uniform bubbles throughout; appeared immediately after installation
- *Solution:* Localised: sand and flood-coat with thin resin to fill pinholes. Widespread: full repair coat required.

*See Lexicon 09, Section 18.1.*

---

### F2. Resin floor is peeling or delaminating

**Cause:** The resin did not bond properly to the substrate.
- *Most likely reason:* Laitance was not removed (most common); substrate was contaminated (oil, dust); primer was omitted; adhesive failure from moisture
- *Distinguishing sign:* Peeling sheets of resin; concrete visible beneath; the back of the peeled resin is often smooth (the bond was at the surface layer only)
- *Solution:* Full removal of the failing system; proper substrate preparation (grinding/shotblasting); DPM if moisture; reinstallation. This is a significant remedial project.

*See Lexicon 09, Section 18.2.*

---

### F3. Polished concrete is stained or scratched

**Staining:** Apply a penetrating concrete-specific stain remover; re-seal after treatment. Deeply embedded stains may require localised grinding.

**Scratching:** Minor surface scratches: machine burnish with a fine diamond pad; re-seal. Deep scratches through the sealer: localised grind, hone, and re-polish the affected area; re-seal.

**Dullness (the whole floor has lost its sheen):** This is normal — the sealer has worn. Solution: machine buff/burnish; apply a new maintenance coat of compatible sealer or impregnator. Full re-polish is only required every 5–10 years in normal residential use.

*See Lexicon 09, Section 9.*

---

## SECTION G — SUBFLOOR PROBLEMS (CAUSING FLOOR COVERING FAILURES)

### G1. Floor covering feels springy / bouncy underfoot

**This is a subfloor structural issue, not a floor covering issue.**
- *Cause:* The subfloor (joists, beams, concrete) is deflecting under load beyond acceptable limits
- *Distinguishing signs:* The springiness is felt everywhere, not just at one point; the floor covering itself is not the problem
- *Solution:* Structural assessment required. In timber floors: additional noggins, sistering of joists, or additional support from below. Do not install a rigid floor covering (tile, stone) over a springy subfloor — it will crack.

---

### G2. Floor covering is showing ridges / lines through the surface

**This is telegraphing — the subfloor imperfection is transmitting through the floor covering.**
- *Most common causes:* Existing tile grout lines showing through vinyl; board joints in a timber subfloor showing through LVT; existing embossed vinyl showing through new vinyl
- *Solution:* The subfloor was not adequately prepared. Options:
  1. Lift the floor covering; apply SLC to the subfloor to achieve flatness within tolerance; re-lay
  2. Apply embossing leveller over an existing embossed surface before re-laying

*See Lexicon 10, Section 19.3.*

---

### G3. Floor covering is failing / adhesive not holding

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "How old is the installation?"
2. "Has there been any moisture, flooding, or condensation?"
3. "Was the floor in a bathroom, kitchen, or basement?"

**Most likely causes in order:**
1. Subfloor moisture above the permitted limit at time of installation
2. Laitance not removed from concrete before adhesive application
3. Wrong adhesive for the floor covering type
4. Contaminated substrate (oil, dust, old adhesive residue)
5. Adhesive applied at wrong tack (too wet or too dry)

*Solution:* Lift the floor covering; assess the subfloor condition; address the specific cause; re-install with correct preparation and adhesive.

*See Lexicon 10 throughout.*

## SECTION H — UNDERFLOOR HEATING PROBLEMS

### H1. Floor over UFH is gapping or cracking

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "What type of floor covering is it?"
2. "Was the UFH commissioned (gradual heating and drying cycle) before the floor was installed?"
3. "Is a floor temperature sensor and limiting thermostat installed?"

**Cause 1: UFH not commissioned before installation (most common)**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Problems appeared within weeks or months of installation; the floor covering was laid over a screed that had not been through a commissioning cycle
- *Solution:* This is an installation defect. The floor covering may need to be lifted, the screed properly commissioned and dried, and the floor covering re-installed.

**Cause 2: Surface temperature exceeding 27°C**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Floor surface is uncomfortably hot to touch; cracking or gapping worsening progressively
- *Solution:* Reduce UFH flow temperature immediately; install a floor-limiting thermostat with a probe in the floor; verify surface temperature with a thermometer.

**Cause 3: Movement joints omitted over UFH**
- *Distinguishing sign:* Cracking in tile floor over UFH at regular intervals; debonding tiles
- *Solution:* Professional assessment; likely requires re-tiling with 3m × 3m movement joints.

*See Lexicon 11, Section 17.*

### H2. UFH is on but the floor does not feel warm

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. "What floor covering is installed over the UFH?"
2. "How thick is the floor covering and any underlay?"

**Most likely cause: Floor covering thermal resistance too high**
- *Distinguishing sign:* An adjacent room with tile or stone heats noticeably better; the UFH system is working (pipes are warm at the manifold)
- *Calculation check:* Ask what the floor covering and underlay are; calculate or estimate R-value; if the combined R-value exceeds 0.15 m²K/W, the floor covering is blocking the heat
- *Solution:* Short-term: increase UFH flow temperature (check this doesn't push surface temperature above 27°C). Long-term: when the floor covering is replaced, specify a lower R-value product.

*See Lexicon 11, Sections 5 and 17.4.*

## SECTION I — PROBLEM PREVENTION — WHEN TO ACT NOW

### I1. Red flag symptoms requiring immediate action

| Symptom | What it could mean | Act now because... |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Water pooling under floor covering | Active moisture source destroying the subfloor | Structural damage accelerates rapidly |
| Mould or musty smell beneath floor | Mould in subfloor/backing | Health risk; structural damage |
| Tiles cracking progressively | Movement joint failure or structural movement | Floor may become unsafe |
| Wood floor buckling significantly | Major moisture event or expansion failure | Risk of trip hazard; structural damage |
| Tiles hollow over UFH | Adhesive failure over UFH | Thermal cycling will worsen rapidly |
| Any floor failure with electric UFH | Risk of electrical fault | Safety risk; turn off UFH; seek professional inspection |

## SECTION J — "I'M NOT SURE WHAT'S WRONG" — THE GENERAL DIAGNOSTIC QUESTIONS

When a customer describes a problem without enough detail to categorise, use this sequence:

1. **"What type of floor covering is it?"** — narrows the lexicon
2. **"How long has the floor been down?"** — new problems vs age-related wear
3. **"Where in the house is the problem?"** — room type narrows likely causes (bathroom → moisture; kitchen → moisture or impact; hallway → wear)
4. **"Can you describe exactly what you see?"** — visual; tactile; audible symptoms
5. **"Has anything changed recently?"** — water event; cleaning method change; furniture moved; UFH switched on for winter
6. **"Did anything happen that might have caused this?"** — flood; dropped object; building work nearby

| "My carpet has waves/ripples" | Insufficient stretching or humidity | Check if seasonal; if not → professional re-stretch |

| "My laminate has raised edges at joins" | Edge swell from moisture — permanent | Replace affected boards; fix moisture source |
| "My LVT has yellow staining" | Rubber mat reaction — permanent | Remove rubber; replace stained tiles |
| "My LVT has bubbles" | Moisture osmosis or no rolling at install | Test subfloor RH; re-bond or lift and relay |
